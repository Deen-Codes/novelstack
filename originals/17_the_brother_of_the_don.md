## Chapter 1 — The Brother

The man at the desk was wearing a black suit with no tie, and he was not Niccolò Castellano.

Adriana noticed this in the order she noticed things, which was the order her grandmother had taught her — *first the door, Adi, then the window, then the people, and the people always last, because the people will be the thing that hurts you and you'll want to know your exits first.* She had noticed the door. She had noticed the window. She had noticed her father, beside her, looking at his shoes.

Then she had looked at the man at the desk, and the man at the desk had not been Niccolò.

The Castellano front office in Brooklyn had two desks, only one of which was ever used. The desk that was never used was Vito's, the family's *consigliere*, who preferred to take his meetings in the back room with espresso and a chair that did not face a window. The desk that was used was Niccolò's. The desk that was used had, on it, always, in this order: a leather blotter, a Montblanc fountain pen lying parallel to the top edge of the blotter, a single Murano paperweight in the colour of a Sicilian sea, and a small silver dish for ash.

The desk Adriana was looking at had, on it, in this order: a leather blotter, a Montblanc fountain pen, no paperweight, and an ashtray that was not the Castellano family ashtray. The ashtray had been brought in. There was already ash in it.

The man at the desk was the one smoking.

—

He was, perhaps, thirty-six. He was taller than Niccolò by half a hand. His hair was darker than Niccolò's — black where Niccolò's had a brown edge — cut shorter, the cut of a man who did not let a barber decide. His eyes were not Niccolò's espresso eyes. His eyes were the colour of a wet Brooklyn pavement in November, slate-grey with something blacker underneath, and they did not, when Adriana came in, look up.

He was reading.

He was reading the contract her sister had signed eight months ago.

He was reading it the way a man reads a contract he has read many times before and is now checking one specific clause for one specific reason.

Adriana looked at his hands.

She hated that she looked at his hands. Sienna had warned her about the Castellano hands. Sienna had said, on the phone from the Hamptons house in May, the night before Adriana had signed her own contract, *Adi, listen to me, do not look at the hands.* And Adriana had said, *what hands*, and Sienna had said, very quietly, *you'll know.*

She knew.

The man at the desk had the Castellano hands. Long fingers. The signet ring on the smallest finger of his right hand — the same Castellano crest, four generations worn — although on this hand the ring sat a little higher on the finger, as if the finger had once been broken and the ring no longer travelled all the way down.

He turned a page of the contract.

He did not look up.

—

Adriana's father said, very softly, in Sicilian, into the silence, '*Adi. Sit down.*'

Adriana did not sit down.

Adriana said, in English, in the careful neutral voice she had built for the Castellano house over eight months, 'I'm sorry. I was expecting Mr Castellano.'

The man at the desk turned a second page.

The man at the desk said, without looking up, 'You're looking at him.'

His voice was lower than Niccolò's. Quieter. The voice of a man whose silence had been the loudest thing in a room for so long he no longer remembered what it had felt like to need to fill it.

Adriana said, 'I was expecting Niccolò.'

The man at the desk closed the contract.

He laid it flat on the blotter. He placed the Montblanc on top of it, parallel to the top edge. He stubbed out his cigarette in the ashtray that was not the Castellano family ashtray, with a small precise pressure, and he looked up at her.

The pavement-coloured eyes met hers.

He said, 'Niccolò is in Rome until Tuesday. I am taking his appointments while he is gone.' He paused. He added, with the very faintest tilt of his head, 'I am his brother.'

—

Adriana's father made a sound.

It was a small sound. It was the sound a man makes when he has, for the first time in many years, met something he had thought was dead.

Adriana's father said, in Sicilian, '*Madonna mia. Renzo.*'

The man at the desk — Renzo, his name was Renzo, Adriana filed this immediately under *facts I will need later* — flicked his pavement-coloured eyes towards her father, and the corner of his mouth moved, slightly, as if he had remembered an old joke and decided against telling it.

Renzo said, in Sicilian, '*Salve, Russo. Sit. Both of you. We have a contract to discuss.*'

Adriana's father sat.

Adriana did not sit.

Adriana said, in English, 'I beg your pardon. Niccolò Castellano does not have a brother. Niccolò Castellano is the only son. I have signed the family tree as part of my own contract eight months ago. There was no brother on the tree.'

Renzo looked at her.

He looked at her the way a chess player looks at a board on which something has been moved that he did not move.

He said, 'You signed Vito Castellano's family tree. Vito Castellano is the *consigliere*. The tree he keeps is the official tree. The official tree omits — for what Vito would call good and sufficient reasons — the elder son. The elder son was sent away when he was twenty-eight. The elder son was reported dead in Catania three years ago. The elder son was, at that time, my preferred status.'

He paused.

He said, 'It is no longer my preferred status.'

—

Adriana, in the part of her brain that was still standing in the doorway and counting exits, thought, *this is the part of the conversation in which I do not panic.* She had a list, in her head, of conversations in which she had not panicked. The night her mother had taken the call about her brother. The morning her father had told her about Sienna's contract. The morning, four months later, she had been told that her contract was also required. She added Renzo to the list now, in advance. She wrote the line *Friday the seventeenth, October. Brooklyn. The brother.* She placed the line under the others.

She breathed, once, slowly, through her nose.

Then she said, 'I came here to discuss my sister's release. Sienna's contract terminates next Wednesday. I have brought, with my father, the agreed-upon release documents. I understood I was meeting Niccolò.'

Renzo opened a folder Adriana had not noticed on the desk.

He took out a single sheet of paper.

He laid it on the blotter, on top of the contract.

He said, 'You are not meeting Niccolò. You are meeting me. And Sienna's release is not the agreement you came here for.'

Adriana looked at the single sheet of paper.

The single sheet of paper was an addendum to her sister's contract.

The addendum was on Castellano family letterhead.

The addendum was signed.

The addendum was signed by Sienna.

The addendum was signed by Sienna in handwriting that Adriana, who had been signed her own sister's birthday cards by Sienna for twenty-six years, knew at a glance.

The signature on the addendum was Sienna's. The date next to it was last Friday.

Adriana said, very quietly, 'When did she sign that.'

Renzo said, 'Last Friday. In Rome. While Niccolò was in a meeting at the Vatican. I am told she signed it without consulting either Niccolò or your family. I am told she did so on the understanding that it would, when produced, terminate the original contract early.'

Adriana said, 'Does it.'

Renzo said, 'It does.'

Adriana said, 'Then why am I here.'

Renzo looked at her.

The corner of his mouth moved again, slightly.

He said, 'Because the addendum your sister signed in Rome has a clause that releases her, in exchange for a substitute. The substitute is named in clause 6(b). Sienna, when she signed, knew the name. Niccolò, when he reads this addendum on Tuesday, will not be permitted to alter it. The name is yours.'

—

There was, for a moment, no sound in the room.

Adriana's father, very slowly, lowered his face into his hands.

Outside the window, a horn sounded, two blocks away, and stopped.

Renzo did not move.

Renzo's pavement-grey eyes were on Adriana's face. They had not, since he had looked up at her, left her face. They had the particular quality, Adriana realised, of eyes that had not, in three years, been allowed to rest on someone they had been looking for.

Adriana said, '*Sienna signed me into this.*'

Renzo said, 'Yes.'

Adriana said, 'For how long.'

Renzo said, 'Six months. Same terms as her original. Identical except for one clause.'

Adriana said, 'Which clause.'

Renzo said, '11.4.'

Adriana, whose sister had told her — in the back of a black car coming over the Verrazzano Bridge in October, eight months ago, on the night Adriana's own contract was first being discussed — that clause 11.4 of the Castellano standard contract was the clause that had quietly, without anyone's permission, made Sienna the unalterable property of the man who had altered it back, said:

'I have read clause 11.4.'

Renzo said, 'You have read Niccolò's version of clause 11.4. The version in the addendum is mine.'

He pushed the single sheet of paper across the desk towards her.

He laid the Montblanc beside it, parallel to the top edge.

He said, 'You may read it now. You may decline, in which case your sister's release does not occur, and you may walk out of this room and your sister will remain at the Hamptons house under the original contract for the remaining seven days. You may sign, in which case your sister is on a flight back to Bensonhurst this evening and you are upstairs by tonight. You will not negotiate. You will not amend. There is no version in which you alter this clause and I initial the alteration. Do you understand.'

Adriana understood.

She understood with a clarity that arrived, in the back of her brain, the way her grandmother's voice had arrived all her life when she needed it — *first the door, Adi, then the window.*

The door was behind her.

The window was to her left.

The man at the desk was watching her with eyes the colour of a Brooklyn pavement after rain, and Adriana, who had been told her whole life that she had her grandmother's nerve and her sister's mouth and her father's eyes, looked at him for what felt like a long time but was, in fact, the length of four seconds. She looked at his hands on the desk. She looked at the cigarette he had stubbed out in the wrong ashtray. She looked at the higher-set signet ring on the smallest finger of his right hand, and she thought, in the part of her brain that was not yet asking any of the questions she would shortly need to ask, *whose finger broke that ring. And when. And why was it not reset.*

Then she walked across the room.

She did not look at her father.

She picked up the Montblanc.

She read clause 11.4 of the addendum.

She read it twice.

She put the pen back down on the blotter, parallel to the top edge of the page.

She said, in the careful neutral voice she had built for the Castellano house over eight months, the voice that had not, in eight months, let her down,

'You wrote this so I would have to ask you a question.'

Renzo said, 'Yes.'

Adriana said, 'I will not ask it.'

Renzo said, 'You will. Just not today.'

He paused.

He said, 'Sign, Miss Russo.'

She signed.

The pen made a small precise sound against the paper. Adriana wrote her name the way her grandmother had taught her — full first name, full middle, full surname, the loop on the *A* of *Adriana* the same loop she had been writing since she was nine — and she pushed the paper across the desk to Renzo, and she stood up, and she looked at her father, who would not meet her eyes, and she said, in Sicilian, the only Sicilian she ever spoke to him —

'*Papa, take Sienna home.*'

Her father stood up.

Her father walked to the door without looking at Renzo.

Her father, with his hand on the door handle, said, in Sicilian, in a voice Adriana had not heard him use since the funeral of his own father, '*Renzo Castellano. I will not forget that this was you.*'

Renzo said, in Sicilian, very quietly, '*You will not need to, Russo. I won't either.*'

The door opened.

The door closed.

—

Adriana and Renzo Castellano were alone in the room.

Renzo did not stand up.

Renzo, very slowly, refolded the addendum she had signed and placed it into a leather portfolio that had been waiting in the top drawer of the desk all morning. He locked the drawer. He put the key into the inside pocket of his black suit jacket. He looked up at her.

He said, 'There is a car for you. It is waiting at the back. Niccolò keeps a suite at the top of the house. You will use it until I have decided otherwise. McCance — the housekeeper, you have not met her yet, you will — will show you up.'

Adriana said, 'I will not be staying in Niccolò's suite.'

Renzo said, 'Then McCance will show you up to the one you will be staying in.'

He stood.

He was, Adriana now confirmed, taller than Niccolò by half a hand.

He said, in the same quiet voice that he had not raised for the entire conversation, that he would not raise, Adriana suspected, in any conversation he would have with her for the entire six months that were about to follow,

'Adriana.'

It was the first time he had used her name.

She had not given him her name.

She did not, this time, correct him.

She said, 'Yes.'

He said, 'The question you have decided not to ask me today — I am going to want you to ask me, eventually, and when you do, I am going to answer you truthfully. You should know that in advance. So that, when the time comes, you know what you are doing.'

She looked at him.

The pavement-grey eyes did not move.

She said, 'I do know what I am doing.'

He said, 'Good.'

Then Renzo Castellano sat back down at his brother's desk, and lit another cigarette, and did not look at her again, and Adriana Russo — who had not, in eight months at the Castellano house, asked a single question she had not first written down on the back of a receipt and folded into the lining of her coat — walked out of the front office, into the corridor, and up the stairs of the Castellano family compound to a suite she had not known existed, in a house she had thought she knew the floor plan of, in the year that her life — although she did not yet know this — was about to be undone, very quietly, by the brother no one had been allowed to mention, in a contract her sister had signed without permission, on a Friday morning in October.

On the stairs, halfway up, she stopped.

She put one hand on the banister.

She said, into the empty corridor, in Sicilian, very softly, the same line her sister had said on the Verrazzano Bridge eight months earlier:

'*Oh, you absolute prick.*'

Then she went up.
