# 06 · The Housekeeper's Lie

**Author:** NovelStack Originals (@novelstackoriginals)
**Genre:** Thriller
**Status to set:** Ongoing
**Mature:** No
**Description (paste into Details tab):**

> Maeve takes a job cleaning for a wealthy couple in a Belgravia townhouse. Three weeks in, she finds a photograph of herself in the wife's bedside drawer, taken when she was twelve. The wife she's been working for, six days a week, has been waiting for her.

---

## Chapter 1 — The Drawer

The photograph was in the drawer of the bedside table on the wife's side of the bed. The one with the lavender hand cream and the rolled-up silk eye mask and the half-empty pack of Nytol that Maeve had been politely not noticing for three weeks.

She had not meant to look.

She had been changing the sheets. The drawer was open. The drawer had not been open when she came in — Maeve was certain of that, she was always certain of that, it was one of the things she was paid to be certain of — and now the drawer was open, and there was a photograph on top of the silk eye mask, face-up.

Maeve looked at the photograph.

The photograph was of a girl, maybe twelve, maybe thirteen, in a school uniform Maeve recognised. The girl's hair was pulled back in a French plait that someone else had done for her, badly, the left side flattening down where it shouldn't have. There was a small chip in the girl's front tooth, just visible because the girl was almost-smiling, the way kids do when someone they don't quite trust is taking the photo.

The school uniform was St Brigid's. Navy blazer. Red piping. The badge on the breast pocket sewn on slightly crooked because Maeve's mother had sewn it on at eleven o'clock the night before the first day of Year 8, and Maeve's mother had been three glasses in by then, and had said *that's good enough, it's good enough,* and had gone to bed.

Maeve dropped the sheet she was holding.

The girl in the photograph was Maeve.

—

Maeve's hands did not shake. This was a thing she had taught them, over years, not to do. They did not shake when her mother died. They did not shake when the man at the council told her there was nothing they could do about the rent. They did not shake when the agency phoned to say the Belgravia couple had specifically asked for her, by name, and the bonus would be three hundred pounds a week on top of the agency rate, and she had said yes before she had finished the call because three hundred pounds a week was three hundred pounds a week.

Her hands did not shake now.

She picked the photograph up by the corner, the way she had picked everything up by the corner her whole life, because that was how you did it if you wanted to keep something nice. She held it up. She turned it over.

On the back, in handwriting Maeve did not recognise, in blue biro, was written:

*Maeve Brennan. Age 12. St Brigid's, autumn term 2009.*

Underneath, in a different hand — same blue biro, smaller, sharper:

*Found her.*

—

The first day Mrs Wakeham had opened the door of the townhouse to Maeve, three weeks ago to the day, she had said, 'Oh. Yes. Come in.'

Mrs Wakeham was in her early fifties. Mrs Wakeham was thin in the way that some women in Belgravia were thin — not from happiness, not from health, from a kind of vigilance. She wore cashmere in a colour that was somewhere between dove and bone. She had small expensive hands.

She had said, that first day, while Maeve stood in the marble hallway with her cleaning kit and her shoes off because the agency notes had said shoes off, 'You're younger than I thought.'

Maeve had said, 'I'm twenty-eight.'

Mrs Wakeham had said, 'No, that's about right. I was thinking of you as a girl, somehow.'

Maeve had not known what to say to that. She had said, 'Where should I start?'

Mrs Wakeham had said, 'Anywhere. You decide. I'm sure you know what you're doing.'

And Mrs Wakeham had walked away down the long marble hallway in her cashmere the colour of bone, and Maeve had thought, *she's lonely, that one,* and had started in the kitchen because the kitchen was always where you started, and had not thought any more about *I was thinking of you as a girl, somehow,* until three weeks later when she opened a drawer she had not opened, and saw a photograph she had not been meant to see, and read, on the back, in handwriting she did not know:

*Found her.*

—

She had to leave the bedroom. She could not leave the bedroom with the sheets half-done. She had to finish the sheets first. She had to finish the sheets first because if she didn't finish the sheets first, Mrs Wakeham would know that something had happened, and Maeve had not yet decided what Mrs Wakeham was allowed to know.

She put the photograph back into the drawer. She did not slide the drawer shut. She left it the way she had found it — open, the photograph face-up on the silk eye mask, the lavender hand cream upright, the half-empty pack of Nytol slightly askew. She tucked the corner of the fitted sheet around the mattress. She smoothed the duvet. She plumped the four pillows in the order she always plumped them — the two small ones first, then the two large ones, then the small ones back on top — and she stood in the doorway, and she looked at the bed, and she made it look as if no one in this room had done anything other than change the sheets.

Then she went downstairs.

Mrs Wakeham was in the morning room with a cup of green tea and a book she was not reading. Maeve knew Mrs Wakeham was not reading the book because Mrs Wakeham had been on page 47 of *The Salt-Wind Almanac* for nine days, and Maeve had been dusting around the book for nine days, and the bookmark had not moved.

Maeve said, 'Mrs Wakeham, I've finished upstairs. I'll do the front room next.'

Mrs Wakeham looked up from the book she was not reading.

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Maeve, dear. Have you got a moment? Sit down. I think we should have a little chat.'

Maeve's hands did not shake.

She sat down on the small embroidered chair opposite Mrs Wakeham, the chair that Mrs Wakeham had once told her had been her grandmother's, a wedding gift in 1947, *please do sit on it, that's what it's for, dear,* and she folded her hands in her lap.

She said, 'Of course, Mrs Wakeham. What about?'

Mrs Wakeham put her cup of green tea down on the marble side table. The cup made a small definite sound against the marble. The sound was the sound of a woman who had decided, some time ago, exactly when she was going to put the cup down.

Mrs Wakeham said, 'You were a pupil at St Brigid's, weren't you, dear. From 2008. Autumn term, Year 8.'

Maeve looked at Mrs Wakeham.

Mrs Wakeham looked back.

Mrs Wakeham said, very gently, 'I've been waiting for you for a very long time, Maeve. Please don't be frightened. We have rather a lot to talk about.'

And in the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse, on a Tuesday in November, with the cup of green tea on the marble side table making its small definite sound against the silence, Maeve Brennan — who had not let her hands shake when her mother died, who had not let her hands shake when the council man had said *there is nothing we can do*, who had not let her hands shake when she had picked up a photograph of her twelve-year-old self out of a drawer she had not been meant to open — Maeve Brennan felt her hands begin, very quietly, to shake.

She did not stop them.

She said, 'Who are you.'

## Chapter 2 — The Chat

Mrs Wakeham did not answer the question.

Mrs Wakeham picked up her cup of green tea, instead, and turned it three small degrees on the marble side table so that the handle pointed back the way it had been pointing before she had set it down. Mrs Wakeham, Maeve had noticed in three weeks, did this with cups. Mrs Wakeham did this with the fruit bowl on the kitchen island. Mrs Wakeham did this with the salt and pepper grinders on the dining-room sideboard. Mrs Wakeham was a woman who, having put a small object down in a small wrong way, would always — eventually — turn it back.

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Don't ask me that one yet, dear. I shall answer it. I shall answer it today. I should rather you let me get to it in my own order. Will you do that.'

Maeve said nothing.

Mrs Wakeham said, very gently, 'Maeve. I am not going to hurt you. I have never been going to hurt you. I have, on the contrary, spent the last sixteen years arranging a small number of things in this house so that no harm might come to you when you arrived. If you would like the door of this room to be unlocked for you at any moment — it is, by the way, not locked — you may stand up and leave. The agency will pay you for the morning. I shall not telephone them. I shall not come after you. You will not, however, ever know why I had the photograph in the drawer, and that, my darling, would be a great pity for both of us.'

She paused.

She said, '*My darling.* I should not have said that. Not yet. I am sorry.'

She put the cup down.

She turned the handle back two degrees.

She folded her hands in her lap.

—

Maeve stood in the doorway of the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse on the Tuesday in November with her right hand still on the brass door handle of the inside of the door, and Maeve thought, in the very precise way Maeve had taught herself to think about things she had not yet decided, *if I leave this room I will spend the rest of my life wondering what she was going to say.*

And underneath that, in a smaller harder voice — the voice Maeve had developed in the last year of her mother's life, the voice Maeve had used to negotiate the rent and to bury her mother and to phone the agency about a placement she could not afford to lose — *and I cannot afford to spend the rest of my life wondering anything.*

She took her hand off the door handle.

She walked across the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse, in the careful neutral steps she had practised on the front-room runner of her own bedsit in Lewisham, in front of the small wall mirror, every morning for three weeks before her first day at the agency, and she sat down on the small embroidered chair opposite Mrs Wakeham — the chair that Mrs Wakeham had told her, on her first day, had been a wedding gift in 1947 from her grandmother.

She folded her hands in her own lap.

She said, 'I am sitting down, Mrs Wakeham. I have not, however, agreed to anything else.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Thank you, dear.'

—

Mrs Wakeham then, in the voice she used at her dressmaker — the small unhurried voice of a woman who had not, in her life, ever needed to finish a sentence quickly — said, 'Six things, Maeve. I shall tell you six things. I have written them down upstairs. I have rehearsed them in front of the dressing-table mirror every Saturday morning for the last eleven years. I am about to be very tedious. You will forgive me. The order matters. May I.'

Maeve said, 'You may.'

Mrs Wakeham said, '*One.* I was at St Brigid's. I was at St Brigid's between 1969 and 1976. I was there as a boarder. The blazer in the photograph is the blazer I wore. The red piping has been the same red piping on the St Brigid's blazer for fifty-six years. I would know it in a darkened room.'

Maeve said, 'I was day, not boarder.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Yes, dear. From the photograph, I had inferred you must have been. The board children kept their badges differently. Yours is sewn the way the day girls sewed theirs, on a Sunday evening, by tired mothers.'

She paused.

She said, '*Two.* I was at school with your mother.'

—

Maeve's hands, in her lap, made the small involuntary movement they had made in the bedroom upstairs.

Maeve did not put them on top of one another. Maeve let them keep moving. Maeve had decided, on the way down the stairs from the bedroom that morning, that the next time her hands began to do this, Maeve would let them, and Maeve would not pretend she had not noticed.

She said, in the careful neutral voice she had practised in front of the small wall mirror in Lewisham, 'My mother was at St Brigid's.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Yes, dear.'

Maeve said, 'My mother never told me that.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'No, dear. I am not surprised. I should imagine your mother told you very little about St Brigid's at all.'

She paused.

She said, '*Three.* Your mother and I shared a room from the autumn of 1972 to the spring of 1975. She was a day girl turned boarder when her father — your grandfather — was posted to Aden in the army for the third time, and her mother — your grandmother — followed him out. Your mother was put in the small room at the end of the east corridor. I had been alone in that room for two terms because the girl I had been sharing with had been expelled in the May for an offence I am not, today, going to bore you with.'

She paused.

She said, '*Four.* Your mother and I were, for two and a half years, the closest two people I have ever been to anyone in my life. Closer than I was to my husband. Closer than I was to my sister. Closer than I have ever been to any single other person, and there were, my dear, a great many of them.'

She did not, when she said this, look away from Maeve.

She let her face carry the line.

Her face, on the line, was the face of a woman whose dressmaker had taught her, in 1981, that the only thing more vulgar than not meaning what you said was looking as if you did not.

—

Maeve sat in the small embroidered chair, and Maeve thought, in the very precise way Maeve had been taught by her mother to think about things her mother had not told her, *Mum. Mum, what did you do.*

The thought arrived, this time, in a different voice from the one it had arrived in three weeks ago in Maeve's own kitchen in Lewisham. The voice was Maeve's voice. The voice had always been Maeve's voice. The voice, this morning, on the small embroidered chair of the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse, was the voice of a woman who had just understood that the question — *Mum, what did you do* — had not, in fact, started with Maeve. The question had started, in 1976, in a small room at the end of the east corridor at a school in Hampshire that Maeve's mother had never, in thirty-five years, mentioned by name.

She said, 'Go on, Mrs Wakeham.'

Mrs Wakeham said, '*Five.* In the spring of 1975 something happened at St Brigid's that the school spent the next nine years arranging itself around the not-happening of. Your mother and I were the only two people in the school, at that time, to whom that thing happened. We left St Brigid's together in the summer of 1975. We agreed, on the train from Petersfield to Waterloo on the morning we left, that we would never speak of it to anyone — not to our families, not to our husbands, not to each other, and most particularly not, ever, to any child either of us would, in the years to come, have. We agreed this on the train. We shook hands on it in the carriage. The carriage was empty. I have not, in fifty years, broken the agreement.'

Maeve said, 'And my mother.'

Mrs Wakeham said, very gently, 'No, dear. As far as I know, she did not break it either.'

She paused.

She said, '*Six.* Two years ago, in the spring of 2023, I received a letter from a man I had never heard of. The letter was very civil. The letter said that he was clearing the contents of his mother's house after her death, and he had found, in a sealed envelope at the bottom of his mother's writing desk, a single photograph and a single typed sheet, and he had, with very great reluctance, opened both. He apologised for opening them. He said that on the back of the photograph, in his mother's hand, was written *Brigid's, autumn 2009. The Brennan girl.* He said that on the typed sheet, in his mother's hand, was written my address in Belgravia, my full name, and a single line that read *if you ever see this child, you have one last opportunity to do the right thing.*'

She paused.

She said, 'The photograph in the letter was the photograph in my drawer upstairs.'

She paused.

She said, 'The dead woman was the third person at St Brigid's in 1975, dear. The third person who, your mother and I had agreed, on the train from Petersfield to Waterloo, never existed.'

—

Maeve did not, when Mrs Wakeham finished speaking, say anything.

The morning room of the Belgravia townhouse was, for a long second, very silent. The clock on the marble mantelpiece behind Mrs Wakeham made a small mechanical sound.

The clock on the marble mantelpiece of the front room of the Belgravia townhouse, Maeve had observed in three weeks, was a Knibb mantel clock from 1789 — Mrs Wakeham had told her this on the first morning Maeve had dusted it, in the small precise voice of a woman who liked it to be known that the clock was a Knibb mantel clock from 1789 — and the clock had, for the entirety of Maeve's three weeks at the house, been wound to the same particular accuracy every Friday at five o'clock by Mr Wakeham, who, every Friday at five o'clock, came in from his study, opened the glass front of the clock with a small brass key he carried on his fob, wound the clock four full turns, closed the glass front, and went back to his study without speaking to either of them.

Mr Wakeham was, this morning, not at home.

Mr Wakeham was, Maeve had been told the previous evening, in Geneva.

Mr Wakeham had been, every other Tuesday of the three weeks Maeve had been at the house, in Geneva. *He goes a great deal for the bank, dear,* Mrs Wakeham had said the first time, *please don't keep his room aired, he likes to find it dusty when he comes home, it makes him feel he has gone somewhere worth going.*

The clock made its small mechanical sound again.

Maeve said, very calmly, 'Mrs Wakeham. Was the third person at St Brigid's in 1975 my mother's friend, or yours.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Hers.'

Maeve said, 'And what happened to her.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'I shall tell you. But not in this room. Not by speaking. There is a manuscript upstairs, dear, that I have been writing since the spring of 2023. The manuscript is in the second drawer of the desk in the south study. I have not, in two years, shown it to a living soul. I had not, in fact, decided whether I was going to. I had been waiting to know whether you would come, and what you would be like when you came, and whether you would be the kind of person to whom a thing of that kind could responsibly be given.'

She paused.

She said, very gently, 'You are.'

She paused.

She said, 'Will you read it, dear.'

—

Maeve sat in the small embroidered chair in the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse, with her hands quietly making in her lap the small movement she had not, today, stopped them from making, and Maeve listened, in the small precise voice that had risen up in her on the train from Lewisham that morning — the voice that had said *first the kitchen, then the bedrooms, then go home and sleep, and do not, on any account, look in any drawer you have not been asked to clean* — and the small precise voice said, very clearly, *Maeve. Read it. Read it today. Read it before Mr Wakeham comes back from Geneva.*

She said, 'I will read it.'

Mrs Wakeham said, 'Thank you, dear.'

Mrs Wakeham, very slowly, lifted her cup of green tea from the marble side table, and turned the handle back the way it had originally been pointing, and held the cup for a long moment in both of her thin expensive hands, and did not drink from it.

She said, almost to herself, in a voice that for the first time that morning was not the unhurried voice of her dressmaker —

'Your mother, dear. Your mother was the bravest person I have ever known. I think you will need to be told that, today, before you read it.'

She set the cup down.

She turned the handle two degrees back the wrong way.

She did not, this time, turn it back.

Then Mrs Wakeham stood up — slowly, with the careful particular slowness of a woman in cashmere the colour of bone who knew exactly what her knees were going to do on her — and Mrs Wakeham crossed the morning room, and Mrs Wakeham held the door open for Maeve, and Mrs Wakeham said, '*The south study, dear. Top of the stairs, second door on the right. The desk is in the alcove. The second drawer is unlocked. The manuscript is in a green ribbon. I shall be in the morning room when you come down. You shall not be interrupted. I have given the cook the day off.*'

And Maeve Brennan, who had not known her mother had been at St Brigid's, who had not known that any of this had been waiting for her at the bottom of a drawer in a bedside table in a Belgravia townhouse for sixteen years, who had been telling herself, on the train from Lewisham that morning, *don't get attached to it, Maeve, three hundred pounds a week is not a thing that exists in this world for women like you, it will end, it will end badly, it always does* — Maeve Brennan walked up the marble staircase of the Belgravia townhouse, on the Tuesday in November, towards the second drawer of the desk in the alcove of the south study, in which had been waiting, for two years, the manuscript Mrs Wakeham had not, in two years, shown to a living soul.

The clock on the marble mantelpiece behind her made one more small mechanical sound.

She did not, this time, hear it.

## Chapter 3 — The South Study

The south study was at the front of the house, on the first floor, with one tall sash window that looked down into the dry plane trees of the square.

It was, Maeve had observed in three weeks of dusting, the only room in the Belgravia townhouse in which Mrs Wakeham kept her own things rather than the family things — *the family things* being, in the precise grammar of Belgravia, the silver and the porcelain and the framed Royal Academy watercolours of horses that had been in the Wakeham family since approximately the moment the Wakeham family had decided to have things. The south study contained, by contrast: a green-leather chair Maeve had been told by Mrs Wakeham not to dust further than the seat *because the back is dyed with the wrong dye and will lift,* a Pre-Raphaelite drawing of two women under a tree that Mrs Wakeham had bought at an auction in 2004 and which Mr Wakeham had not, by Mrs Wakeham's account, ever quite forgiven, and a writing desk.

The desk was a small walnut Davenport.

The Davenport had three drawers on the right-hand side, two drawers on the left, and one shallow secret drawer that — Mrs Wakeham had pointed out on Maeve's first morning, with the small particular pride of a woman who had been the only person in her own house to know how to open it — slid out when one pressed a moulded bead on the inside of the right-hand top drawer.

Maeve had not, in three weeks, opened the secret drawer.

Maeve had not, in three weeks, opened any of the drawers of the Davenport.

Maeve had been instructed, very politely, on her first morning, that *the Davenport is mine, dear, I'm afraid — you may dust the top and the legs but the drawers and the inside, dear, are not the sort of thing the agency would want you putting yourself in.*

Maeve, on the first morning, had said, *yes, Mrs Wakeham.*

—

Maeve opened the second drawer of the right-hand side of the Davenport.

The drawer was unlocked.

The drawer had, on top, a single sheet of cream-coloured A4 paper with a typed line at the top of it that read, in the typeface of an old Imperial typewriter that Maeve had noticed in the corner of the south study three weeks ago and had assumed was decorative:

> *For Maeve Brennan, who I have always known would, one Tuesday, come up the stairs of my house and read this.*

> *Eleanor Wakeham, née Dorrien.*
> *Belgravia, the second of April 2023.*

> *Read at your own pace, my darling. Do not read it quickly. There is nothing in here that needs to be hurried. There is, on the contrary, a great deal that needs to be very slowly believed.*

Maeve sat down in the green-leather chair at the writing desk of the south study of the Belgravia townhouse with the single typed sheet in her left hand, and Maeve breathed once, slowly, through her nose, the way Maeve had been taught to do at the dentist when she was nine and the way Maeve had been doing all morning — and Maeve lifted the cream-coloured A4 sheet, and underneath it, in a green silk ribbon tied in a single careful bow, was a typed manuscript of perhaps three hundred pages.

The manuscript was titled, at the top of the first page, in the same Imperial-typewriter typeface:

> *THE LIE WE AGREED TO.*
> *A memoir, of sorts, by Eleanor Dorrien Wakeham.*
> *To be read, one day, by Maeve Brennan.*

—

Maeve untied the green silk bow.

She did not, when she untied it, untie it quickly. Maeve had been taught by her mother how to untie a ribbon. *You always undo a ribbon, Maeve, by pulling the long end. The long end. Look — see how the bow disappears when you pull the long end? If you pull the short one you make a knot, and then you've a knot. Always the long end, darling.* Maeve had pulled the long end of the green silk bow with her thumb and forefinger, and the bow had disappeared the way her mother had said it would, and Maeve had laid the ribbon down on the leather inlay of the writing desk, and Maeve had turned to the first page of the manuscript, and Maeve had read.

The first page of the manuscript read:

> *I shall begin with the third girl, because she is the part I have never been able to make myself begin with, and the only way I have ever been able to begin a thing I cannot begin is to begin with it.*
>
> *Her name was Tessa Halligan. She was a day girl. Her father was a maths teacher at the local grammar; her mother kept the house. They lived in a small terrace on the edge of Petersfield, where I had been to tea once, in my first term, in a kitchen that smelled of coal-tar soap and the small unfailing kindness of a mother who had not quite worked out that her daughter had been put, by accident, into a school full of girls whose fathers wore boots one wouldn't, oneself, ever own. Tessa Halligan had a small chip in her front tooth. Tessa Halligan had a French plait her mother did for her, on a Sunday evening, badly, the same way I have noticed, in the photograph that has been in my drawer for sixteen years, your own mother used to do yours. Tessa Halligan was the third of us in the small room at the end of the east corridor, between November 1973 and the spring of 1975, on the nights when she was allowed to stay over, which were Fridays, and on the Sundays after Sports when she didn't feel like the bus home.*
>
> *I begin with Tessa because Tessa was the bravest of us, and Tessa was the youngest of us, and Tessa is the one who, on the night of the second of April 1975, did the thing that the three of us had spent the autumn agreeing that one of us would, one day, have to do, and which neither I nor your mother had quite been able to make ourselves believe, until that night, that any one of us was going to.*
>
> *Tessa Halligan went into the headmaster's study, at twenty past nine on the evening of the second of April 1975, with a letter she had typed on her father's Olivetti on the previous Sunday at home, and Tessa Halligan said the thing aloud.*
>
> *She said it to the right person.*
>
> *The right person was the one person at St Brigid's who, your mother and I would understand by the end of the night, could not, must not, would not, ever, be the person it was said to.*
>
> *Tessa did not come back to the room.*
>
> *I am going to tell you, today, where she went instead. I am going to tell you what your mother and I did, on the morning of the third of April 1975, when she had not come back. I am going to tell you what I did, on the night of the fourth, in the small room at the end of the east corridor with your mother sitting on the edge of the bed and her hands very white in her lap, that has made me unable to look any one woman of my age and class in the eye for fifty years. I am going to tell you, last, what the third person to whom the agreement was made — the woman in the letter I have been sent in the spring of 2023 by her son in Surrey, the woman who has gone, now, where Tessa went — was, then, to me and to your mother, and what she did, and what she made us agree to that we should never, on any account, have agreed to.*
>
> *And I am going to tell you, my darling, why this is the manuscript I had to write for you, and not the conversation I could have had with you, downstairs, in the morning room. The reason is very simple. The reason is that I cannot, even today, say any of this aloud.*
>
> *I am eighty-one. I am going to die in this house, very probably this winter, of a small thing in my chest the consultant has been quietly explaining to me for eleven months. I have arranged my affairs. There is one thing I have not arranged. That thing is you. I have been arranging the conditions of the arrangement of you, my darling, with the care of an old woman who has not done anything well, in her life, that she did not do slowly — and you have, very kindly, walked up the stairs of my house this morning to allow me to do it.*
>
> *Read on, Maeve. The next sixteen pages are about the second of April 1975. They are not, on the whole, pleasant. After the sixteen pages we shall move to what your mother did in 2009, which is the part that has to do, today, with you.*

Maeve put the page down on the leather inlay of the writing desk.

Maeve looked, for a long moment, out of the tall sash window of the south study at the dry plane trees of the square.

A black taxi went past the front of the house with its light off.

A delivery bicycle went past with a small wicker box on the back, bouncing.

Somewhere downstairs, in the morning room, the Knibb mantel clock made a small mechanical sound.

—

Maeve read on.

—

She read for forty-one minutes.

She did not, in forty-one minutes, stand up from the green-leather chair.

She did not, in forty-one minutes, drink the small glass of water Mrs Wakeham had left on the writing desk on a coaster of marquetry that had been, Mrs Wakeham had told her three weeks ago, *a wedding gift from a man my husband does not, even today, know I once turned down.*

She did not, in forty-one minutes, allow her hands to do the small involuntary movement they had begun to do in the morning room downstairs. She did not, in forty-one minutes, allow her face to do any of the things her face would, by the count of any honest observer, very reasonably have done.

She read, in the careful neutral way she had been taught by her mother to read the rejection letters from the council and the bank and the agency over fifteen years, every word of the sixteen pages about the second of April 1975 in a small room at the end of the east corridor at a girls' boarding school in Hampshire, the room she had not until two hours ago known her mother had lived in for two and a half years, on the night a girl called Tessa Halligan had walked across a quad in the dark to a study door she should not, on any account that anyone in that school had been brave enough to articulate aloud, ever have opened.

She turned, in the forty-second minute, to page seventeen.

Page seventeen began:

> *In the autumn of 2009, my darling, your mother telephoned me. She had not telephoned me, by then, in thirty-four years. She telephoned me at five past ten in the evening, from a phone box at the back of Lewisham railway station, and she said, in the voice I had not heard in thirty-four years and which, in three seconds, was thirty-four years closer to me than my husband's voice in the next room — she said, '*Ellie. It's me. It's Cath. I need you to come up to London tomorrow. I have got into something. I have got my daughter into something. I am going to ask you to take her, in the long run, when I can't.*'*

That, on the cream A4 sheet that morning, had also been written.

## Chapter 6 — The Winding

Mr Wakeham came into the morning room at one minute to five.

He came in, as he had come in every Friday of three weeks, in a charcoal suit and a tie of dark navy silk, with his small gold pocket watch on a fine gold fob across the small of his waistcoat, with the small brass key for the glass front of the Knibb clock on the same fob, in its accustomed place between the watch itself and a small jet seal his mother had given him in 1968.

He paused, on the threshold.

He paused, on the threshold, the small fraction of a second a man pauses when he comes into a room in which he was expecting to find one person and has found two.

He looked at Mrs Wakeham, in the small embroidered chair against the south wall, with the dove-grey wool dress, with the small jet earrings, with her hands folded in her lap.

He looked at Maeve, standing — not sitting — at the inside of the morning-room door, with her overall on, with her hands also folded, at her waist, with her right hand by her left wrist in the small particular way she had been taught at the agency to fold them in the presence of the gentleman of the house.

He did not, for a beat, say anything.

He said, then, '*Eleanor. The girl. I had thought she would, at this hour, be making my tea.*'

Mrs Wakeham, from the small embroidered chair, said in the voice she had used at her dressmaker for fifty years, '*Maeve is here, dear, because I have asked her to be here. I should rather, today, that the winding of the clock be observed. I should rather that the household see it done. I have not, in some weeks, been quite well, and I should like, today, that the girl learn how it is done so that she can, in time, do it for me on Friday afternoons that I am not able to.*'

Mr Wakeham looked, at the words *not quite well*, very briefly, at the small enamel clock-face on his pocket watch.

He looked, then, at his wife.

He said, in the small even voice he had used in the house for forty-two years, '*Eleanor. The winding of the clock is mine.*'

Mrs Wakeham said, very gently, '*Yes, dear. And on the Friday it stops being yours, I should like the girl to have stood in the room with you on the Friday it was yours for the last time. There is no agenda, dear. There is the small kindness of a wife who would rather not be the only person in this house, when the time comes, who knows how it is done.*'

Mr Wakeham did not, for a beat, say anything.

He looked at Maeve, then, for the first time in three weeks, directly.

His eyes were the colour Maeve had not, in three weeks, been able to settle on a name for — a pale flat blue at the centre going gradually grey at the rim, the colour of the underside of a lid of an old enamel pot.

He looked at her for the length of three full seconds.

He said, '*Very well. The girl may watch. The girl is not to touch.*'

He turned, then, to the Knibb mantel clock above the fireplace, and he lifted the small gold pocket watch out of the small pocket of his waistcoat, and he opened the fob, and he detached, from the fob, the small brass clock-key in the accustomed place between the watch itself and the small jet seal, and he stepped up onto the small wooden footstool that had stood, in front of the morning-room fireplace, for forty-two years for this single purpose, and he addressed the cabinet of the clock.

—

Maeve, standing at the inside of the morning-room door with her right hand by her left wrist, watched.

Maeve had been watching the small brass key on the fob, between the watch and the jet seal, for three weeks. Maeve had been thinking about the key on Tuesday night in the locked carer's room. Maeve had been thinking about the key on the green-leather chair of the south study on Wednesday and Thursday and on Friday morning. Maeve had been thinking about the key, in particular, in the eight hours between her breakfast tray for Mrs Wakeham and the moment Mr Wakeham had come into the room.

She had not been thinking, in the eight hours, about how she would do it.

She had been thinking, in the eight hours, about how he would not, while she did it, know she had done it.

The answer, she had decided at twenty past two on the Friday afternoon, in the small kitchen of the Belgravia townhouse, while she was washing — for the third time, in the rinse water of the supper plates she had not yet cooked the supper for — the spoon she had used at lunch, had to do with Mrs Wakeham's eyes.

He had not, in forty-two years, looked at his wife while he wound the clock.

He had looked, in those forty-two years of Fridays, only at the cabinet of the clock.

The cabinet of the clock was a hand and a half high, and a hand wide, and stood on the mantelpiece of the morning room at a height — when Mr Wakeham stood on the small wooden footstool — of perhaps four inches above his eyeline. To wind the clock, Mr Wakeham would, in the next four minutes, turn the small brass key in the keyhole at the right of the cabinet four full turns. To turn the key four full turns, Mr Wakeham would lift his right hand from his fob to the right of the cabinet of the clock, and would, for the four turns, look at the keyhole.

His right hand, for the four turns, would be on the key.

His left hand, for the four turns, would rest on the top of the cabinet, the way his right hand had once rested on the leather inlay of his desk at the school in 1975, the way his right hand had rested in 1981 on the satin of the train of the wedding dress Eleanor had been wearing on the eleventh of June at St Margaret's, Westminster.

In the four turns, Mr Wakeham would not look down.

In the four turns, Mr Wakeham would not look at his wife.

In the four turns, Mr Wakeham would not look at Maeve, because Maeve would be standing — by Eleanor's morning instruction — in the precise corner of the morning room that was reflected in the small framed mirror above the fireplace beside the clock, and in the small framed mirror above the fireplace beside the clock, what was visible to a man standing on the small wooden footstool in front of the clock was not Maeve at the inside of the door but the empty wall beside the morning-room door.

Maeve was, by the small framed mirror, invisible.

Maeve was standing exactly where Eleanor — for forty-two years of Fridays — had been standing.

Eleanor had taken, this Friday, the small embroidered chair against the south wall.

—

Mr Wakeham turned the key once.

The Knibb made the small mechanical sound it made on the first turn.

Maeve, at the inside of the morning-room door, did not move.

Mr Wakeham turned the key twice.

The Knibb made the small mechanical sound it made on the second turn.

Maeve, at the inside of the morning-room door, did not move.

Mr Wakeham turned the key three times.

The Knibb made the small mechanical sound it made on the third turn.

Mrs Wakeham, in the small embroidered chair against the south wall, who had not for forty-two years stood up from the writing table by the window of her own bedroom on a Friday at four minutes past five — Mrs Wakeham, on this Friday, made a small involuntary noise in her throat.

The noise was very small.

The noise was the noise of a woman who had been told, by a consultant in eleven months of Wednesday-afternoon appointments she had not told her husband she had been to, that the small thing in her chest would, when it came for her, come for her with the small unwarning of a thing that had been waiting.

The noise stopped.

Mr Wakeham, on the small wooden footstool, on the third turn of the key, with his right hand still on the key and his left hand still on the top of the cabinet — Mr Wakeham did, for the first time in forty-two years of Fridays, look down.

He looked at Eleanor.

—

Maeve crossed the morning-room rug.

She crossed it in the four small careful steps she had practised on the front-room runner of her own bedsit in Lewisham, in front of the small wall mirror, every morning for three weeks before her first day at the agency, while her mother had been dying in the bedroom upstairs. She crossed it in the silence the Belgravia townhouse had been built to swallow. She crossed it in the four small steps Eleanor Wakeham had been practising, in her own head, for forty-two years of Fridays, in a small embroidered chair against the south wall of the morning room, on a footstool that was the wrong height and for a clock that was the wrong shape.

She arrived behind Mr Wakeham at the small wooden footstool.

She did not, when she arrived, put her hand on his fob.

She put her right hand, very calmly, on the small jet seal his mother had given him in 1968 — the small jet seal that had been worn smooth in the smallest finger of his mother's right hand for fifty years and the smallest of his own for fifty-seven, and which was, between the watch and the brass key on the fob, the one object on the fob that any man would feel a stranger's hand on, instantly.

Mr Wakeham — on the small wooden footstool, on the third turn, with his right hand still on the brass key in the keyhole, his left hand still on the top of the cabinet, his eyes still on his wife — did not at first feel Maeve's fingers, because Maeve had put her fingers on the small jet seal and not on the fob itself.

Then he did.

He looked down.

He saw Maeve's small white hand against the dark grey of his waistcoat, with her fingertips on the small jet seal his mother had given him in 1968.

He said, in the voice he had used at the school in 1975, '*Take your hand off the seal, girl.*'

Maeve took her hand off the seal.

She did not, however, take her hand off the fob.

She had, in the small motion between his looking down and his speaking — in the small motion that her right hand had been practising, in the small front-room runner of the bedsit in Lewisham, every morning for three weeks — moved her fingers from the jet seal to the small brass clock-key in the accustomed place between the watch and the jet seal, and had taken the key from the fob with the small soft sound of a fob being relieved of a small light object that the fob had not been expecting to be relieved of.

She held the key, by its ring, between the tip of her right index finger and the pad of her right thumb.

She stepped back from the footstool.

She held the key, openly, where Mr Wakeham could see it, by her own waist.

She said, in the careful neutral voice she had built for three weeks at the agency before she had ever set foot in the Belgravia townhouse, '*Mr Wakeham. Step down from the stool.*'

—

Mr Wakeham, on the small wooden footstool, with his right hand still on the brass key of the cabinet that was no longer the key Maeve was holding but the OTHER one — the one Eleanor had taken out of the drawer of the writing desk and had, that morning, in the eight hours of Maeve's other movements, very carefully put into Mr Wakeham's keyhole in the cabinet of the clock, so that he would not, when he came down from the stool, find his own key missing from his own keyhole — Mr Wakeham did not, at first, understand.

He looked at his right hand.

His right hand was still on a brass key in a keyhole.

He looked at Maeve.

Maeve was holding, at her waist, a brass key the size of a postage stamp, with a green silk thread tied through its eye.

He looked at his right hand again.

The brass key in his keyhole did not have a green silk thread through its eye.

He looked at the brass key in Maeve's hand.

The brass key in Maeve's hand had, on its bow, a small scratch on the lower edge — a scratch he had made, in 1984, on the morning Eleanor had given him the key to the cabinet of the clock as her wedding-anniversary gift to him, when he had set the key down on the marble of the mantelpiece while he had drunk a glass of port and the key had slid, on the marble, a hand's-width into the surround of the fireplace and had scratched on the iron of the surround a small mark which had been on the lower edge of the bow of the key Mr Wakeham had carried on his fob for forty-one years.

The key in Maeve's hand had the scratch.

The key in his own keyhole did not.

His mouth, for the first time in forty-two years of Fridays, opened on the threshold of saying his wife's name.

He did not, on the threshold, say it.

—

Maeve, with her own hand by her waist, with the brass key between her right finger and thumb, with the small framed mirror above the fireplace reflecting the empty wall beside the morning-room door behind her, said —

'*Eleanor. The cabinet.*'

Mrs Wakeham, on the small embroidered chair against the south wall, stood up.

Mrs Wakeham, in the dove-grey wool dress with the small jet earrings, walked across the morning-room rug — in the small even steps of a woman who had once, in 1975, walked across a quad in the spring of her sixteenth year to a study she had wished she had never gone near — and Mrs Wakeham, at the small wooden footstool, said in the voice she had used at her dressmaker for fifty years —

'*Edwin. Step down from the stool, my dear.*'

Mr Wakeham stepped down from the stool.

He stepped down because he had been, in forty-two years of Fridays of being asked by Eleanor for no single thing whatsoever, asked for something for the first time, by his wife, in a voice he had not, in any single morning of those forty-two years, heard her use.

He stepped down, and he turned, and he stood in the middle of the morning-room rug of his own Belgravia townhouse, and he looked, very steadily, at the two women who had — between them, in the small front-room runner of a bedsit in Lewisham and in the small embroidered chair against the south wall of his own morning room — been practising the four-step crossing of his rug for forty-two and three years respectively.

Mrs Wakeham took the brass clock key, with the green silk thread, out of Maeve's right hand.

Mrs Wakeham stepped up onto the small wooden footstool herself — slowly, with the careful particular slowness of a woman whose knees would shortly remind her she was eighty-one — and Mrs Wakeham turned, in her own right hand, the small brass clock-key of her own wedding-anniversary gift, in the keyhole of the cabinet of the clock that contained the typed paragraph her own husband's mother had agreed in 1975 to make disappear and which Mrs Wakeham had, in 1983, on the morning after their anniversary, while her own husband had been at chapel, taken out of his desk drawer and put inside.

The keyhole of the cabinet of the clock was, however, already in use.

The key in the keyhole was — Mrs Wakeham noted, with the small particular calm of a woman who had been waiting forty-two years to find out which of the two keys would, in the end, fit — the same one Mrs Wakeham herself was, at this moment, holding the second of in her own right hand.

Mrs Wakeham smiled, very small, to the cabinet.

She left her own key in the air.

She took the key that was already in the keyhole — the one Mr Wakeham had, at the third turn, still had his own right hand on, before he had stepped down to find out why his wife had made the small involuntary noise in her throat — and she turned it the fourth turn.

The Knibb made the small mechanical sound it made on the fourth turn.

The cabinet, on the fourth turn, very softly clicked open.

—

Inside the cabinet, on top of the small brass weights of the Knibb clock from 1789, in a small green silk pouch identical to the one in the secret drawer of the Davenport in the south study, was a single folded sheet of typewriter paper the colour of old butter.

Mrs Wakeham, on the small wooden footstool, in the dove-grey wool dress, with the small jet earrings, with her own right hand on the small brass clock-key her husband had carried on his fob for forty-one years — Mrs Wakeham, very calmly, took the small green silk pouch out of the cabinet of the Knibb clock from 1789.

She held the pouch in her left hand.

She closed the cabinet.

She did not, when she closed it, take her husband's key out of the keyhole.

She stepped down from the small wooden footstool.

She stood, on the morning-room rug, beside Maeve.

She turned, in the dove-grey wool, to her husband of forty-two years, and she said, in the small careful voice she had built for fifty years at her dressmaker, in the small careful voice she had not, in forty-two years of Fridays, used in this room —

'*Edwin. We are leaving the house this afternoon. Your housekeeper of three weeks is leaving with me. The taxi is on the corner of Eaton Square. The flat in Pimlico is in my own name. Tessa's letter goes by courier, at half past five, to a man on Bishopsgate whose name I shall not, at this hour, give you. You shall not, by half past six this evening, be a man at large in this house. I have nothing further to say to you. I have not, my dear, in any morning of forty-two years of Fridays, had a single thing further to say to you.*'

She paused.

She said, '*Maeve. Your coat.*'

—

Maeve went into the front hall.

She put on her coat.

She came back into the morning room.

She held out her arm.

Mrs Wakeham took her arm.

The two of them walked, in the small even steps of two women who had — in the small front-room runner of a bedsit in Lewisham and in the small embroidered chair against the south wall of the morning room respectively — been practising this walk for fifty years, out of the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse, down the marble hall, through the front door which Maeve held open with her free hand, down the four shallow stone steps of the front, and along the pavement in the direction of Eaton Square.

The taxi was on the corner.

The driver was the driver Mrs Wakeham had used for nine years.

The driver, when he saw them, got out and opened the back door.

The driver did not speak.

The driver had not, in nine years, ever spoken on a Friday afternoon to Mrs Wakeham, because the Friday afternoons of those nine years had been the afternoons of Mr Wakeham's clock, and the driver had, in nine years, known the difference between a Friday on which his fare wished to be spoken to and a Friday on which she did not.

Mrs Wakeham got into the taxi.

Maeve got into the taxi.

The driver shut the door, very gently.

The driver got into the front of the taxi.

The driver, in the rear-view mirror, met Mrs Wakeham's eyes for the first time in nine years, and said, very quietly, '*Where to, madam.*'

Mrs Wakeham said, in the small careful voice she had used at her dressmaker for fifty years —

'*Pimlico, please, Stephen. The flat. The new one. The one I have been furnishing on Wednesdays.*'

The driver said, '*Yes, madam.*'

The taxi pulled away from the kerb.

Mrs Wakeham, in the back of the taxi, did not look back at the Belgravia townhouse.

Maeve, in the back of the taxi, did look back, once, at the front door of the Belgravia townhouse — which was still open, with no one in the doorway — and Maeve thought, in the small precise voice that had risen up in her in the morning room three days ago and was, now, the voice she would have for the rest of her life —

*Tessa. The man on Bishopsgate has it at half past. I am sorry it took fifty years.*

The taxi turned the corner.

The Belgravia townhouse went out of the rear window.

In the front, the driver said, very quietly, to the windscreen — to no one in particular, to the two women in the back who had not asked him to say it, to the small green silk pouch in Mrs Wakeham's left hand which contained the typed paragraph her granddaughter Tessa Halligan had typed on her father's Olivetti in March 1975 — '*Stephen Halligan, madam. I have been ready for forty-three years.*'

The driver was Tessa Halligan's brother.

Mrs Wakeham, in the back of the taxi, said, very quietly, '*I know, my dear. I have known since you took the job in 2016. Thank you for waiting.*'

Then the taxi drove, in the slow even speed of an old black Bentley taxi belonging to a driver who had been waiting forty-three years to deliver his sister's typed paragraph to a man on Bishopsgate, north along the slow back-streets of Belgravia towards the river, and the small mechanical sound of a Knibb mantel clock from 1789 was, for the rest of the afternoon, the only sound in the morning room of a house in Belgravia in which Mr Edwin Wakeham — who would, by half past six that evening, be sitting in a small interview room on Bishopsgate with two officers of the Metropolitan Police and a copy of his stepmother's note from the third of April 1975 on the table in front of him — would, for the next forty-two minutes, be entirely alone.

## Chapter 7 — Pimlico

The flat in Pimlico was on the top floor of a narrow white house on a side street, with two windows that looked into a quiet square of plane trees and a third that looked into the back gardens of the parallel terrace, and Mrs Wakeham had, since March of the previous year, furnished it in the way of a woman who had been thinking, for forty-two years, about exactly the room in which she would, in the end, choose to be.

The front room had two armchairs — one in green leather, one in soft dove-grey wool — facing a small unlit gas fire. Between them was a small walnut side table on which sat, the afternoon Maeve and Mrs Wakeham came in, a small electric kettle, two cream-coloured cups, a small dish of sugar lumps with a pair of brass tongs, a small jug of milk under a beaded muslin cover, and a small enamel biscuit tin with a print of the Norfolk coast on the lid.

On the mantelpiece over the small unlit gas fire, in a small silver frame, was a single black-and-white square photograph of three girls in St Brigid's blazers — Eleanor in the middle, Cath on the right, and Tessa on the left, with her badly-done French plait over the left shoulder and the small chip in her front tooth.

The photograph had been on the mantelpiece, Maeve later understood, since the morning Mrs Wakeham had signed the lease.

—

Maeve made the tea.

Mrs Wakeham, in the green-leather armchair, sat very still with her hands folded in her lap. She had not, since the taxi had pulled away from the kerb of Eaton Square, said anything. The small green silk pouch from inside the cabinet of the clock was on the small walnut side table, beside the kettle.

Maeve poured. She added milk for Mrs Wakeham. She handed Mrs Wakeham the cup.

Mrs Wakeham took the cup in both hands.

She did not, for a long moment, drink.

She said, in the voice that was not, this afternoon, the voice of her dressmaker — the voice was, for the first time in fifty years, the voice of a sixteen-year-old in a small room at the end of the east corridor at a school in Hampshire in the spring of 1975 —

'*Maeve, my darling. I have a small thing in my chest the consultant has been explaining to me on Wednesday afternoons for eleven months. I have known, for some weeks now, that I should not see Christmas. I am telling you, this afternoon, in the front room of a flat I have been furnishing on Wednesdays for the year because I did not, my darling, wish to die in the bed I had shared with him.*'

She paused.

She drank, very slowly, from the cup.

She said, '*I have nine weeks, my dear. Perhaps ten. I had wanted, in those nine weeks, the privacy of this room with you in it. I have arranged the rest of the will. The Belgravia townhouse goes to a charity for girls of school age in residential boarding. The Davenport in the south study comes to you. The small green silk pouches you have read this morning are to be transferred, on the second of April every year, to a small reading room of the National Library in Edinburgh that has agreed, in correspondence with my solicitor, to keep them. The flat is yours, my dear. The first of the agency wages I shall make sure get to your bank account on the seventh of each month for the rest of your life is in the small drawer of the side table behind your chair.*'

She paused.

She said, '*Will you stay, my darling.*'

—

Maeve, in the dove-grey wool armchair, with her own cup of tea in both her hands, looked at Mrs Wakeham.

She looked, in the small particular way Maeve had been looking at her own mother on the last Tuesday of her own mother's life — *Maeve, my darling, will you stay tonight, I should rather not be alone* — and Maeve, very quietly, very calmly, did not allow her face to do any of the things her face would, by the count of any honest observer, very reasonably have done.

She said, '*Eleanor. Of course I shall stay.*'

Mrs Wakeham, in the green-leather armchair, in the small flat in Pimlico that she had been furnishing on Wednesday afternoons for the year, with the small green silk pouch on the side table that contained, in the typed paragraph, the last thing her best friend's daughter had asked of any of them — Mrs Wakeham, very quietly, set the cup down on the small walnut side table.

She turned the handle, by the small particular habit of fifty years, three small degrees back the way it had been pointing before she had picked the cup up.

She left it that way.

Then Mrs Wakeham, for the first time since the spring of 1975, in the careful precise way of a woman who had spent fifty years not being able to, allowed her own face to do the thing that her face had, for fifty years, been carrying.

She wept.

She did not weep loudly. Mrs Wakeham did not weep loudly because Mrs Wakeham, by her own dressmaker, had not been taught how. She wept very quietly, in the way her best friend Cath Brennan had once taught her in 1975 in a small room at the end of the east corridor — *the trick, Eleanor, is to do it into something soft so the room never knows it has happened* — and she did it, this afternoon, into the soft dove-grey wool of the right sleeve of the dress that, that morning, she had put on for the last time in the bedroom of the Belgravia townhouse.

Maeve, in the dove-grey wool armchair opposite, set her own cup down.

Maeve crossed the small front room of the flat in Pimlico.

Maeve knelt down beside the green-leather armchair, the way Maeve had knelt down at the side of her own mother's bed on the last Tuesday of her own mother's life, and Maeve put her right hand, very gently, on the back of Mrs Wakeham's left hand.

Mrs Wakeham did not, this time, ask her to take her hand off.

Maeve said, in the small precise voice that had risen up in her in the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse three days ago, '*Eleanor. I shall be here. I shall not, for the nine weeks, leave the room. I shall sit with you on the Wednesdays the consultant comes, on the afternoons the man from Bishopsgate may come back with questions, on the evenings my own father — Tessa's father — may, when he hears, telephone. I shall make the tea. I shall make the soup. I shall make the small dish of one half-grapefruit and the small dish of two oatcakes and the small jar of honey and the small linen cloth folded into a flat triangle every morning that you ask. I shall hold your hand on the last morning. I shall, on the last morning, do, my dear, exactly what you ask me to do, and I shall not, on the last morning, leave the room until you have.*'

She paused.

She said, '*And after the last morning, I shall — every second of April for the rest of my life — take a small green silk pouch from the Davenport in the south study, and I shall send it to Edinburgh, the way you have arranged. And I shall, on the second of April after my last morning, leave the last pouch in the bottom drawer with a note for the next woman who will, my dear, in her turn, be Tessa's. It will not, then, be your job.*'

Mrs Wakeham, in the green-leather armchair of the small flat in Pimlico, lifted her face from the soft dove-grey wool of her own right sleeve, and she looked at Maeve, kneeling, and she said —

'*Tessa would have liked you, my darling. Tessa would have liked you so very much.*'

Maeve, kneeling on the rug of the small flat in Pimlico, with her right hand still on the back of Mrs Wakeham's left hand, said in the careful neutral voice she had built for three weeks at the agency before she had ever set foot in the Belgravia townhouse —

'*Eleanor. I should like to think I would have liked her too.*'

Outside the window of the small flat in Pimlico, in the quiet square of plane trees, a black cab went past with its light off.

A delivery bicycle went past with a small wicker box on the back, bouncing.

Somewhere, three streets away, in a small interview room on Bishopsgate, a man whose name was Edwin Wakeham, who had been raised under his stepfather's name as Edwin Hollis, was, in the careful precise voice he had used at the school in 1975, finally being asked the question Tessa Halligan had walked across a quad in the dark to ask him fifty years ago.

He would not, on the asking, answer it.

He would, for the next eleven months, in the careful precise voice he had used at the school in 1975, refuse to.

It would not, in the end, matter.

The typed paragraph in his own stepmother's hand at the foot of the headmaster's note, which Mr Stephen Halligan had handed to the man on Bishopsgate at twenty-eight minutes past five on the Friday afternoon, would, for the man on Bishopsgate, by the morning of the following Monday, be enough.

## Chapter 8 — The Cousin

The man from Bishopsgate came to the flat in Pimlico at ten past eleven on the Saturday morning.

His name was Detective Inspector Conor Halligan, of the Metropolitan Police. He was perhaps forty-eight. He was the son of Mr Stephen Halligan, the driver of the old dark-green Bentley taxi who had been waiting forty-three years at the kerb of Eaton Square, and he was — Maeve worked out in the small precise way Maeve had been working things out for three days — Tessa's nephew, and so, by the small precise arithmetic of the typed paragraph, Maeve's first cousin once removed.

He was, Maeve noted in the small particular way of women who had been raised on the kindness of strangers in the houses of Lewisham, the first person in the morning room of the Pimlico flat who had not, in three weeks, been thinking about Tessa for fifty years.

He had been thinking about Tessa, he told Mrs Wakeham, very gently, in the green-leather armchair, since the autumn of 1981, when he had been ten years old, and his father had sat him down at the kitchen table of their small terrace in Dagenham, and his father had told him — once, in nine sentences, the way his father had said only the things that mattered — what he believed had happened, on the second of April 1975, to the older sister his father had not been able to find a single trace of in six years of looking.

He had been a detective inspector, Conor Halligan said in the careful neutral voice of a man who had been waiting forty-three years to be in this room, on the express understanding that should ever the question reopen, he would be the officer to whom the file came.

The file had, by ten past ten on the Saturday morning, come to him.

—

He sat in the dove-grey wool armchair, in the morning light of the Pimlico flat, with a cup of tea on the small walnut side table that Maeve had made for him with milk and one sugar lump because his father had — at the kerb on the corner of Eaton Square half an hour earlier, in the soft Sicilian-Long-Island accent of a man who had been driving a black Bentley taxi in Belgravia for nine years — told her that his son took it that way.

Mr Stephen Halligan was, this morning, downstairs at the kitchen of the Pimlico flat, in the small wooden chair by the back window, making, very quietly, in the unhurried way of a man who had been making it for forty-three Saturday mornings, a soup he had been intending to bring to the upstairs room only when the conversation upstairs was finished.

Mrs Wakeham, in the green-leather armchair, with the small green silk pouch from inside the cabinet of the Knibb mantel clock on the small walnut side table beside her, said in the small careful voice she had used at her dressmaker for fifty years —

'*Conor, my dear. I am going to give you four documents, this morning. I am going to tell you, on each of them, who in the household has known its provenance for how long, so you can be the man at Bishopsgate who, on Monday, knows which witness to compel and which witness has, in the small ways the law accepts, already volunteered. I am going to be very tedious, my dear. You will forgive me. I have arranged the order. May I.*'

DI Conor Halligan said, '*Mrs Wakeham. You may.*'

He took out a small leather notebook, the same kind, Maeve noticed in the part of her brain that was still cataloguing, that DI Halligan's father had been carrying in the inside pocket of his coat at the kerb of Eaton Square.

He uncapped a fountain pen.

He looked, very steadily, across the small walnut side table, at Mrs Wakeham — who had married his great-aunt's killer in 1981, who had carried that fact for forty-two years in the small careful voice of her own dressmaker, who would be dead by Christmas — and DI Conor Halligan said, very quietly —

'*Go on, then, Eleanor.*'

She went on.

## Chapter 9 — Six Weeks

The first six weeks were quieter than Maeve had expected.

The press did not, in the first six weeks, find them. The story Mrs Wakeham and DI Halligan had agreed on with the Bishopsgate office on the Saturday afternoon was small and careful, and the small care of it held the story off the front pages of the broadsheets for fifty-one days. *A historical complaint, of the eighteen-seventies kind that did sometimes still emerge, relating to a girls' boarding school in Hampshire in the spring of 1975. A man in his early seventies, formerly a bursar of the school and the headmaster's son-in-law, now retired from the City, was helping the police with their inquiries. The investigation was, the small careful statement said, expected to take some weeks. No name had been released.*

Mr Wakeham's name was not released until the eleventh of December.

By the eleventh of December, Mrs Wakeham was in the bed in the small back bedroom of the Pimlico flat that Maeve had, in the middle of November, moved her into, because the green-leather armchair in the front room had, by the middle of November, become a chair Mrs Wakeham could no longer be helped out of in the evening.

The eleventh of December was a Thursday.

The front of *The Times* on the morning of the eleventh of December carried a small photograph of Mr Wakeham in his charcoal overcoat, on the steps of Bishopsgate, with the lining out, and a small careful caption of three lines that did not name Tessa and did not name the school.

Mrs Wakeham read the front of *The Times* in bed, with her glasses on, with the small dish of one half-grapefruit on the bed-tray Maeve had brought up at eight, with Maeve in the small chair beside the bed, and Mrs Wakeham said, in the small voice that was, by then, all that was left of the voice of her dressmaker —

'*Maeve. Stephen has the morning off, hasn't he. Tell him to come up after lunch. I should like to play him a small piece of Schubert before this afternoon. He used to play it for his sister when she was small. I have, in the second drawer of the writing desk by the window, the score. He shall not be able to read it himself. I shall play it. The piano is the one your great-grandmother left to your great-aunt Tessa in 1957, my dear. I bought it back from the auction house in 1992. It has been in the small front room of this flat, under the dust-cloth, since the spring of last year. I had not been able to play it. I shall, this afternoon, with Stephen here. And I should rather, my dear, that you sit in the dove-grey armchair while I do.*'

Maeve said, 'Yes, Eleanor.'

Mrs Wakeham said, very quietly, '*Thank you, my darling.*'

Mr Stephen Halligan came at half past two. The piano had been brought out from under the dust-cloth that morning by Maeve and the small wiry man from the building's maintenance company, who had not asked any questions and had not been told any, and the piano stood now in the corner of the small front room of the Pimlico flat with the lid open and the keys soft yellow with the years.

Mrs Wakeham was helped, from the bed in the back bedroom, by Maeve and by Stephen, in the small soft dove-grey dressing-gown Mrs Wakeham had been wearing in the flat for two weeks, to the piano.

Mrs Wakeham sat down at the piano.

Mrs Wakeham played, for nine minutes and seventeen seconds, the small piece of Schubert she had been intending to play for Stephen Halligan since the spring of 1992 when she had bought the piano back from the auction house in Edinburgh in his sister's name — and Mr Stephen Halligan, in the dove-grey wool armchair, in the soft Sicilian-Long-Island accent of a man who had been waiting forty-three years to hear his sister played by the only other person on the earth who had known her — wept, very quietly, in the careful precise way of a man who had been taught, at the kitchen table of his small terrace in Dagenham in 1981, that the trick was to do it into something soft so the room never knew it had happened.

The room knew, this afternoon, that it had happened.

The room had been built, on a Wednesday afternoon in March of the previous year by a woman who had been thinking about this exact afternoon for forty-two years, to know.

## Chapter 10 — The Garden

Mrs Wakeham was lifted out of the back bedroom of the Pimlico flat for the last time, by Maeve, on the morning of the twenty-third of December.

She was lifted into the green-leather armchair in the front room.

The day was bright. The plane trees of the quiet square outside were in the small bare winter shape Mrs Wakeham had been buying, every Wednesday afternoon since the previous March, the flat for. The small electric kettle on the small walnut side table had been switched on. The small enamel biscuit tin with the print of the Norfolk coast on the lid had been opened. The cups had been laid out — three of them, today, because Mr Stephen Halligan would arrive at eleven with the small bunch of white camellias he had been bringing every Tuesday and Friday for six weeks.

Maeve sat in the dove-grey wool armchair.

Maeve held Mrs Wakeham's left hand.

Mrs Wakeham, in the soft dove-grey dressing-gown, in the green-leather armchair, with the small framed photograph of three girls in St Brigid's blazers on the mantelpiece behind her, said in the smallest voice Maeve had ever heard her use —

'*Maeve, my darling. Three things. May I.*'

Maeve said, '*Eleanor. You may.*'

Mrs Wakeham said, '*One. The pouch in the cabinet of the Knibb clock has been moved, by the solicitor on Tuesday, to the small reading room of the National Library in Edinburgh that has agreed to keep it. They will keep it on a shelf with the name TESSA HALLIGAN on the spine of its small green silk box. They have written to me. The shelf is in the reading room on the second floor of the George IV Bridge building. The reading room is open on Wednesdays and Fridays. You may, my dear, on any Wednesday or Friday of the rest of your life, go and read it. You shall not need an appointment. The librarian on duty has been told.*'

She paused.

She said, '*Two. The Davenport in the south study of the Belgravia townhouse has been removed, on Tuesday, from the house, before the house was handed to the charity. It is in the small bedroom of this flat. The second drawer contains all the pouches I have not yet sent. The first pouch — the one with the small enamel hairbrush with TH on it — is yours, my dear, to keep. The brush belonged to your grandmother. She was thirteen when her mother gave it to her. She had it in her overnight bag in 1975. It is small enough to go in any handbag you will, in the rest of your life, ever carry. I should like you to carry it, my dear. I should like you not to be without it.*'

She paused.

She said, '*Three. There is a small garden, at the back of this flat, at the bottom of the iron stairs. I had it dug, last March, by the small wiry man from the maintenance company. He did not ask questions. He put down four box-hedge plants in the corners and one small bird-bath in the middle. He laid a flagstone path. There is no plaque. There is no marker. There is, however, in the very small bed beside the flagstone path, on the day Tessa would have been sixty-seven — the second of April — a small white camellia tree, which I had brought to me last spring from a nursery in Cornwall and which will, my dear, in the springs to come, flower in the first week of April, every year, for as long as it lives.*'

She paused.

She said, '*Stephen knows about the camellia. The cabinet of the Knibb in Edinburgh will have, each second of April for the rest of his life, a small white camellia delivered to its shelf. He has agreed to do this himself. He shall, on the second of April after my last morning, also bring a small white camellia to this flat, and shall leave it on the small walnut side table, on the morning of the day you take the new pouch from the Davenport to Edinburgh. You shall not need to write to him. He will come.*'

She paused.

She said, very softly, '*My darling. That is the three things.*'

She closed her eyes.

Maeve, in the dove-grey wool armchair, with her hand still on Mrs Wakeham's hand, did not, for a long second, do anything.

Then Maeve said, very calmly, in the small precise voice that had risen up in her in the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse seven weeks ago and which was, this morning, the voice she would have for the rest of her life —

'*Eleanor. I have understood the three things. I shall do all of them.*'

She paused.

She said, very softly, '*Are you tired, Eleanor.*'

Mrs Wakeham, in the green-leather armchair, with her eyes closed, with her left hand in Maeve's right, with the dove-grey wool dressing-gown over her thin shoulders and the small jet earrings — the small jet earrings Mrs Wakeham had been putting in for forty-two years of Fridays — still in her ears, said, in the smallest voice —

'*I am, my darling. I have been, for a very long time.*'

Maeve said, '*Sleep then, my dear.*'

Mrs Wakeham slept.

She did not wake.

The small mechanical sound of the small enamel bedside clock on the writing table by the window — the small enamel bedside clock that had been on the writing table by the window of every house Mrs Wakeham had lived in since 1981, which had been wound by Mrs Wakeham herself, every Friday morning, with the small brass key on the small ribbon she kept in the small ring-dish on her bedside table — made, at twenty past three on the afternoon of the twenty-third of December, the small mechanical sound it had been making, in the small flat in Pimlico that Mrs Wakeham had been furnishing on Wednesdays for the year, since the previous March.

Maeve, in the dove-grey wool armchair, did not let go of her hand.

She did not, this time, weep.

She would, on the morning of the next day, the twenty-fourth of December, the morning her own great-uncle Stephen Halligan would come at eight and would help her cover the small framed photograph on the mantelpiece with a small linen cloth, weep.

She would weep, then, the way her own mother and her own grandmother and her own great-aunt and the woman who had been her own grandmother's best friend at school in 1975 had all, in the various rooms of their various lives, taught her — into something soft, so the small front room of the small flat in Pimlico in the quiet square of plane trees would not, on this December morning, have to know it had happened.

The small flat in Pimlico knew.

The small flat in Pimlico had been built, by a woman who had been thinking about this exact morning for forty-two years, to know.

## Chapter 11 — The Second of April

The second of April, the following spring, was a Friday.

Maeve was, by then, twenty-nine.

The small flat in Pimlico had been hers, by the small careful arrangement of Mrs Wakeham's solicitor, since the eighteenth of January. She had been working, on the Mondays, Wednesdays and Fridays, at a small charity for girls of school age in residential boarding, the same charity Mrs Wakeham had left the Belgravia townhouse to. She had been on the train to Edinburgh, on the Tuesday and the Thursday afternoon, once a fortnight, with a small green silk pouch in the inside pocket of her good coat, and had been delivered, on the platform at Waverley, by the librarian on duty at the National Library, the small white camellia she had been given to bring back to the second flat — the second flat being a small flat above a tea-shop on Marylebone Road that her cousin DI Conor Halligan, since the eleventh of February when Mr Edwin Wakeham had at last entered a plea, had taken on, for himself and for his father.

Her own father — Mr James Brennan, of Lewisham, the man who had raised her, the man who had been Cath Brennan's husband for thirty-one years, the man who had not, until the conversation Maeve had had with him in the small kitchen of the Lewisham flat on the Saturday after the twenty-third of December, known any of this either — was, this morning, on the train down from Lewisham with a small bunch of yellow tulips in his right hand, which had been her own mother's small particular flower, and which were not, this morning, intended for Tessa.

Maeve was in the kitchen of the Pimlico flat at quarter to seven.

She made the tea. She put the kettle back on the hob. She took, from the second drawer of the Davenport in the small bedroom, the small green silk pouch on top of the pile — the third pouch of the year, the one Mrs Wakeham had written, on the cream A4 sheet at the bottom of the secret drawer of the Davenport, was the one Maeve was to take, this April, herself — and she put the pouch into the inside pocket of her good coat.

She went down the iron stairs at the back of the flat to the small garden.

She knelt down beside the small white camellia tree that the small wiry man from the maintenance company had planted, in the small bed beside the flagstone path, on a Tuesday afternoon in March of the previous year.

The tree was in flower.

The tree had nine white camellias on it, in the first week of April, in the second flowering of its life, which had been the first flowering Mrs Wakeham had not been alive to see.

Maeve cut one camellia. She cut it the way her own mother had taught her to cut a tulip from the small vase on the kitchen window-sill of the bedsit in Lewisham — *at the angle, Maeve, always at the angle, the angle keeps it drinking* — and she laid the camellia on the flagstone path, beside the bird-bath.

She did not, this morning, cry.

She had finished the morning's small weeping the previous evening, in the careful precise way of a woman who had been raised by her adoptive mother in a Lewisham bedsit and by her great-aunt's best friend in a Pimlico flat and by her great-uncle Stephen in a small Marylebone tea-shop, all of whom had taught her, separately, the same trick — to do it into something soft, so the room never had to know it had happened.

She picked up the camellia.

She went back up the iron stairs to the small flat.

She put on her good coat. She put on Mrs Wakeham's small jet earrings, which Maeve had, by the small careful arrangement of Mrs Wakeham's solicitor, on the eighteenth of January received. She put the white camellia into the small slip-pocket of the inside breast of the good coat, beside the small green silk pouch.

She picked up her handbag.

Inside the handbag, in the small inside pocket where her own mother had taught her, on a Sunday afternoon in Lewisham in 2008, to keep the one small thing she would not, on any day of her life, want to be without — was the small enamel hairbrush with the letters TH on the handle in red.

She closed the door of the small flat in Pimlico.

She went down the front stairs.

She walked along the quiet square of plane trees, in the first week of April light, in the careful neutral steps her own mother had taught her on the front-room runner of the bedsit in Lewisham every morning of her childhood, and she went to Pimlico station, and she took the train to King's Cross, and she met her father — Mr James Brennan, with the small bunch of yellow tulips in his right hand — at the platform at twenty past nine, and she kissed him on the cheek, and she took his arm, and the two of them walked together, in the small careful steps of a father and the daughter she had always, in every single morning of his thirty-one years of being her father, been, to the booking office, and they bought two tickets to Edinburgh.

In the inside pocket of her good coat, the small green silk pouch and the small white camellia rested side by side.

The pouch was warm.

The camellia was, in the way camellias were at this time of year in the first week of April, very faintly cold.

Outside the booking office, in the soft April morning, a black cab went past with its light off.

A delivery bicycle went past with a small wicker box on the back, bouncing.

Somewhere, three streets away, in a small interview room on Bishopsgate that Mr Edwin Wakeham would not, in the rest of his life, again be required to enter, a small mechanical sound was, on this Friday morning, finally, not being made.

## Chapter 12 — The Reading Room

The reading room of the National Library on George IV Bridge, on the second floor, was open on the Wednesdays and the Fridays, at ten.

Maeve and her father were at the door at quarter to.

The librarian on duty was a woman of perhaps fifty, in a soft grey woollen cardigan over a navy linen dress, with the small enamel name-badge of the library at her throat and her hair pinned back from her forehead with two plain black grips. She did not, when Maeve and her father came up the stairs at quarter to, look surprised. She had been told, by the head of acquisitions in November, the conditions of the bequest. She had been on duty, every Friday morning, on the second of April for the rest of her career, by the small standing arrangement of her name on the staff rota.

She said, when Maeve came in, '*Miss Brennan. The shelf is ready. May I take the pouch.*'

Maeve, in her good coat, in the small jet earrings, said, '*Thank you, Miss MacLeod. I should like to place it myself, this morning. I have my father with me.*'

Miss MacLeod said, '*Of course, my dear. The shelf is at the back of the room, on the right, beside the window. The small green silk box has the name on its spine. You will find the shelf by the camellia.*'

Maeve took her father's arm.

The two of them walked, in the small careful steps of a father and a daughter who had — between them, in the small unhurried way Lewisham fathers had been walking with their daughters in the morning lights of Lewisham reading rooms for a hundred and fifty years — practised this morning's walk for thirty-one years, to the back of the reading room.

The shelf was beside the window.

On the shelf, at the centre, was a small green silk box of the size and shape of a bookbinder's slip-case, with a small white linen label on the spine, on which had been written, in iron-gall ink, in the small slanting confident hand Maeve had been writing in a Pimlico flat for three months — the hand Mrs Wakeham had, on a Tuesday afternoon in the south study of the Belgravia townhouse five months ago, told her she had always known would, in her turn, come back to this exact shelf:

> *TESSA HALLIGAN.*
> *Born 18 May 1958.*
> *St Brigid's, autumn 1972 to spring 1975.*
> *Kept here, on the second of April every year, by her sister, and her sister's daughter, and her sister's daughter's daughter, in turn.*

Beside the box, in a small thin glass vase, was a small white camellia.

The camellia was the small white camellia Mr Stephen Halligan had, by the express to King's Cross at six o'clock that morning, sent up on the eight from Euston. The camellia had arrived at Waverley at one minute past nine. Miss MacLeod had collected it from the platform at twenty past, and had brought it up to the shelf in the small thin glass vase at twenty-five to ten.

Maeve took, from the inside pocket of her good coat, the small green silk pouch she had carried up from Pimlico that morning.

She placed it inside the small green silk box.

She closed the box.

She turned the box, with the small label of TESSA HALLIGAN, very gently, three small degrees, so that the label faced the way Mrs Wakeham had taught her, in the morning room of the Pimlico flat on the morning of the twenty-second of December, to face it — *out to the window, my darling, not in to the room, so she can see the bridge.*

She took, from the same inside pocket of her good coat, the small white camellia she had cut from the small tree in the garden of the Pimlico flat that morning.

She laid it, beside the one Mr Stephen Halligan had sent up from Marylebone, in the small thin glass vase.

She straightened up.

She took her father's hand.

She did not, this morning, in the small reading room of the National Library on George IV Bridge on the second floor on the second of April in the first week of April light, weep.

She had, in the small careful arrangement of seven months of having been Tessa's granddaughter, finished the morning's small weeping the previous evening.

She said, very quietly, to the small green silk box on the shelf — in the careful neutral voice she had built for three weeks at the agency in 2025 before she had ever set foot in the Belgravia townhouse, in the small precise voice that had risen up in her in the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse seven months ago, in the small smaller voice she had been given in the kitchen of the Lewisham bedsit in 2009 by the woman who had raised her, in the voice she shared with the woman in the small silk pouch on the shelf, the woman she had never met, the woman whose name was on the spine of the small green silk box that morning, the woman who would, every second of April for the rest of Maeve Brennan's life and for the rest of any daughter Maeve Brennan would have, be on a shelf with a small white camellia at the back of the reading room of the National Library on the second floor on George IV Bridge —

'*Tessa. We're here.*'

Outside the window, on George IV Bridge, the small mechanical sound of the bus going down towards Princes Street was, for the second of April in the first week of April light, the only sound.

In the inside pocket of Maeve Brennan's good coat, beside the small slip-pocket where the small enamel hairbrush with the letters TH in red had lived for one hundred and one days, the small green silk pouch was, for the first time since the spring of 1975, empty.

She put her free hand on her father's arm.

The two of them walked, in the small careful steps of a father and the daughter who had — between them, in the careful precise way Lewisham fathers and their daughters had been walking out of reading rooms together for a hundred and fifty years — practised this morning's walk for thirty-one years, out of the reading room of the National Library on George IV Bridge on the second floor, in the soft April morning, on the second of April, in the first week of April light, in the spring of the year that Maeve Brennan had finally, in the small careful arrangement of all the women who had been waiting for her, come home.

*— End.*
