## Chapter 1 — The Contract

The Don of the Castellano family was wearing a charcoal three-piece suit, no tie, the top button of his shirt undone, and he was reading a contract.

He had been reading it for four minutes and twelve seconds. Sienna had been counting.

She had been counting because the alternative was thinking about the fact that her father, sitting next to her, had not lifted his head once since they had walked into the room. Her father was looking at his shoes. Her father, who had bounced Sienna on his shoulder at her First Communion, who had run a restaurant in Bensonhurst for thirty-one years, who had once made the entire Russo family stand up at a wedding and call her *la regina* until she had cried with laughing — her father was looking at his shoes, in front of a man whose name was a sentence the rest of New York used to end conversations.

Niccolò Castellano turned a page of the contract.

Sienna watched him turn it.

He had — and she hated that she had noticed this, hated it, was filing it under *things to be furious about later* — beautiful hands. The hands of a man who had not done a day of physical labour in his adult life and yet had, somehow, the knuckles of one who had. Long fingers. A signet ring on the smallest finger of his right hand, a Castellano family crest worn down by four generations of Castellano sons turning it absently when they read contracts.

He was turning it now.

Sienna stared at the ring. She stared at the ring because she would not, she absolutely would not, stare at his face.

—

The face was a problem.

The face was a problem because the face belonged to a man who, in any other context, in any other life, on the cover of any other magazine, Sienna would have stopped on the street to look at twice. Dark hair, cut short the way men cut their hair when they did not want anyone to comment on it. A jaw that looked engineered. Eyes the colour of espresso left in the cup for a minute too long, which was a thing Sienna had never thought about an eye colour before, and which she would have very much liked to un-think now, please.

He was thirty-four. The internet had told her that, last night, at three in the morning, when she had not been sleeping. He had taken over the family three years ago when his father had been arrested. The internet had also told her that no one called him Nico. The internet, in fact, had implied, in the careful indirect way of internet sources that did not wish to be named, that the last person who had called him Nico had not called anyone anything, since.

He was, the internet had said, ruthless.

Sienna had read the word *ruthless* about him in three separate articles, last night, at three in the morning, and she had thought — sitting in her childhood bedroom in Bensonhurst, with the wallpaper she had picked when she was eleven, with a glass of her father's wine on the bedside table, with the contract that she would sign in eleven hours sitting unopened on the duvet — *good. Let him be ruthless. Ruthless I can work with.*

—

He turned the last page.

He closed the contract. He laid it flat on the desk. He placed the fountain pen — a Montblanc, of course it was a Montblanc, Sienna noticed in the part of her brain that was still cataloguing — on top of the contract, parallel to the top edge.

He looked up.

He did not look at her father.

He looked at her.

He said, 'Miss Russo.'

His voice was lower than she had been expecting. Quieter. The voice of a man who had never in his life needed to raise it.

He said, 'Have you read this.'

She said, 'Yes.'

He said, 'All of it.'

She said, 'All of it.'

He said, 'Page seventeen.'

She had been waiting for page seventeen.

She had been waiting for page seventeen since two o'clock in the morning, when she had reached page seventeen for the first time in her childhood bedroom in Bensonhurst, and had read clause 4.2(c) twice, and had sat very still for a long time, and had said — out loud, to the wallpaper she had picked when she was eleven — *oh, you absolute prick.*

She had then, very calmly, picked up her pen, and changed clause 4.2(c).

She had initialled the change.

She had put the contract on top of the duvet.

She had finished her father's wine.

She had gone to sleep.

—

Niccolò Castellano was looking at her.

He said, 'You altered clause 4.2(c).'

She said, 'I did.'

He said, 'Your father did not authorise that alteration.'

She said, 'My father is not signing this contract. I am. The terms in 4.2(c) as drafted were unacceptable. I have made them acceptable. If you would also like to make them acceptable, you may initial the change. If you would not, we will go home, and you will collect my father's debt the long way.'

There was a silence.

The silence was very large.

The silence was, in fact, the largest silence Sienna had ever sat in, and Sienna had sat in some silences in her life, and the silence in the front room of the Castellano family compound in Brooklyn on the morning of October the seventeenth, with her father next to her looking at his shoes and her hands folded in her lap with a calmness she did not feel and the Montblanc fountain pen sitting parallel to the top edge of a contract she had altered without permission — that silence was the largest silence Sienna Russo had been in.

Her father did not move.

Niccolò Castellano did not move.

The clock on the marble mantelpiece behind him made a small mechanical sound.

—

Then Niccolò Castellano did something Sienna had not expected.

He smiled.

It was a small smile. It was not a friendly smile. It was the smile, Sienna would think later, of a man who has been bored for a very long time and has, very suddenly, become unbored.

He picked up the Montblanc.

He turned the contract to page seventeen.

He read her amended clause 4.2(c).

He read it twice.

He looked up at her, and the small not-friendly smile became something quieter, and he initialled the change.

He pushed the contract across the desk towards her.

He said, 'Sign it, Miss Russo.'

And then, just as her fingertips touched the pen, he said — softly, in the same quiet voice he had not raised, would not need to raise, would not raise for the entire six months that were about to follow —

'Sienna.'

She looked up.

He was watching her with his espresso eyes.

He said, 'You should read clause 11.4 a little more carefully. You'll find I altered it, too.'

She did not read clause 11.4.

She did not look down.

She signed.

The pen made a small precise sound against the paper, in the silence of the front room of the Castellano family compound, on the morning of October the seventeenth, in the year that Sienna Russo's life — although she did not yet know this, although she would not know it until she got back to her childhood bedroom in Bensonhurst that evening and finally read clause 11.4 and felt the world tilt forty degrees underneath her — became, in ways she had not authorised, the property of the man across the desk who had smiled at her exactly once, and would do so again, in private, in the back of a black car in the rain, in seventy-three days' time.

She signed her name.

She signed her name and she stood up and she did not look at him again and she left the room, with her father trailing silently behind her, and she got into the back of her father's car, and she said —

'Drive.'

Her father drove.

And it was only when they reached the Verrazzano Bridge, and the lights of Brooklyn were behind them, and her father had not said a single word for forty minutes, that Sienna Russo took the contract out of her bag, and turned to page eleven, and read clause 11.4.

She read it.

She read it again.

She put her head against the cool glass of the car window.

She said, very quietly, to the window, '*Oh, you absolute prick.*'

In her hand, the contract was warm.

In the rear-view mirror, her father's eyes met hers — for the first time that day — and her father, very softly, said:

*'Cara, what have you done.'*

## Chapter 2 — The House

The Hamptons house was at the end of a private road that did not, on any map Sienna had pulled up on her phone in the back of her father's car on the Verrazzano Bridge, exist.

The private road was three quarters of a mile long. The private road was lined, on both sides, by hedges of pleached lime that had been pleached, Sienna had been told later by the woman who looked after them, by a Frenchman in Sag Harbor every May since 1979. The hedges were eleven feet high. The hedges admitted no view, in either direction, of the houses they bounded. There were three houses on the private road. The Castellano house was the last one. The Castellano house had no number on the gate.

The gate had a small brass intercom panel set into the pillar on the right, and a small camera angled down from the pillar on the left, and the gate itself was a thing of black iron worked into a pattern that Sienna would later understand was the same four-generation Castellano crest worn down on the smallest finger of the right hand of every Castellano son to read a contract — but on the night Sienna's father's car pulled up at the gate, the gate was simply a gate, and the camera was simply a camera, and the small brass intercom on the pillar said nothing.

The gate opened.

The gate opened before her father had touched the button on the intercom.

Her father said, in Sicilian, very softly, *'They saw the car at the end of the road.'*

Sienna said, in English, '*They saw the car when it left the bridge.*'

Her father did not, after that, say anything.

—

The drive went on for another four hundred yards.

The drive was gravel. The gravel was, Sienna noted, the small white limestone gravel that she had only ever seen in the drives of the very small number of houses in Brooklyn Heights she had cleaned at for a summer job in her second year at Fordham, when her mother had been having the bad year and her father had not yet been having the conversations with the Castellano family that would, six years later, end on the morning of the seventeenth of October with Sienna signing her name beside a Montblanc fountain pen.

The drive went past a stand of cedars.

The drive went past a small white-painted boathouse on the left, with a single lantern lit in its upper window, although there was no boat in front of it and no water visible from the drive.

The drive went past a small white-painted chapel on the right, with a single lantern lit in its porch, although there was no priest in front of it and no service Sienna had ever heard of being held there.

The drive ended at the front door of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay.

The house was three storeys of grey-painted clapboard with a slate roof and a long deep porch and a single brass lamp burning, very quietly, on the white wooden table beside the door. The house had, in three of the upstairs windows on the right-hand side, lights on, although the upstairs windows on the left-hand side were entirely dark.

The front door was open.

The front door was open in the way the gate had been open — not by someone, not for someone, but in the small ahead-of-itself way of a house that had been told, at some moment between the toll booth on the Verrazzano Bridge and the end of the private road, that something it had been waiting for was on its way.

Niccolò Castellano was not standing on the porch.

—

The woman standing on the porch was perhaps fifty.

She was wearing a black wool dress, single-breasted, with a thin white piping at the collar. She wore no jewellery. Her hair was pinned at the back of her head in a way that had not been the fashion for any decade Sienna could name. Her hands were folded in front of her at her waist, the right one over the left, the fingers very still. She did not, when the car came up the gravel and stopped, move from the porch.

Sienna's father got out of the car.

Sienna's father said, in Sicilian, '*Marta.*'

The woman on the porch said, in English, in a soft Italian accent worn down by thirty years of Long Island, '*Mr Russo. Mr Castellano sends his regrets. He is in town with the lawyer. He will be back at ten.*'

She paused.

She looked, for the first time, at Sienna in the back of the car.

She said, in the same soft Italian accent, 'You are Miss Russo. I am Marta. I run this house. I have run this house for thirty-one years. You will see me every morning at seven and not very often after that. Your room is the south corner of the second floor. Your case is to be sent up to it. You are to come into the kitchen now and have something to eat. Mr Castellano has been very clear that you are not to spend the first hour of your time in this house standing in a corridor.'

Sienna said, 'I'm not hungry, Marta.'

Marta said, 'I have been told to disregard that, Miss Russo.'

She paused.

She said, very gently, 'And I have agreed.'

—

Sienna's father did not come into the house.

Sienna's father stood on the gravel drive with his hands in the pockets of his good coat and his face set in the small particular set of a man who had, for the first time in his fifty-eight years, been told by his daughter to do something he did not, on every fibre of his Sicilian-American body, wish to do — and he had agreed to do it, on the cool glass of the back window of his own car, on the Verrazzano Bridge, an hour and a half ago.

Sienna stood on the porch.

Sienna looked at her father, in the gravel drive of a private road that did not exist on any map, in the white limestone gravel light of the single brass lamp on the white wooden table beside the door of a house she had not, until two hours ago, known her sister's contract had ever specified, and Sienna said, in English, in the careful voice she had built for the family table for twenty-six years —

'Papa. Go home.'

Her father said, in Sicilian, '*Sienna.*'

Sienna said, in Sicilian, '*Go home, Papa. Bring Adriana over for Sunday lunch. Tell mum I am at the airport on a layover from Boston. Tell her I shall ring. I shall ring on Sunday at four. I shall ring on Sunday at four every Sunday for the next six months. Tell her that. Do not, on any account, tell her where I am. Do not, on any account, tell Adriana where I am. Do not, on any account, do the small face you are doing now in front of mum or you will lose her in six minutes and you will spend the rest of my contract paying for it. Look at me. Look at me, Papa. Look at me.*'

Her father, on the gravel drive, looked at her.

Sienna said, in English, in the careful voice, 'Look at me until you have stopped doing the face, Papa.'

He looked.

She looked back.

He looked, eventually, until his face had done what she had asked it to do.

He nodded, once.

He got back in the car.

The car drove away down the private road of pleached lime hedges that did not exist on any map, and the gate at the end of the road closed behind him, and the white limestone gravel of the drive was, for one held second, the only sound on the porch.

Then Marta, behind Sienna, said very gently, 'In, Miss Russo. The chicken will go cold.'

—

The kitchen of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay was on the ground floor at the back, with three tall windows that looked out, in the daylight Sienna would later see them in, over a long sloping lawn to the silver line of the bay.

On the night of the seventeenth of October, the windows showed the lawn in the rectangle of the kitchen light and nothing beyond. The kitchen smelled, when Sienna came in, of garlic and rosemary and the small particular smell of a piece of cold roast chicken that has been waiting on a counter under a piece of muslin for the moment its diner sits down.

The chicken was on the counter under a piece of muslin.

The chicken was beside a small dish of olives. The olives were beside a small loaf of bread on a wooden board. The bread was beside a small bowl of fresh figs, halved, with a knife on the rim of the bowl. The figs were beside a small carafe of white wine and a single glass with the small precise plain cut Sienna would later understand had been the Castellano table glass for four generations.

There was no plate set for Niccolò.

There was no second glass.

There was, at the head of the long oak table, a single place laid for one — Sienna's.

Sienna sat down.

She did not, at first, eat.

Marta sat down opposite her. Marta poured the wine. Marta cut Sienna a small piece of the bread, the way Sienna's grandmother had cut bread for her at five, and Marta put it on the side plate at Sienna's right hand.

Marta said, 'Eat, Miss Russo.'

Sienna ate.

The chicken, she noted in the part of her brain that was still cataloguing, had been cooked by a person who knew how to cook a chicken. The wine had been opened, by someone, in the last half hour. The figs were Italian.

Marta watched her eat.

Marta did not, in the time Sienna ate, take her eyes off Sienna's face.

Marta said, after a while, in the soft Italian-Long-Island accent, 'You altered clause 4.2(c).'

Sienna said, with her mouth full of bread, 'I did.'

Marta said, 'He read it twice.'

Sienna said, 'I know. I was there.'

Marta said, 'You were not there for the bit afterwards, when he sat in this kitchen at the place where you are sitting and read it a third time on his phone while I made him an espresso he did not drink. You were not there for the bit after that, when he went out onto the porch with the espresso and did not come back inside for forty minutes. I was. I have been on this property for thirty-one years, Miss Russo. I have seen him do that twice. Both times on the porch. Both times with an espresso he did not drink. The first time was when his father was arrested in 2021 and he was told that the family was, from then on, his. The second time was tonight.'

Sienna put her bread down.

She said, 'Marta. Why are you telling me this.'

Marta said, very quietly, 'Because, Miss Russo, the contract you signed this morning has, on its second-to-last page, a clause that he wrote with the same fountain pen and that you did not, today, read carefully. The clause is 11.4. The clause says, in its English version, that for the duration of your engagement to him you are not, by anyone in this house including him, to be made to feel that you are, in any sense, a man's property. The clause says that you are, for six months, to be a woman in his house who is treated as a woman in her own house. The clause says that should I, Marta Lima, who has run this house for thirty-one years and have spoken to him very plainly only once in those thirty-one years, in 2021 — should I, today, on any single morning of the six months, judge that he has fallen below the standard of that clause, I am to ring his lawyer and the entire contract is, on my single phone call, void.'

She paused.

She said, 'I am telling you this, Miss Russo, because he asked me to. He asked me to in the kitchen tonight, when he came in from the porch, before he left for the lawyer in town. He said it to me in Sicilian. He said it like a man who has, in the small precise way of men who have learned a thing in the very hard way, decided something. He said, *Marta, tell her about 11.4. Tell her about 11.4 in the kitchen. Tell her about 11.4 before I am in the house with her.*'

Marta paused.

She added, in English, very gently, 'And I have. So now you know. You may eat your fig now, Miss Russo. It is a very good fig. I picked it this afternoon.'

—

Sienna ate the fig.

It was a very good fig.

She did not, in the time she ate the fig, say anything.

She finished the wine in her glass. She did not let Marta pour her another. She thanked Marta in Sicilian, in the small careful Sicilian she had been raised on, and she stood up, and she let Marta show her up the back staircase to the south corner of the second floor.

The south corner of the second floor was a room of perhaps twenty feet by sixteen, with one tall sash window that looked out over the lawn and the bay, with a four-poster bed in dove-grey linen, with a small writing desk under the window, with a single tall narrow bookcase against the inside wall on which had been placed — Sienna noticed in the very particular way of women noticing, at the end of a day in which they had signed a contract on a desk in Brooklyn — eleven books, paperback, in a neat row, the spines all out, the small white labels of a recent purchase still on the upper corner of each.

The eleven books were all hers.

The eleven books were the eleven books on the small shelf of her childhood bedroom in Bensonhurst, which she had not been into in nine months, which had a wallpaper she had picked at eleven, which had — in the small pile on the dressing table beside the bed — exactly these eleven books, in exactly this order, with the wear patterns Sienna had been adding to them since she was thirteen.

These were, however, fresh copies of those eleven books. New paperbacks. Not hers.

He had not, Sienna understood as she stood in the doorway of the south corner of the second floor of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay, taken the books off her shelf. He had, somewhere, sent someone to find out which eleven books were on the shelf in her childhood bedroom, and he had had someone go out and buy them, and he had had someone arrange them, in order, on the small narrow bookcase against the inside wall of the bedroom in which Sienna would, for the next six months, sleep.

He had not, on the bookshelf, put his.

Sienna put her hand on the doorframe of the bedroom of the south corner of the second floor of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay, and Sienna thought, in the small precise voice she had built for the family table for twenty-six years, *Sienna. Stop noticing the books. Stop noticing the books. Stop, on the night of the day you have signed a six-month contract on a desk in Brooklyn, noticing the books.*

She walked into the room.

She closed the door behind her.

She did not, immediately, lock it.

She thought, in a smaller voice — the voice she would not until two weeks from this evening be able to admit out loud, even to the wallpaper she had picked at eleven, even to a phone box outside a Sag Harbor café on a Sunday morning at four o'clock — *Oh, you absolute prick.*

She did, after a long moment, lock the door.

She did not, however, take her hand off the brass key.

The car was, by the gravel of the drive, well past the gate by now. The drive was, in the dark beyond the window, very quiet. The gate would not, by anyone Sienna was beginning to understand he could be, be opened from the inside for the next nine hours. He would, by Marta's account, be back at ten. It was a quarter past eight.

She stood at the window of the south corner bedroom of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay, with her hand on the brass key of a door she had locked, and she watched the slim silver line of the bay, and she waited for the headlights at the end of the private road.

## Chapter 3 — Clause 11.4

The headlights at the end of the private road came at five past ten.

Sienna saw them from the window. She had not, in the hour and forty minutes between Marta closing the bedroom door and the headlights at the end of the road, slept. She had not, in the hour and forty minutes, undressed. She had not, in the hour and forty minutes, lain on the dove-grey linen of the four-poster bed in any position from which the door to the corridor was not, at all times, in her line of sight.

She had, in the hour and forty minutes, read clause 11.4 of the contract.

She had read it five times.

The contract was on the writing desk under the window. The contract — six pages of close-typed Castellano family legal-letterhead, with Sienna's initials in pencil at the foot of pages one to five and Sienna's full signature at the bottom of page six in the careful round handwriting her grandmother had taught her in 2002 — sat on the writing desk where she had laid it on coming into the room, parallel to the top edge of the leather inlay, the Montblanc that was not Niccolò's Montblanc but a small black ballpoint she had taken from her own bag laid parallel to the top edge of the contract.

Clause 11.4 was on page eleven.

The contract was six pages.

The pagination — Sienna had noticed in the third reading — ran 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 11. Page 6 ended at the foot of the page in the small typeset of a footer that read *page 6 of 6.* Page 11, on the following sheet, ran on as if it had been removed from a longer document Sienna had not been shown, and bore at its head the small typeset of a header that read *page 11 of 14.*

There was no page 7, 8, 9, 10, 12, 13, or 14 in the contract.

There was only page 11.

There was only clause 11.4.

The clause had been inserted, by him, on a single page that did not belong to the contract she had read in his front office that morning. Page 11 had not been on the desk when Niccolò had pushed the contract towards her at twenty past ten in the morning. Page 11 had been put into the contract — Sienna would later be able to date the moment to the gap of nineteen seconds during which Niccolò Castellano had, on standing up from the desk, walked to the window of the front office and stood with his back to her looking out into the courtyard of the Brooklyn compound, while a small precise sound she had not, at the time, identified had come from the desk behind her.

The sound had been him sliding a single sheet of paper into the back of the leather portfolio.

The single sheet of paper was page 11.

—

Clause 11.4 read:

> *4. Conduct of the parties during the term of the engagement.*
>
> *11.4 The First Party (hereinafter "Mr Castellano") agrees, for the entire duration of the term of the engagement set out in clause 2, to conduct himself, in respect of the Second Party (hereinafter "Miss Russo"), as follows:*
>
> *(a) Mr Castellano shall not, at any time, enter any room of the Property in which Miss Russo is alone without first knocking at the door of that room and being granted express verbal permission to enter by Miss Russo;*
>
> *(b) Mr Castellano shall not, at any time, touch Miss Russo without first having received, on the same day, express verbal permission to do so from Miss Russo herself, such permission to be revocable at any moment and without explanation;*
>
> *(c) Mr Castellano shall not, at any time, address Miss Russo by any name other than her given name "Sienna" or, where Miss Russo herself requests it, "Miss Russo";*
>
> *(d) Mr Castellano shall not, at any time, attempt to extract from Miss Russo any account of the contents of any private correspondence, telephone call, or written record of hers conducted with persons outside the Property;*
>
> *(e) Mr Castellano shall not, at any time, prevent Miss Russo from leaving the Property by any means at her disposal, including the keys to any vehicle on the Property, the security gate at the entrance to the Property, or the use of any telephone within the Property to summon a vehicle from outside;*
>
> *(f) Mr Castellano shall, on or before the seventh day of every calendar month of the term of the engagement, deposit into a bank account in the sole name of Miss Russo, at a bank of Miss Russo's choosing, the sum of $35,000 USD, which sum is irrevocable and is not contingent on the continuation of the engagement nor on the performance by Miss Russo of any obligation under this contract.*
>
> *11.4.1 The standard of conduct set out in clause 11.4(a)–(f) is to be assessed, on a daily basis, by Marta Lima, housekeeper of the Property since 1994, in her sole and unappealable discretion. Mrs Lima is empowered, on any single morning of the term of the engagement, to make a single telephone call to the legal representative of Mr Castellano (Mr Vito Castellano of 14 Court Street, Brooklyn, NY 11201) which call shall, on its connection, render the entirety of the present contract void and discharge Miss Russo from all further obligation under it. Mr Castellano shall, on the receipt by his legal representative of such telephone call, have no recourse, no right of appeal, and no avenue to renegotiate.*
>
> *11.4.2 Mrs Lima shall not, in making any such telephone call, be required to provide cause, evidence, or explanation. Her judgment alone is sufficient.*
>
> *11.4.3 The four-million-dollar debt owed by the family of Miss Russo to the Castellano family shall, on the placing of such telephone call, be — by Mr Castellano's signature on the present page — fully extinguished and not, on any subsequent date and for any reason, reinstated.*
>
> *11.4.4 Miss Russo shall not, at any time, be informed by Mrs Lima or by any other party to this contract of the threshold of conduct at which Mrs Lima would consider the present clause violated. The threshold is, by Mr Castellano's signature on the present page, Mrs Lima's alone.*
>
> *Signed: Niccolò V. Castellano. Date: 16 October.*

The clause had been signed by him the day before he had handed her the contract.

The clause had been signed in his own pen, in his own hand, in the small clear ink Sienna had watched him initial 4.2(c) with that morning.

The clause was, by every reading of it Sienna's degree in international law at Fordham had given her, the cleanest single act of legal self-binding she had ever seen put on a Castellano family letterhead.

He had, for six months, on a single piece of paper she had not been shown until she had got into bed in his house, given his housekeeper the power to free her.

—

Sienna had finished reading it for the fifth time, on the writing desk under the window of the south corner of the second floor of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay, at four minutes past ten on the evening of the seventeenth of October, and Sienna had laid the contract down, and Sienna had walked to the window, and Sienna had watched the headlights at the end of the private road of pleached lime hedges that did not exist on any map come up the drive in the small white limestone gravel, with the small even speed of a car driven by a man who was not in any hurry.

The car was a black Mercedes saloon.

The car had, in the back seat, no one.

The car had, in the front seat, only the driver, who got out, opened the back door for him, and stood with the door open against his hip and his face turned away as Niccolò Castellano got out of the back seat — in the charcoal suit, no tie, the top button of his shirt undone — and walked across the gravel to the porch of his own house, and did not, on his way to the porch, glance up at the lit window of the south corner of the second floor.

Sienna watched him.

He did not glance up.

He walked across the porch, opened his front door — which had been, since Marta had let Sienna in, closed and Marta had locked it — with his own key, went into the house, closed the door behind him, and did not, in the next forty-one minutes by Sienna's count on the small brass mantel clock she had not, before this evening, noticed sat on the writing desk, come up the back staircase to the second floor.

He went, instead, to the kitchen.

Sienna heard, very faintly through three closed doors and a long corridor, the sound of him saying, in Sicilian, to Marta — *'Si è seduta. Ha mangiato. Mi hai detto del fico.'*

She had sat. She had eaten. You told her about the fig.

Marta said, in Sicilian, very calmly — *'Le ho detto del fico, Mr Castellano. Le ho detto di 11.4. Le ho detto che dovrebbe dormire prima che lei provasse a farti la parola. Adesso, mi pare, è alla scrivania a rileggere la pagina undici.'*

I have told her about the fig. I have told her about 11.4. I have told her she should sleep before you try to speak to her. Now, I should think, she is at the desk re-reading page eleven.

Niccolò said, in Sicilian, in the same quiet voice he had not raised that morning at the desk in Brooklyn, '*Sì. Marta. Grazie. Vai a letto. Domani mattina non venire prima delle otto. Non busserò alla sua porta. Non mi vedrà fino a colazione.*'

Yes. Marta. Thank you. Go to bed. Tomorrow morning do not come before eight. I shall not knock at her door. She will not see me until breakfast.

There was a beat.

He said, in English, very quietly, into the kitchen of his own house on Sagaponack Bay — for, Sienna understood, in the careful precise way of women who had been listening at doors all their lives, her own benefit, with the door at the top of the back staircase very slightly ajar — '*And Marta. If at any moment in the next six months you believe I have not kept page eleven, you will, with my full thanks, lift the telephone. I have meant every word of clause 11.4. I shall not be in this house, on any morning, in any way that has not earned its place in it.*'

Marta said, in English, '*Yes, Mr Castellano.*'

Marta said, in Sicilian, '*Buona notte, Niccolò.*'

The kitchen light, by the slow softening at the floor of the corridor that Sienna could see from the gap of her own bedroom door which she had, without quite admitting it to herself, opened the half-inch at the bottom an hour ago, went out.

The footsteps of Marta went up the front staircase to the third floor.

The footsteps of Niccolò Castellano did not go anywhere.

She heard, faintly, the small precise sound of a single dining chair being scraped back on the kitchen floor.

She heard the small precise sound of him sitting down.

She heard, after a long beat, the small precise sound of a glass being placed on the kitchen table, and the small precise sound of a tap being turned on, and the small precise sound of water running into a glass for a count of perhaps four seconds, and the small precise sound of the tap being turned off, and the small precise sound of the glass being placed back on the kitchen table.

Then she heard nothing for a long time.

She understood, in the part of her brain that had been raised on the kitchen of a restaurant in Bensonhurst by a father who had taken meetings with men in that kitchen for thirty-one years, that he was sitting at the kitchen table of his own house on the night his contracted fiancée had moved into his second floor, and he was drinking a glass of water from his own tap, and he was waiting to hear her come down the back staircase — and he was waiting in a way that, by clause 11.4(a), he could not himself walk towards.

She stood, with one hand on the brass key of her own door, at the gap she had opened at the bottom of it.

She thought, in the small precise voice she had built for the family table for twenty-six years, *Sienna. Lock your door. Lock your door and go to bed. The thirty-five thousand dollars goes into your account on the seventh of November whether you go down those stairs tonight or not.*

She thought, in a smaller voice that was not the family-table voice and was not the voice of her sister and was not, even, the voice of her grandmother — the voice was new, and the voice was the voice of a woman who had read clause 11.4 five times in the last hour and forty minutes — *Sienna. He is sitting at his kitchen table drinking a glass of his own tap water and waiting to find out whether you are the woman who, on the first night, would come down. He is going to be sitting there until you have gone to bed. If you do not go down, he will sit there until two. If you go down, he will, by the clause he wrote, not touch you, will not speak to you in a name that is not yours, will not ask you what you have read on page eleven. He has made it impossible for him to do any of the things the rest of his name would, in any other man's house, have made him do. You do not have to be afraid of him tonight. You may, if you wish, simply go down and ask him a question.*

She thought, very quietly, *I have one question.*

She did not, in the south corner of the second floor of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay, lock the door.

She took her hand off the brass key.

She put her hand on the brass handle.

She opened the door, very quietly, into the corridor.

—

She walked, in the small careful steps her grandmother had taught her on the polished parquet of the Russo house in Bensonhurst, in her bare feet, down the corridor and down the back staircase to the kitchen.

The kitchen light was off.

A single small bulb above the back step had been turned on. The bulb cast a small soft yellow light into the kitchen as far as the long oak table.

Niccolò Castellano was sitting at the long oak table. He had his sleeves rolled to the elbows. His suit jacket was over the back of the chair behind him. His glass of water was on the table in front of him, half full. His Montblanc was beside the glass. His phone was beside the Montblanc, screen-down. His hands were folded on the table, the right one over the left, the signet ring on the smallest finger of his right hand catching the small soft yellow light.

He did not, when she came in, stand up.

He did not, when she came in, look up.

He looked at his own hands on the table.

He said, in English, in the same quiet voice he had not raised at the desk in Brooklyn, '*Miss Russo.*'

He said, after a beat, '*You have read 11.4.*'

She said, in English, in the careful voice, 'I have.'

He said, 'You have a question.'

She said, 'I have one.'

He said, 'Ask it.'

She sat down at the long oak table opposite him.

She did not, when she sat, allow her hands to do the small involuntary movement they had begun to do at the bottom of the back staircase.

She said, very quietly, 'Why.'

Niccolò Castellano lifted his eyes from his own hands on the table for the first time since she had come into the kitchen of his house on Sagaponack Bay, and he looked at Sienna Russo across the half-full glass of water and the Montblanc and the screen-down phone and the small soft yellow light from the back-step bulb — and he looked at her in the small particular way of a man who had, that morning, in his own front office in Brooklyn, smiled exactly once at a woman who had altered clause 4.2(c) in her childhood bedroom in Bensonhurst at two o'clock in the morning, and was, now, in the kitchen of his own house at six minutes past eleven on the evening of the seventeenth of October, being asked the only question he had, in his entire adult life, ever wanted to be asked.

He said, very quietly, in English, '*Because I am thirty-four years old, Sienna Russo, and I have spent the whole of my adult life inside the house that my father built for me, and I have not, in any single morning of those years, been allowed to find out what kind of man I would be if there had been, in any single room of that house, a single other person whose presence in the house was not the property of the family. I do not know what kind of man that is. I have decided, this autumn, that I should very much like to find out. I cannot, by every other clause of every other contract on my desk, find out alone. I have, this morning, in my front office in Brooklyn, by altering a single page of a contract and adding a single page that wasn't on it, asked one specific other person to be in this house with me, for six months, on the strictest terms I could write into the asking, while I find out.*'

He paused.

He said, '*That is the answer to the question, Sienna. The question, I should also say, is the only one you have, on the night of your first day in this house, been brave enough to come down those stairs to ask, and the bravery — by every page of clause 11.4 that I wrote yesterday afternoon at this table — is exactly the property of yours that I have committed, by the signing of my own pen, not to touch.*'

He paused.

He said, very softly, in Sicilian, the small line his own grandmother had taught him at five — '*Adesso, cara, va' a letto.*'

Now, dear, go to bed.

—

Sienna sat for a long second at the long oak kitchen table of the Castellano house on Sagaponack Bay opposite the man whose name was a sentence the rest of New York used to end conversations, and Sienna thought, in the small precise voice she had built for the family table for twenty-six years, in the new voice she had built in the last hour and forty minutes at the writing desk under the window of the south corner of the second floor, in the small smaller voice she had not until this evening admitted she had — *Sienna. Stand up. Stand up. Stand up before you do or say a thing that is on the wrong side of his clause and that he has, by the only clause he signed in his own pen yesterday afternoon, made it impossible for him to refuse.*

She stood up.

She did not, on standing, look at his hands.

She said, in the careful voice, 'Goodnight, Mr Castellano.'

He said, in the careful voice, '*Niccolò.*'

She paused, with one hand on the back of the chair she had stood from, and she said —

'Niccolò.'

The corner of his mouth did not, this time, do the small thing it had done in the front office.

He did not smile.

He said, very quietly, '*Goodnight, Sienna.*'

She walked, in the small careful steps her grandmother had taught her, in her bare feet, across the kitchen and up the back staircase and along the corridor of the second floor to the south corner bedroom.

She closed the door behind her.

She locked it.

She stood, for a long second, with her back against the inside of the door, and her right hand still on the brass key.

She did not, in that long second, allow her face to do any of the things her face would, by the count of any honest observer, very reasonably have done.

She thought, in the small new voice that had risen up in her in the last hour and forty minutes — and would, over the next six months, become the voice she would use most often in this house —

*Oh, Niccolò. You absolute prick. You absolute, careful, terrifying prick.*
