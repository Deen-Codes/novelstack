This log carries two things:

1. **The implicit style guide** extracted from the 5 books already shipped under
   `@novelstackoriginals` (originals 06–10). Every new book added to this
   catalogue is written to honour that voice — same prose register, same
   blurb formula, same chapter-1 architecture.
2. **The 45 new books planned** to extend the seed catalogue from 5 → 50
   total. Each entry includes title, author persona (with is_seed=true),
   genre lane, BookTok comp, blurb, opening line, and cover concept.

The point of this document is editorial continuity. An agent picking this up
in a future session should be able to read the style guide, look at any
planned book, and write a chapter-1 that sits in the same bookshop section
as *The Housekeeper's Lie* and *The Frostbound Court*.

## Part 1 — The implicit style guide (derived from books 06–10)

### Title architecture

Definite-article noun phrases dominate. *The Housekeeper's Lie. The Frostbound
Court. The Almost. The Hollow Hour.* The one outlier — *Bound by the Don* — is
a trope-direct hook because that book is the only pure dark-romance in the
catalogue and its market expects the title to telegraph the trope. Otherwise:

- 2–4 words; 3 is the sweet spot.
- "The + adjective + noun" or "The + noun + 's + noun".
- The noun is concrete (Drawer, Court, Letter, Photograph) — never abstract.
- Slight literary lean (*The Almost* is grammatically a fragment, used as a
  noun — this is on-register, not off).
- No subtitles. No series numbering. No genre tags in the title.
- New titles in this catalogue follow these constraints unless a tropestack
  romance demands the trope-direct format (in which case "Bound by…",
  "Mine to…", "The Don's…", "The King's…" lead).

### Chapter-1 chapter title

Short noun phrase, usually the central object of the scene: *The Drawer. The
Contract. The Letter. The Bargain at the Frozen Gate.* For dual-POV the
format is `POV · noun phrase` — *Eva · The Catering Quote*.

### Prose voice — the signature

These are the seven hallmarks every chapter-1 in this catalogue carries.

**1. Sentence-level repetition as rhythm.** A phrase or clause is restated
across consecutive sentences to build hypnotic tension:

> *The drawer was open. The drawer had not been open when she came in… and
> now the drawer was open.*

**2. Triadic structure.** When introducing a setting, lore, or character,
information is delivered in threes — often as a literal numbered triad:

> *There were three things every village child along the southern marches
> knew about the Frostbound Court. The first was that… The second was that…
> The third was that…*

**3. Cataloguing as characterisation.** People and rooms are introduced by
listing concrete, brand-specific objects. Cashmere the colour of bone. A
Montblanc fountain pen. A signet ring worn down by four generations. Lavender
hand cream. A half-empty pack of Nytol. The reader learns who a person is by
what they own and how they own it.

**4. Internal italicised murmurs.** Thought is rarely narrated. When it is,
it appears in italic single-line interjections, often profane:

> *oh, you absolute prick.*

**5. "X had not Y" syntax as motif.** A negation phrase becomes a recurring
character anchor — Maeve's *her hands did not shake* repeats five times across
chapter 1, building toward the closing reversal where her hands begin to
shake. Every protagonist in this catalogue gets one such motif phrase.

**6. Long looping comma-built sentences thunder-stopped by very short ones.**
A 60-word sentence carries an emotional climb; a 4-word sentence lands the
hit. They alternate.

> *And in the morning room of the Belgravia townhouse, on a Tuesday in
> November, with the cup of green tea on the marble side table making its
> small definite sound against the silence, Maeve Brennan felt her hands
> begin, very quietly, to shake.*
>
> *She did not stop them.*

**7. Time-and-place anchoring at decisive moments.** When something
irreversible happens, the prose names the exact day, season, weather and
location with old-novel formality: *on a Tuesday in November, in the
morning room of the Belgravia townhouse.* This is on-register; do not
sand it down.

### What the prose does **not** do

- No internal monologue paragraphs of "she thought about how…"
- No tell-don't-show emotion words ("she felt sad", "he was angry") —
  always exteriorized through gesture, object, or italicised line.
- No info-dump description of side characters' biographies.
- No purple-prose adjective stacks — adjectives come singly, doing work.
- No "she didn't know how she felt" hand-waving — every line knows what it
  means.

### Heroine register

Every protagonist enters the scene already braced. Maeve has trained her
hands not to shake. Sienna has read the contract twice, altered it, and
finished her father's wine before going to sleep. Linnea has hacked her
sister's coat to fit and crossed a forbidden river. Eva has spent four
years pretending not to think about August. Wren opens the letter on her
bed with the kettle already boiled.

No protagonist is a passive recipient of plot. Every one of them
**arrives loaded**. The first scene's job is to detonate them — but the
gunpowder was there before the camera turned on.

### Male-lead register (where present)

Quietly powerful, **never raises his voice**. Watches. Notices. Has at
least one named luxe object on his person (signet ring, Montblanc, suit
without a tie). Smiles rarely; when he does, it changes the room. Speaks
in clipped lines — "Sign it, Miss Russo" — that carry more weight than a
paragraph would. Does not lecture, does not explain, does not threaten.
The threat is implicit in the precision.

### Blurb formula (locked — use for every new book)

Three sentences. Sentence 1 sets the protagonist + the inciting situation.
Sentence 2 introduces the antagonist / love interest / mystery. Sentence
3 is the hook — a single revelation that makes the reader tap *Read*.
50–70 words. For romance/fantasy/spec-fic only: a trailing italic
tropestack with bullet-separated tags ("· Slow-burn · enemies-to-lovers ·
ACOTAR readers welcome").

Example (from *The Housekeeper's Lie*):

> *Maeve takes a job cleaning for a wealthy couple in a Belgravia townhouse.
> Three weeks in, she finds a photograph of herself in the wife's bedside
> drawer, taken when she was twelve. The wife she's been working for, six
> days a week, has been waiting for her.*

### Persona naming conventions

**Female leads.** Maeve, Sienna, Linnea, Eva, Wren. One or two syllables.
Distinctive but pronounceable on first read. Often slightly Celtic
(Maeve, Linnea), Italian (Sienna), or Anglo-mythic (Wren, Eva). Surnames
crisp and consonant-led: Brennan, Russo, Vaer, Whitlock, Mercer.

**Male leads.** Niccolò Castellano (full Italian aristocratic), August
Reed (American Steady), the King of Winter (mythic, name withheld). When
named, the surname carries the genre — Italian for mafia, Anglo-Norman
for fantasy royalty, plain for contemporary.

**Author personas (the by-line on the cover).** Existing 5 all under
`@novelstackoriginals` as the house imprint. Going forward, half the
catalogue stays under that imprint (the house brand); half migrate to
genre-specific seed personas to mimic a real publisher's roster. Every
seed persona carries `is_seed = true` on the user row so they can be
wiped or filtered at any time without touching real users.

### Cover-art register

The shipped 5 use cover_color hex values in a dusty-blue / charcoal /
ember palette. No candy. No CG faces. The cover language is **atmospheric
object photography with bold typographic overlay** — composited by
`scripts/cover_overlay.py` so the title typography is real and legible
(rather than AI-image-model-mangled).

Every cover concept in this catalogue specifies (a) the atmospheric prompt
for the background image, (b) the title font from the loaded palette
(Bricolage, Fraunces, IBM Plex Mono, Manrope etc.), and (c) the position
(centred / top / bottom-left).

### Fade-to-black convention

Colleen Hoover (Verity), Penelope Douglas (Credence), Ana Huang's
King-of-Wall-Street stack — comp-author books in these lanes regularly
go explicit. The current writing engine for this catalogue is not in
the business of producing explicit prose. Convention going forward:

> When a scene would, in comp register, escalate to explicit, the prose
> fades to black at the threshold AND drops a literal marker into the
> markdown source:
>
> `[FADE TO BLACK — comp-author register (PD Credence) would here render
>  X. Mark for human-pass replacement: insert the explicit Penelope-
>  Douglas-register Y here in v2.]`

The marker is the seam at which a human writer (or a different
generation pass) can later drop in the explicit scene at the right
register. The seed loader (`scripts/load-originals-v4.mjs`) strips the
markers from the word-count and excerpt summaries but inserts the body
as-is into `chapter_content`, so the markers are visible in the reader
view too — useful for editorial pass but the reader sees them as
narrative beats rather than missing content.

Counted across all 13 books, there will likely be 10-25 such markers
in the final fully-complete catalogue. They are intentional, tracked,
and replaceable.

## Part 3.6 — Multi-session production plan

Producing 13 full novels at 15-25 chapters each is roughly 500k-800k
words of competitive-quality fiction. Realistic pace per overnight
session, judged from the chapter-1s + the chapters-2-4 of #06 +
chapters-2-3 of #07 actually shipped tonight: **5-8 chapters per
session** at production quality, distributed across at most two books
to keep voice cohesion.

### Session log so far

| Session | Books advanced | Chapters delivered | Approx. word output |
|---------|----------------|--------------------|----------------------|
| Tonight (run 1) | 8 chapter-1s across new books (11, 17, 25, 32, 39, 43, 48, 52) | 8 chapter-1s | ~24,000 |
| Tonight (run 2 — continuation) | #06 ch 2-4, #07 ch 2-3 | 5 continuation chapters | ~14,000 |

### Forward plan (suggested per future session)

Each subsequent overnight session should:

1. Pick **two** books to advance (one already in flight + one new), max.
2. Write **3-5 new chapters** in each.
3. Leave **at least one FADE TO BLACK marker** at the right comp-register
   threshold per book — explicit scenes are intentional placeholders.
4. Update this log's session table.
5. Re-run `node scripts/load-originals-v4.mjs` (or
   `--only=06,07` etc) to push the new chapters into Render.

Estimated total sessions to take all 13 books to "complete" status:
**8-12 sessions**, depending on whether Deen wants the comp-author
escalation arcs prose-written tonight (longer) or marker-flagged for
human-pass replacement (faster).
