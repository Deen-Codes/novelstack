For three months, Marcus Hale had stood at the same spot on the same touchline every Saturday morning, eleven minutes' walk from a flat that still didn't feel like his, and had not spoken to the man who stood twelve feet to his left.

There was a system to a touchline, and Marcus had learned it the way you learn anything in the first raw year after a marriage ends: by watching other people who seemed to know. You arrived a little early. You found a spot where you could see your kid without crowding the goal. You did not get involved in the politics of the snack rota. And you kept, by unspoken civic agreement, twelve feet between yourself and the next parent, a respectful national-border distance that said *we are both here for our children and we need not pretend to be more than that.*

The man twelve feet to his left was, Marcus had worked out from three months of pickup-and-dropoff observation, called Owen. He had a daughter, Freya, who played left wing with a ferocity that suggested she had things to work out, the way kids of a certain divorced household did. Owen wore the same dark green waterproof every week. He brought a thermos. He shouted encouragement at the whole team rather than just his own child, which Marcus had privately noted as the mark of a decent man, and he never, ever shouted at the referee, which Marcus had privately noted as the mark of a rare one.

They had nodded at each other perhaps forty times. They had never said a word.

This Saturday was cold in the particular flat grey way of late November, the pitch gone heavy, the kids' breath visible, and Marcus had his hands jammed in his pockets and was watching his son Daniel — nine years old, playing right back, taking it all far too seriously, which Marcus recognised because Daniel had got that from him — when Owen, twelve feet away, said something.

Not to Marcus. To the pitch. But out loud, into the cold, in a tone of pure weary affection: "He's offside again. Freya. Sweetheart. We have *talked* about the line."

And Marcus, before he had cleared it with the part of himself that managed the twelve-foot border, laughed.

It wasn't a big laugh. It was barely a laugh at all, just a breath with some shape to it. But it was the first sound he'd directed at another adult on that touchline in three months, and Owen turned his head.

"Sorry," Marcus said. "It's just — mine does the exact same thing. The offside thing. I've drawn him a diagram. I've drawn him an actual diagram, with arrows."

"Did it work?"

"It did not work."

Owen smiled. He had a good smile, the kind that arrived slowly and seemed faintly surprised at itself, and up close — twelve feet was apparently close, Marcus's standards had recalibrated — he was perhaps Marcus's own age, mid-forties, with the specific tiredness that Marcus had come to recognise in the mirror and in other men at school gates: not unhealthy, just *worn,* the tiredness of someone carrying a full bag a long way without complaint.

"Owen," he said.

"Marcus."

"I know." Owen said it, and then looked briefly like he wished he hadn't, like the knowing gave something away. "I mean — Daniel's dad. The kids talk. Freya mentioned a Daniel who's good at maths and bad at sharing the ball."

"That's him." Marcus felt something he hadn't felt on the touchline before, a small unfreezing. "Freya's the one who scored that goal against the yellow team. The one where she —"

"The one where she celebrated for a full minute and got booked for time-wasting in an under-tens match." Owen closed his eyes briefly, a man revisiting a private cross to bear. "Yes. That's my daughter. I was very proud and very mortified, which I'm told is just parenting now."

Marcus laughed again, properly this time, and on the pitch the whistle went for some infraction only the referee had seen, and the two of them stood there in the flat grey cold, and the twelve feet between them did not get smaller exactly, but it stopped feeling like a border. It started feeling like a thing you could, if you wanted, simply walk across.

"They do this every Saturday," Owen said, watching the kids reset. "The least we could do is not stand here in silence like a pair of strangers at a bus stop."

"I've been standing here in silence for three months," Marcus admitted. "I think I'd forgotten there was another option."

Owen looked at him then — a real look, the kind that takes a half-second too long, the kind Marcus had not been on the receiving end of in longer than he wanted to count — and said, mildly, "Well. There's another option." And then he turned back to the match and shouted, "GOOD, Freya, that's the line, that's exactly the line," and Marcus stood twelve feet away that were no longer twelve feet, and felt the cold morning shift very slightly under him, like ground that had been frozen a long time and had just, somewhere far down, begun to give.
