The coffee at the touchline came from a hatch at the side of the clubhouse, was served by a teenager who hated his life, and was, by universal touchline agreement, terrible.

It was so terrible that drinking it had become a kind of joke, and the joke was the point. The Saturday after they'd first spoken, Marcus got to the hatch and found Owen already there, already holding two of the grey-brown cups, and Owen handed one over without asking, the way you'd hand a comrade something in a trench.

"I got you one," Owen said. "I want to be clear this isn't a kindness. Misery wants company."

It became the thing. That was how it built — not in any dramatic way, because nothing at this stage of life built dramatically, but cup by terrible cup, Saturday by Saturday. They drank the bad coffee and watched the kids and talked, and the talking, Marcus noticed, did the thing good talking does: it went sideways into the real stuff without anyone steering it there.

He learned that Owen had been divorced four years to his own one. That Freya split her weeks, Tuesday-to-Saturday with Owen, and that Owen had decorated her room three times trying to make a flat feel like a home, and had finally accepted that a home was not a paint colour. Marcus, hearing this, felt his own un-homelike flat with its still-boxed kitchen things, and said so, and Owen nodded like a man recognising terrain.

"It does get better," Owen said. "The flat. It stops being the place you ended up and starts being the place you live. It took me about two years. I won't lie to you and say one."

"Two years." Marcus blew on the undrinkable coffee. "Daniel's mum and I — it was amicable. Everyone keeps using that word, amicable, like it's a prize. And it is, I suppose. Nobody threw anything. But amicable still means you stand in a flat on a Wednesday night when your kid's at the other house and you don't know what you're for."

He hadn't meant to say that. It was more than the bad coffee had asked for. But Owen didn't flinch from it, and didn't rush to fix it, and didn't do the thing other touchline parents did, which was change the subject back to the league table. He just stood with it a moment, the way Marcus was learning Owen stood with things.

"What you're for," Owen said eventually, "on the Wednesdays. I found that's the actual work. Not the divorce. The Wednesdays after." He turned his cup slowly in his hands. "Took me a long time to be able to say this without it sounding like a tragedy, so bear with me. But I spent about three years being only a father. Just that. Freya's dad, full-time, even on the days she wasn't there — I'd kept being her dad in the empty flat, if that makes sense, because being her dad was the one part of the old life that hadn't broken, so I held onto it with both hands and let everything else go quiet." He glanced at Marcus, and there was that half-second too long again, the look that had no business being as warm as it was over coffee this bad. "And the quiet's safe. But you can't actually live your whole second half in it. At some point you have to find out if there's a you that exists on the Wednesdays. Apart from the kid."

On the pitch a goal went in — theirs, the kids', a scramble that Daniel had somehow been at the centre of — and a ragged cheer went up, and both men cheered too, automatically, and then there was a beat after the cheer where they were just looking at each other again, two worn men in waterproofs, holding terrible coffee, in the flat grey light.

"And have you?" Marcus asked. "Found out. If there's an Owen on the Wednesdays."

Owen considered the question with the seriousness it had, apparently, earned.

"I think I'm finding out," he said, "fairly recently. Last few Saturdays, actually." And he didn't look away when he said it, and he didn't dress it up, and Marcus felt the bad coffee go entirely forgotten in his hand, because there was no mistaking it — the sentence had been offered deliberately, gently, the way you offer something across a distance you've decided you'd like to close, and it was now lying in the cold air between them, waiting to see what Marcus would do with it.

What Marcus did with it was not look away either.

"Same hatch next week?" he said. His voice came out steadier than he felt. "I'll buy the terrible coffee. Since it's apparently not a kindness, I should take a turn."

Owen smiled, slow and surprised at itself.

"Same hatch," he said. "Next week."
