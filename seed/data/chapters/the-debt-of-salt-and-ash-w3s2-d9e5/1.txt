They say you always know the collector before he knocks. The salt on the threshold goes grey. The fire forgets, for a moment, how to burn. And the dog, if you keep one, presses itself flat to the floor and will not look at the door.

Saela kept no dog. But the fire forgot itself at half past nine that evening, sinking to a sullen blue, and she set down her grandmother's account-book and watched the salt line at the doorstep turn the colour of old bone, and she knew.

She had known for a year, in truth. Knowing the exact hour was only a refinement.

The knock came. Three times, unhurried, the knock of someone who has never in his existence needed to knock twice.

Saela opened the door because there was no version of this evening in which the door stayed shut, and refusing to open it would only have meant losing the small dignity of having opened it herself.

The collector was tall, and dressed in the grey the Court of Cinders wore the way other powers wore gold — to announce that it did not need to impress anyone. His hair was the colourless near-white of ash that has finished burning. His eyes were the same. And his face was beautiful in the specific, unsettling way of the Court's made things, beautiful as a blade is beautiful, with nothing in it that had been put there to be loved.

"Saela Orne," he said. "Granddaughter of Maven Orne. I am Vaire, of the Court of Cinders, and I am here to collect a debt."

"I know what you are here for." Saela was proud that her voice held. She had practised it, the way you practise a thing you will only get to do once. "My grandmother's contract. I have read it. I have read it a great many times."

"Then you will not waste my evening pretending to be surprised." He did not smile. She would learn, in time, that Vaire almost never smiled, and that this was not coldness so much as economy — he had been made for a purpose, and the purpose had not included smiling, and he did not perform what he did not feel. "May I come in? The contract specifies that the collection be conducted with courtesy. I would rather not conduct it on a doorstep."

It was such a strange small thing — a creature of the Court of Cinders asking permission to enter — that Saela stood aside before she had decided to.

He came in. The fire shrank further from him. He looked once around the small room — the account-book, the worn chairs, the three generations of a family that had lived modestly and well in the shadow of a debt none of them had spoken aloud — and Saela had the distinct impression that he was reading the room the way she had read the contract. Closely. For loopholes.

"Your grandmother," Vaire said, "signed an agreement with the Court three of your generations ago. The terms were these. The Court would lend the Orne line a working — a small magic, a turning of luck — sufficient to save this household from a ruin that would otherwise have ended the family entirely. In exchange, the Court was owed a marriage. A daughter of the Orne line, of an age to wed, given in union to a member of the Court, at a time of the Court's choosing." He let the words settle. "The Court has chosen. The time is now. The daughter is you."

"I never agreed to it."

"No," Vaire said. "Your grandmother agreed to it. That is how debts work, Saela Orne. They are not paid by the hand that incurs them. They are paid by the hand the contract can reach."

Saela looked at the account-book on the table — her grandmother's hand, her mother's hand, her own — three generations of careful entries, every one of them written, she understood now, in the shadow of this exact knock.

"And if I refuse," she said, "to be collected."

For the first time something moved in the ash-coloured eyes. Not warmth. But something — attention, perhaps, the flick of interest a hunter gives to prey that turns instead of running.

"Then the working is recalled," said Vaire, "and three generations of saved fortune are unmade in a night, and you will watch this house and everyone the contract still touches fall into the ruin your grandmother bought you out of. That is the cost of refusal. The Court is very old, and very patient, and it does not make threats. It makes terms." He inclined his head, a precise small movement. "I will give you until dawn to make your farewells. At dawn we travel to the Court, where the marriage will be solemnised. I am sorry. I am not permitted to be sorry, in fact, but I find I am, and I have not yet decided what to do about it."

It was, Saela thought, the only honest sentence either of them had spoken all evening. And she filed it away — the way her family had always filed things away, in careful entries, for later — because a collector who could be sorry was a collector who could, perhaps, be reasoned with. And Saela Orne had not spent a year reading a contract in order to walk quietly into the Court of Cinders.

She had spent a year looking for the loophole. And she was almost, almost certain she had found one.
