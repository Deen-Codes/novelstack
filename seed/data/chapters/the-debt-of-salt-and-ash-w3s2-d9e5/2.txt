They travelled at dawn, as the contract required, and they travelled on foot, because the Court of Cinders did not lie within any country a horse could reach.

Saela had expected silence. Vaire did not strike her as a creature who filled the air. But the road to the Court was long, and ran for hours through a grey country of standing ash-trees that did not move in the wind, and somewhere in the third hour she decided that silence was a luxury she could not afford.

"The contract," she said, "is badly drafted."

Vaire did not break stride. But his head turned, fractionally, and she had his attention again — that hunter's flick.

"The Court does not draft badly," he said.

"The Court drafted three generations ago, and the Court was confident, and confidence drafts badly. I have read it four hundred times, Vaire. I have read it more often than anyone living in the Court has read it, because to the Court it is one debt among thousands and to me it is the only document that has ever mattered." She kept her voice even, the account-book voice, the voice of a woman presenting figures. "The contract owes the Court *a marriage*. A daughter of the Orne line, of an age to wed, given in union to a member of the Court. It specifies the giving. It specifies the daughter. It does not specify the duration."

This time Vaire did stop.

The ash-trees stood around them, grey and patient and not moving, and the colourless eyes studied her with something that had advanced well beyond a hunter's interest into something Saela could not yet name.

"Say the rest of it," he said quietly. "You have not walked three hours building to a clause about duration for the pleasure of mentioning it."

"The contract owes a marriage," Saela said. "It does not owe a *long* one. It does not owe heirs, or fidelity, or a lifetime. It says the union shall be solemnised. It does not say the union shall be kept. A marriage solemnised at dawn and dissolved at dusk is still, by every word the Court wrote, a marriage. The debt would be paid. The contract would be satisfied. And I would walk free, having given the Court precisely what it bargained for and not one hour more."

For a long moment Vaire said nothing at all. The grey country held its breath.

"That loophole," he said at last, "has occurred to others. It is the first thing every clever debtor finds. And it is the first thing the Court closes. You will not be permitted to dissolve the union, Saela. The Court will see to that. There will be wardings on the marriage older and crueller than the contract itself. You will be married into the Court of Cinders, and you will stay married into it, because the Court will make leaving impossible long before you think to try."

"Then I will need," said Saela, "someone inside the Court who knows where the wardings are weak."

She watched the sentence land. She had aimed it carefully. And she saw — for the second time, the first having been in her grandmother's house, when he had said *I am sorry* — something in Vaire's made and beautiful face that the Court of Cinders had not put there.

"You are asking the collector," he said slowly, "to help you escape the collection."

"I am asking the only creature who has been honest with me in three generations," Saela said, "whether he intends to spend eternity delivering daughters to a Court he is sorry for delivering them to. You said you had not decided what to do about being sorry. I am offering you something to do about it."

The ash-trees did not move. Vaire looked at her, this woman who had read a contract four hundred times, and Saela looked back, and somewhere in the long grey silence of that road a thing began that neither the Court of Cinders nor either of them had drafted, and that no contract in any hand would ever quite be able to reach.
