"You will think me a foolish old woman," Beatrice told Constable Drupe, "but I should like you to unlock a pantry."

They stood in the lane outside the Crane house. Edith's pans had been returned; Edith herself sat at her own kitchen window like a woman convalescing from a fever, which, in a sense, she was.

"Mrs. Crane's pantry?" said Drupe.

"Mrs. Crane's pantry. With Mrs. Crane present, and with you present, and with me. I want three witnesses to what is, and is not, inside it."

The pantry was small and cold and immaculate, because Edith Crane was a woman who would have dusted a pantry on the morning of the apocalypse. Shelves of preserves climbed every wall, each jar labelled in the same neat hand. Beatrice read the labels as a scholar reads a shelf — left to right, top to bottom.

"Edith," she said. "Your competition marmalade. The jar you submitted to the final. When did you make that batch?"

"March. The last of the good Sevilles." Edith's voice was thin. "I made six jars. One for the show. Five for the shelf."

"Five for the shelf." Beatrice counted the marmalade. There were four.

"There should be five," said Edith, and then heard it, and her hand went to her mouth.

"Constable," said Beatrice, "I want you to note that there are four. Edith made six jars in March. One went to the fete and was, we now know, the jar from which Cuthbert Aimsley took his last and fatal taste. Five should remain. Four remain. One jar of Edith Crane's marmalade left this house, and it did not leave it in Edith Crane's hand."

Drupe wrote, slowly. "How would anyone get in here? The pantry's locked. The back door's locked."

"It is now," said Beatrice. "Edith, who has been into this pantry since March? Think. Not who *could*. Who *did*."

Edith Crane closed her eyes. "The doctor, when Robert was poorly — no, he never came back here. The meter man. The girl from the chemist with Robert's prescription, she waited in the kitchen — Beatrice, half the village has stood in my kitchen this spring, Robert being ill, people bringing things, people being kind—"

"People being kind," Beatrice repeated, and there it was again, that cold click, louder now.

A village full of people bringing soup to a sick man's house. A back door left unlatched out of trust, the way trusting houses do. A pantry a careful guest might step into, on the pretext of putting a casserole somewhere cool, and step out of with one jar of competition marmalade under a coat.

"Whoever did this," Beatrice said slowly, "needed a jar that was unmistakably yours, Edith. Your batch, your hand on the label, your peel cut your particular way — so that when Aimsley died over it, no one would doubt for one moment whose marmalade had killed him. They could not simply *buy* marmalade. It had to be the genuine article. It had to be stolen from this exact shelf."

She turned to Drupe.

"Constable. I should like the list. Every person who brought food to this house while Robert Crane was ill. The whole village was kind to the Cranes this spring." Her mouth set. "One of them was kind with a coat large enough to hide a jar."
