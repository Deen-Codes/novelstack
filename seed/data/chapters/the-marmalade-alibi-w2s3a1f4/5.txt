The fete field, the morning after, looked like the aftermath of a small and cheerful army. Trestle tables lay folded; bunting sagged; a single lost glove flagged from a hedge. And at the edge of the bottom field, by the willow, the grass told Beatrice a story in three plain sentences.

The first sentence was a patch of flattened green where a man had stood a while.

The second was a low branch, rubbed pale, where something — a hat, say — had been hung and later snatched down in a hurry.

The third was a line of bruised grass leading not toward the stream and the ducks, but away from them. Up the slope. Toward the marquees.

"There," said Beatrice. "He stood. He hung the hat. He went up."

But it was not Beatrice who found the thing that mattered. It was Sam Ottle, the under-gardener, a stringy boy of nineteen who had been raking the field since dawn and who Beatrice had asked, on a hunch, to simply tell her anything that had struck him as odd.

"There's this," said Sam, and held out his rake's catch.

It was a glove. A gardening glove, canvas, the right hand, and the fingertips of it were stained a deep marmalade orange — but not, Beatrice saw at once, evenly. The orange was concentrated at the tips and the pad of the thumb, exactly where a hand would grip a small jar's lid and twist.

"Where," she asked, and her voice had gone very quiet, "did you find this, Sam?"

"Hedge bottom. Behind the Preserves tent." He pointed. "Tucked right under, like someone shoved it. I near missed it."

Beatrice held the glove in both hands. A man planning to lift a poisoned jar, set it aside, and then upend a table of breaking glass would be a fool to do it bare-handed — fingerprints, cuts, the lot. He would wear a glove. And then, the deed done, he would need that glove gone, fast, and a hedge bottom behind a tent is a thief's idea of gone.

"It's a left," said Sam helpfully. "No — sorry. Right. Right hand."

"Right hand," Beatrice repeated.

In his notebook, dying, Cuthbert Aimsley's pen had dragged downward and to the left — the natural fall of a right hand losing its strength. He had been judging with his right. The killer's stolen spoon, thrown eleven feet, had landed to the *right* of the body.

And Reginald Thorne — Beatrice had watched him for eleven years hand silver medals back to the committee with a stiff little bow — Reginald Thorne was left-handed.

She stood in the trampled field with the orange-tipped glove in her hands and felt, not triumph, but the cold small click of a thing that does not fit.

"Sam," she said. "Who in this village gardens, and is right-handed, and had every reason in the world to want Reginald Thorne blamed for a murder?"

Sam Ottle scratched his head. "Well," he said. "That'd be most of the Pebble household, wouldn't it. Mrs. Pebble does all her own beds. Proud of it."

Hyacinth Pebble. Chairwoman of the fete. The woman who had looked at a corpse beside spilled marmalade and said *heart* before anyone had so much as knelt down.

Beatrice put the glove very carefully into her shopping bag.
