It is a strange thing about a village that a murder, once solved, becomes almost at once a story — and a story, in Lower Tilbury, is a kind of weather. It blew through the autumn. By Christmas it had thinned to an anecdote. By the following spring it was simply a thing that had happened, the year of the marmalade, the way other years were the year of the flood.

Nan Tewkes was tried in the county town. The lab matched the staining on the glove, in the end, and matched the residue in the jar to the residue in four jars from Edith's pantry shelf, and the slow careful pattern of it — a little poison, a little more — was, the prosecution said, the most patient cruelty they had met in twenty years. Nan said almost nothing throughout. She had said her piece, Beatrice thought, in a parlour over a cup of tea, and had nothing left she wished to add.

Robert Crane came home from hospital in September, thinner, slower, but with the colour back in him and a great deal to say about hospital food. He sat in his garden in the long evenings and looked at his water meadow, which stayed a meadow, and which the parish — in a fit of feeling — voted that summer to make a common, so that it might go on being a meadow after Robert Crane no longer could.

Edith never boiled marmalade again. She told Beatrice this once, quietly, and Beatrice did not argue with her, because some things a person sets down are not to be picked up by a friend and pressed back into the hand.

As for the fete: it was held again the next July, because Lower Tilbury fetes are not so easily stopped. There was a Preserves and Pickles marquee, and there were nine jars in the final, and there was a new judge — a brisk woman from two counties over who took no notebook and, so far as anyone could discover, no bribes.

Beatrice Mott did not enter. She stood at the back of the marquee instead, with a cup of weak tea, and watched the village she had taught and buried and married and now, she supposed, defended, file past the tiered display of jars and admire them.

Reginald Thorne won the gold. Eleven years of silver, and at last the gold, in a year when the only thing that had truly changed was that the man weighing the jars was honest. He came to find Beatrice afterward, the rosette in his hand, and he did not quite know what to say, and so he said, "It was a near thing, you know. For me. I was very near being— You might have let them think it was me."

"I might," Beatrice agreed. "But it would not have been true. And an untrue thing, Reginald, however convenient, has a way of spoiling on the shelf." She nodded at his medal. "Thick-cut?"

"Thick-cut," he said. "Always was."

"There," said Beatrice Mott, and finished her tea. "Then it was only ever the judging that wanted fixing. Not the marmalade. The marmalade, my dear, was always perfectly good."

And she put down her cup, and went out into the bright field, where the duck race was just beginning, and every single soul in Lower Tilbury was exactly where they appeared to be.
