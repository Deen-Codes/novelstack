The list, when Edith and her neighbours had finished assembling it, ran to nineteen names. Beatrice spread it on her kitchen table beside the index cards and looked at it for a long time.

Nineteen kind people. She crossed off, at once, the four who were known to have been at the duck race in plain unbroken view — children clinging to them, hands full of jam-jar prizes, faces witnessed. She crossed off the bedridden. She crossed off Edith herself, and Robert, and the vicar, who had been pouring tea for forty in the refreshment tent with three other women swearing to it.

That left eight.

And of those eight, Beatrice now did a thing she had not let herself do until the facts compelled it. She wrote, beside each name, two words: *gardens?* and *handed?*

It was slow work. It meant a morning of telephone calls dressed as idle gossip — *and how is your delphinium, dear, do you do your own beds?* — and Beatrice was very good at idle gossip, having spent a career listening to six-year-olds explain things.

By lunchtime, three names had a right-handed gardener's tick beside them. Hyacinth Pebble. The butcher's wife, Nan Tewkes. And — Beatrice's pen had hesitated here, then written it plainly, because a clue you flinch from is a clue you have not earned — young Sam Ottle, the under-gardener, who had found the orange glove.

She sat with that for a while.

It was Sam who had handed her the glove. Sam who had been raking the field unsupervised since dawn — dawn, with the whole evidence of a murder lying about for the picking-up. A man who has committed a crime, Beatrice knew, sometimes cannot resist managing the discovery of it. He hands you the clue himself, watching your face, because the not-knowing whether you suspect him is worse than the risk of you doing so.

But Sam had no quarrel with Aimsley. Sam had not come second for eleven years. Sam had not, so far as anyone knew, ever paid a bribe or been refused a medal. The motive, that great engine of every murder Beatrice had ever read of, simply was not there for Sam Ottle.

Unless Sam Ottle had not done it for himself.

Beatrice reached for the notebook's facts again — the back pages, the private sums. *E.C. £40. R.T. £75. Pebble £40.* Aimsley had been paid by the Pebbles. And the Pebble household, Sam had said himself, did all its own beds.

Sam Ottle was the Pebbles' under-gardener.

The telephone rang before she could finish the thought. It was Constable Drupe, and his voice had the flat shaken quality of a young man who has just been told something he would rather not know.

"Mrs. Mott. The lab report. On the marmalade. It wasn't — it wasn't put in at the fete." A breath. "The poison was cooked into the jar. Whoever did this, did it back in March. It was in that jar from the day it was made."

Beatrice set down her pen.

March. The jar had been poisoned in March — in Edith Crane's own pantry, in Edith Crane's own batch, months before anyone could have known Cuthbert Aimsley would taste it at a fete in July.

"Constable," she said slowly, "that changes everything. That means the killer did not steal a jar to frame Edith. The killer poisoned the marmalade *in the making.* Which means the killer was in that pantry, with Edith's pans, in March." She closed her eyes. "It means the killer was never trying to murder a judge at all. Aimsley tasting that jar in July was an accident. The marmalade was meant for the house. The marmalade was meant for Robert Crane."
