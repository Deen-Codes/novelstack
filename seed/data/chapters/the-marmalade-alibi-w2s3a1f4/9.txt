Beatrice did not believe in confrontations. Confrontations were how clever people gave guilty people time to think. She believed instead in tables — in setting out, plainly, before witnesses, the things that were true, and letting the guilty party watch their own ground disappear.

So she set a table. Literally. She invited Nan Tewkes to tea, and she invited Constable Drupe to sit quietly in the parlour with the door ajar, and she invited Edith, who had a right to be there, and she laid out the good cups.

"Nan," she said, when the tea was poured. "I have been making a marmalade of my own. Not the boiling sort. The other sort." She set down, one by one, on the cloth between them, three things. The orange-tipped right-hand glove. Aimsley's black notebook. And a single jar of marmalade — the one untouched survivor from the fete, lid still down.

Nan Tewkes was a broad, capable woman with flour-pale forearms and a face that had spent fifty years being pleasant. It went on being pleasant now, but the pleasantness had become a thing she was holding up, like a tray.

"I don't follow you, Beatrice."

"You poisoned Edith's March batch," said Beatrice, without heat, the way one states the date. "You have boiled jam in that kitchen twenty years; no one alive knows that pantry better. You did it slowly, a little each jar, because you needed Robert Crane's illness to look like an illness — so that when at last he died, it would be the long sad end of a long sad decline, and his meadow would pass to Edith, and Edith, grieving and short of money, would do what you have heard her half-say a dozen times this hard spring: she would sell."

The teacup in Nan's hand did not shake. That, Beatrice thought, was almost the saddest part — that she had practised even this.

"The fete was never your plan," Beatrice went on. "You did not know, when you poisoned the show jar back in March, that Edith would enter that particular batch. When you saw it on the competition table you must have felt the ground open. A regional judge, about to taste a spoonful meant to kill a man over months, in one fatal mouthful, in front of the village. So you did the only thing left. You waited for the duck race, when every eye was at the stream. You put on your gardening glove. You lifted Edith's jar clear and set it safe, because if it smashed with the rest no one could ever prove what was in it — and you needed it found, found and labelled and damning, so the village would say *Edith's marmalade killed him* and never, ever ask *what was Edith's marmalade doing slowly killing Robert.* You upended the table. You took the spoon from his hand so the police would test the jar and not the man. And you threw the spoon, and you hid the glove in the hedge, and you went back down the field and were Nan again."

The parlour was very quiet. Through the ajar door, the small sound of Constable Drupe not writing — listening.

"You can prove none of it," said Nan Tewkes, and her voice was still pleasant, and that was the moment Beatrice was almost certain.

"I can prove some of it," she said. "The glove is a right hand, and you garden, and Reginald Thorne — whom you were so content to let us suspect — is left-handed. The lab can match what stained these fingertips to what is in this jar. And the notebook—" she touched it "—lists a payment from *Pebble,* not from you, which is the one place your plan was clumsy, Nan, because you wanted us looking at the bribery and the medals, and there never was a medal in it at all." She paused. "But you are right that the proof is thin. So I did not ask you here to be proven guilty."

"No?"

"No. I asked you here so that you would know that *I* know. And so that Robert Crane would stop eating the marmalade." Beatrice's voice did not rise, but something underneath it had gone to iron. "He is in hospital as of this morning, Nan. Edith took him at dawn. He is going to recover. There will be no slow sad decline and no grieving widow and no meadow to sell. Whatever you did this for — it is already over. It failed the moment a vain old judge picked the wrong jar and made it loud."

For the first time the pleasantness slipped from Nan Tewkes's face, and what was under it was not rage, and not fear. It was a terrible, flat tiredness.

"He'd have let the whole village rot," she said quietly, "rather than let those houses get built. My brother. My nephews. A meadow." Her hands had begun, at last, to shake. "A *meadow,* Beatrice."

"Yes," said Beatrice Mott. "And a man. You forgot, somewhere in the arithmetic, that it was also a man." She stood, and crossed to the parlour door, and opened it wide. "Constable Drupe. I think you had better come and have your tea."
