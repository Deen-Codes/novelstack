Beatrice did not sleep that night. She did what she always did instead, which was to lay the whole affair out on the table and walk around it until it stopped lying to her.

A poisoned jar made in March. Made, that is, in Edith's kitchen, by Edith's hand, from Edith's fruit — and yet Beatrice did not for one instant believe Edith Crane had poisoned her own preserves. So: someone had got at the batch during the making. The day of the boiling. Marmalade is a long all-day affair; a kitchen on jam day is a kitchen with its door open for the steam, neighbours drifting in and out, the kettle always on.

Robert Crane had been ill since the winter. *Ill,* the village said, vaguely, kindly. Beatrice had assumed influenza, then a weak chest, then simply age. She had never asked. One did not. And in not asking, she realised now with a slow descending shame, she had let a man be slowly murdered in plain sight of a whole watchful village for the better part of a year.

Because if the marmalade had been poisoned in March, and Robert ate the household marmalade, and Robert had been ill since the winter — then March was not the first jar.

She telephoned Edith at an hour no decent person telephones.

"Edith. Forgive me. Robert's illness. The stomach trouble. When did it begin — truly begin — and does it ease, ever? Does it ever ease?"

A long silence on the line. Then Edith, in the small flat voice of a woman watching a floor tilt: "It eases when he's away. When he stayed with his sister in the spring he was right as rain in a fortnight. We thought — we thought it was the rest. We thought it was getting away from worry."

"It was getting away from the marmalade," said Beatrice gently. "Edith. Who taught you your marmalade? Who stands in your kitchen on jam day? Who has, all these years, stood at your elbow on the one day of the year your pantry's secrets are spread open on the table?"

And Edith Crane said the name, and it was not Hyacinth Pebble, and it was not Reginald Thorne, and it was not the butcher's wife.

It was Nan Tewkes.

"Nan and I have boiled jam together for twenty years," Edith whispered. "She comes every March. She — Beatrice, why would Nan—"

"Why," Beatrice agreed. "That is the whole of it now. The how I think I see. The why I do not." She was already reaching for her coat, at five in the morning, because some things will not wait for a decent hour. "Edith. Robert's will. Forgive me again. Is Robert a man with anything to leave?"

The silence this time was different. It was the silence of a woman turning over a stone she has stood beside her whole life without once looking under.

"Robert," said Edith Crane at last, "owns the meadow. The water meadow, down by the stream. It's been Crane land four generations. It's worth nothing as a meadow." A breath. "But Nan's brother is the one who's been wanting to build on the far side of the village. He's been to every parish meeting for two years. He needs the meadow. Robert always said — Robert always said he'd see it under grass till he died before he'd see houses on it."

"*Till he died,*" said Beatrice softly. "Yes. I rather think that was the difficulty."
