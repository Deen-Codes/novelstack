The vicarage smelled of furniture polish and the particular damp of a building that has been holy longer than it has been heated. The Reverend Tobias Frame poured tea with the careful generosity of a man who had decided, some years ago, that tea was a thing he could reliably do well.

"Reginald Thorne," he said, when Beatrice asked. "Yes. A sad case, in his way. Such a gifted hand with a preserve, everyone says so, and yet always — always — the silver." He set down the pot. "He took it harder each year. Last summer he said something to me after the fete that I confess I found troubling."

"What did he say?"

"He said that Aimsley was a fraud, and that one day the village would know it." The vicar's brow creased. "I took it for sour grapes. A man does say things, after eleven silvers."

Beatrice turned her teacup a quarter-turn on its saucer, which was what she did instead of letting her face show that a card had just slid into place.

"Vicar. You were near the marquee when Aimsley was found. You crouched beside me. Cast your mind back to the half-hour before. The duck race was at the bottom field, by the stream — a good two hundred yards from the Preserves tent."

"It was."

"Reginald Thorne's alibi is that he was seen at the duck race. By whom, exactly?"

The vicar thought. He was not a quick man but he was an honest one, and honesty, Beatrice had long held, was worth three quick men in a pinch. "I saw his hat," he said at last. "His green hat, by the willow. Everyone saw his green hat. We all said, there's Reginald, sulking by the willow, won't even watch the ducks properly." He stopped. The teacup paused halfway to his lips. "But I could not, now you make me say it aloud — I could not swear I saw his *face*."

"A hat by a willow," said Beatrice softly, "is not a man. It is a hat."

"You think he hung his hat there. And walked back up to the marquee while everyone's eyes were on the stream."

"I think," said Beatrice, "that a man who has come second for eleven years has had a very long time to learn precisely where everyone's eyes will be." She rose, and her knees, for once, did not complain; they seemed to understand the occasion. "Vicar, I should like to see the field. Specifically I should like to see the path between the willow and the Preserves marquee, and I should like to see it before it rains, because I believe a man in a hurry stepped off the mown grass, and mown grass forgives a footprint far faster than a flowerbed does."

The vicar was already reaching for his coat.

"There is one thing more," Beatrice added, at the door. "Aimsley's judging spoon. The bone-handled one. It was thrown clear — eleven feet, into the long grass. A dying man drops a spoon. He does not throw it." She buttoned her own coat. "Someone took that spoon out of his hand on purpose, Vicar. And I have begun to wonder whether it is because of what was on it."
