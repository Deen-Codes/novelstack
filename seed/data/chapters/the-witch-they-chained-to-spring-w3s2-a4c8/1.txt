Castellan Roeve had walked nine witches to the spring field, and he had taught himself, over nine years, not to learn their names.

It was the only mercy the duty allowed him, and he had grown ruthless in taking it. A name was a handle by which a person could be carried into your sleep. So he did not ask, and he did not listen when the priests said it, and for nine years the women he escorted to the field had been, in the ledger of his mind, simply *the witch* — a role, a function, a thing the kingdom required the way it required rain.

It had worked. Mostly. It had let him sleep, mostly. He had told himself that this was the same as being at peace, and he had been wrong, and he had known he was wrong, and he had gone on telling himself anyway, because the alternative was to look directly at what he did each spring, and a man cannot look directly at that and continue to do it.

The tenth witch told him her name before he could stop her.

"Maren," she said. They were three days out from the capital, on the cart road that ran east toward the spring field, and she had said it into the evening quiet the way you set down a stone you have been carrying — deliberately, and with relief. "My name is Maren. I can see you working not to ask it, Castellan. I have watched you do it since the city gate. You have a whole craft of not-asking, and it is very practised, and I want you to know it has just failed, because I have told you, and now you cannot un-know it."

Roeve did not answer at once. The cart creaked. The two soldiers behind kept their distance, as soldiers always did from the spring cart; nobody wanted to ride close to it.

"That was unkind of you," he said finally.

"Yes." She did not sound sorry. She was younger than he had let himself notice — they were always younger than he let himself notice — with a steady dark gaze and a stillness that was not calm but something more deliberate, the stillness of a person rationing herself. "It was unkind. I have decided to be unkind, Castellan, for the months I have left, because kindness is a thing you spend expecting a return, and I will not be alive in the autumn to be repaid. So I am going to be honest instead, which is cheaper, and which I can afford. I am Maren. I am twenty-three. I have a sister who does not yet know, and a cat who will be looked after, and I make — made — passable cheese. Those are the things that are true about me. I would like at least one person on this road to carry them, instead of carrying *the witch*."

Roeve looked at the road ahead, which ran straight and pale toward a horizon he had reached nine times and ridden back from alone.

He knew what waited there. He had watched it nine times, which was nine times more than any other living man, and the knowledge sat in him like swallowed iron. The kingdom's harvest failed — had always failed, would always fail — unless a witch was bound to the spring field at the planting, her magic rooted into the soil and drawn out of her slowly, season-slow, feeding the crops as it drained, until by the harvest the field was golden and the witch was a husk the priests buried at its edge with what they considered honour. Nine times Roeve had stood at that field's stone wall and watched a woman give the kingdom her life one day at a time, and nine times he had ridden home and not learned her name and called the not-learning peace.

It was not peace. He understood that now, with a clarity that frightened him, sitting on a cart beside a witch named Maren who made passable cheese. It had never been peace. It had been a wound he had simply refused to look at, and a wound unlooked-at does not close. It festers. Nine of them. Nine wounds, nine springs, and he had carried every single one whether he learned the names or not, because the mind keeps its own ledger and does not ask the heart's permission.

"You should not have told me your name," he said, very low.

"Why not?"

"Because," said Castellan Roeve, and heard his own voice do something it had not done in nine years of this duty — heard it break its discipline, heard it become the voice of a man rather than a function — "because for nine years not-knowing was the only thing that let me deliver them. And you have just taken it from me, Maren. You have made it impossible for me to do this the way I have always done it." He turned and looked at her directly, the tenth witch, the first whose face he would carry knowing the name that went with it. "And I find — I find I am not sorry. I find I have been waiting nine years for someone to make it impossible. So. We have three days to the field. Tell me about the cat. Tell me about the sister. Tell me everything that is true about you, and tell it slowly, because I am going to need every hour of those three days to decide whether I am still a man who can hand you over that wall — or whether I am about to break the only oath I have ever kept."
