They had been twelve. They were nine. The three who were missing were not missing the way the dead are missing, and Vesh made herself walk through the accounting of them once a season, the way you walk the boundary of your land — not because you enjoy it, but because a thing un-walked grows over, and a thing grown over can hide a sinkhole.

The first to go had been Teor, who carried the god's gift of joy. When the silence came, joy was the cruelest gift to be left holding, because Teor's gift still worked, and so Teor went on feeling, helplessly, the bright unbearable thing — joy with nothing under it, joy as a reflex, joy in a world he had stopped being able to believe in. He had lasted two years. Vesh did not say, even to herself, the manner of it. She said only that the silence had taken him slow, through the very gift that had once made him the most loved of all of them.

The second had been Ositha, gift of certainty, who had simply walked north into the ice one morning to find the god and bring it back, certain — to the last, gift functioning perfectly — that it could be done. They had found her staff. They had not found her.

The third was the one Vesh did not walk toward even once a season, and so this was the chapter where she made herself, because the warband was about to go up a frozen pass and a captain who would not look at her own history had no business asking eight people to trust her with theirs.

The third was Calle. Gift of valor. And Calle had not died of the silence.

Calle had decided that a god who would abandon twelve sworn champions was a god worth being avenged upon, and that since the god could not be reached, the next best target was everything the god had ever been worshipped by. Calle had gone to the army that worshipped the silence — there was always such an army, in a bad century; faithlessness organizes faster than faith — and Calle had given them the gift of valor, and a thing sworn to the silence with a saint's valor at its head was precisely the kind of thing Vesh did not like.

The army coming up the pass toward the starved valley was that army. And the saint at its head, the one who would be standing on the other side of whatever line the Broken Saints chose to hold, was Calle.

Vesh told the warband this on the third night of the march, because they deserved to hear it from her and not from an arrow.

"Some of you guessed," she said. The fire was small; the pass had narrowed; the cold had the particular patience of high country. "The contract didn't name the enemy captain. I named her to myself two ranges ago and I've been carrying it like a stone, and that was wrong of me, and I'm setting it down now. The army we're hired to stop is led by Calle. Our Calle."

No one spoke for a while. Old Sister Eyne worked her jaw. Halrik's ruined laugh did not come.

"Then we're not walking up a pass to fight strangers," Brannoc said finally, quietly. "We're walking up a pass to fight a saint."

"We're walking up a pass," Vesh said, "to fight a person who was given the gift of valor by the same hand that gave me steadfastness, and who took the silence and turned it into a reason to hurt the helpless. And I have thought about this every step of three days, and here is where I've landed, and you can argue it, that's what the fire is for." She looked around the small ring of broken, aging, gifted faces. "Calle says the god's silence means nothing means anything. I say the god's silence means it was always us. It was always nine — twelve — ordinary stubborn people choosing, with no one checking the arithmetic, to stand between the strong and the starving. The god leaving didn't break that. The god leaving revealed it. And if I'm right, then the valley still deserves the line, and Calle is just one more strong thing we have to stand between." She picked up a stick, fed the fire. "If I'm wrong, we find out the expensive way, near the top of the pass, in about four days."
