The valley was called Anvil Low, and it had hired the Broken Saints with a strongbox of coins, three barrels of salted goat, and the particular silence of people who know they cannot afford the thing they are buying and are buying it anyway.

Vesh stood in the valley's one hall — low-beamed, smoke-dark, the whole population fitting inside it with room to spare — and she looked at the faces of the people she had been paid to die in front of, and she did the thing she had learned to do since the god went quiet, which was to look very hard at the actual situation instead of the story she would have liked it to be.

The actual situation was bad.

Anvil Low had four hundred souls and one defensible position — the throat of the pass, where the trail pinched between a cliff and a frozen gorge. The army coming down on them had, by the scouts' count, something near nine hundred fighting strangers, sworn to the silence, led by a saint with the god-gift of valor. The valley's own fighters numbered perhaps sixty, and they were farmers, and they would die in the first exchange and Vesh would not insult them by pretending otherwise.

So she did not pretend.

"You hired nine of us," she said to the hall. "Nine is not enough to beat nine hundred. I want to say that plainly, here, in the light, before anyone's child believes otherwise. We cannot win the pass for you by killing the army in it."

The hall stirred. A man near the back — the valley's reeve, grey and gaunt with the winter — got slowly to his feet.

"Then what," he said, "did we buy."

"You bought a choice you couldn't make for yourselves." Vesh moved to the long table and set her hands flat on it. "An army sworn to the silence does not march for plunder. Anvil Low has nothing worth nine hundred mouths. They march to make a point — that the faithless are strong and the helpless are theirs, and a starved valley that throws open its gate proves the point and feeds the legend. But a starved valley that holds, even for a day, even at the cost of nine mercenaries and sixty farmers, costs that legend more than the valley is worth. Saints know the arithmetic of stories. Calle especially. We are not here to save you with our swords. We are here to make you too expensive to be worth the lesson."

"That sounds," said old Sister Eyne, who had earned the right to interrupt her captain in front of anyone, "very like a long way of telling these people to dig in and bleed."

"It is exactly that," Vesh said. "And I will not dress it as anything else. But there is a second half." She straightened. "I knew the woman leading that army when she was the kindest of twelve. I am going to walk up the pass under a parley flag, before a single arrow flies, and I am going to stand in front of Calle and offer her the one thing the silence took from all of us — a person from before, who remembers when she was holy, telling her she still can be. It will probably fail. Calle has had years to become what she is, and a parley flag is thin cloth against that. But it costs us nothing to try it first, and if it fails, we will have lost only my walk up a hill, and the valley will still be dug in behind me."

The reeve looked at her for a long moment, this hard tired woman who had told his people, without flinching, that some of them would die and that their saviors' plan was mostly arithmetic and one desperate conversation.

"The old saints," he said, "the ones in the stories — they'd have promised us the god would send a sign."

"I know," Vesh said. "I used to promise that. I believed it." She picked up the strongbox of the valley's coins and, deliberately, set it back down unopened, untaken, on the table between them. "No god is sending a sign, reeve. There's only us, and you, and four days, and a choice. But I have come to think that nine people walking up a pass with no sign and no promise and no god behind them, choosing the line anyway — I have come to think that is not the absence of the holy thing. I have come to think it might be the whole of it. Now. Show me your gorge, and your cliff, and every farmer who can hold a spear, and let us be expensive together."
