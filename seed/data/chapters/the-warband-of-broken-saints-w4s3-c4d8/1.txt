The god stopped answering on a Tuesday.

Vesh Holloway remembered it was a Tuesday because she had been mending a boot, and she had thought, with the small irritation of an ordinary afternoon, that she would finish the stitch and then say the dusk prayer, and the dusk prayer would warm her hands the way it always had, the way it had every dusk for nineteen years of sworn service. She finished the stitch. She said the prayer.

Nothing.

Not a refusal. A refusal would have been something — a god turning its face away is still a god with a face. This was the other thing. This was a prayer going out into a room that she had always believed was occupied and finding, suddenly and without ceremony, that it was not, and had perhaps not been for some time, and that she had been talking to the furniture.

That was eleven years ago. Vesh was forty-three now, and she was still mending boots, and she had not said a prayer since.

The Order of the Sworn Twelve had been the bright thing of the age once. Twelve champions, each chosen by the god Aurel, each given a single sliver of the god's attention to carry — Vesh's had been steadfastness, a small dull useful gift, the gift of being the one who did not break. They had been holy. They had been certain. Children had been named after them. And then the god had gone quiet, all at once, all twelve of them feeling the silence in the same dusk hour, and the bright thing of the age had become twelve middle-aged people standing in an empty room holding gifts from someone who was no longer there to have given them.

Some of them had not survived the silence. Vesh did not like to count that part.

The nine who remained had become, by slow and unglamorous degrees, a warband. You could not be a saint of a dead god; there was no living in it, no temple would feed you, the faithful looked at you the way you look at a widow who keeps setting two plates. But you could be nine hard people who knew how to hold a line, and there was always, in a bad century, a living in that.

So they took contracts. They guarded caravans and broke sieges and cleared the things that came down out of bad country, and they were good at it, because the god's gifts had not left when the god did. That was the cruel joke at the center of Vesh's life. Her steadfastness still worked. She still could not be broken — not by fear, not by pain, not by the long cold nights. The gift remained, perfectly functional, like a lamp still burning in a house whose owner had died. She just no longer had any idea who had lit it, or whether it had ever meant what she'd been told it meant, or whether she had simply, all those years, been a stubborn woman who had mistaken her own spine for the touch of a god.

"You're doing the thing," said Brannoc, settling onto the log beside her. He was the warband's healer, which had also once been a holy office and was now just a man with steady hands and a bag of herbs. "The boot thing. You mend that boot when you're chewing on something. That boot has been mended forty times. It is more thread than boot."

"There's a contract," Vesh said. "A valley two ranges north. Winter's starved them and there's an army coming up the pass — a real one, sworn to something, and you know I don't like the ones sworn to something." She pulled the thread tight. "They can't pay much. They can pay enough. And it's the kind of job that nine of us walk into and fewer than nine of us walk out of."

Brannoc was quiet. The fire ticked. Around it the others slept or pretended to — Halrik with his ruined laugh, the twins who never used their names anymore, old Sister Eyne who had carried the god's gift of mercy and now used it mostly to decide who among the enemy wounded got the quick death.

"You're going to take it," Brannoc said. It wasn't a question.

"I'm going to take it." Vesh set the boot down. "Here's the thing I'm chewing, Bran. Nineteen years I led the Twelve and I never once doubted a single order I gave, because I believed there was a god behind me checking my arithmetic. If I sent good people to die, it was because a god agreed it was worth it. That was the deal. That was the whole architecture of being me." She looked at the fire. "Now I'm going to walk eight people I love up a frozen pass to hold a line for strangers, and there is no god behind me, and no one is checking my arithmetic, and if I'm wrong they just die and it just means nothing. And I find I still want to go. I find I think it's still right." She finally looked at him. "I don't know if that makes me brave or if it makes me exactly the kind of monster the silence was supposed to leave behind."

Brannoc picked up the over-mended boot and turned it in his healer's hands.

"It makes you," he said slowly, "the captain. Same as before. The only thing that's changed is you've found out you were always the one checking the arithmetic. The god never was." He handed the boot back. "Take the contract, Vesh. We'll all complain. We'll all come."
