My mother died in the autumn, and she did not unteach the arithmetic, because a person cannot unlearn in a season what they have been all their life. But she did one thing, near the end, that I have decided to count.

She sent for me. Not the steward — she sent a letter, in her own hand, the writing gone spidery, and the letter said only: The archive is yours. The hill is yours. Come and tell me its name before I go, so that I die knowing it.

I went. I sat by the bone chair, which had been moved to her bedchamber because she could no longer be carried to the throne, and I told her the name of the god under the hill, and I told her it had been my grandmother's name, and I watched my mother — small, dry, precise, dying — receive the information that the daughter she had despised had taken the name of the mother she had despised and turned it, by an act of will, from a wall into a god.

"That is either the cruelest thing you could have done to me," my mother said, "or the kindest. I find I cannot tell which."

"Neither could I," I said, "while I was doing it. I've decided that's how you know a thing is true. The clean choices are usually somebody else's name for what you wanted anyway."

She almost smiled. I will not claim she smiled. But something near her mouth considered it, and then she closed her eyes, and three days later she was gone, and the kingdom of Anwe needed a queen.

It did not choose me. I want to be honest, because this is the part the criers got wrong, and the criers always get the endings wrong because endings are where they most want a shape. I did not walk out of a hill and onto a throne. Temperance was crowned — clever Temperance, who had looked at me in the throne room a year before as though I were a portrait, and who came to me the night before her crowning and said, plainly, "I do not know how to be a queen who is not our mother. You went somewhere and came back changed. Teach me."

So that is what I have been. Not the queen. The thing the queen comes to. I keep the archive, which under my hand has stopped being a record of grudges and started being a record of debts — who owes what, to whom, since when — because I have come to believe that a kingdom is only the sum of its unpaid promises, and a kingdom that loses count of them ends up drinking salt.

I keep the hill, too. Four times a year I walk the orchard road, and I go down past the mark that no longer says NOT PAST HERE — I had it recut; it says BE WELCOME, SPEAK THE NAME — and I sit in the temple where the candied watchmen weep their sweet water, and I speak with the god who was a thirst, and we are, the two of us, something I do not have a clean word for. Not friends. We are two beings who were each raised as a hunger by people who could not afford to see us as persons, and who got out, and who check on each other.

"You still have not chosen a name," the god said to me, last spring, in the cool weeping dark. "You gave me yours a year ago and you are still walking the world as no one."

"I'm not no one," I said. "I'm the woman who keeps the archive. I'm the woman who goes back for people. I'm the woman who paid the debt." I drank the sweet water from my cupped hands, the way I had the first day. "I've decided those are better than a name. A name is what you're given. Those are what I am."
