The road to the mines ran three days through orchard country, and the orchard country was dying politely, the way well-bred things do.

The apple trees still held their leaves. But the leaves had a grey cast, like cloth washed too often, and the fruit when it came down was hollow — I split one with my grandmother's knife and found a powdery white core where the seeds should have been. Salt. The trees were drinking the same poisoned table as the wells, and they were turning it into something that looked like an apple and would kill you slowly if you trusted it.

I thought about my mother, choosing the black floor.

On the first night I slept in a barn whose owner had already left for the coast, where the rumour said the water still ran sweet. He had taken his family and his goats and left a barn full of hay and a wall on which someone — a child, by the height of it — had drawn a sun in charcoal. I lay under that charcoal sun and did not sleep well. I was nineteen and I had never been alone before. The keep had always been full of people declining to look at me. There is a particular silence to being unseen in a crowd, and I had mistaken it, all my life, for solitude. It is not. I learned the difference in that barn. Real solitude has no one in it to do the not-seeing, and it is colder, and it is more honest, and by the grey morning I found that I preferred it, which told me something about myself I was not ready to hold.

On the second day I met the miners.

They were coming the other way — up the road, toward the orchards, toward anywhere-but-here. Forty of them, maybe more, with their lamps still strapped to their belts and their faces the colour of the salt they had spent their lives cutting. They did not look like men fleeing. They looked like men who had been turned out of a house by something they would not name even to each other.

Their foreman was a broad woman named Calla Drust, and she stopped when she saw the queen's seal on my coat, and she laughed, but the laugh had no floor under it.

"You're the one they're sending." She looked me up and down — the rope, the oilcloth map, the knife. "Gods. A girl and a grandmother's knife. She really does want it back."

"Wants what back?"

"The hill." Calla Drust spat, and the spit was white. "We dug past the old mark. The deep mark, the one cut into the rock by men dead a thousand years. There's a seam down there, sweetest salt you ever saw, and the company said cut it, and we cut it, and then the wells went bad three valleys over and a sound started coming up the shaft." She stopped. The miners behind her had gone very still, the way a flock goes still when a hawk's shadow crosses. "A drinking sound. Like the biggest throat in the world, swallowing, slow. You'll hear it. You'll wish you hadn't."

"Then come back with me," I said. "Show me the deep mark. I can't close what I can't find."

"No." She said it gently, which was worse. "We left a man down there. Old Pell, his lamp went out and we — we did not go back for him. You understand? Nobody went back. And I have to live the rest of my life having not gone back, and I cannot do it twice." She pressed something into my hand. A lump of raw salt, fist-sized, glassy. "Old miner's charm. It sweats when the air goes wrong. When it runs wet, you stop, and you think very hard about whether your queen's good opinion is worth your blood."

I closed my fingers on it. It was dry. For the moment, it was dry.

"Why are you helping me," I said, "if you won't come?"

Calla Drust looked at me for a long moment, and there was something in her face I had been hungry for my whole life and had never once seen aimed at me — not pity, pity I knew. This was closer to respect, and it was terrible, because respect from a frightened woman is a kind of farewell.

"Because somebody's daughter is walking into that hill," she said, "and the least I can do is make sure she walks in knowing." She stepped around me. The miners flowed after her, lamps swinging, up toward the grey orchards and the rumour of sweet water.

I went on alone, with rope and oilcloth and two kinds of salt — the kind that was killing my kingdom, and the kind that would tell me when the killing had found me.

By dusk on the third day I stood at the mouth of the mine. It was not a cave. Caves are made by water and time and have no opinion of you. This was a door, square-cut, framed in black timber, and above it, worn nearly smooth, someone had carved three words in the old tongue. It took me a while to be sure of them. When I was sure, I sat down in the salt dust and held my grandmother's knife and read them again, because I am my mother's daughter and I do not turn back from a thing merely because it has warned me.

WE BURIED IT THIRSTY, the door said.
