I carried Old Pell up ninety ramps. It took the better part of a day, and I will not pretend it was anything but terrible — a body is heavier than its living weight, as though death adds the years a person did not get to spend — but I had decided in the hill that I would walk out of it as someone who went back, and you do not become that person by changing your mind on the stairs.

Calla Drust's miners had not gone far. I found them camped at the orchard's edge, and when I came out of the treeline with a dead man over my shoulders and sweet water in a flask, Calla Drust stood up very slowly, and she looked at the flask, and she looked at Pell, and she sat back down and put her salt-white face in her hands, and the sound she made was not grief and was not relief but the place where the two of them meet and have no name.

They buried Pell properly, in orchard ground that was already, by morning, putting up green.

It took me eleven days to walk back to the keep, and I spent them being no one. That sounds bleak. It was not. I had given my name to a god under a hill, and the strange light thing I had not expected was how much room that made. A name is a set of instructions other people have written for you. Walking the orchard road as no one, I found I could not hear my mother's voice telling me what the name Eira meant — unloved, fourth, spendable — because the name was gone, given to better use, and in the quiet where it had been I could hear, faintly, for the first time, what I myself thought. It turned out I thought a great deal. It turned out I had simply never been able to hear it over the noise of being someone else's word.

The wells of Anwe ran sweet again three days before I reached the city. Word travels faster than a woman on foot. By the time I came to the salt-gate the kingdom already knew the Brine Year had ended, and the criers were already calling it a miracle, and a miracle is a thing that happens to a kingdom rather than a thing done by a person, so no one at the gate knew to connect the end of the salt to the dust-coloured young woman walking in alone with an empty flask and a grandmother's knife.

That suited me. I went up through the city unrecognised, which is to say unwalled, and I climbed to the West Keep, and I crossed the forty feet of black floor that doubled the torches, and I stood before my mother in her chair of pale bone.

She was thinner. The dying had moved on in her while I was gone. But her eyes were exactly as I remembered — small and dry and precise — and they moved over me, the dust and the knife and the empty flask, and I watched her do the arithmetic, and I watched the arithmetic fail to balance, because the daughter she had sent to be a cork had walked back in upright.

"You closed it," she said. Not a question. My mother did not ask questions she could not predict the answers to, and the answer she predicted was the wells, which had gone sweet, which proved the errand done.

"I named it," I said. "It wasn't a monster, mother. It was a debt. The first queen of Anwe made a promise to the thing in the hill and broke it, and buried the broken promise, and buried her own people standing guard over it, and we have all been drinking the interest on that loan for a thousand years and calling it weather." I crossed to the dais. I did not kneel. I noticed that I did not kneel, and I noticed that she noticed. "I paid it. I gave the hill a name and a place in the archive and a promise that we would keep speaking it. That's the price of sweet water now, and it's a price the kingdom pays every day, forever, with its mouth. I've already begun writing it down."

My mother was silent for a long time. The torches doubled in the black floor, and our two reflections hung beneath us, drowned, the way they always had.

"You were supposed to be the cork," she said at last. Quietly. Not cruel. Almost — and this is the thing I had waited nineteen years to hear and had stopped, somewhere on the orchard road, needing — almost wondering. "I sent you down to be the cork."

"I know," I said. "I'd like to talk to you about that. About what it means that you could send a daughter to be a cork, and which of us taught you that some daughters are corks, and whether you would like, before you die, to unteach it. Because you are dying, mother, and you have three daughters left, and one of them is going to sit in that bone chair, and I would very much like it not to be a person who has learned arithmetic from you."
