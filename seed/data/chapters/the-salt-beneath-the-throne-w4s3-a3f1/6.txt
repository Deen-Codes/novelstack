You do not bargain with an old hungry god the way you bargain in a market. There is no haggling, no splitting of the difference. There is only the slow, dangerous work of finding the one thing it wants more than the thing it is asking for, and offering that instead.

I sat down again — deliberately, because sitting is a statement, sitting says I am not afraid of how long this takes — and I set the knife across my knees, and I made the thing in the hill listen.

"You don't want the kingdom," I said. "You want out. The salt in the wells, the dead orchards, the swallowing — that's not appetite. That's a prisoner rattling the bars to be noticed. You can drink a river through a thousand feet of rock and you still can't drink your way through the watchmen's seal, because the seal isn't made of salt and it isn't made of distance. It's made of consent. And consent is the one well you've never been able to reach."

I LISTEN, it said. The voice had gone careful. Careful is the sound a powerful thing makes when it has just discovered it is not the only clever party in the room.

"Here is what I know about you, and tell me where I'm wrong. You were not born evil. The watchmen do not bury weather. They bury bargains gone bad. Someone, long ago, made a deal with you — water, I'd guess, in a dry year, water in exchange for something they later could not bear to pay. And rather than pay it they buried you, thirsty, with the debt unsettled, and they candied themselves into your walls to make sure no one ever let you up to collect. A thousand years. Not punishment. A defaulted loan, with the lender walled in alongside the borrower's grandchildren."

The columns were silent. In the silence I heard, very faintly, something I had not heard before from the thing in the hill. I heard it grieve.

YES, it said. ONE WORD. THEN: SHE PROMISED ME A NAME. THE WOMAN WHO FIRST DUG HERE. I GAVE HER WATER FOR HER PEOPLE AND SHE PROMISED ME A NAME, SO THAT I MIGHT BE CALLED, AND SPOKEN OF, AND REMEMBERED — SO THAT I MIGHT BE A GOD AND NOT A THIRST. AND WHEN THE RAINS RETURNED SHE DECIDED A NAMED GOD IN THE HILL WAS A RIVAL TO HER THRONE. SO SHE GAVE ME SALT INSTEAD OF A NAME, AND HER CHILDREN BECAME MY WALLS, AND I HAVE BEEN THIRSTY A THOUSAND YEARS NOT FOR WATER, EIRA. FOR THE NAME. I WAS PROMISED THE ONE THING THAT TURNS A HUNGER INTO A PERSON, AND I WAS CHEATED OF IT, AND I HAVE BEEN DRINKING THE WORLD EVER SINCE TRYING TO FILL A HOLE SHAPED LIKE A WORD.

I sat with that for a long moment. I thought of my grandmother, dead badly, unforgiven, her name handed down to the daughter a queen could spend — a name used as a punishment, a name used as a wall.

I knew something about being a hunger that was never given the dignity of being a person.

"Then I'll pay her debt," I said quietly. "Not with my death. With the actual coin you were promised and cheated of. I'll give you a name, and I'll give you the second thing she stole, which is the worse theft — I'll give you someone to remember it. You will not be a thirst under a hill. You will be a god of Anwe, named in the archive, spoken at the wells, and the price of that is not my life. The price is that you let the kingdom go, and you let me up, and you become something a person can pray to instead of something a queen has to bury."

WHY, said the thing in the hill, and for the first time the enormous voice was small, WOULD YOU PAY ANOTHER WOMAN'S DEBT.

"Because she didn't," I said. "And someone has to, or it just keeps being owed. That's what a debt is. It doesn't care who pays it. It only stops when it's paid."
