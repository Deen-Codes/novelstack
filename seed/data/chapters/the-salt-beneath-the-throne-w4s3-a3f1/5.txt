I want to be honest about the hour that followed, because it is the hour the whole story turns on, and a turning hour deserves the truth.

For a long while I considered simply agreeing.

It would have been so easy, and that is the trap of it — the thing in the hill did not threaten me, did not loom, did not show me a single tooth. It reasoned. It sat in Old Pell's quiet body and it laid the argument out the way my tutors had once laid out sums, and the sum came to this: the kingdom of Anwe was drinking salt and would die of it, every laundress and orchard child and indifferent sister, unless a life freely given sealed the hill. My life was the cheapest one available. My own mother had appraised it and found it so. I had spent nineteen years agreeing with that appraisal. Why balk at the final figure now, merely because I was the one being spent?

That is how it nearly took me. Not with terror. With arithmetic, and with the long soft habit of believing myself worth less than the people who had taught me so.

What stopped me was the boy in the salt column.

I had been staring at him without seeing him — the candied watchman-child, the one holding the lamp that died a thousand years before my birth. And somewhere in that arithmetic hour my eye finally rested on his face, and I saw that he had been perhaps eight years old, and I understood that he had not freely given anything. Eight-year-olds do not freely give their lives to seal monsters under hills. Eight-year-olds are brought. Eight-year-olds are walked to the graveside by adults who have decided, in clever chairs, that the child is a price the world can afford.

And the thing in the hill had just told me — proudly, as a craftsman tells you of his materials — that the seal required a life freely walked in and freely given.

So either the boy had not sealed anything, and the thing was lying about its own lock.

Or the seal did not need a willing death at all, and the thing wanted me willing for some entirely separate reason of its own.

"You're thirsty," I said. My voice was very loud in the candied silence. "You've told me so a dozen ways. A thing that thirsty doesn't explain the rules of its own cage to the only person in a thousand years who walked down here able to read them. Unless." I stood up. My knees ached. My grandmother's knife was warm in my hand from how hard I had been holding it. "Unless you can't open the cage yourself. Unless the watchmen built it so the only hand that can unlock it is a hand that unlocks it freely. And you have spent a thousand years thirsty, in the dark, talking — talking, that's all you've ever been able to do — waiting for someone lonely enough, and unloved enough, and starved enough for a use, that she would walk down here and call her own death a gift, and turn the key from the inside."

The swallowing stopped.

This time the silence was not savouring. This time it was the silence of a gambler watching a card he did not expect.

"I am the cork," I said, "only if I agree to be. That's not the lid of your prison. That's the latch. And my mother — clever, dry, careful — my mother did not send me down here to die. She sent me down here because she genuinely did not know the difference, and neither did I, until just now, looking at a child who was carried to his grave by people exactly like her."

THE KINGDOM WILL DIE, the voice said, and it was not kind anymore. It had dropped the borrowed mouth of Old Pell and come up the columns from everywhere at once. EVERY WELL. EVERY CHILD. THEIR SALT IS ON YOUR HANDS NOW, EIRA, UNLESS YOU GIVE.

"Maybe," I said. "But if the only door out of your hill is one I open for you, then I am not the cheap fourth daughter down here. I am the only thing in the world you cannot have. And we are going to talk, you and I, about what that's worth — because I came down to bargain, and you have just told me, in your pride, exactly how much leverage I hold."
