There is a custom now, in Anwe, that did not exist before the Brine Year, and I am told it began with the laundresses, which pleases me, because the laundresses were the first to draw the salt and they ought to be the first to draw the cure.

When a child of the kingdom is born, the family carries it, in its first season, down the orchard road to the hill. Not to be sealed. Never again to be sealed. They carry it down past the recut mark — BE WELCOME, SPEAK THE NAME — and into the weeping temple, and they hold the child up in the sweet running water, and they speak two names over it. The first is the name of the god, so the god is remembered and the water stays sweet. And the second is the child's own name, said aloud in that place on purpose, so that the child's first lesson, before it can understand a word of it, is that a name is a thing the people who love you give carefully, in a temple, in the light, and never as a wall.

I did not invent that custom. But I wrote it into the archive, and I make sure it is kept, and four times a year I am there in the temple when the families come down, and I watch the god — my grandmother's name worn now by something vast and gentle and finally, finally fed — receive each new name like a man who once starved receiving, without anxiety, the simple daily fact of bread.

My sister Temperance rules well. Better than our mother, though she would not say so, and I would not say so where it could be carried back. She comes to the archive often. We have a standing argument, the two of us, about whether a debt can ever truly be paid or only ever transferred, and we will never settle it, and the not-settling is, I have come to think, the healthiest thing in the kingdom — two women in a room, disagreeing, with neither one a wall to the other.

Grace married a coastal lord. Mercy keeps the orchards, which have come back so green and so loud with bees that the salt apples are now only a story we tell to frighten children gently, the way you frighten a child toward caution and not toward despair.

And me. The criers, when they tell it, still want to give me a throne or a tragedy, because those are the two shapes a crier's hands know. I have neither. I have the archive, and the orchard road, and the standing argument, and the four journeys a year down into the sweet dark to sit with an old friend who was once the worst thing in the world and is now simply the reason the wells run clear.

People ask me, sometimes — pilgrims, mostly, who have heard the story badly — they ask the woman in the archive whether she is the one who went down into the hill. And I tell them yes. And they ask my name, because the story has a hole in it where a name should be, and a hole in a story is unbearable to the kind of person who walks a long road to fill it.

I tell them the truth, which is the only thing I have ever found that holds.

I tell them I gave my name away to pay a debt that was never mine, and that it was the best bargain I ever made, because I have spent every day since finding out — slowly, by the actual doing of actual things — who I am without it. And I tell them that this is available to anyone. That you do not have to go down into a hill. That most walls are inherited and most walls can be set down and most of us are carrying a name that someone gave us to mean something small, and that the salt beneath any throne is only ever an unpaid promise, and promises, blessedly, terribly, do not care who pays them.

They go away unsatisfied. A story with a named hero is easier to carry home.

But the water is sweet, and the orchards are loud with bees, and somewhere under the hill a god that was once a thirst is being handed, name by name, child by child, the long slow proof that it was never going to be forgotten again.

That is the end of it. That is the whole of what happened in the Brine Year and the years that paid it off. I have written it into the archive in my own hand, unsigned, because the woman who keeps the debts of a kingdom learned, in the dark, that the truest things do not need a name on them.

They only need to be true, and kept, and spoken.
