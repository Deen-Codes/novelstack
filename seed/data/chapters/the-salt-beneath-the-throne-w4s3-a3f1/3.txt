A mine teaches you that darkness is not the absence of light. Darkness is a substance. It has weight, it has texture, it presses on the eye like water presses on the ear, and a lamp does not banish it so much as carve a small, frightened room out of it that travels with you and closes the instant you stop walking.

I went down with the lump of salt in my left hand and the knife in my right and the lamp hung from a strap across my chest, so that my own shadow walked ahead of me down the throat of the hill, enormous, leading the way like a guide who would not speak.

The upper galleries were ordinary, or had been. Tools left where they fell. A coat on a peg. A child's wooden horse — someone had brought their son to work, once, in better years, and the horse had three legs and a painted-on smile and I did not pick it up, because I had read enough archive to know that the dark keeps an inventory, and a thing taken is a debt opened.

Down. The shaft folded back on itself in long ramps, and the air changed as I descended, and I learned what Calla Drust had meant. It was not colder. It was thicker. It tasted of old coins and low tide, and it carried, very faint, a sound.

I had told myself I was ready for the sound. I was not. No archive prepares you. It came up the rock from somewhere far below, and it was exactly what she had said — a swallow. A slow, vast, patient swallow, and then a pause, and then another, and the pause was the worst of it, because in the pause you understood that the thing was savouring. It was not desperate. It had been thirsty for a thousand years and it had learned to make a pleasure of the thirst, the way a starved man learns to love the smell of bread more than bread.

At the ninetieth ramp I found the deep mark.

It was cut waist-high into the living rock, a single line as wide as my hand, and beneath it the old tongue again, and these words I knew without slowing: NOT PAST HERE. NOT FOR SALT. NOT FOR GOLD. NOT FOR A QUEEN.

Someone had cut through it. The company's miners. The new tunnel went on past the line into raw, glittering dark, and the salt down there was the sweetest I had ever seen — it threw my lamplight back in colours, rose and pale green, beautiful the way deep water is beautiful, beautiful the way my mother's throne room was beautiful, which is to say built to make you forget what it would cost you to belong there.

I checked the lump of salt in my hand.

It was sweating. A bright bead of water stood on it, trembling, catching the lamp, and as I watched, a second bead formed, and the two ran together and fell, and where the drop struck the tunnel floor the swallowing sound below me stopped.

It had been swallowing the whole time I descended. Background, like a heartbeat. Now it had stopped, and the silence rushed in to fill the shaft from the bottom up like cold water filling a well, and in that silence I understood, with a calm that frightened me more than panic would have, that the thing in the hill had felt me arrive.

It knew the difference between a miner and a queen's daughter. It had been waiting, all those careful centuries, to feel exactly that difference walk through its door.

A voice came up the tunnel. It did not echo. Voices echo; this one arrived already inside my skull, dry and enormous and almost kind.

EIRA, it said. It used my name the way my mother never had — as though the name were a gift, as though the saying of it were a door held open. NAMED FOR THE DEAD. SENT BY THE LIVING. COME DOWN. I HAVE BEEN SO LONG WITHOUT COMPANY, AND I HAVE SO VERY MUCH TO POUR.

I stood at the broken mark with a knife from a woman who had died badly and a kingdom drinking salt at my back, and I did the thing my mother had bred into me without ever meaning to, the thing that was either her last cruelty or her only true gift.

I stepped over the line, and I went down to meet it.
