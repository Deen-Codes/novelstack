They called it the Brine Year, though it was not yet half over, and already the word had the weight of a thing carved into stone.

It began at the Lower Wells, where the laundresses drew water before dawn. The first bucket came up cloudy. The second came up grey. By the time the sun cleared the orchard wall, every rope in the kingdom of Anwe was hauling salt from the deep, and the children who tasted it spat and laughed, because children do not yet know which jokes the world refuses to take back.

I was not a child. I was nineteen, and I was standing at the parapet of the West Keep watching my mother's banners go limp in a windless heat, and I understood before the criers did. The rivers were not drying. The rivers were being drunk.

My name is Eira sa Voln, and I was the fourth daughter of a queen who had wanted three.

You should know that early, because everything that follows is shaped by it the way a riverbed is shaped by the water that abandoned it. My sisters were named for virtues — Grace, Mercy, Temperance — and I was named for my grandmother, who had died badly, and whom my mother had not forgiven for it. A child learns the temperature of a house. I had learned mine. I kept to the cold rooms, the archive and the salt-cellar and the long gallery where the dead were painted, and I let my sisters have the warm ones.

So when the steward came for me that evening, I assumed I had broken something.

"The Queen wants you," he said. He would not look at me. That, more than the words, told me the shape of the night.

The throne room of Anwe was built to humble. Forty feet of black floor, polished until it doubled the torches, so that anyone crossing it walked across their own drowned reflection. My mother had chosen that floor. She chose everything. She was small and dry and precise, like a letter folded too many times, and she was sitting very straight on a chair of pale bone, and she was dying. I saw it the way you see weather coming. Something in the slack of her left hand, the careful way she had arranged her own shoulders, as though holding a structure that wanted to fall.

"Eira," she said. Not daughter. She had never spent that word on me.

"Mother."

"The wells." She did not waste breath on preamble; I will give her that, always. "You have read the archive. You read everything, they tell me, like a mouse in a granary. So tell me what is drinking my kingdom."

The court was watching. Grace and Mercy and Temperance stood near the dais in their bright colours, and they were watching too, and I felt the old familiar thing — that to be seen by my mother at all was a kind of danger I had spent my life avoiding, and now it had found me anyway.

"There is an account," I said carefully. "Older than the founding. The hill the salt mines run under is not a hill. It is a barrow. Something was buried in it, and the burying was meant to be permanent, and the salt was the seal."

"Was."

"The miners have gone deeper this generation than any before. The records say there is a depth past which one does not dig. The records do not say the miners obeyed it."

My mother closed her eyes. When she opened them, she had decided something, and I have learned since that the worst moments of a life arrive looking exactly like that — like a small woman deciding.

"You will go down and find it," she said. "And you will close it, or you will bargain with it, or you will die in the attempt and spare me the cost of your keep. Anwe cannot lose a useful daughter to this. It can lose you."

The black floor held my reflection very still. I want to tell you that I was brave, that something rose in me. It did not. What rose in me was a small, ugly, grateful warmth, because for the first time in nineteen years my mother had found a use for me, and I was my mother's daughter, and I had been starving.

"Yes," I said. "I'll go."

Grace was crying. Mercy had turned her face away. Temperance, who was cleverest, was already looking at me as though I were a portrait in the gallery — someone to be remembered, framed, and walked past.

That night I packed by candlelight. Rope, flint, the archive's oldest map traced onto oilcloth, and a knife that had belonged to my grandmother — the badly-dead one, the one I was named for. I did not pack hope. I did not own any. But I packed the knife, and I have come to believe that a knife carried by someone who expects nothing is a more honest thing than all the prayers in Anwe.

In the morning the wells came up white as bone, and I walked out the salt-gate alone, toward the hill that was not a hill, and behind me the kingdom drank its breakfast of brine and pretended, the way kingdoms do, that someone else's daughter was a price and not a wound.
