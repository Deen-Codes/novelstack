A name is not a small thing to give. I want to be clear about that, because it would be easy to read what I did as generosity, and it was not generosity. It was surgery, and I performed it on myself.

To name the thing in the hill I had to give it a name with weight, a name with a life already poured into it, because a brand-new word is just a sound and the thing had been cheated with sounds before. It would know. It had a thousand years of being lied to and it would taste a hollow name the way I had tasted a salt apple.

So I gave it the only name I owned that had been treated the way it had been treated.

I gave it my grandmother's name. I gave it Eira.

You understand what that cost. It was my name too. We had shared it, the badly-dead woman and I, the way you share a coat that does not fit either of you. To set it down in the salt and say this is yours now, carry it, be called by it was to walk back up out of the hill as someone I would have to invent from nothing. I did it anyway. I had spent nineteen years as a name my mother used to remember a grudge. I found, kneeling in the candied dark, that I was not sorry to put it down. A name that has only ever been a wall is no great loss to a girl who has just learned she can be a door.

"Eira," I said, to the hill, the first time. "That is your name now. It was my grandmother's. She died unforgiven and unremembered, and you have been unremembered a thousand years, and I am tired — I am so tired — of the people in my family being walls. Be a god. Be named. I will write you into the archive with my own hand and the laundresses will speak you over the wells and you will never be a thirst again."

The hill drank the name.

I felt it happen. Not a sound — a settling, the way a house settles when a great weight is finally set down on a foundation built to bear it. The columns around me did not crack or blaze. They simply, slowly, began to weep — clear water, true water, sweet water, running down a thousand years of candied watchmen and pooling at my knees, and I cupped it in my hands and drank, and it was the first water in the kingdom of Anwe that had not tasted of salt since the Brine Year began.

THANK YOU, said the god in the hill, and the voice was not enormous anymore and it was not small. It was the size of a person. EIRA. He tried it. He tried his own name and I heard, in the trying, the exact sound a starved thing makes at the smell of bread when it finally, finally understands the bread is real and is for him. I WILL HOLD THE WATER SWEET. I WILL HOLD IT FOR AS LONG AS I AM SPOKEN. AND THE GIRL WHO CAME DOWN — WHAT WILL SHE BE CALLED, NOW THAT SHE HAS GIVEN HER NAME AWAY?

I stood up in the rising sweet water of the hill that was a barrow that was, now, a temple. Old Pell's body had gone soft and ordinary, just a tired dead miner I would carry up to Calla Drust so that somebody, this time, had gone back for him.

"I don't know yet," I said. "I think that's the point. I think I get to find out."
