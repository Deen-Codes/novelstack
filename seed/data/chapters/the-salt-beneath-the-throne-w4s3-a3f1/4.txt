Past the broken mark the tunnel stopped pretending to be a tunnel.

The company's men had cut it square and braced, the way miners do, but a hundred paces in the bracing simply stopped, and the walls opened out, and the salt was no longer cut — it was grown. Columns of it rose floor to roof, fluted and fused, and the lamplight ran along them and found, embedded in the translucent stone, things that had once been alive.

I made myself look. The archive had taught me that the things you refuse to look at are the things that later climb into your sleep, and I had decided, somewhere on the ninetieth ramp, that I would arrive at the bottom of this hill with my eyes open if I arrived at all.

So I looked, and I saw the miners.

Not Calla Drust's miners. The old ones. The ones who had done the burying. They stood inside the salt columns in the postures of work — a man with a pick raised, a woman bent to a basket, a boy holding a lamp that had gone out a thousand years ago — and the salt had taken them slowly, the way honey takes a bee, and they had not decayed. They had candied. Their faces were calm. That was the part the archive had not warned me of and could not have: the calm. They had not been frightened when the salt closed over them. They had been finishing a job.

WE BURIED IT THIRSTY, the door had said. And here were the gravediggers, still standing at the graveside, because some graves require an eternal watch and the watchmen had simply agreed to become the watch.

I found Old Pell near the centre.

I knew him because he was not candied. He was recent — Calla Drust's man, the one whose lamp had failed, the one nobody had gone back for. He sat against a salt column with his knees drawn up and his dead lamp in his lap, and he was not frightened either, and I knelt by him and understood that the calm in this place was not peace. It was the thing's table manners. It did not gulp. It did not frighten the food. It sat you down, and it spoke to you in your own skull in a voice almost kind, and it poured, and you grew quiet, and quiet, and still.

The salt in my hand was running like a wrung cloth now. Water sheeted off it and vanished before it struck the floor — drunk, I understood, drunk out of the very air, because down here the thing did not wait for wells. Down here it was the well.

"Eira." Old Pell's mouth had not moved. His eyes had not opened. But the voice was his, borrowed, held up like a puppet, and behind it I could hear the enormous patient thing working the jaw. "She sent you to close me. Did she tell you how? Did your clever queen, in her clever chair, give her unloved daughter the method? Or did she give you only the errand, and trust the hill to handle the rest?"

I did not answer. I had read enough trial accounts to know that a thing which wants you talking wants you talking for a reason.

"You may close me," the voice went on, agreeable, terrible. "It can be done. The old watchmen did it. But the seal is not made of salt, child. Salt is only the lid. The seal is made of a life, freely walked in and freely given, and that is why your mother sent the daughter she could spend." A long, savouring pause. The swallowing sound, very close now, almost gentle. "She did not send you to bargain with me, Eira. She sent you to be the cork."

I stayed kneeling by Old Pell in the candied dark, and I found that I was not surprised, and I hated that I was not surprised, because a person who is not surprised by such news has been told a version of it every day of her life until the worst possible truth arrives sounding like home.
