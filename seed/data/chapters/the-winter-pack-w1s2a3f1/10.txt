Spring came late to Halloran Ridge, the way it always did, but it came.

The snow went off the open ground first, then the trees, then the high country, until only the far peaks kept their white. The county road thawed and cracked and was mended. A tow truck did, eventually, come for Wren's car — she watched it go down the mountain with something that was not quite grief and not quite laughter, the last loose thread of an old life finally pulled free.

She did not go with it.

There was no ceremony for what she chose, in the end. Wren had braced for one — some old rite, some moonlit thing with words in a language older than the ridge. But the pack, it turned out, did not mark its deepest things with ceremony. It marked them with ordinariness. One morning she simply woke in the cedar-smelling room and understood that it was her room, that the quilt was her quilt, that the weather system of the house had long since stopped treating her as weather that might pass.

Maren taught her the cinnamon bread. Tobin taught her, with enormous patience and total inaccuracy, the name of every wolf in the pack. And Elias taught her, slowly, over a season, that the fated thing was not a cage and never had been — that it was only a door that had opened once, in a snowstorm, and that everything good after it had been a choice, hers, made fresh each day with all the truth in her hands.

On the first warm night of the year the pack ran the high ridge for the joy of it.

Wren ran with them.

Not changed — she would never be changed, that was not her road, and she had stopped wishing it were. She ran the only way a human could run with a pack of wolves under a spring moon: on the broad warm back of the one who had pulled her out of the cold, her hands buried in his ruff, her laughter streaming away behind her on the wind.

The valley opened below them, green now, the rivers loud with melt. The pack poured out across the high meadow in the silver light, Tobin quick among them, Maren grey and unhurried and queenly at the rear. And Elias carried her up to the roof of the world the way he had that first impossible week, and stopped, and let her slide down into the cool spring grass.

The whole pack gathered on the height. Their breath did not show; the night was kind. Below them the valley held its hundred small lights, and above them the stars came out one after another, patient, in no hurry at all.

Wren stood in the grass with one hand in the wolf's fur and looked at the life she had not driven into the mountains to find, and had found anyway.

"All right," she said softly, to the pack, to the ridge, to the man inside the wolf. "All right. I'm home."

And the wolf leaned his great head against her, gently, the way the whole pack had taught itself to lean — and Wren, on the roof of the spring, leaned back, and stayed.
