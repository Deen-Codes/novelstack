She stayed for the bread, and then she stayed for the afternoon, and then it was three days, and Wren had stopped pretending to herself that she was counting them as a guest.

The Hallorans absorbed her with a quiet, practiced grace. Maren put her to work kneading dough and never once made her feel useless. The nine-year-old, whose name was Tobin, decided she belonged to him and followed her through the house narrating the entire history of every dent in every wall. A pack, she was learning, was not a single thing. It was a weather system. It had moods. It closed around its own.

And it had, slowly, closed around her.

On the fourth day Elias asked if she wanted to see the run.

"From the inside," he said. They were on the back porch, and the cold was a clean blade, and his breath did not show though hers did. "I can't change you, Wren. That's not how it works and I'd never want it to be. But the wolf can carry. If you'll trust me — if you'll get on my back like the ridiculous fairytale it is — I can take you up the high country the way we see it."

It should have frightened her. A week ago it would have. But Wren had watched this man kneel to be eye-level with a child, had watched him stand in a doorway rather than cross a line, and she found that the fear she reached for simply wasn't where she'd left it.

"All right," she said.

He changed at the treeline, and she made herself watch, because looking away felt like a small cowardice and she was done with those. It was not grotesque. It was not even slow. It was like watching a held breath let go — a blurring, a folding, and then the wolf stood where the man had been, vast and dark, steam rising off the warmth of him into the blue air.

He was enormous. His head came to her shoulder. And his eyes, when he turned them on her, were Elias's eyes exactly, patient and amused and faintly afraid, and that was what undid the last knot of her nerve.

"You are absurd," she told him, and put her hands in the deep warm fur of his ruff, and climbed on.

The run was the most alive Wren had ever been.

The world went by in a rushing silver blur, the snow soundless under his paws, the cold streaming past her face while the heat of him soaked up through her hands and her knees. The pack ran with them — she could see the others, dark joyful shapes pouring through the trees, Tobin small and quick among them — and they ran the high ridge under a sky going lavender at its edges, and Wren laughed out loud into the wind because there was nothing else a body could possibly do.

At the top of the world Elias stopped, and the pack scattered out across the open snowfield, and the whole valley lay below them blue and gold and endless.

Wren slid down off his back. She stood in snow to her shins with one hand still buried in his fur.

"I think I understand now," she said, quietly, just to him. "Why you couldn't watch it drive away."

The wolf leaned his great head against her, gently, the way Tobin leaned on her in the kitchen, the way the whole pack had learned to lean.

And Wren, on the roof of the winter, leaned back.
