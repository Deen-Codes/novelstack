The snow came down like the sky had given up holding it.

Wren had driven through bad weather before — three winters in Montana taught a person that much — but this was different. This was the kind of storm that erased the road a hundred feet ahead and left only the small lit cone of her headlights and the wipers ticking like a slow, frightened heart. She leaned forward over the wheel as if those few inches would help her see.

They didn't.

The engine made a sound she had never heard it make, something between a cough and a sigh, and then the dashboard lights dimmed and the heater stopped pushing warm air against her knees. She coasted. The car rolled to a stop in the soft shoulder, nosed gently into a snowbank, and went quiet.

For a moment Wren just sat there. The silence was enormous. No engine, no radio, only the hiss of snow against glass and the wind working its long fingers under the door seals.

"Okay," she said out loud, because the sound of a voice — even her own, even shaking — was better than nothing. "Okay. Think."

Her phone had one bar and then no bars. The little call she tried dropped before it rang. She was somewhere on the county road that climbed toward the ridge, somewhere between the gas station that had been closed and the town she had not yet reached, and the temperature outside the car was the kind of cold that did not negotiate.

She knew what they told you. Stay with the vehicle. The car is a box; a box is shelter. But the car was already going cold, and the box was filling slowly with her own frightened breath, and she could feel the panic climbing her throat hand over hand.

Wren pulled her coat tighter, tucked her hands into her sleeves, and watched the windshield go white.

She must have drifted, because the knock startled her so badly she cried out.

A shape stood at her window. Tall — taller than the door — bent down so a face could find the glass. Snow had crusted on the shoulders of a dark coat. The man knocked again, gently this time, and made a small turning motion with his gloved hand. Roll it down.

She cracked the window two inches. Cold poured in like water.

"Your car's done," the man said. His voice was low and even, almost bored, the voice of someone who had pulled a great many people out of a great many snowbanks. "You can't stay out here. It'll be twenty below by midnight."

"There's a town —"

"There's a town nine miles up. You won't walk it and your car won't drive it." He straightened, looked back over his shoulder at something she couldn't see, then leaned down again. "My family's place is close. We've got heat and a phone line that works when the cell towers don't. You can call whoever you need to call."

Wren hesitated. Every careful instinct a woman carries told her to stay in the locked car. But the locked car was a refrigerator with a dead battery, and the man's face, what she could see of it, was patient and unbothered and faintly, strangely, kind.

"I don't even know your name," she said.

"Elias." He said it like it cost him nothing. "Halloran. The ridge is named after us, if that helps you trust me. It probably shouldn't."

She almost laughed. The almost-laugh was what decided her, in the end — that a stranger in a blizzard had thought to be funny.

She gathered her bag, her dead phone, the thermos that still held a swallow of cold coffee, and pushed the door open against the wind.

The snow took her breath instantly. Elias steadied her with one hand under her elbow, and even through two coats and the glove, she felt how warm his grip was — unreasonably warm, furnace-warm, as though the cold simply didn't reach him.

He guided her toward a truck idling on the road, its exhaust unspooling red in the taillights.

"How did you even see me?" she asked, the wind tearing the words half away. "Out here. In this."

Elias looked down at her. For just a second, in the wash of the taillights, his eyes caught the light the way an animal's do at the edge of a road at night — a flat bright shine, there and then gone.

"We're good," he said, "at finding things in the dark."

He opened the truck door for her. Wren climbed in, and told herself it was only the cold that made her shiver.
