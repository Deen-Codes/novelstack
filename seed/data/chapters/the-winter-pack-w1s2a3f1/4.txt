He told her standing by the door, because she had asked him to stay where he was, and he had.

He told her plainly, without drama, the way a man reads a difficult letter aloud. The Hallorans were a pack. Not in the loose human way people used the word — a true pack, a bloodline that ran back further than the ridge had carried their name. They changed. Not at the moon's command, that part was a story; the moon only made it easy, made it sweet, the way a downhill road makes walking sweet. They had run the high country last night because the storm had been beautiful and because they were happy. The wolf at the treeline that had looked up at her window had been him.

Wren sat down on the edge of the bed halfway through. Not because her legs failed her. Because it seemed like the thing a person did, while the floor of the world quietly rearranged itself.

"And the reason you lied about the phone," she said, when he had finished.

Elias was quiet for a moment. "There's a thing that happens to us," he said. "Rarely. Most of us never feel it once. We don't choose it and we can't undo it and we have a very old, very embarrassing word for it." He set his empty mug on the dresser. "Fated. The pack would say I'm fated to you. That something in me recognized something in you the second I pulled your door open, and has not stopped recognizing it since."

The fire of his certainty had gone strange and gentle. He looked, Wren thought, like a man confessing to a crime he hadn't meant to commit.

"That's a lot," she said, "to put on a woman whose car broke down."

"I know it." He spread his hands. "That's why I'm standing by the door. It's a claim, Wren, and you didn't agree to be claimed. It can't bind you. Nothing about it gets to bind you. If you want, I'll call the tow myself, right now, and you'll be down the mountain before lunch and you can decide I was a lonely man telling stories in a snowstorm." His voice did not waver, but something behind it did. "I'll hate it. But I'll do it. The whole point of the thing — the only honest version of it — is that you get to choose, every single day, with all the truth in your hands."

Outside the window the sky had gone pale gold. Somewhere downstairs a kettle began, very faintly, to sing.

Wren looked at the cold coffee, and the strange quilt, and the man who had carried snow into her room and stood the whole conversation in a doorway so she would never feel trapped.

"I'm not getting in a tow truck," she heard herself say, "at least not before breakfast. That stew last night was the best thing I've eaten in a year and I refuse to leave before I find out about breakfast."

It was not a declaration of anything. They both knew that. But Elias smiled — really smiled, for the first time since she'd met him, and it changed his whole hard face — and stepped back from the door to let her through it.

"Maren makes cinnamon bread," he said, "on snow mornings."

"Then I'm staying for the bread," said Wren, "and we'll see about the rest of it."
