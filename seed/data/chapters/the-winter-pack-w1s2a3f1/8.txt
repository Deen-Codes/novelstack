Maren told them, at the kitchen table, with the third storm building blue-black against the windows.

She told it reluctantly, the way she had said the lines were down — gently, as though even now she wanted to spare her own family the weight of it. Gareth had gone south two years ago to a pack near the river country, a pack the Hallorans had old and friendly ties with. He had gone as a guest. He had come back, Maren said, because that pack had asked him to leave — and a pack did not ask a guest to leave for a small thing.

"He fate-claimed a girl there," Maren said. "Or he said he did. A girl whose family had standing, and land, and a quarrel with their own alpha. And when it suited him to be done with it, he released the claim and walked away, and called it the old law, and let her wear the shame of it." Her hands were flat on the table. "He did to that child exactly what he is setting up to do here. He learned that a fated claim is a thing you can pick up and put down to move people around like furniture. And nobody stopped him, because everybody was too polite, and because he is charming, and because the old law is on his side if you only read half of it."

Wren let out a slow breath. "Then we read the other half."

"There is another half," Maren said, and for the first time something fierce came up in her, the iron under the grey hair. "The oldest law of all. A claim made falsely — a claim used as a tool — is no claim. The pack that learns of it is bound to say so. I have the river pack's word in a letter, Wren. It came in autumn. I never showed Elias because I never thought we'd need it." She looked at her son. "I am ashamed of that. I thought keeping a thing quiet was the same as keeping the peace. It isn't. It's just letting the loud man win slower."

The full moon rose the next night into a sky finally swept clean.

The pack gathered in the great room, all of them, the whole weather system of the family pressed into one space, and Gareth stood by the fire with his charming smile and waited to pull his lever.

He did not get to.

Wren stood up first — before he could speak, before the room could settle into the shape he wanted — and she did not argue for her place, and she did not plead. She simply said, "Before the pack votes on me, the pack should hear about the river country," and she put Maren's letter into Maren's hands, and Maren read it aloud in a voice that did not shake.

Wren watched the room turn. It turned the way it had turned toward her on the first night — a closed circle deciding, all at once and silently, what it would do. Only this time it was not opening to let someone in. It was closing against someone who had always thought himself safely inside it.

Gareth's smile arrived everywhere and landed nowhere, and then, slowly, it stopped arriving.

"This is sentiment," he said. "This is one outsider and one old letter —"

"This is pack law," said Maren, "all of it, and not the half you like. Sit down, Gareth."

He looked around the room for a single ally, the way he had looked for witnesses on the first day.

He did not find one.
