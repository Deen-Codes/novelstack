The trouble, when it came, did not come from outside.

Wren had half expected it to. In the stories — and she had, by now, made Elias tell her a great many of the stories — the danger to a pack always came over the ridge from somewhere: a rival, a hunter, a thing with a grudge. But the Halloran trouble walked in the front door wearing a familiar face.

His name was Gareth. He was Elias's cousin, and he had been away — "down south," Maren said, with the particular flatness she used for things she did not approve of — and he came home with the second storm of the month, stamping snow off his boots in the entry hall, and the whole warm weather system of the house changed pressure the moment he did.

He was charming. Wren noticed that first, and distrusted it second. He kissed Maren's cheek and roughhoused with Tobin and shook Wren's hand with a smile that did all the right things and reached none of the right places.

"So you're the human," he said.

"I'm Wren," said Wren.

"Sure." Gareth's smile didn't move. "And Elias is — what. Keeping you?"

"Gareth." Elias had come in from the back, and his voice was very quiet, and the quiet of it was worse than a shout would have been.

"It's a fair question, cousin." Gareth turned, easy, unbothered, and spread his hands to the room — to the pack, Wren realized; he was speaking to the pack, the way a man speaks who wants witnesses. "Pack law. We all grew up on it. You don't bring an outsider under the roof past three nights without the pack speaking on it. You don't fate-claim where the pack can't weigh in. Those aren't my rules. They're the old ones. And you've broken both, and everybody in this room has been too polite to say so."

The great room had gone silent. Even the fire seemed to hush.

Wren felt her face go hot. Not with embarrassment — with anger, clean and sudden. "If there's a vote to be had about me," she said, "I'd like to be in the room for it. I've had enough of decisions about my life happening over my head."

Gareth looked at her with something that was almost respect and entirely cold. "It's not about you," he said. "That's the part you don't get, sweetheart. It was never going to be about you."

And Wren understood, then, the shape of the real thing. It was not about her at all. It was about Elias — about a cousin who had come home wanting something, and had found a clean and lawful way to take it, and the lever he meant to pull was her.

"Pack speaks at the next full moon," Gareth said, to the room, to the old rules, to his cousin's still and furious face. "Three nights from now. Everybody gets a voice." He smiled again, that smile that arrived everywhere and landed nowhere. "Even the human, if Elias insists. I'm nothing if not fair."

He went up the stairs whistling.

Maren let out a long breath. Tobin had gone to stand very close to Wren's side, fists balled, a nine-year-old's whole loyalty on furious display, and Wren put a hand on his shoulder without looking down at him.

"He can't make you leave," Elias said. He had crossed the room to her. His voice still had the dangerous quiet in it, but under the quiet, now, she heard the fear again — the same fear from the doorway on the first morning, the fear of watching a thing drive away. "Wren. Whatever the pack says. He can't —"

"I know he can't make me leave," Wren said. "I'm not worried about that." She looked up the empty stairs where the whistling had gone. "I'm worried that he's about to make you choose between me and your pack in front of everyone you love. And I think that's exactly what he wants. And I think we have three days to make sure it doesn't work."
