Gareth left before the moon was down.

It was not a banishment, exactly. The pack did not have the stomach for that, and Maren — who could have pushed for it — chose instead the harder, kinder thing. He would go back south, she said, and he would carry the river pack's letter and the Halloran answer to it, and he would stand before the girl he had shamed and her family, and he would say, out loud, in front of her pack, what he had done. And then he could make whatever life he could make.

"That's mercy," Wren said to Elias afterward, on the back porch, the cold clean and enormous around them.

"That's the worst thing she could do to him," Elias said. "He'll have to be honest in a room full of people. For Gareth that's the end of the world." He almost smiled. "My mother is more frightening than I am. I keep learning it."

The storms had blown themselves out. The sky over the ridge was thick with stars, more than Wren had ever seen, the kind of sky that only opens after the worst of the weather.

She had been here three weeks. Her car was still nosed into a snowbank on the county road, and she had not once, in days, thought about a tow truck.

"I have to ask you something," Elias said. "And I need you to hear that the door is still open. It will always be open. That was never a trick, Wren. The choice is yours every day, the way it was the first morning."

"I know that," she said. "It's why I'm still standing on your porch."

He turned to face her. The fear was in his eyes again — but it was a different fear now, she had learned to read his face the way she'd learned to read the pack, and this was not the fear of a thing driving away. This was the much older, much sweeter fear of asking for something and being told yes.

"Then stay," Elias said. "Not because a fairytale word says you have to. Not because your car died in the right snowbank. Stay because three weeks ago a stranger pulled you out of the cold, and you have spent every day since making this house warmer than it was — and I would like, very much, to spend the rest of my life finding out what else you'll make of us."

The wind moved in the high trees. Far off, on the ridge, a single wolf lifted its voice to the swept-clean sky, and was answered, and answered again, the whole pack singing the storm's end.

Wren stepped in close to the furnace warmth of him. She put her cold hands flat against his chest and felt his heart going, fast, under all that heat — not bored now, not patient, not anything but hers.

"I came up this mountain to start over," she said. "I think I'd like to start over here."

Elias kissed her on his mother's back porch under a sky full of stars, and it was not the wild thing the stories had promised — it was better than that, it was a small warm certain thing, the kind of thing a person could build a life on top of.

Behind them, in the great room, the pack pretended very hard not to be watching, and did a terrible job of it, and Tobin cheered.
