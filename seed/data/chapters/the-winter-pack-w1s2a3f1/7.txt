They had three days, and Elias spent the first of them not speaking to her.

Not coldly. That was what made it hard to fight. He was unfailingly gentle — he brought her coffee, he sat across from her at meals — but he had gone somewhere behind his own eyes, and Wren knew the look, because she had worn it herself in the months before she'd packed a car and driven into the mountains to start over. It was the look of a person deciding to do a noble, stupid thing for someone else's good.

She cornered him in the woodshed on the second evening.

"You're going to tell the pack to vote me out," she said.

Elias didn't deny it. He set the axe down very carefully against the wall. "If they vote with Gareth," he said, "and I've stood against them, the pack splits. Do you understand what that is, Wren? It's not a disagreement. A split pack — families don't recover from it. Tobin grows up in a house cut in half. My mother —" His voice caught, and he mastered it. "If I tell them, before the vote, that I release the claim. That I'll see you safely down the mountain myself. Then there's nothing to vote on. Gareth's lever has no weight on it. The pack stays whole."

"And you," Wren said.

"I'll manage."

"You won't, actually." She crossed the shed and stood close to him, close enough that the furnace warmth of him reached her through the cold. "That's the thing you keep getting wrong, Elias, and it's the thing Gareth is counting on. You think this is a story about you giving something up so everyone else is safe. It's not. It's a story about a man so used to being the one who carries the weight that he can't imagine a pack carrying it back."

Elias looked down at her. The lantern light moved on his face.

"You stood in a doorway," Wren said, more quietly, "on the first morning, so I would never feel trapped. You have spent every day since making sure every choice in this is mine. So let me make this one. I am not asking you to choose me over your pack. I'm telling you that's a false choice, and that Gareth built it, and that the way you beat a man who builds false choices is you refuse to stand inside the one he made for you."

"And how," Elias said, rough now, "do we do that."

"We don't go to the vote trying to win me a place." Wren felt the plan settle into her, clean and certain, the way the run had settled into her on the high ridge. "We go to the vote and we put Gareth on trial instead. Pack law cuts both ways. He invoked the old rules — fine. Let's invoke the one about a packmate who comes home and uses the law as a knife. Your mother doesn't approve of where he's been, Elias. I've watched her face every time it comes up. Somebody in this house knows what Gareth was doing down south. We have one day to find out what it was."

For a long moment Elias just looked at her — the human, the broken-down car, the thing that had walked in out of the snow.

Then, slowly, the noble stupid look went out of his eyes, and something far better came into them.

"Maren," he said. "We need to talk to my mother."
