In the days after the diagnosis — and the diagnosis came quickly, with a brisk consultant and a word, "early," that was meant to be a comfort and functioned instead as a starting gun — I found myself doing something strange. I found myself taking an inventory of my mother.

Not of her possessions. Of her. I would catch myself studying her across a room the way you study a coastline from a departing ship, trying to fix the shape of it in memory before the distance takes it. The consultant had been kind and clear. What was coming would not arrive all at once. It would come the way the tide comes in over a long flat beach — not as a wave, but as a slow disappearance of the sand, a thing you only notice by what is no longer there.

So I made my inventory. Here is some of what was on it.

My mother could, and frequently did, identify the exact provenance of any piece of furniture in any room she entered, including other people's rooms, including, memorably, a hotel lobby. My mother had a laugh she used for charm and a different, rarer laugh, low and surprised, that she could not control, and the second laugh was the only sound she made that I have ever fully trusted. My mother had never once told me she was proud of me. My mother had told other people she was proud of me, knowing it would travel back, which she considered, I think, to be the same thing, and which is not the same thing, and the gap between those two is roughly the size of my whole life.

My mother knew how to enter a room. I want to put that on the inventory properly because it is the thing I will miss with the most shameful intensity. My mother could not love simply, or apologise, or sit with another person's pain without tidying it — but she could enter a room. She could pause in a doorway for exactly the right half-second and gather every face toward her without appearing to want them, and it was a kind of art, an art with no museum, an art that would die with her and leave no copy.

I wrote none of this down. The inventory lived in my head, and I added to it in the cracks of the days, while I sat in waiting rooms and read leaflets about powers of attorney, while I telephoned Daniel in Singapore and listened to his exemption restate itself in the careful language of logistics, while I telephoned Frances and managed, as predicted, her reaction to the news rather more than the news.

And underneath the inventory, all that first fortnight, a question sat and would not move. It was not a kind question. It was the question this whole account has been circling, the question I had spent thirty-four years and a great deal of geography avoiding, and the diagnosis had simply walked up and set it on the table between us where it could no longer be ignored.

The question was this. A mother and a daughter run an account between them. The mother makes deposits — years, sacrifices, the raw fact of having kept a small helpless thing alive — and the daughter, the theory goes, repays it across a lifetime, and at the far end, when the mother is failing, the daughter is meant to pay the balance, gladly, because that is what the account was always for.

But my mother had not made ordinary deposits. My mother had given me a beautiful composed childhood that was also a performance I was conscripted into and punished for fluffing. She had given me a key and called it a welcome. She had built me, deliberately, into a daughter who could not let a phone ring out, and now she was going to spend the last years of her life cashing in the very competence she had wounded me to install.

So what did I owe her? Truly. Not what would I do — I already knew what I would do, I would do all of it, I would move into the house I escaped and I would manage the slow going-out of the tide, because the limescale is the kettle and there is no rinsing it. But underneath the doing, what did I actually owe? And if I paid it all, every last unfair pound of it, gladly, with my own one life — would the account, at the end, finally be closed?

Or would I simply have spent myself, completely, settling a debt that was never honestly mine, to a woman who would not, even at the end, even once, be able to say the words that would have made it a debt worth paying?

I did not have the answer. I want to be honest, since honesty is the only thing I have decided this account will be: I still do not entirely have it. But I packed a second, larger bag, and I drove back down the dark motorway to my mother's beautiful house, and I began.
