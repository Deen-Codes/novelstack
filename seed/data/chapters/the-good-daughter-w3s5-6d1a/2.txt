My mother's house is beautiful. I have always hated how beautiful it is, because the beauty is load-bearing — it holds up a version of our family that was never true, and it does the job so well that even I, who lived inside the lie, half believe it when I am standing in the hall.

Vivienne Hartley does not have a home. Vivienne Hartley has a set. Every room is composed. There are fresh flowers in the hall whether or not anyone is coming, because the flowers are not for guests, the flowers are for the house's ongoing performance of itself. There is a particular blue in the drawing room that she chose over the course of, I am not exaggerating, two years. My mother has spent her whole life being the kind of woman other women describe, with a complicated little pause before the word, as "glamorous."

I let myself in with a key I have always had and have always resented having, because a key implies a welcome, and my relationship with this house has never been a welcome. It has been a summons.

She was in the drawing room, in the chair by the window, and for one cowardly second I thought Pauline had inflated after all, because my mother looked exactly like my mother. Upright. Immaculate. A silk scarf at her throat in November, indoors, because Vivienne Hartley has never once in her life dressed for comfort, comfort being, in her view, a small surrender that leads to larger ones.

"Camille," she said. "What a nice surprise. You should have telephoned, I'd have done something about lunch."

It was ten past six in the evening.

"It's evening, Mum," I said, carefully, watching her. "I've come because Pauline called me. She said you weren't well this afternoon. At the supermarket."

And there — there it was. I saw it cross her face like the shadow of a bird across a lawn, quick and then gone. A flicker of something that was not my mother. A blankness. For perhaps a second and a half, Vivienne Hartley, who had an answer for everything, who had spent seventy-one years never once being caught without the right line, did not know what the supermarket was. I watched her not know. I watched her feel herself not knowing, which was worse, infinitely worse, because the not-knowing frightened her and I had not seen my mother frightened since my father's funeral and I had, if I am being as honest as I have promised to be, never seen her frightened of something she could not turn into someone else's fault.

Then the shadow passed. The bird flew on. And my mother's face closed over the gap as smooth as water closing over a stone, and she said, with the exact, polished, slightly cruel lightness she has used on me my entire life:

"Pauline is a silly woman with nothing to occupy her. I was perfectly well. I was simply tired, and I sat in the car for a moment, and the whole street has apparently decided to make a drama of it." She looked at me, and her eyes were sharp again, fully sharp, and she delivered the line she had clearly been saving, the line that was the real reason the gap frightened her, because the line was a door she needed to slam before I could get my foot in it. "I hope you haven't driven all this way to manage me, Camille. You do so love to manage."

And the terrible thing — the thing I sat with later, in the dark of my childhood bedroom, the thing this whole book is really an attempt to understand — the terrible thing is that she was right.

I do love to manage. She made me that way, deliberately, over thirty-four years, because a daughter who manages is a daughter who can be relied upon to clean up after a woman who refuses to. And now the disease coming for her was going to require, more than anything else in the world, a daughter who could manage. She had spent my whole life building the exact tool she was now going to need, and she had built it out of me, and neither of us was going to be able to say so out loud, because saying so out loud was not a thing the women of my family had ever once learned how to do.

"I haven't come to manage you," I lied, gently, the way you lie to someone standing on a ledge. "I've come to stay a few days. That's all. Just a few days, Mum."

It was not going to be a few days. I think we both knew that. But she let me have the lie, and I let her have the supermarket, and we sat in the beautiful composed drawing room as the November dark came down, two women trading the small mercies of dishonesty, which, in my family, has always been the closest thing we have to love.
