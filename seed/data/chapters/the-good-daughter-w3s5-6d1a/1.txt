The phone always rings for me. That is the first thing you need to understand about my family, and possibly the only thing.

There are three of us. My brother Daniel lives in Singapore and has a wife and a job that involves the word "logistics" and a standing, unspoken exemption from family emergencies on the grounds of distance. My sister Frances lives forty minutes away and has a standing, unspoken exemption from family emergencies on the grounds of being fragile, a fragility she has cultivated for so long and so successfully that it now functions, in our family, like a medical condition you are not allowed to question.

And then there is me. Camille. The reliable one. The one the phone rings for.

I did not choose this. I want that on the record before I tell you anything else, because the story I am about to tell could very easily be read as the story of a martyr, and I would rather be almost anything than a martyr. A martyr at least gets the comfort of believing she chose the cross. I never chose anything. The role of reliable daughter is not assigned at a meeting. It accretes. It builds up over decades, one phone call at a time, the way limescale builds up in a kettle, and by the time you notice it the kettle simply is the limescale, and there is no version of the kettle underneath that you could rinse back to.

The phone rang for me on a Thursday in November, at 4:50 in the afternoon, which is a detail I keep, the way you keep the exact details of the moments that turn out to be hinges.

It was my mother's neighbour, Pauline. Pauline had a voice that always sounded faintly thrilled to be the bearer of news, any news, and I had learned over the years to subtract a certain percentage from Pauline for theatrical inflation. But what Pauline said that Thursday did not inflate. It deflated everything around it.

She said my mother had been found, that afternoon, in the supermarket car park, sitting in her car, in the driver's seat, not driving. Just sitting. She had been there, Pauline said, for "a good while" — long enough that a shop assistant had noticed, and then noticed again, and finally gone out to ask if she was all right. And my mother — my mother, who has never in her life been anything other than perfectly, blade-sharp, terrifyingly all right — had looked up at this stranger and said, with what Pauline described as "a kind of pleasantness," that she was waiting for her husband to finish the shopping.

My father has been dead for nine years.

I stood in my kitchen with the phone against my ear and I felt the floor of my life do something. Not drop. It was subtler than dropping. It tilted. It tilted the way a ship tilts, slowly, so that for a moment everything still seems to be in its place, and only the marbles start to roll, and you watch the marbles roll and you understand, before anything has actually fallen, that everything is going to.

"Camille?" Pauline said. "Are you still there?"

"I'm still here," I said. Of course I was still here. I am always still here. That is the entire function I serve.

I did not call Daniel in Singapore. I calculated the time difference, the logistics, the standing exemption, and I did not call him. I did not call Frances, because calling Frances would mean managing Frances's reaction to the news on top of the news itself, and I did not, in that moment, have a spare hand for the management of Frances.

I called no one. I simply picked up my car keys, the way I have picked up my car keys a thousand times before in response to a ringing phone, and I drove to my mother's house, the house I grew up in, the house I spent the entire first half of my adult life carefully, deliberately, expensively escaping.

And somewhere on the motorway, in the dark, with the cats-eyes streaming past, I understood that this phone call was different from all the others. The others had been crises. A crisis has a shape. A crisis has a beginning and a worst part and an end, and the reliable daughter drives over and applies herself to it and the crisis resolves and she drives home.

This was not a crisis. This was a door, and behind the door was the rest of my mother's life and a very large part of what remained of mine, and I was driving toward it at seventy miles an hour because the phone had rung, and the phone always rings for me, and I had never once, in thirty-four years, learned how to let it go to voicemail.
