The sea at Saltmarsh did not so much meet the land as negotiate with it, and the negotiation went on day and night, in all weathers, and the land was slowly losing.

Cole Hadley had grown up forty miles inland and had never trusted any of it — not the flat brown water, not the marsh that was neither sea nor field but some undecided thing between them, not the way the horizon out here was so low and so wide that a man could feel, standing on the sea wall, faintly outnumbered. He had been the detective inspector for this stretch of coast for two years, and in two years he had learned that Saltmarsh had its own arithmetic. The tide took things out. And every few years, on its own schedule, answerable to nobody, it brought something back.

This morning it had brought back a man.

The body lay where the ebb had left it, on the long grey shelf of mud below the wall, and the constable who had called it in — young, local, a boy named Pettifer who had clearly not slept since — stood a careful distance from it with his hands pushed deep in his pockets.

"Tide turned about five," Pettifer said. "Dog walker found him. He's been in a while, sir. You can —" the boy stopped. "You can tell."

You could tell. Hadley had seen enough of what the sea did to a body to read the rough clock of it, and this man had been in the water some days, perhaps a week. Middle-aged. Heavyset. Fully dressed, which was itself a thing worth noting, because the accidental drownings out here, the ones that were genuinely accidents, were mostly summer people who went in light. This man had gone into the cold brown sea in a shirt, and trousers, and a good heavy waxed coat, dark green, the collar still turned up as though against a wind that had long since stopped mattering to him.

Hadley crouched beside the body and did not touch it and looked at the coat for a long time.

"Sir?" said Pettifer.

"This coat," Hadley said. "Photograph it before anything else. Every inch of it. The inside, the lining, the label."

"It's just a coat, sir. Lot of men round here have a coat like that."

"A lot of men round here have a coat like that," Hadley agreed, and stood, his knees complaining, and looked out at the flat brown water that had carried this stranger in and would, if he let it, take the meaning of him back out again on the next ebb. "But not this coat. I know this coat, Pettifer. There's a tear at the right cuff, mended with the wrong colour thread, green thread on a black repair, and I have seen that exact mend before, in a property photograph, in a file."

He made himself say the rest of it quietly, because the boy was young and the morning was cold and there was no profit in frightening him before the day had properly started.

"This coat belonged to Edmund Reach," Hadley said. "Edmund Reach went into this sea fourteen months ago. We found him. We buried him. His widow chose the stone." He turned up his own collar. "So I should very much like to know how a dead man's coat came back to shore fourteen months later — with a different dead man inside it."

The wind came across the marsh, low and steady and smelling of salt and the cold underside of things, and out past the mud the tide had already turned, and was already, patiently, beginning to take the morning back.
