Hadrek did three that morning before the bell, and he did them clean, and he did them in the order the plague-town's reeve had written on the slate — youngest first, because the young ones held on longest and the families could not bear the watching.

It was a trade. He wanted that understood, by anyone, by himself most of all. It was a trade the way ditching was a trade, or tanning, or the burying-work — something the world needed doing and flinched from, so it found a man with a steady hand and a deadened place behind his eyes and it paid him to do it and then it did not invite him to supper. Hadrek had been the man with the steady hand for eleven years. He carried one blade for it. Narrow, short, kept keener than any blade he owned, and he did not use it for anything else, not bread, not rope, because a thing should be only one thing.

The plague did not kill. That was the cruelty the mercy-trade existed to answer. The grey sickness took a body the way rust takes iron — slow, and total, and from the inside — and it did not have the decency to finish. It left people in the last room of themselves for weeks, sometimes for a season, locked in a failing flesh that would not quite stop. So the plague-towns, when the priests had wrung their hands and gone, sent for a man like Hadrek. And the man like Hadrek came, and read the slate, and was honest, and was quick, and the families paid him and hated him and were, in the deepest unspeaking part of them, grateful, and he took the coin and did not stay the night.

The fourth name on the slate was in a house at the edge of the town, near the burnt orchard, and Hadrek knew before he reached the door that something about it was wrong, because there was no smell.

The grey sickness had a smell. Every plague-house in eleven years had carried it, sweetish and low, the smell of a body losing. This house had woodsmoke and old apples and nothing else. He went in anyway, because the slate said go in, and he found a girl of perhaps sixteen sitting up in a clean bed with her hands folded, watching the door, waiting for him.

She had no plague-mark. He looked. He always looked; it was the one piece of honesty the trade truly required, that the man with the blade be sure. The grey sickness greyed the lips and clouded the eye and laid its bruise-coloured map across the throat, and this girl had none of it. Her lips were chapped and her eyes were frightened and clear and her throat was a girl's throat, unmarked, healthy, alive.

"You're the mercy man," she said.

"You're not sick."

"No." She did not look away from him, and he respected that, distantly, the way he respected a steady hand in anyone. "No, I'm not. But I put my own name on the reeve's slate, and I paid the reeve to let it stand, and I have been sitting here three days waiting for you, because you are the only person who comes to this town that nobody dares to follow, and the only one who carries a blade that's allowed to be used. So I need you to do two things, mercy man, and I'll pay you for both, and the first one is —" her folded hands tightened, just slightly — "the first one is to not call out. Not yet. Not until I've told you the second."

Hadrek stood in the doorway with eleven years of deadened quiet behind his eyes and his one honest blade at his hip, and he found, to his own faint surprise, that he had not called out.

"Talk, then," he said. "Quick."

"The grey sickness isn't a sickness." The girl said it flat and fast, a thing rehearsed in three days of waiting. "It doesn't spread like one. It doesn't take who a sickness takes. I've watched it two years, town to town, I've kept count the way nobody else keeps count, and I can tell you exactly who the grey takes and it is never, ever the people you'd expect. It takes the ones who saw something. Every grey-struck body in every town I've followed it through — every one — was someone who, a season before they greyed, said out loud that they had seen a thing they could not explain." Her clear frightened eyes held his. "It's not a plague, mercy man. It's a silence. Something is making people quiet, slow, from the inside, and dressing it as a plague, and sending for men like you to finish the witnesses before the witnesses can finish a sentence. And you have ended forty-one of them. I counted those too."

The house was very quiet. Woodsmoke. Old apples. No sweetish low smell of a body losing, because there was no losing body here, only a girl who had paid a reeve to write her a death she did not have.

"And the second thing," Hadrek said. His voice did not do anything. It had not done anything in eleven years. But he noticed, the way you notice a draft, that his hand had not moved toward the blade. "You said two things. Not calling out was the first."

"The second," said the girl, "is that I saw the thing too. Six days ago. And that means the grey is already coming for me, the slow way, the way it came for the other forty-one — and I would rather, mercy man, I would so much rather, be ended quick and honest by your blade than ended slow and quiet by whatever you have actually been working for." She unfolded her hands at last, and laid them open in her lap, and looked at the man the plague-towns paid and hated and would not invite to supper. "So. I've told you. Now you know what your honest trade has honestly been. And you still have a slate with my name on it, and a blade that's only ever one thing. So choose, and choose now, because I can hear someone on the orchard path, and they are not coming to pay you."
