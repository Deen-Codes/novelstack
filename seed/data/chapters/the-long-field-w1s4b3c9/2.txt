A rational woman, Orla tells herself that night, does not run an experiment once.

She is sitting in the cottage with the slate roof, and she has poured a glass of wine she is not drinking, and she is being, with great effort, a person who measures things. The field took nine minutes. Very well. There are explanations. She walked more slowly than she believed; people do, on flat featureless ground, with no landmarks to pace against — the eye has nothing to push off. The distance is simply greater than it looks; a church tower is a large object and large objects read as nearer than they are. Her own unease, the *attention* she felt, was the ordinary product of a city woman alone in a wide quiet space, nothing more, a nervous system trained on traffic and crowds misfiring in the silence.

All of that is true. All of that is sensible. And all of it would be quite enough to let the matter go, except for one detail that Orla cannot make sit down, and the detail is this: the church tower never got closer, and then she arrived.

That is not how distance works. Distance, real distance, resolves. You walk toward a thing and it grows, stone by stone, window by window, the gradual honest mathematics of approach. The tower had not grown. It had hung at the same size, the same grey patient size, for what felt like the whole crossing — and then, in the last stretch, without her noticing the join, she had simply been at the gate beneath it. As if the field had not been a distance to be covered at all. As if it had been a duration to be served.

Nine minutes. Not nine minutes of walking. Just nine minutes.

She goes back on the Sunday. She tells herself she is going back to disprove the Saturday — to walk it briskly, deliberately, eyes down on her own boots this time, counting her own paces, gathering the hard ordinary data that will dissolve the whole thing. She is a teacher. She will show her working.

She counts her paces. That is the plan, and she keeps to it: she starts the stopwatch at the gate and she walks and she counts, one, two, three, watching her boots come down on the firm green grass, and she keeps her eyes off the tower entirely, because the tower is the trick, the tower is the thing the story wants her looking at.

She counts to four hundred. At her stride, on level ground, four hundred paces is a long way — it is, she knows, comfortably the width of any five-minute field and most of a six. She counts to four hundred and she keeps her eyes down and she does not stop, and she counts to five hundred, and the grass keeps coming up under her boots, firm and green and identical, pace after pace after pace, and she counts to six hundred and her mouth has gone dry.

She looks up.

The church tower is in front of her, grey and patient, exactly as far away as it was at the gate.

And the field — Orla stops walking, and the silence comes in around her stopping like water filling a footprint — the field is not the same field. Not quite. It is the same green, the same flat white sky, the same long emptiness, but the *attention* is here, and it is closer now than it was on Saturday, it is not the diffuse unease of a city woman in a wide space, it is specific, it has direction, it is the precise sensation of standing in a room with someone who is behind you and very still.

There is something at the far hedgerow.

She does not see it move. She would swear afterward — to herself, in the cottage, with the wine still untouched — that she does not see it move. It is simply that her eye, sweeping the far hedge for the gate, snags on a place in the hedge that is darker than hedge, taller than hedge, a vertical absence in the green, and the vertical absence is not part of the hedgerow and it is not a post and it is not a tree, and it is, she understands with a calm that frightens her more than panic would, facing her.

It has been facing her, she thinks, for some time.

Orla stands in the middle of the long field with six hundred paces behind her and the tower no nearer and the thing at the far hedgerow patient and dark, and she has a choice, and she knows she has a choice, and the choice is the whole of the nine-minute story laid bare. She can go on. She can walk the rest of it, walk toward the tower and the gate and, yes, the far hedgerow, and find out what nine minutes in this field actually purchases. Or she can turn around and walk back, and go round by the lane, and never ask again, and one day be the woman in the shop counting some young thing's change twice.

She turns around.

She is not ashamed of it. She will think about that later and decide she is not ashamed of it, because turning around is, in the end, the rational act, and she has spent the whole weekend insisting she is a rational woman.

But here is the thing she carries back to the cottage, the thing that keeps the wine untouched and the lamp on long past midnight. When she turns around, the first gate — the village gate, the gate she started from — is right there. Close. Thirty paces, no more. She has walked six hundred paces into the field and the start of it is thirty paces behind her.

The field had let her walk six hundred paces and had not let her leave the gate at all. It had held her, the way you hold a coin in a closed fist, somewhere in the nine minutes, while she counted and counted and believed she was crossing it.

And the thing at the far hedgerow had simply been waiting to see whether, this time, she would finish the walk.
