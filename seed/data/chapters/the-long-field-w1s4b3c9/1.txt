There is a field behind the village, and it takes nine minutes to cross.

The new schoolteacher learns this on her third day, not because anyone sits her down and tells her, but because of the careful way they don't. She has asked the woman in the shop the simplest possible question — the quick way to the church, for the harvest service — and the woman has gone still over the till, and then said, with a brightness laid flat over something else, that the lane is the way, dear, the lane is always the way, you go round by the lane.

"But the field's right there," the teacher says. She can see it from the shop window. A long ordinary green field, a gate at this end, the squat grey tower of the church at the far. "It can't be five minutes across."

"You'd think," says the woman in the shop. And she gives the teacher her change, and counts it twice, and does not say anything else.

The teacher's name is Orla Brennan and she is twenty-nine and she has come to the village the way people come to villages — running from a city and a man and a version of herself she would like to leave at the last station. She has a cottage with a slate roof and a job teaching eleven children in one room, and she has decided, with the firm bright determination of the recently unhappy, that she is going to find all of it charming.

So she finds the field charming. She finds the not-talking-about-it charming. By the end of the first week she has heard, in pieces, from children and from the man who delivers the coal and from old Sefton who sits outside the pub, the local thing about the long field — that it takes nine minutes to cross, and always has, and that you go round by the lane.

"Nine minutes," Orla says to Sefton, smiling, because it is a small good story and she wants to hold it up to the light. "It's a five-minute field. Six if you dawdle."

"Walk it, then," says Sefton. He does not smile back. He is very old and his eyes are the colour of the field in winter. "Walk it and time it. Everyone does, once. You'll walk it and you'll think, I'll walk it faster, I'll walk it in four. And it'll take you nine. And then you'll go round by the lane like the rest of us, and you'll stop asking, and one day you'll be the one not telling some other young thing why."

Orla laughs, because that is what you do, and Sefton watches her laugh the way the woman in the shop watched her count her change.

She decides to time it properly. She decides this lying awake in the cottage with the slate roof, listening to a countryside silence so total it has a sound, and the deciding feels like the first genuinely her thing she has done since she got off the train — a small rational experiment, a teacher's experiment, a way of being a person who measures things rather than a person things happen to.

She does it on a Saturday, in good light, at two in the afternoon. She wears proper boots. She takes her phone, and she has the stopwatch ready, her thumb on it at the gate, and the field lies in front of her exactly as long and as green and as ordinary as it has looked all week, and the church tower sits at the far end of it, near enough that she can see the individual stones.

Orla starts the stopwatch and walks.

She walks at a good pace, an unhurried but committed pace, the pace of a woman proving a point. The grass is firm. The ground is level. There is nothing in the field at all — no livestock, no crop, no obstacle, just the green going on under a flat white sky — and she keeps her eyes on the church tower and she walks, and it is, she thinks, a perfectly pleasant walk, and she will reach the far gate in five minutes and she will look at the stopwatch and she will have her small rational answer.

The church tower does not seem to be getting closer.

She notices this somewhere around what she assumes is the three-minute mark, and she tells herself it is a known trick of the eye, that a single fixed point at a distance always seems to hang back, that this is exactly the sort of thing the nine-minute story is made of. She does not look at her phone. She has decided not to look at her phone until the gate; the not-looking is part of the experiment, the discipline of it.

She walks. The church tower hangs in front of her, grey and patient. The grass goes on, firm and green. The white sky does not move.

It is a long while before Orla admits to herself that she would very much like to look behind her, to see how far the first gate has fallen back, to anchor herself against the start of the thing rather than its endless end. And it is a longer while before she admits the second thing, the colder thing, which is that she does not entirely want to turn around — that the field has acquired, somewhere in the last few minutes, a quality it did not have at the gate, a quality she can only describe to herself, walking, as *attention*.

When she reaches the far gate at last and stops, breathing harder than the flat walk should have made her breathe, she stands with her hand on the cold top bar and she looks down at her phone, at the stopwatch she has not touched.

Nine minutes. Nine minutes and two seconds.

And the village behind her, when she finally makes herself turn and look back across the long ordinary green, seems very far away indeed — much further than a nine-minute field has any business being.
