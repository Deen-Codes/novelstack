Orla goes to Sefton, because Sefton told her she would.

He is outside the pub, as he always is, in the cold flat light of the Monday, and he watches her come up the village street and he does not look surprised, and that — his lack of surprise — is its own small horror.

"You walked it twice," he says, before she has spoken. "Most only walk it once. You're a stubborn one." He moves along the bench, an invitation. "Sit down before you fall down. You've the look. We all had the look, after."

She sits. Her hands, she notices, are not steady, and she lets them not be steady, because she has run out of the energy required to perform a rational woman for the village.

"What is it," she says. "The field. What is it."

"I don't know." Sefton says it simply, and it is, she realises, the first wholly honest sentence anyone in the village has given her. "Nobody knows. That's the truth of it, and you'll have to make your peace with the truth being so small. It's old. Older than the church, older than the village name. My grandfather went round by the lane and his grandfather did. It takes nine minutes and it always has and there's a thing at the far hedge, and the deal — " he says the word carefully, the way Orla has learned, this past week, that the village says all its careful words " — the deal is that you don't finish the walk."

"That's not a deal. A deal has two sides."

"It does." Sefton looks out at the field, visible at the end of the street, long and green and patient under the white sky. "Our side is we don't cross it. We go round by the lane, every one of us, every time, even when it's raining, even when we're old and the lane's twice the distance and our hips are gone. We give it that. We give it the not-crossing. And in exchange — " his voice does not drop, exactly, but something in it closes a door " — in exchange it stays a field. It stays nine minutes. It doesn't get longer. The thing at the hedge stays at the hedge. We keep our side, and it keeps its side, and the village goes on, and that's been the arrangement for longer than there have been words for it."

Orla sits with that. The cold of the bench is coming up through her.

"And if someone finishes the walk?"

Sefton is quiet for a moment. A long moment. The kind the village specialises in.

"There was a man. Before your time, before most folk's time here now. Came from away, like you. Didn't believe it, like you. And he was prouder than you, I think, or angrier, and he walked it, and he wouldn't turn round at the six hundredth pace the way you did — that was sense, that, that turning round, whatever it cost your pride. He walked it to the far hedge." Sefton's winter-coloured eyes are still on the field. "He came back. I want to be fair and say that. He came back the next morning, walking up this very street, and he was — whole. All his pieces. He went back to teaching the children, same as before."

"Then —"

"It took him nine minutes, Orla." Sefton turns and looks at her directly now and his face is kind, which is the worst of it, his face is genuinely kind. "He went into the field and it took him nine minutes to cross and he came out the other side. But the nine minutes the field gave back to him weren't the same nine minutes the rest of us were standing here living. We had our nine minutes. He had — something else. He'd been somewhere, in the field, in the duration of it, and the somewhere had been long, longer than he had words for, and he never said how long and we never asked, because you could see, you could see in him, that the answer was a number that had broken him to count. He looked the same. He taught the children for thirty more years. But he never once, in thirty years, walked anywhere quickly again. Couldn't bear it. Couldn't bear the idea of arriving."

The field lies at the end of the street. Orla looks at it. Nine minutes. A long ordinary green field, a gate at each end, the church tower at the far.

"Why are you telling me," she says. "All of you. Why the not-saying for a week and then this. You could have just let me go round by the lane and never know."

"Because you walked it twice." Sefton stands, slowly, his old hips telling on him, and he looks down at her on the bench. "Once is a fright and a story. Twice is a person the field has noticed. It noticed you, Orla, at the six hundredth pace, when the thing at the hedge looked at you and you looked back. And a person it's noticed has to be told, properly, so they understand the size of the choice — because the field will offer it to you again. It always offers it again, to the ones it's noticed. Some quiet afternoon you'll be standing at that gate, not even meaning to, and the church tower will look so close, and the lane will seem so far, and you'll think, just this once, I'll just see."

He picks up his stick.

"Go round by the lane," he says. "That's all. That's the whole of the wisdom of this village and it took us a thousand years to learn it and it fits in five words. Go round by the lane." And he walks away up the street, slow and certain, an old man who has kept his side of an arrangement his whole life — and Orla sits on the cold bench and looks at the long field, and the field looks back, patient, nine minutes wide, and somewhere along the far hedgerow a vertical darkness stands exactly where the hedge should be, and waits, the way it has always waited, for her to decide she is a rational woman who would simply like to see.
