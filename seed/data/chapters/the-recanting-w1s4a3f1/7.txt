The man's name was Lawrence Keel, and the calm on the telephone, when it came again, was the calm of a man who had decided how the conversation would end before it began.

"You've been busy, Dana." He used her first name like a hand on her shoulder. "Vane is an old man frightened of his own obituary. Pruitt's girl is grieving and unreliable. And a forgotten jacket proves only that some other person was once near a car park. None of it is a story. You understand that. None of it is a story until a witness stands up and makes it one. Stories need a mouth."

"You need me to lie," Dana said. "Still. After everything. You need me to get up in fifteen days and be certain again."

"I need you to do nothing at all. That's the beauty of your position, and I'd like you to appreciate it." His voice did not rise; it never rose. "You don't have to lie. You only have to not recant. You simply confirm your original statement — which is, after all, a true account of what you told the police nineteen years ago — and you decline to speculate beyond it. No perjury. No risk. You merely fail to become helpful in a new direction. Inertia, Dana. The whole world runs on it. I am only asking you to run on it too."

"And if I recant."

A pause. She had learned, by now, to read his pauses, and this one was not hesitation. It was him giving her time to be afraid.

"Then you will have made yourself a problem," he said, "and I have spent thirty years in a line of work that is, at bottom, the management of problems. I would not enjoy it. I want you to believe that. There is no appetite in me for any of this. But I have built a life, Dana, a real one, with people in it I am responsible for, and I built it on the simple fact that nobody can prove what they cannot remember and cannot say. You are the last person alive who can say. I would much rather you chose silence. I am asking you, calmly, to choose silence, because the alternative is a great deal of grief for a man — Calvin Roe — whom you have never even met, and who is, when all is said, a stranger to you."

Dana stood at her window and looked at the porcelain wren, facing out into the dark.

"He wrote to me," she said. "Calvin Roe. He wrote and told me that after the hearing it stops being a decision and becomes only a regret. He's a stranger, you're right. But he's a stranger who's spent nineteen years in a cell that you built and I furnished. So I've met him, Mr. Keel. I've met him every single day for nineteen years. I just didn't have the courage to look at him until now."

"That's sentiment," Keel said. "Sentiment fades by morning. You'll wake up and be a person who wants to keep her quiet life, and you'll do the easy, true, defensible thing, and in a year you'll have stopped thinking about it." He let the line breathe. "Go to bed, Dana. Think about your house. Think about Maureen Pruitt's house, and how easy houses are. And in the morning, be sensible."

The line went dead.

Dana did not go to bed. She sat down at her table with the clean sheet of paper, and the transcript, and Calvin Roe's small neat letter, and she did the only thing she had left, which was to think it through to the end like an adult and not a frightened girl.

Keel was right about one thing, and only one. Her recantation, alone, was thin. *I am no longer certain* was not a story. A jury — a hearing — could be told that an aging witness had simply lost her nerve, been got at by the defence, gone soft with guilt. Her word against the weight of a nineteen-year-old conviction. Vane would help; Vane's confession would help enormously; but Vane was a frightened old man protecting his obituary, and Keel would say so, and it would not be entirely untrue.

What she needed was not to be believed. What she needed was to make herself a worse problem to silence than to let speak. She needed the thing Keel had been most careful never to do on the telephone, not once, in any call.

She needed him to threaten her where it could be heard.

Dana looked at the wren for a long time. Then she picked up her phone, and she did not dial Keel, and she did not dial Vane. She dialled the prosecutor's office, the new young one running the review, and when the duty line answered she said, very steadily, that her name was Dana Holt, that she was an original witness in State versus Roe, that she intended to recant her testimony fully at the hearing — and that in the meantime she had been receiving calls, and a delivery, from a man who did not want her to, and she would like all of it placed on the record now, tonight, dated and logged, so that whatever happened to her next would already have a name attached to it.

It was, she realised as she said it, the first genuinely brave thing she had done in nineteen years. It was also a trap, and she had just stepped into the middle of it on purpose, and pulled it shut, with herself inside as the bait.
