The young prosecutor running the review was called Aisha Bell, and she was thirty-four and had inherited the Carrow case the way you inherit a damp problem in a house you've just bought, and when Dana finished talking Aisha Bell was quiet for exactly three seconds and then became, very fast, extremely useful.

They met the next morning, not at the office but in a room above it, and Aisha Bell listened to the whole of it — the transcript, Pruitt, Vane, the jacket, the bird, the calls — and she did not once tell Dana to be careful or to leave it to the professionals. She said, "He's never threatened you in plain words on a recorded line. Has he."

"No. Never. He's far too calm for that."

"Then he's disciplined, and disciplined men have one weakness, which is that they're certain their discipline will hold." Aisha Bell tapped her pen on the table, and Dana thought of another pen, nineteen years ago, resting beneath a photograph, and felt the strange vertigo of the past folding onto the present. "Mr. Keel believes you'll be sensible because that's what the calm always believe — that everyone, given time, becomes as cold as they are. He left you a porcelain bird because intimidation that can't be proven is the only kind he permits himself. So we're going to give him a reason to break his own rule."

The plan was not complicated. The best ones never were; Dana had learned that the hard way, from a man whose plan had been simply *get two frightened people to point.*

She would call Keel. One last time. She would tell him she had thought about her quiet house and Maureen's easy house and that she was, after all, going to be sensible — and she would ask him, the way a frightened woman bargaining for her life would ask, to promise her that nothing would happen to her or to Maureen if she stayed silent. She would make him say it. She would push, and waver, and push again, the way she had once been so good at performing certainty, except now she would perform fear, which was easier, because most of it would be real.

And Keel, if he was the man Vane had described — calm, careful, thirty years deep in the management of problems — would do one of two things. He would refuse to say anything incriminating, in which case they had lost nothing. Or he would do the thing such men did when they believed they had finally won, when the problem had folded and become inertia again: he would relax, just slightly, into the pleasure of being obeyed, and he would make the promise specific. He would tell her exactly what would not happen to her, which meant naming exactly what could.

It took Dana most of the afternoon to be ready. Aisha Bell sat across from her, and a quiet technician sat in the corner, and the porcelain wren stood on the table between them all because Dana had insisted on bringing it, and at four o'clock she dialled.

Keel answered on the third ring. "Dana." Warm. Pleased, almost. He had heard the time of day and the day of the week and concluded, correctly, that this was the call of a woman who had slept on it.

"I've been thinking," Dana said, and let her voice do what frightened voices do. "About the house. About Maureen. I don't — Mr. Keel, I'm not a brave person. I want you to know that. I built a small life because I'm not brave, and I don't want to lose it, and I don't want anyone hurt because of me. I'll do what you said. I'll confirm the old statement and I won't speculate. I'll be inertia." She made herself stop, made herself breathe audibly. "But I need to hear you promise me. I need you to actually tell me that if I stay silent, nothing happens to me, and nothing happens to Maureen Pruitt. I can't do this on faith. I need you to say it."

The pause this time was different. It was a man savouring a thing.

"There," Keel said softly. "There it is. The sensible Dana. I knew she was in there." And then, because he had won, because the problem had folded, because thirty years of discipline had been built on the certainty that everyone eventually turned as cold as he was and here was the proof of it warm in his ear — Lawrence Keel relaxed, and made his mistake.

"You have my word," he said. "Stay silent, give the old statement, walk out of that hearing, and I promise you nothing happens. Not to you, not to the Pruitt woman. Recant, and I promise you the opposite, just as clearly — I will have to treat both of you as problems, and I am very good at problems, and neither of you would enjoy how good. But that's the door you won't open, because you're sensible. So. We understand each other, and you can put the bird in a drawer and forget all of it, and be at peace."

Dana looked across the table at Aisha Bell, and Aisha Bell was already nodding, slowly, her eyes closed, the way you nod at the end of something.

"Yes," Dana said into the phone, and for the first time in nineteen years there was no performance in her voice at all, nothing rehearsed, nothing dressed for anyone. "Yes, Mr. Keel. I think we finally understand each other."

She ended the call and set the phone down beside the porcelain wren, and the room was very quiet, and the recording light on the small device in the corner was still steady and red, and Lawrence Keel's calm, careful, thirty-year discipline was sitting on a hard drive with his voice attached, promising harm in plain words to a woman who had only ever pretended to be cornered.
