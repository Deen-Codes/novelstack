Maureen Pruitt lived in a terraced house with a hanging basket that had given up for the season, and when she opened the door she looked at Dana with the wary patience of a woman who had recently been to a great many official appointments.

"I'm not buying anything," she said, "and I've found Jesus already, so."

"My name is Dana Holt." She had practised this in the car and it still came out wrong. "I gave evidence at the same trial as your father. The Carrow case. Nineteen years ago."

The patience went out of Maureen's face and something more complicated came into it. She did not invite Dana in. She did not close the door either. She stood holding it, half a barricade, and said, "He talked about that. Right at the end. When the morphine had him."

"What did he say?"

"You'd better come through," Maureen said, in the voice of a woman doing something against her own advice.

The front room was warm and crowded with the small ornaments of a long life, and Maureen did not sit, so Dana didn't either. They stood among the porcelain birds and the framed photographs like two people waiting for a bus.

"My dad was a good man," Maureen said. "I want that said first. He was a good man and he never did a dishonest thing in his life that I knew of, and it ate him. Whatever it was. It ate him for twenty years and I never knew the name of it until the end."

"He said the trial."

"He said a name. Vane. He kept saying it. And he said —" Maureen stopped, and folded her arms, and Dana understood that the woman had been carrying this for eleven weeks and had nobody safe to set it down with. "He said, *I told it the way the man wanted it told.* Over and over. *I told it the way the man wanted.* And he'd cry, and he wasn't a man who cried."

The room was very warm. Dana made herself breathe.

"Did he say what he actually saw? The night of it?"

"He said he didn't see a face." Maureen's voice had dropped, as if the porcelain birds might carry tales. "He said he saw a man go past him and he never got the face, it was dark and it was quick, and he told that to the police plain as anything the first time. And then a man came to see him. Not in uniform. And after that my dad knew the face very well indeed, well enough to point at it in a courtroom, and he never had a peaceful night again as long as I knew him."

Dana sat down then, because the chair was there and her body needed it. A porcelain wren watched her from the mantel.

"Vane was the prosecutor," she said. "He coached me too. But he never — it wasn't like that. He never told me what to say. He just made certainty feel like the helpful thing."

Maureen looked at her for a long moment. "My dad didn't say the man who came to see him was the prosecutor," she said. "I assumed it, all these years, the way you'd assume. But he never said it. He just said *the man.* And right at the very end, the last clear thing he said to me, he said: *the man wasn't from the court at all. That's what I should have told them. The man was never from the court.*"

Outside, a car went past, slow, and Dana's whole body turned toward the sound before she could stop it.

She thought of the voice on the telephone. *Someone who'd like the old story to stay the old story.* She had assumed — the way you'd assume — that it was the State. That the prosecutor's office, or someone in it, was protecting an old conviction, a closed file, a clean record. That made a kind of sense. Institutions protected themselves; everyone knew that.

But Gerald Pruitt, dying, had wanted his daughter to know one thing above all others. The man was never from the court.

If the man who had taught two frightened witnesses to see Calvin Roe's face was not the prosecutor, was not the police, was not the machine of the State doing the ordinary corrupt thing that machines did —

Then he was someone who had needed Calvin Roe convicted. Someone for whom an innocent man in prison was not an error to be tidied away but a result. A roof. A door, closed and locked, with the real thing safe behind it.

"Maureen," Dana said carefully, "did your father ever say what the man looked like?"

"No." Maureen unfolded her arms at last, and sat, heavily, across from Dana, and the two of them were just two tired women in a warm room. "But he said one thing about him. He said the man was always very calm. He said in twenty years the thing he couldn't forget was how calm the man was, like none of it cost him anything. Like it was just admin." She looked at the cold hearth. "My dad said a guilty man would have been frightened. It was the calm that told him the man wasn't guilty of the killing at all. He was guilty of something he thought was tidier."

Dana drove home in the dark with the heater on full and still could not get warm. The hearing was sixteen days away. And the question had changed shape entirely, and grown much larger, and become much worse.

It was no longer: did Calvin Roe kill Susan Carrow.

It was: who needed everyone to be certain that he had.
