Theodore Vane was seventy-one now and had retired from the bench, because the prosecutor who had built his career on the Carrow conviction had later become a judge, the way the useful ones did. Dana found him in a coffee house near the cathedral, every Thursday, alone with a broadsheet, a fact she learned from his old chambers in the manner of a woman who had finally stopped being polite about her own life.

He recognised her. That was the first surprise. He looked up from the broadsheet and across nineteen years and said, "Ms. Holt," and folded the paper, and gestured at the chair opposite as though they had an appointment.

"You knew I'd come," she said.

"I hoped you wouldn't. But the jacket has been in the paper, and a man in my former position learns to tell the difference between what he hopes and what he should expect." His voice was exactly as she remembered it, low and unhurried, and for one sick moment she thought, *it's him, the telephone, it was him all along* — and then she made herself listen properly, and it was not. The cadence was wrong. Vane's calm was the calm of authority, of a man used to rooms arranging themselves around him. The calm on the telephone had been something else. The calm of a man who had nothing to lose because he had already decided, long ago, that nothing was worth losing.

"I'm going to ask you a question," Dana said, "and I'd like you to understand that I already know most of the answer, so a lie will only tell me you're still in it."

Vane inclined his head. An old courtroom courtesy. *Proceed.*

"You coached me. You coached Gerald Pruitt. You taught two people to be certain of a face. I've spent nineteen years believing that was you protecting a conviction — ambition, a clean record, the ordinary rot. But Gerald told his daughter the man who came to him wasn't from the court. So either you're lying to me now, or someone used you, and I want to know which, because I have sixteen days and a hearing and a porcelain bird on my windowsill, and I'm finished being a useful object for men who are calmer than me."

For a long moment Theodore Vane said nothing. The coffee house carried on around them, the hiss of the machine, somebody's laughter.

"I will tell you something," he said at last, "that I have never told anyone, and I will tell you because I am seventy-one and the jacket means the truth is coming whether I assist it or not, and a man would rather be the one who carried it than the one who was dragged behind it." He turned his coffee cup a precise quarter-turn. "I believed Calvin Roe was guilty. I want that on the record between us, even if the record is only this table. I genuinely believed it. The case came to me as a strong case. Two witnesses. A man with a history. I did not invent the certainty, Ms. Holt. I was given it."

"By whom."

"By a colleague." He said the word with great care. "A man who was, at that time, an investigator attached to the case. He brought me the witnesses already — shall we say — oriented. He brought me Pruitt's certainty and yours, packaged, complete. And I, God forgive me, I was forty and ambitious and the package was convenient, and I asked the questions a careful prosecutor asks but I asked them of a structure I had not built and did not look beneath. I rehearsed you. I made your certainty presentable for a jury. But I did not create it. I received it." He looked at her directly. "And I knew, the way you know a thing you have agreed not to know, that the man who handed it to me was not handing me the truth. He was handing me a result. And I took it, because it was convenient, and a conviction is a conviction, and I told myself the system would catch any error later."

"It didn't."

"No. It took nineteen years and a forgotten jacket and a dead porter and you." Vane's hands were not quite steady on the cup. "The system does not catch errors, Ms. Holt. It rests on them, the way a building rests on a wall. People catch errors. Occasionally. At great cost. Usually too late."

Dana's mouth was dry. "I need his name."

And Theodore Vane, who had built a career and a judgeship and a comfortable retirement on a foundation he had always known was rotten, looked across the table at the woman whose young certainty he had dressed for a jury, and he gave her the name, and the name landed in Dana's memory and the wall came down all at once, completely, the way a wall does when the last thing holding it has finally let go.

She knew the face. She had always known the face. He had been in the prosecutor's office on all three afternoons, a quiet man at the edge of the room, and she had registered him the way you register furniture, and her certainty about Calvin Roe had been built in a room with that man standing in the corner of it, watching it set.

"He's not retired," Vane said quietly. "He's not in any office you could write to. He left the work a long time ago and went into the sort of business where calmness is an asset and questions are discouraged. He has done very well. And Ms. Holt — he will already know you have been to see Maureen Pruitt, and he will know, within the day, that you have been to see me. I am sorry for that. I should perhaps not have let you sit down."

"He's known for weeks," Dana said. "He left a bird on my step." She stood up, and found that her legs held her, and that the fear was there but it was underneath the anger now, where it could be managed. "Thank you. I mean that. You've just done the first honest thing in this entire case, and you did it nineteen years too late, and I'll still need you to do it again, in fifteen days, in front of a court, where it costs you something."

Vane looked up at her. The old authority was gone from his face. What was left looked almost like relief.

"Yes," he said. "I rather think I will."
