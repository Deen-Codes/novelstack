The new evidence had a name, and the name was in the local paper four days later, because the prosecutor's office had decided to get ahead of the story.

DNA. A jacket. The county had finally tested an item of clothing recovered near the scene in the original investigation and never analysed, because in those days you did not test what you did not need to test, and you did not need to test anything once you had two witnesses who could see a face. The jacket had been in an evidence locker for nineteen years. The jacket did not belong to Calvin Roe.

The paper was careful. The paper said the review was ongoing and no conclusions had been reached. But Dana read between the lines the way you learn to read a thing that concerns you, and what the lines said was: there is physical evidence pointing somewhere else, and the only thing standing between Calvin Roe and an open door is the testimony of two eyewitnesses, and one of those witnesses is dead.

She was the door now. She was the whole door.

That evening she did something she had been circling for days. She wrote a letter to Calvin Roe.

It took her four hours and eleven attempts, and the version she finally sealed was short and said almost nothing, because everything she wanted to say was either a confession or a question she had no right to ask. *I was a witness at your trial. I have been thinking about that night. I would like, if you are willing, to write to you. I am not sure what I have to offer you except that I am no longer certain of things I once said I was certain of.* She read it back and it seemed both inadequate and dangerous, and she posted it before she could change her mind, walking to the box at the end of the road at ten at night so that the act would feel decided.

Three days later, two things arrived.

The first was the reply from Calvin Roe, and it was not what she had braced for. She had braced for rage, and she would have deserved it. Instead the letter was small and very neat, the handwriting careful in the way of a man who has had a great deal of time to practise being patient, and it said: *I knew there were two of you who never really saw me, because I know I was never there, and a man works out, over enough years, that the people who were certain of him must have been given their certainty by someone. I have wondered for nineteen years who that someone was. If you have begun to wonder the same thing, then you and I are finally, after all this time, looking at the same question. I am not angry with you, Ms. Holt. I stopped being able to afford anger a long time ago. But I would ask you one thing. Whatever you decide to do, decide it before the hearing. After the hearing it stops being a decision and becomes only a regret.*

Dana sat with that letter for a long time.

The second thing that arrived came that night, and it did not come in an envelope. She heard the gate, and then nothing, and when she opened the door in the morning there was an object on the step, placed there with the same unhurried care she had heard in the voice on the telephone.

It was a porcelain wren.

It was the wren from Maureen Pruitt's mantelpiece. Dana knew it the instant she saw it; she had spent an hour looking at it across a warm crowded room. Someone had been in Maureen's house, or had taken it from her, and had carried it across the city, and had set it down on Dana's step where the morning would find it, and the message did not need words. The message was: *I know where you have been. I know who you have spoken to. I can reach the things you care about and the things other people care about, and I am very calm, and none of this costs me anything.*

Dana phoned Maureen with her heart going hard, and Maureen answered on the second ring, alive and irritated and entirely fine, and said no, nothing was missing, what an odd thing to ask, and Dana stood in her hallway and understood that the wren on her step was not Maureen's wren at all.

It was a copy. He had found one like it. He had wanted Dana to spend ten minutes in pure cold terror believing he had been inside that house, and he had wanted her to understand, once the terror passed, exactly how easily he could arrange the next one to be real.

She did not throw the wren away. She set it on her own windowsill, facing out, and she looked at it for a while, and then she did the thing the calm man had not accounted for.

She got angry, properly, all the way down. Not the dense compressed anger of the last weeks. The clean kind. The kind that decides things.

She took out a clean sheet of paper and began, methodically, to write down everything she actually remembered about the three afternoons in the prosecutor's office — every face that had been in that building, every name she had heard, every man who had stood at the edge of a room being calm. Because the calm man had made one mistake, and Dana had just seen it.

He had been there. Nineteen years ago, to teach two witnesses their certainty, he had had to be in the room. Which meant that somewhere in the concrete of her memory, underneath the testimony, behind the wall — Dana had seen his face too.
