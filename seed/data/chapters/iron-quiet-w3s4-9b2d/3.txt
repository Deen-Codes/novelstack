Silas Crane let her in. That was the first thing, and Hanne held onto it: he could have rung the small quick bell and had her taken on the step, and he did not, he stood aside and let her carry the humming child into the warden-house, and that meant something, even if neither of them yet knew what.

The warden-house was a low stone room with a stove and a long table and, against the far wall, the thing the children of Mercy were taught never to look at directly — the listening-board, a panel of dark glass and dull metal where the posts on the ridges reported what they heard. Hanne had never been this close to it. It was smaller than the dread of it. That, she would think later, was true of almost everything.

"Sit," Crane said. "Hanne. Sit down. The board has already logged it. You understand that. There is a sound on the record between the dusk bell and the dawn bell, with a bearing on your kitchen, and the record goes up the line at noon. I cannot un-log it. I do not have that — there is no warden alive who has that."

"I know," Hanne said. She sat. She kept the baby against her. "I did not come to ask you to make it not have happened. I came so that the person sitting in this room when you decide what it means is me, and not a closed door, and not my sister, who carried this child and has not slept and does not yet know. Whatever this costs, Silas, I want it to land on me. I am asking you to let me be the one it lands on."

Crane sat down across the table from her. He was quiet a long moment, and the baby hummed into the quiet, low and even, and Hanne watched the warden listen to it — really listen, the way you listen to a thing instead of for it.

"Forty years," he said at last, "I have sat at that board and logged sounds. Do you know what they are, mostly? They are not rebellion. They are not signaling. In forty years I have logged a man crying out in a nightmare. A woman in labor who could not stay silent and God forgive us all that we asked her to. A boy of nine who slammed a door because his father had died that day and he was nine and he forgot. That is what the board catches, Hanne. Not enemies. Just people, failing to be silent, in the ordinary human ways." He looked at the bundle in her arms. "And now a baby three weeks old who hums because humming is what she is. That is the sound that goes up the line at noon."

"Then what happens," Hanne said, "when it goes up the line."

Crane did not answer straight away, and the not-answering was its own answer, and Hanne made herself hold still inside it.

"What happens," he said slowly, "is what has always happened, and what I have spent forty years telling myself was not my doing because the board is only a board and the line is only a line. They send for the source of the sound. They take it somewhere quieter. And the township is told that Mercy has been kept safe, and the township, which is very good at it, says nothing." He put his hands flat on the table. "That is the machine, Hanne. Not the listening-posts. The machine is the board, and the line, and the noon report, and forty years of a township agreeing not to see the wagon leave. I am a part of the machine. I have always been a part of the machine. And you have walked into my house and made me look at the part I play, with a humming baby in your arms, and I find that I cannot — "

He stopped. Outside, the township was waking; Hanne could hear, faintly, the small permitted sounds of a Mercy morning, doors and kettles and low voices, the daylight life that the night took back every dusk.

"There is a thing I have never done," Crane said, very low, as though even now, in his own house, in the daylight, he was afraid of being heard. "In forty years. The board logs the sound. But the noon report — the noon report is written. By me. By hand. The line receives what I write. And I have never, not once, written anything but the truth, because I told myself the truth was not mine to shape." He looked at her, and his tired face had something in it now that was almost terror and almost hope. "You said whatever this costs, you want it to land on you. Hanne. Listen to me carefully. If I write that report differently — if I write that the posts caught a fault, an animal, a wind in the eaves, anything but a child — then it does not land on you. It lands on me. It lands on me the way nothing has landed on a warden of Mercy in forty years. And I am asking you, because I have not got the courage to decide it alone any more than you have: is that a thing we do?"

The baby hummed, low and even and content, into the warden-house, into the morning, into the small dark glass of the listening-board that caught everything and judged nothing and only ever passed it up the line.

And Hanne Weir, who had no children of her own by a choice the township had never forgiven, looked down at her sister's unnamed daughter, and then up at the warden, and felt the whole iron quiet of her whole life come, at last, to the single point where a person stops being silent.

"Yes," she said. "It is. Get the paper, Silas. We have until noon, and we are going to write it together, and then we are going to find out what kind of township we have all been keeping all this time."
