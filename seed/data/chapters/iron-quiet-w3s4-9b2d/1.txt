The bell for the Listening Hours rang at dusk, the way it had rung every dusk of Hanne Weir's life, and the township of Mercy went still the way a held breath goes still.

She was forty-two years old. She had never, not once, heard a sound made between the dusk bell and the dawn bell. That was not a figure of speech. It was the plain shape of the world. From the moment the bell stopped ringing until the moment it rang again, the people of Mercy did not speak, did not sing, did not let a door slam or a kettle sing or a child cry. They padded the floors with felt. They oiled the hinges. They taught the babies, before they taught them anything else, before walking, before names, the one lesson that mattered: that the night was when the machines listened, and a township that wished to keep its mercy must give the machines nothing to hear.

Hanne moved through her kitchen in the grey light and did the evening's work with hands that had done it ten thousand times. She set the felt slippers by the door. She wedged the soft cloth into the window-frame where the wind liked to whistle. She checked the baby.

The baby was new. Three weeks old, and not Hanne's — Hanne had no children of her own, by a choice the township had never quite forgiven her, and that was a different and older quiet she carried. This baby was her sister Del's, and Hanne kept it on the nights Del worked the drying-sheds, because that was what the women of Mercy did, they passed the children between them through the long careful nights like a lamp that must not be allowed to go out.

The baby was a girl. They had not named her yet. In Mercy you did not name a child until its first month was safely done, until you were sure the night had decided to let you keep it.

Hanne stood over the cradle in the last of the light and watched the unnamed girl sleep, and she felt the old useless ache she always felt over a sleeping child, the want she had said no to and could not, on quiet nights, entirely silence in herself.

Then the dawn was hours off, and the dusk bell's echo had long since died, and the township of Mercy lay under the Listening Hours like snow.

And the baby began to hum.

It was not loud. Hanne would say that, afterward, to the wardens, again and again, as though the not-loudness could be a defense — it was not loud, it was the smallest sound, you could have stood in the lane and not heard it. But she was standing over the cradle, and she heard it, and her whole body went to ice.

It was a low, even, tuneless hum. The sound a person makes who is content and does not know they are making it. It came from the baby's chest, steady, unbroken, and it did not stop when Hanne lifted the child, and it did not stop when she held the small warm weight against her shoulder, and it did not stop when she pressed her own trembling hand flat and gentle over the baby's mouth, which she knew even as she did it was a thing you must never do, and which she did anyway, in the dark, because every instinct she had been raised with was screaming the same single word.

Silence. Silence. The machines are listening. Silence.

The baby hummed on, into her palm, untroubled, the way water runs.

Hanne stood in the black kitchen with her sister's unnamed daughter humming against her hand, and she understood three things at once, the way you understand things when the floor of your life tilts.

She understood that this child could not stop. That the hum was not mischief, not a habit to be trained out the way you trained out a door-slam. It was simply what the child was.

She understood that by the laws of Mercy, a sound made in the Listening Hours was the gravest thing a township could produce, and that the wardens did not ask whether a sound had been made on purpose.

And she understood, with a cold that went all the way down, that the nearest listening-post stood on the ridge above the drying-sheds, where her sister Del was working the night, and that there was no felt thick enough, no cloth wedged tight enough, no hand pressed gentle enough, to keep the machines on that ridge from hearing what was happening in this kitchen, hour after hour, until the dawn bell rang.
