Hanne did not sleep. She sat the whole of that first night in the corner of the kitchen furthest from the window, the baby in her lap, the baby humming, and she did the only thing she could think to do, which was to wrap the child in everything soft the house contained and hold the bundle close against her own body, as though her own ribs and the township's blankets and the dark could between them swallow a sound the size of a held note.

She knew it would not be enough. She knew it the way you know the weather is turning. But a person does what is in front of them to do, and what was in front of Hanne was a humming child and a hundred soft things, so she used the soft things, and she waited for the dawn, and she listened past the hum for the other sound, the sound she had heard only twice before in her life and had prayed never to hear again.

It came an hour before dawn. The wardens' bell.

Not the township bell — the township bell rang the Hours, dusk and dawn, slow and known. The wardens' bell was different. It was small and quick and it rang from the warden-house, and it meant that the listening-posts had heard something in the night, and that when the dawn bell freed the town to speak, the wardens would already be walking the lanes, following the sound back to its house.

Hanne sat with the bell's small quick voice in her ears and felt something in her go very calm and very clear, the way a person goes calm when the worst thing has stopped being a fear and become only a fact to be managed.

She had until the dawn bell. That was the shape of it. She had until the dawn bell to decide what she was going to say, and to whom, and she was not going to spend that time being afraid, because fear was a luxury and she had a baby to keep.

She thought about her sister. Del would be coming off the night-shift at the drying-sheds, walking home in the grey, not knowing. Del who had carried this child nine months and bled to bring her and had not yet been allowed to name her. The wardens would go to Del's house first; the child was registered to Del's house. Hanne thought about her sister opening her own door to the wardens after a night's work, with no warning, no time, nothing in her hands.

So when the dawn bell finally rang, and the township of Mercy let out its long-held breath and began, cautiously, to make the small permitted sounds of morning, Hanne Weir did not wait in her kitchen for the wardens to find her.

She wrapped the baby — still humming, the hum softer now in the daylight but unbroken, unbroken, it had not stopped once — and she put on her own felt slippers out of a habit too deep to break even now, and she walked out into the lane and turned, not toward her sister's house, but toward the warden-house on the rise, because if a thing was going to be decided about this child she would be the one standing in the room when it was decided, and not her exhausted sister, and not a closed door.

The lanes of Mercy were waking around her. She passed old Toomes sweeping his step, who saw the bundle and the look on her face and went still. She passed the Aldous girls, who heard the hum — she watched them hear it, watched their faces change — and who said nothing, because the people of Mercy had four decades of practice at saying nothing, and that practice, Hanne thought, cut both ways. It could be a wall to hide behind. It could also be the silence in which a township let a thing be carried off and told itself it had not seen.

The warden-house door was open. Warden Silas Crane stood in it, as though he had been waiting, and perhaps he had; the listening-posts reported to him before they reported to anyone.

He looked at Hanne. He looked at the bundle. He listened, and his face — Hanne had known Silas Crane her whole life, had sat behind him in the schoolroom, and she watched his face do something complicated and tired.

"It was your kitchen," he said. Not a question. The posts gave him bearings; the bearings gave him a house.

"It was," Hanne said. "And it is not what you think, and before you ring anything or write anything or send for anyone, you are going to stand here and listen to the whole of it, Silas, because you have known me forty years and you owe me that much, and because what you decide in the next hour you are going to have to carry for the rest of your life."

And the baby hummed on, low and even and content, into the morning, into the open door of the warden-house, where the machines, Hanne knew, were also always listening.
