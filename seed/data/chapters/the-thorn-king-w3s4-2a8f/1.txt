The old king of Calder died screaming, and they sent for his daughter the same night, and the rider who carried the summons did not slow his horse once across two hundred miles, because a crown that has just killed its wearer does not like to be kept waiting.

Sera Vane heard the news the way exiles hear all news of home — late, and from a stranger, and with the particular cold understanding that whatever had happened, it had happened to a country that no longer thought of her as its own.

She was in the border town of Marrow when the rider found her, working the only trade an unwanted royal daughter could carry into exile, which was the trade of being useful and asking nothing. She mended things. Tack, mostly, and tools, and once a week the broken pride of men who came to drink. She had been four years gone from Calder and she had built, out of those four years, a life so small and so quiet that some mornings she could almost forget the size of the thing she had been thrown out of.

The rider wore the black-and-iron of the royal house, and his horse was nearly dead under him, and he knelt in the mud of Marrow's only street to deliver his message, which told Sera more than the message itself. You did not kneel to a mender of tack. You knelt to a thing the crown had decided to want again.

"Your father the king is dead," the rider said. "The court of Calder summons the king's blood home. All of it. You are the last of it."

Sera stood in the doorway of the work-shed with a half-mended bridle in her hands and felt the small quiet life she had built shiver, the way still water shivers before something large surfaces under it.

"He disowned me," she said. "Four years ago. Before the whole court. There are witnesses, there is a sealed decree, I am not the king's blood, the king made certain of it. You have ridden two hundred miles to the wrong woman."

"The decree is void," the rider said, "because the man who sealed it is dead, and a dead king's decrees of disinheritance die with him — that is the old law of Calder, older than your father, older than the crown itself. You are the king's blood again. You were the king's blood again the moment he stopped breathing." He was still kneeling. He had not looked up. "And there is no one else. The fever took your brothers two winters past. You are the last living person of the line that the crown of Calder will consent to sit upon a head. The court does not summon you because it loves you, lady. It summons you because the crown will not be worn by anyone else, and a crown that goes unworn in Calder is not an absence. It is a wound. And the country is already bleeding."

Sera knew, then, what had killed her father, because every child of Calder was raised on the knowing of it, the way other children were raised on prayers.

The crown of Calder was not a circle of gold. It had been, once, in a founding so old it had curdled into legend — but the founding kings had paid for their country in a way that countries are sometimes paid for, and the price had set into the crown the way rot sets into a beam. The crown of Calder grew thorns. Inward. It fit a true heir's head and then it bit, slow and deep, year upon year, drinking the wearer down, and the reigns of Calder were measured not in decades but in how long a person could bleed for a country before the country had drunk them empty. Her father had reigned thirty-one years. Her father had died screaming. Those two facts were the same fact.

"How long," Sera said, "before the court expects me to put it on."

"Nine days," said the rider. "The crowning must be done within nine days of a king's death, or the crown is held to have refused the line, and a refused crown — " he stopped, and for the first time looked up at her, and his face was the face of a man delivering a thing he did not want to be the one to deliver. "A refused crown takes the country instead of the king. Nine days, lady. You have nine days to come home and bleed for Calder the way your father bled, and his father, and every Vane back to the founding."

Sera Vane stood in the doorway of her small quiet exile with a half-mended bridle in her hands, and looked down the long road that led back to the country that had thrown her out and now could not survive without her, and began, already, to count.

Nine days. Nine days to decide whether to set the thorns into her own head and reign and bleed and die screaming like every Vane before her — or to do the thing no Vane in the whole long rotten history of Calder had ever once dared to do, and find a way to break the crown instead.
