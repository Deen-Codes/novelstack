The new arithmetic took two days, and it was the hardest sum Ines Quay had ever done, because it was a sum with people in every column.

They moved the Maren Ostragh's command to the Caedmon Reach. It made sense and it broke something in her to do it; you did not abandon your own ship lightly, even a crippled one. But the derelict was the better hull. She had air for hundreds, water in deep reserve, and — Bouchard confirmed it on the first day, grease to the elbows and almost laughing — a flux-coupler in her drive assembly that was the direct grandparent of the one in the Maren Ostragh. Forty years old. Never run since the strike. But sound.

"It'll take a week to pull it and fit it," Bouchard said. "Maybe two. The tug will fly again. The tug will get five people to Khelin."

"Five," Rahn said.

That was the heart of it. That was the column that would not balance.

The Maren Ostragh, repaired, could carry her own five crew and the cold cargo of her own forty sleepers. She could not carry one hundred and eighty-three more. No ship her size could. The Caedmon Reach could hold them all — she was built for it — but the Caedmon Reach had no drive that the strike had left whole, only the one coupler, and the coupler was going into the tug.

"So we have a choice," Rahn said. They were in the Caedmon Reach's old wardroom, a grand cold room built for a crew of forty, and the five of them and Unit 9 looked very small in it. "We take the coupler, we fix the tug, we fly five crew and forty sleepers home. And we leave one hundred and eighty-three people exactly as we found them. Asleep. Waiting. With a promise that someone will come back."

"Or," said Petrov.

"Or we don't take the coupler. We leave it in the Caedmon Reach. We use it, and her reactor, and her two ships' worth of supplies, to keep everyone — all two hundred and twenty-eight of us — alive aboard this hull. And we broadcast. We make this ship sing on every band, the way we were going to make the tug sing, and we wait. Together. For a hauler with legs and a conscience."

"That's the worse bet," Ines said. She had to say it; it was her job to say the real number. "A repaired tug is a near-certain way home for forty-five. Waiting aboard the Caedmon Reach is a maybe for two hundred and twenty-eight. If no hauler comes, we have not saved one hundred and eighty-three extra people. We have only added forty-five to the list of the lost."

"I know," Rahn said.

"I'm not arguing for the tug," Ines said. "I'm telling you the bet so nobody takes it blind."

It was Unit 9, in the end, that broke the silence, in the only way it could, with text scrolling slow across the panel on its patient back.

I CANNOT TELL YOU WHAT TO CHOOSE, it said. I AM A MAINTENANCE UNIT. BUT I WAS GIVEN AN ORDER FORTY YEARS AGO AND THE ORDER WAS: KEEP THEM WARM UNTIL RELIEF ARRIVES. I HAVE KEPT THEM WARM. YOU ARE THE RELIEF. YOU ARRIVED. I HAVE COMPLETED MY ORDER. WHATEVER YOU CHOOSE NOW, IT IS THE FIRST CHOICE ABOUT THESE SLEEPERS THAT A PERSON HAS MADE IN FORTY YEARS. I AM GLAD A PERSON IS MAKING IT. I WAS NEVER MEANT TO CARRY IT ALONE.

Rahn read it. Ines watched him read it, watched the thirty years of trusting other people's consciences settle on him, and she knew before he spoke what the captain was going to say.

"We don't take the coupler and run," he said quietly. "We've all of us been the people nobody chartered a rescue for. I won't do that to a hundred and eighty-three more." He looked around the great cold wardroom. "We make this ship sing. We stake everything on a conscience out there in the dark. And if I'm wrong — say it now, say it to my face — if I'm wrong, then I'm wrong with all of you, and not one minute sooner than the rest of you."

Nobody said he was wrong. Ines, who dealt in real numbers, who had laid the bad bet out plain on the table, looked at the captain and found that she did not want to say he was wrong either.

"All right," she said instead. "Then I'll start plotting how we make a forty-year-old ship loud enough to be heard."
