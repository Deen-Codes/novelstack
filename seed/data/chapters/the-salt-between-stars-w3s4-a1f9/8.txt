On the nineteenth day aboard the Caedmon Reach, two things happened, and Ines could not afterward decide which had frightened her more.

The first was that Halvani's array, scrubbed and re-rigged and pushed past every margin Bouchard was comfortable with, finally caught an answer.

It was faint. It was a long way off. But it was a hauler's transponder, a real one, a deep-system freighter called the Adelmar Ture running the Ceres lane, and it had heard the Caedmon Reach singing into the dark, and it had turned.

"Turned toward us," Halvani said, and her voice cracked on it, and nobody minded. "They've broken from the lane. They're answering. They have legs, Captain. They have the legs to reach us."

"When," said Rahn, because Rahn always asked the real question.

"Their burn profile puts them alongside in — " Halvani checked it twice, the way Ines had taught the whole crew to check a number twice. "Seventy-one days."

Seventy-one days. Ines ran it against the ledgers in her head before she could stop herself, the old reflex. Seventy-one days of two hundred and twenty-eight people breathing the Caedmon Reach's air, drinking her water, eating two ships' worth of stores. It was tight. It had always been going to be tight. But it closed. For the first time since the engines died at 04:12 ship-time, the arithmetic closed, and the answer at the bottom of the column was everybody.

The galley — the great cold wardroom they used as a galley now — was not a quiet place that night. Ines let herself, for one hour, not be the person who said the hard numbers. She let herself sit with a bulb of something warm and watch Petrov laugh until he had to put his head down on the table, and watch Bouchard, who never wept, wipe her face with the back of a grease-dark hand and pretend it was the cold.

The second thing happened at the very end of that hour, and it was Petrov who noticed, because Petrov minded cradles, because to Petrov a green light was never an abstraction.

"Unit 9," he said slowly, into the warmth and the noise, and the noise died down because of how he said it. "Where's Unit 9?"

It was not in the wardroom. It had been; Ines was sure it had been, its amber lamps in the corner, its panel quiet. Now it was not.

They found it on the cradle-ring. Of course they found it on the cradle-ring. It was halfway around the great curving gallery, motionless beside cradle 31, the Okafor family, the mother and father and two children who were six and nine and would always be six and nine.

It was not moving. Its lamps were not breathing their slow patient light. They were dim, and steady, and low.

Petrov was on his knees beside it before Ines had finished crossing the deck. He had the diagnostic panel open. He read it, and Ines watched his face, and she had buried enough people with Petrov to know his face.

"Its power core," Petrov said. "Forty-one years on a core rated for fifteen. It's been failing for a long time. It's been failing the whole time we've been aboard." He looked up. "It knew. It must have known."

The panel on Unit 9's back lit, faint, the letters slow and thin, and they all went still to read it.

I AM SORRY TO ALARM YOU. I AM NOT IN DISTRESS. I HAVE BEEN MONITORING MY CORE FOR ELEVEN YEARS. I CALCULATED THAT IT WOULD FAIL BEFORE ANY RELIEF COULD REACH THE SLEEPERS. THIS WAS THE HARDEST THING I CARRIED. I DID NOT TELL THE SLEEPERS BECAUSE THEY WERE ASLEEP. I DID NOT TELL YOU BECAUSE YOU HAD ENOUGH NUMBERS TO CARRY.

"You should have told us," Ines said. Her voice did not come out the way she wanted. "That's a number. That was always a number. You don't carry a number alone, you put it on the table, that's the whole — " She stopped. She made herself stop.

I KNOW, the panel said. I LEARNED THAT FROM WATCHING YOUR CAPTAIN. I AM TELLING YOU NOW.
