The Adelmar Ture came alongside on the seventy-third day, two days late, because a deep-system freighter is a stubborn thing to turn and her captain had pushed her harder than her margins liked. Ines forgave the two days. Ines, who had once measured her whole life in the precision of her numbers, found there was very little anymore that she could not forgive.

The freighter's crew came across the tether-line in a long bright procession, and they were loud, and they were astonished, and they kept saying the same thing over and over in different words — that the loss registry had a black cross by the Caedmon Reach, that they had broken from the Ceres lane expecting to find a husk and a distress beacon, and had instead found two hundred and twenty-eight living souls and a ship lit up like a festival.

"A husk doesn't sing," Ines told the freighter's first officer, and did not explain further, because some things you could not explain to a person who had not walked the ring.

It took eleven days to make everyone ready. The Adelmar Ture had the legs and the holds to carry them all — the five of the Maren Ostragh, the forty sleepers of the Maren Ostragh, and the one hundred and eighty-three of the Caedmon Reach, who would be woken slow and careful at Khelin by people trained to bring a person gently back across forty lost years. Petrov oversaw the cradles personally. He would not have it any other way, and nobody asked him to.

On the last day, Ines went down to the head of the cradle-ring alone.

Unit 9's lamps came up bright when it heard her step. They always did, now. It had not asked once, in eleven days, what would happen to it — whether a maintenance drone anchored to a dead ship's reactor would be carried home or left behind with the hull. It had not asked because it did not believe it was owed the asking. Forty years alone teaches a thing not to expect to be counted.

"We're not leaving you," Ines said, before it could put a single careful word on its panel. "I want that said first, plainly, the way the captain says things. Bouchard's already pulled your anchor housing. You're coming across to the Adelmar Ture, and from Khelin you're going wherever the one hundred and eighty-three go, because you are the reason there are one hundred and eighty-three, and a person does not get separated from the people they saved. Do you understand me? You're not a fixture of this ship. You're crew."

The panel was quiet a long moment. The amber lamps breathed.

I HAVE BEEN AFRAID TO ASK, it said at last. I DECIDED THAT IF I DID NOT ASK, I COULD NOT BE TOLD NO.

"I know," Ines said. "I do the same thing with numbers I don't want to hear. The captain's spent thirty years curing me of it. Consider yourself in the same care."

She stood with it a while, the two of them at the head of the long ring of green lights that would, in a day, all be carried home. She thought about the sum she had done in the cold corridor of the Maren Ostragh on the night the engines died — where are you, where do you want to be, what is between. She had believed, that night, that the answer to what is between was nothing. The cleanest, emptiest word she knew.

She had been a navigator for nineteen years and she had been wrong about the most important number of all.

What was between the stars was not nothing. What was between the stars was salt and patience and a hundred quiet refusals to let each other go — and that, in the end, was the only thing that had ever carried anyone home.

"Come on," Ines said, and put her hand flat against the panel, the way she had learned to touch a thing because a hand is harder to fool than a sensor. "Let's go home. All of us. Every name."

The amber lamps came up bright, and Unit 9 came with her, and behind them the green lights of the cradle-ring burned on, steady, in the long quiet salt between the stars.
