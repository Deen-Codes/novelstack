The light was a maintenance drone, and it had been alone for forty years, and it was very glad they had come.

It told them so. Not in words at first — it had no voice, only a chassis the size of a large dog, a cluster of soft amber lamps, and a set of manipulator arms folded against its body like a sleeping insect. But it had a panel on its back, and when Petrov knelt to it the panel lit, and text scrolled, and the text said: CREW DETECTED. CREW DETECTED. WELCOME ABOARD. PLEASE ADVISE: HAS RELIEF ARRIVED?

"It thinks we're a relief crew," Petrov said softly.

"Tell it the truth," Bouchard said.

"Tell it carefully," said Ines.

Petrov typed, two-fingered in his suit gloves, slow. NOT RELIEF. OUR SHIP IS DISABLED. WE NEED HELP. WHAT HAPPENED HERE?

The drone was still for a moment. Its lamps dimmed and brightened, dimmed and brightened, and Ines had the uncomfortable sense of a mind arranging four decades of held words into an order it could give.

The text, when it came, came slowly.

I AM UNIT 9. MAINTENANCE. THE CAEDMON REACH WAS STRUCK BY DEBRIS IN TRANSIT YEAR ONE. THE STRIKE OPENED THE FORWARD HULL. CREW LOSS WAS TOTAL WITHIN NINE HOURS. CAPTAIN'S FINAL ORDER LOGGED: PRESERVE THE SLEEPERS. MAINTAIN THE CRADLES. KEEP THEM WARM UNTIL RELIEF ARRIVES.

Ines read it twice. The cold of the corridor seemed to come up through her boots.

"Forty years," Petrov said. "It's been holding a captain's last order for forty years."

I HAVE MAINTAINED THE CRADLES, the panel scrolled. I HAVE KEPT THEM WARM. POWER WAS A DIFFICULTY. I REDUCED SHIP SYSTEMS TO MINIMUM. I PUT THE SLEEPERS' DECK ON THE REACTOR'S LONGEST LINE. I HAVE WALKED THE RING EVERY DAY TO CHECK EACH CRADLE BY HAND. I AM SORRY FOR HOW LONG IT HAS BEEN. I DID NOT KNOW HOW TO MAKE THE RELIEF COME FASTER.

Bouchard had turned away. Ines saw her shoulders, in the suit, and knew that whatever was happening on Bouchard's face she did not want witnessed.

"Unit 9," Ines said aloud, and Petrov typed it as she spoke. "The sleepers. How many are still viable?"

The drone's lamps did their slow dim-and-brighten. Then: OF TWO HUNDRED CRADLES, ELEVEN FAILED IN THE FIRST DECADE BEFORE I LEARNED THEIR FAULTS. SIX MORE FAILED SINCE. I COULD NOT SAVE THEM. I HAVE THEIR NAMES. ONE HUNDRED AND EIGHTY-THREE CRADLES REMAIN GREEN. ONE HUNDRED AND EIGHTY-THREE SLEEPERS ARE ALIVE. THEY HAVE BEEN ALIVE THE WHOLE TIME. THEY ARE WAITING FOR THE RELIEF I PROMISED THEM.

Ines stood in the cold corridor of a ship the company had written off with a small black cross, and she understood that the arithmetic had just changed shape again, and grown enormous.

They had come here to save five lives with a stranger's supplies. They had found a drone that had spent forty years, alone, in the dark, refusing to let one hundred and eighty-three strangers die.

"Captain," she said into the channel, and now her voice was not quite level. "You need to hear all of this. And then we need to do some new arithmetic, because the number of people on this rescue just got a great deal larger."
