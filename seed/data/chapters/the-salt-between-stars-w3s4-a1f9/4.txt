The Caedmon Reach resolved out of the dark on the fourth day, and she was beautiful and she was wrong.

Beautiful, because a Brae-class hauler was a beautiful thing — a long spine, a great ringed bulk of cradle-decks, a flared engine bell at the stern like the throat of a horn. The shipwrights of forty years ago had built ships that meant to be looked at. Ines watched her grow in the forward port and felt the old ache she always felt at a well-made ship, the ache that had pulled her off Ceres at seventeen and never let her go.

Wrong, because she was lit.

Not all of her. Most of the Caedmon Reach was dark, a black mass against the stars. But along one curve of the cradle-ring a dim amber light moved in the ports, slow, the colour of a banked fire, and it did not stay still. It travelled. It went from port to port along the ring the way a watchman goes from window to window.

"Tell me that's a reflection," Bouchard said.

"It isn't," Halvani said. "There's no light source out here to reflect."

They matched velocity over six careful hours, Ines feeding the helm in increments so small they cost almost nothing, until the two ships hung together in the dark, the dead giant and the small live tug, close enough to throw a line. Closer, Ines saw the hull's long scars — micrometeor pocking, four decades of it, and one great gouge along the spine that no micrometeor had made. Something had struck the Caedmon Reach once, hard, long ago. Perhaps that was the unknown in the loss registry. Perhaps not.

They drew lots for the boarding party, because Rahn would not order anyone across, and the lots gave it to Ines, to Petrov, and to Bouchard. Rahn would hold the bridge. Halvani would hold the comms. Ines did not like leaving the captain behind and she saw that he did not like being left, and neither of them said so, because there was a child's worth of fuel between them and home and no room left for things that did not need saying.

They crossed on a tethered line, three suited shapes against the immensity of two hulls and the deeper immensity beyond, and Ines did not look down, because down was four hundred years of falling and a navigator knew better than to look at it.

The Caedmon Reach had a boarding lock amidships, and the lock had power. The panel lit under Bouchard's glove, a soft amber, the same banked-fire colour as the travelling light. The outer door opened smoothly, without protest, as though it had been oiled yesterday and not abandoned for forty years.

"That's not right," Bouchard said over the suit channel. "Nothing on a dead ship works that smooth."

"This ship isn't dead," Petrov said. "We keep saying dead. It isn't dead. It's just not crewed."

They cycled through. The inner door opened on a corridor, and the corridor had air — Ines's suit read it, cold and thin and stale but breathable, an atmosphere kept alive for forty years by something that had decided it was worth keeping.

And down the corridor, far off, the amber light was moving toward them. Unhurried. The way a host comes to meet a guest at the door.

"Captain," Ines said into the channel, and was surprised her voice was level. "We're aboard. We have air, we have power, and we are not alone."
