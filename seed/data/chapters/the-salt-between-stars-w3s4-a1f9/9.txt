There was, it turned out, a way. There was almost always a way; that was the thing the academy on Ceres had never quite said out loud, that arithmetic was not fate, that a number on a ledger was only a number until somebody decided what to spend.

Bouchard found it, grease to the elbows, two days of not sleeping in her eyes. Unit 9's power core could not be repaired and could not be replaced — there was no core aboard either ship that would fit a forty-year-old maintenance chassis. But a core was only a vessel for power, and the Caedmon Reach had a reactor, and a reactor was power without end.

"We hard-line it," Bouchard said. "We run a dedicated feed from the reactor to the cradle-ring's maintenance bus, and we anchor Unit 9 to the bus. It stops being a thing that walks the ring on its own core. It becomes a thing that lives on the ship's power, the way the cradles do." She wiped her face. "But it's a tether. A physical line. Unit 9 stops being mobile. It can't walk the ring anymore. It'll be anchored at the bus junction for — for as long as the ship runs."

Ines watched the drone take that in. Its lamps did their slow patient breath.

"You'd be giving up the ring," she said to it. "You've walked the ring every day for forty years. We'd be asking you to stop."

The panel lit. YOU ARE NOT ASKING ME TO STOP CHECKING THE SLEEPERS. YOU ARE ASKING ME TO STOP DOING IT WITH MY FEET. The letters paused, and then went on. WHEN I WALKED THE RING I CHECKED EACH CRADLE BY HAND, BECAUSE A HAND IS HARDER TO FOOL THAN A SENSOR, AND BECAUSE THERE WAS NO ONE ELSE TO DO IT. BUT THERE IS SOMEONE ELSE NOW. THERE ARE FIVE OF YOU. AND IN SEVENTY DAYS THERE WILL BE A WHOLE SHIP'S CREW MORE. THE RING DOES NOT NEED MY FEET ANYMORE. IT ONLY NEEDED THEM WHEN I WAS ALONE.

So they did it. It took Bouchard a day and a night to lay the line, and Ines and Petrov to move Unit 9 — gently, the way you move something that matters — to the junction at the head of the cradle-ring, where it could see the long curve of green lights stretching away in both directions. They anchored it. Bouchard threw the feed.

Unit 9's lamps came up bright. Brighter than Ines had ever seen them, a strong steady amber, no longer the banked and careful fire of a thing rationing its last decade of life.

THE CORE IS OFFLINE, the panel said. I AM RUNNING ON THE SHIP. I CAN FEEL THE WHOLE RING FROM HERE. ONE HUNDRED AND EIGHTY-THREE GREEN. I HAVE NOT BEEN ABLE TO SEE THEM ALL AT ONCE BEFORE. IT IS A GOOD THING TO SEE.

And then, after a moment, more slowly:

THANK YOU FOR NOT LEAVING ME AS A NUMBER ON THE LEDGER. I SPENT FORTY YEARS REFUSING TO LEAVE THE SLEEPERS AS NUMBERS. I DID NOT EXPECT ANYONE TO DO THE SAME FOR ME. I FIND THAT IT IS — the letters paused, the way they always paused before the hardest words — I FIND THAT IT IS A GOOD THING TO BE COUNTED.

Ines stood at the head of the ring with her hand flat on the cold glass of cradle 89, the habit she had not been able to put down, and looked down the long gallery of green lights, and at the bright amber lamps of the drone that had kept them, and she thought about distance. About the salt-dark between the stars that she had loved her whole life for being clean and empty and free of grief.

It was not empty. That was the thing she had been wrong about, all nineteen years. It had never once been empty. It was full of ships, and the ships were full of people, and the people were full of the small daily refusals that kept each other alive. Anwar Reyes in cradle 12. The Okafors in 31. Solenne Vyas the teacher of music in 88. A captain forty-one years dead whose last order had been four words long. A drone that had spent four decades turning those four words into a town that still breathed.

The distance between stars was not empty. It was only quiet. And quiet, Ines had learned, was not the same thing at all.
