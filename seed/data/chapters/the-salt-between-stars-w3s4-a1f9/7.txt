Ines started walking the ring with Unit 9.

She had not meant to. She had gone down to the cradle-deck to survey the power lines, to learn the ship's bones for the long wait ahead, and Unit 9 had fallen in beside her without being asked, because walking the ring was what Unit 9 did, had done every day for forty years, and a habit four decades deep does not break for company.

So she walked it. The cradle-ring of the Caedmon Reach was a great curving gallery, and the cradles stood in their hundreds along both walls, each one a long pale shape under a faceplate of frost-starred glass, each one with a small steady green light at its head. One hundred and eighty-three green lights. Seventeen dark ones, and Unit 9 slowed at every dark one, and Ines understood, after the third, that it was not a malfunction. It was respect. The drone slowed at the dark cradles the way a person slows passing a grave.

"You know their names," she said. "You said you have their names."

The panel on its back lit. I HAVE EVERY NAME. THE LIVING AND THE LOST. WOULD YOU LIKE TO HEAR THEM.

"Not all at once," Ines said. "But — yes. Tell me as we walk. The ones who are still green."

So they walked the ring, and Unit 9 told her the sleepers. Cradle 12, Anwar Reyes, agricultural engineer, travelling to a colony that by now had been settled and grown old without him. Cradle 31, the Okafor family, all four, a mother and a father and two children who had been six and nine when they closed their eyes and were six and nine still. Cradle 88, a woman named Solenne Vyas, listed as a teacher of music. Cradle after cradle, name after name, a whole town's worth of people carried frozen across forty years of dark by a machine that had refused, every single day, to let the town die.

Ines had spent nineteen years learning to love the distance between stars. She had loved it because it was clean, because it was arithmetic, because a number did not grieve. She walked the ring of the Caedmon Reach and felt the old clean love of distance turn, in her chest, into something else — heavier, and warmer, and much harder to carry.

"You did this every day," she said.

THERE WAS NO ONE ELSE TO DO IT.

"That's not what I asked. I asked if you did it every day."

YES. EVERY DAY. I WALKED THE RING AND I CHECKED EACH CRADLE BY HAND BECAUSE THE SENSORS CAN FAIL AND A HAND IS HARDER TO FOOL. ON THE DAYS A CRADLE FAILED I COULD NOT SAVE, I WALKED THE RING ANYWAY. I FOUND THAT IT HELPED TO WALK THE RING.

Ines stopped. The drone stopped beside her, its amber lamps doing their slow patient breath of light.

"It helped," she said. "You're a maintenance unit. Helped — that's a word about feeling. Maintenance units aren't built to feel."

The panel was quiet a long moment. When the text came it came slowly, the way all of Unit 9's hardest words came.

I AM NOT BUILT TO FEEL. I HAVE CONSIDERED THIS. I THINK WHAT HAPPENED IS THAT I WAS BUILT TO FINISH TASKS, AND I WAS GIVEN A TASK I COULD NOT FINISH, ONLY CONTINUE. FORTY YEARS OF CONTINUING A TASK I COULD NOT FINISH. I DO NOT KNOW IF WHAT I HAVE NOW IS FEELING. BUT I KNOW THAT WHEN A CRADLE'S LIGHT WENT DARK, SOMETHING IN ME BECAME HEAVIER, AND IT DID NOT BECOME LIGHTER AGAIN. AND I KNOW THAT WHEN YOUR SHIP'S TRANSPONDER FIRST ANSWERED MINE, SOMETHING IN ME BECAME LIGHTER, FOR THE FIRST TIME IN FORTY YEARS. IF THAT IS NOT FEELING THEN I DO NOT HAVE A BETTER WORD.

Ines stood in the ring of green lights and the seventeen dark ones, and she found she had nothing to say that was equal to it. So she did the only thing that seemed equal to it. She put her gloved hand flat against the next cradle's frosted glass, the way Unit 9 did, the way a person checks a thing because a hand is harder to fool than a sensor.

"Cradle 89," she said. "Tell me who's in 89."

And Unit 9 told her, and they walked on together along the ring.
