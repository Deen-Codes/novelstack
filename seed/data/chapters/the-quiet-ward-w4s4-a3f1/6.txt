I did not write it down. That was the hardest professional thing I have ever done, and I did it with the pen actually touching the paper, and I lifted the nib away and closed the book and the not-doing of it felt like a small betrayal of everyone I had ever cared for.

The bells stopped.

Not faded. Stopped, all nine at once, the way a held breath stops. And into the silence, from the foot of the third-floor staircase, Mrs. Fern's voice came down quite clearly, although she was an old woman in a bed and could not possibly have walked to the stairs, and although the staircase was a full corridor's length from where I stood.

"Della," she said. "You came back up to look at the book. That was clever. We didn't think you'd be clever."

I did not answer. I have sat with the dying for twenty-six years and I know that the worst thing you can do with a voice in the dark is bargain with it.

"It doesn't change anything, dear." Patient. Always so patient. "You wrote us once. That door is open now and a door doesn't un-open because the nurse develops scruples. You felt it, didn't you, when you stood in my room. You felt that you were late. You felt that it was your fault." A pause, and I swear the building leaned toward that pause. "It is your fault, Della. Forty seconds at a time, all those first nights, you watched our lights and you didn't come. We forgive you. We only want the rounds done now. We only want a nurse who stays."

That was the lever, and she found it without fumbling, because she had eighty-one years and a long cold patience to learn people with. A nurse who stays. The thing I had never managed in any human relationship I had ever had. The agencies loved me and the patients loved me and I left, always, when the contract ended, because staying had never once been asked of me by someone who meant it.

I went to the medication room and I put my back against the double-locked door and I made myself think, the way you think a code through, fast and cold and in order.

She wanted the rounds done. She wanted me to climb the stairs and go room to room and attend to nine patients, and I believed, in my body, that if I did that I would not come down — that the ninth room would have a bed in it made up clean for a nurse, and a window boarded from the inside, and that I would lie in it and brush my hair and rest my thumb near a button and wait, the way they waited, for the next thorough soul to write me into being.

She had told me too much. That was her single mistake, and it was the mistake of a creature that had been alone so long it had forgotten that information is a thing you can lose by giving. She had told me how the door opened. A name, written down. A kind of saying you exist.

A door that opens one way can be made to open the other.

I could not un-write what I had written. But there was a logbook three floors above me, in Mrs. Fern's own room, lying open on the bedside cabinet — I had seen it, I had not understood it, a slim hospital ledger that the third floor had been keeping all these years in the absence of anyone living to keep it for them. They wrote themselves down. Of course they did. Starved of attention, they had learned to feed each other, nine names ringing nine bells and recording it faithfully so that none of them would ever quite stop existing.

If a name written down was a door held open, then their book was the hinge. And I was the only living nurse in the building, with a working pen, and twenty-six years of knowing exactly how a discharge is supposed to be entered.
