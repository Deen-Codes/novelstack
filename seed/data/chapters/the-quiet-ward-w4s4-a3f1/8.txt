A discharge entry is a small and specific thing. Name. Room. Date. Condition on discharge. Destination. I had written ten thousand of them. My hand knew the shape of it the way a hand knows a signature, and that knowing was the only thing that kept the pencil moving while the room came apart around me.

The lamp went out. The bells filled the dark. The cut-flower sweetness curdled into something older and the air pressed on my eyes, and Mrs. Fern was saying my name, over and over, no longer patient, the long cold patience of eighty-one years finally spent, and the voice was coming from very close and from all around and from inside my own ears. Della. Della. You can't. We aren't in any book to be discharged from. We made our own book. You can't send us anywhere, you stupid late woman, there's nowhere to send us —

But there was. That was the thing she had got wrong, the second mistake, born of the same long loneliness as the first. She thought a discharge needed a destination on a map. It does not. I have signed the discharge of more people than I can count whose destination was not a place, and the form has a word for it, a plain kind word that the living use to mean the one journey that needs no address, and the word is rest.

I wrote it nine times. I wrote the names from the newspaper cutting, which I had read slowly and thoroughly because I am a thorough person and that thoroughness was, in the end, the whole of my defence. Edith Fern, room 31. Condition on discharge: at peace. Destination: rest. Then the next, and the next, my torch gripped in my teeth now because the lamplight had failed in every room at once, my handwriting going across the page as level as I could hold it while something with no patience left at all howled the length of the corridor and tried, I think, to make me afraid enough to stop.

It could not make me afraid. I have sat with the genuinely dying, and there is a particular fear that belongs to that work, and it is not a loud fear, and a howl is not it. The howl was just grief that had been kept too long in a locked corridor. I had heard grief before. I kept writing.

On the ninth name the noise thinned. Not stopped — thinned, the way a fever breaks, the way a patient who has fought all night against the necessary thing finally, finally lets the hands do their work. I wrote the last line. Destination: rest. And I closed the ledger, and I held it shut against my chest with both arms, and I said into the dark the thing I have said over more beds than I will ever count, the small true sentence that is the last round of all.

"There now. You're all seen to. You can go home."

The torch in my teeth showed me room thirty-one. The bed was made up with clean sheets, turned down, empty. There was no old woman in it. There was no lamp, and the boarded window let in, around the edges of the boards, the first grey of a morning that had been coming all along while I could not see it. The corridor behind me was just a corridor — dust, neglect, a bruise-blue carpet, nine ordinary doors. The silence in it was, at last, the restless honest silence of a building with nobody in it.

I carried the ledger downstairs. I was shaking by then, properly, the delayed way you shake after a code, after the body finally lets you feel what it would not let you feel while there was work to do.
