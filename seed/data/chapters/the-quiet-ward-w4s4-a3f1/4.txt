Mrs. Fern talked, and I wrote, and the boarded window behind her gave no sign of the night passing.

There were nine of them on the third floor, she said. Nine who had been patients at Aldgrove in what she called the bad winter, though she could not give me a year and I did not press her, because the very old often keep time by weather rather than numbers and I have never found it stops them being accurate about what matters. Nine patients in the long convalescent rooms, recovering from the things people recovered from then — pneumonia, broken hips, the slow grey aftermath of being old in a cold building.

"There was a fire," she said. "Not a big one. That's what they'd tell you, if they told you anything. Not a big fire. It started in the linen store and it never even reached the rooms. We didn't burn, Della. I want you to be clear on that, because people hear fire and they think the worst, and the worst wasn't the fire."

"What was the worst?"

"The doors." She said it simply. "They locked the corridor doors at night, you see, back then. For our safety. So we wouldn't wander, so we wouldn't fall on the stairs. And the night the linen store caught, the man who had the keys was downstairs, and the smoke came up the stairwell before he did, and the doors stayed locked." Her folded hands did not move. "It wasn't the fire. It was the waiting. We rang our bells and we waited and the panel downstairs lit up like a fairground, Edith always said, and nobody came up because nobody could come up, and the smoke was very slow and very patient and so in the end were we."

I wrote nine patients. Locked corridor. I wrote it in my small steady hand and the writing did not shake, and I have wondered since whether that steadiness was strength or whether it was the first sign that the third floor had already begun, gently, to keep me.

"After," Mrs. Fern went on, "they didn't like to talk about it. The home nearly closed. There was an inquiry, and a settlement, and the third floor was shut, and the new staff were told it was the structure. And the thing about not being talked about, Della — the thing nobody warns you of — is that it's very like not being discharged. We were never sent home and we were never written down and so we are still here, all nine of us, in our rooms, ringing our bells, doing what a patient does." She finally looked away from me, toward the boarded window. "Waiting for the rounds."

"Why me?" I asked. "Petrie heard the panel. Others must have. Why did the door open for me?"

"Because you wrote us in the book." She turned back, and her pale eyes were very direct. "The first night. Room thirty-one, you wrote, light only, no patient in residence. You were wrong about the last part, but you were the first in years to put pen to us at all. That's a kind of attention, dear. That's a kind of saying you exist." She smiled, and there was something underneath the smile now, something I should have read more carefully than I did. "And we have been so hungry to be attended to."

The lamp flickered. Down the corridor, very faintly, I heard another bell begin — not the panel sound, the real thin sweetness of a bedside button, and then another joining it, and another.

"They've heard you're here," Mrs. Fern said softly. "They've heard a nurse is doing rounds. Della — they have been ringing for such a long time. Will you go to them? Will you do it properly, every room, the way it should always have been done?"

I looked at the doorway, and the dark corridor beyond it, and the small chorus of bells calling me on into it. And God help me, I am a thorough person, and they had been waiting, and I stood up.
