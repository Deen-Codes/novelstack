Sandra Plume found me at the nurses' station at half past six, sitting very straight in the chair with the third floor's slim ledger squared on the desk in front of me and both my hands flat on top of it. She stopped in the doorway. She is not a woman who is easily stopped, and she stood there a long moment reading my face the way she reads the logbook every morning, looking for the empty pages.

"You went up," she said.

"I did the rounds," I said. "All nine rooms. They're done."

I watched her understand it. I watched forty years of running this ward, of training nurse after nurse in the discipline of the full page and the unwritten floor, of keeping the lid pressed down by the simple violence of refusing to acknowledge — I watched all of it meet the thing I had done, and I saw that she did not know whether to thank me or to never forgive me.

"We kept it stable," she said at last, and her voice was not steady either. "Twenty-three years I kept it stable. You don't feed a thing and eventually —"

"Eventually nothing. That's what you were waiting for." I turned the ledger around so it faced her and opened it to the discharges, nine of them, my level handwriting, the kind plain word at the end of every line. "You can't starve grief to death, Sandra. You can only make it wait. They weren't a thing that needed managing. They were nine patients who were never attended to, and you trained twenty years of nurses to walk past their lights, and the cruelty of that —" I stopped. She was crying, without sound, in the doorway, an old woman who had done a frightened thing for a long time because no one had shown her another door. I have no appetite for being cruel to the frightened. "It's done now," I said, more gently. "They've been discharged. Read it. A discharge is a release. That's all it ever was."

She read it. She read all nine entries, and her finger moved down the page the way mine had moved down the newspaper list, slow, checking, a nurse to the bone the same as me. When she reached the end she sat down in the other chair as though her legs had been waiting for permission, and she said, very quietly, "Edith Fern. She was the first one I ever didn't write down. I was twenty-six. They told me it was kinder."

"It wasn't kinder. But you were twenty-six and they told you, and you believed them, and you kept eleven living people safe every night for twenty-three years by carrying a thing nobody should have to carry alone." I closed the ledger. "You can put it down now."

We did the morning handover together when Petrie came in, and we did not tell him, because some things are not handover and this was one. But I wrote the night in the official logbook. I wrote all of it, in my small upright hand, every event in the same ink as the ordinary events, because I had finally understood Sandra's catechism properly and she had had it exactly backwards.

You do not keep the dead out by leaving the page empty. An empty page is a locked door — it is the silence that made them, the not-being-attended-to that they died inside of. You write it down. You write it true. You give the grief its forty seconds and its name and its proper discharge, and then it can rest, because being written down, in the end, is only another way of being seen, and being seen, in the end, is the whole of the work.
