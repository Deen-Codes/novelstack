I stayed at Aldgrove. That surprised the agency, who are used to me leaving when a contract ends, and it surprised me, and I think it would not have surprised Edith Fern at all. She had known what the lever was. A nurse who stays. She had only been wrong about which way I would be made to stay.

The third floor is open now. We had the boards taken off the windows first — that was the very first thing, before anything else, daylight let back into nine rooms that had been dark for twenty-three years. The structural survey, when it finally happened, found the floor perfectly sound. It always had been. The structure was never the reason.

We are turning the upper rooms into a day space. A library, a place by the windows where the first-floor patients can sit in the afternoon and watch the lane. Sandra fought me on it at first, out of a habit of fear so old she had stopped being able to see it as fear, and then one afternoon she came up the stairs on her own and stood a long time in room thirty-one in the daylight, and she came down and said, "Yes. A library. Edith would like that. She was a great reader."

The call panel still has its three extra rows of lights. I asked the electrician about disconnecting them properly and then I asked him not to, and I cannot entirely tell you why, except that a panel that can register the upper floor is a panel that is honest about the building it serves, and I have come to believe in honest buildings.

The lights have never come on again. I do my rounds at ten, twelve, two, and four, and I write everything in the book, the ordinary nights in the same ink as that one extraordinary one, and the upper rooms are quiet — not the watching quiet of that first week, and not the locked quiet of the twenty-three years, but the third kind, the good kind, the rest I wrote nine times into a slim ledger that now sits on the library shelf where anyone may read it.

Sometimes, on the two o'clock round, I go up. I don't have to. The rooms are empty and they are only rooms. But I go up, and I walk the corridor, and I look in at each open lamplit door — we leave the lamps on, low, all night now, nine small yellow lights, and that was my idea and I will not apologise for it — and I say, to nine rooms that no longer answer, the thing you say on the last round of a long night.

"All seen to. Rest now."

And then I go back down to the living, and I sit under the panel with its dark and patient lights, and I keep the pages full. Not empty against the dead. Full, and true, and seen — for the living and the dead alike, because I have learned that there was only ever one ward, and one duty, and it does not end at a sealed door. It never did. It only waited, the way patients wait, for a nurse thorough enough to climb the stairs.
