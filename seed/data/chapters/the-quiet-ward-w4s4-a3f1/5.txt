I did not go to the other rooms that night. I want that on the record, because what saved me, in the end, was a single small act of professional habit, and the habit was this: before I respond to a chorus of bells, I find out which patient is in genuine distress, and I prioritise. You cannot run to nine rooms at once. You triage. And triage, that night, meant going back downstairs first to check the official logbook, because something Mrs. Fern had said was scratching at me.

You can't discharge what isn't written.

I went down. The first floor was warm and ordinary and eleven living people slept in it, and the contrast was so violent that I had to stand at the nurses' station for a moment with both hands flat on the desk. Then I opened the logbook — not the current one, but the row of older volumes Sandra kept on the shelf below, the institutional memory of the place going back decades.

I found the bad winter. It was not hard. The handwriting changed three times on a single page and then there was a gap, and then a new hand entirely, and pasted into the binding was a cutting from a local paper. NINE DIE IN CARE HOME FIRE TRAGEDY. I made myself read all of it. The fire had been small. The deaths had not been burns. Smoke inhalation, the coroner found, in a corridor whose doors had been locked against a key-holder who could not reach them in time. There was a list of names. I ran my finger down it the way I run my finger down a drug chart, slowly, checking, and the seventh name was Edith Fern, aged eighty-one, of room thirty-one.

Underneath, someone — Sandra, I was suddenly certain it was Sandra, years younger — had written a single line in pencil. They are not in any book after this one. Keep it that way.

And I understood. Sandra's whole strange catechism turned over in my mind and showed me its other face. Empty pages, not empty rooms. Write everything, especially when nothing happens. She had not been training me to be thorough. She had been training me to keep the pages full of the living and starved of the dead — because attention is food, and a name written down is a door held open, and for years the staff of Aldgrove had survived their nights by the discipline of never, ever, writing the third floor into being.

I had broken that on my first shift. Light only, no patient in residence. Eight words. I had set a place at the table.

Above my head, through the ceiling, the bells were still ringing. They had not stopped since I left Mrs. Fern's room. Nine rooms, nine bells, and now that I knew what they were I could hear the change in them — not the patient sweetness of a request, but something with an edge under it, something that had been politely asking for a very long time and was beginning, slowly, patiently, to insist.

I picked up my pen. My hand, for the first time that night, was not steady.

The honest thing, the trained thing, the thing I had been hired and drilled and praised to do, was to write the night down. To record that I had gone to the third floor, that I had spoken with Edith Fern of room thirty-one, that there were nine of them and they were ringing. To be thorough.

I held the pen over the page and I felt the whole weight of my working life pushing the nib down toward the paper, and I felt Mrs. Fern three floors of waiting away, patient, certain, already half-written, and I understood that the kindest and most thorough nurse in the world was exactly the wrong person to be alone in this building tonight.
