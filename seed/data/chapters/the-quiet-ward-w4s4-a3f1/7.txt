I will not pretend the climb was brave. Bravery is for people who have a choice and take the hard one. I had worked out that the building would not let me simply leave — the front door, when I tried it, was not locked and yet I could not make my hand close on the bar, the way you cannot make yourself step off a height — and so going up was not courage. It was the only direction left.

The NO ACCESS strap lay where I had unclipped it on the first night. The fire door at the top still stood open on its folded towel. But the third-floor corridor had changed. All nine doors stood open now, and from each doorway a low lamp-light spilled yellow onto the bruise-blue carpet, and the air had the cut-flower sweetness thick enough to taste, and the silence was no longer attentive. It was satisfied. It was the silence of a ward that has its nurse at last.

They were all awake. I could feel that without looking into the rooms. Nine of them, propped on nine sets of pillows, brushed and tidy and folded-handed, listening to my shoes on the carpet.

"Rooms twenty-eight to thirty-six," Mrs. Fern's voice said warmly from somewhere ahead. "Start at the far end, dear, and work back. That's how it's done properly. We'll be so good for you. We've had so much practice at being patient."

I did not start at the far end. I walked straight down the centre of the corridor, not looking left or right into the lamplit rooms, although things at the edges of my vision sat up a little to watch me pass, and I went into room thirty-one.

She was exactly as I had left her. Small, neat, white hair brushed, hands folded, thumb near the button. Only her eyes had changed. The kindness was still in them but it had thinned, the way a smile thins when a guest stays past the moment they were wanted, and I understood that the kindness had always been the kindness extended to a thing you intend to use.

"You didn't start at the end," she said.

"No." I went to the bedside cabinet, and the slim ledger lay open on it, and a pencil lay in the gutter of the binding. "Mrs. Fern, I'm going to do the rounds. I'm going to attend to every patient on this floor, properly, the way it should have been done the night of the fire. I'm a thorough person. You chose me for it. You were right to."

Something flickered across the washed-blue eyes. Not fear — they were long past fear — but a kind of recalculation.

"The rounds," she said slowly, "are done by going to the rooms."

"The rounds," I said, "are done by attending to the patients. And the last thing you do for a patient, Mrs. Fern, when there is genuinely nothing left to be done for them in this place — the kindest and most thorough thing — is you arrange for them to go home. You discharge them. You write it in the book."

I picked up the pencil.

She moved then, faster than an old woman in a bed has any business moving, her folded hands coming unfolded, and the lamp guttered, and down the corridor all nine bells began at once and this time there was nothing sweet in them at all. But I have changed dressings with patients screaming, and held people while they fought me, and finished the necessary thing with steady hands a thousand times because steady hands were the only gift I had. I put the pencil to the ledger. And in my small upright trained handwriting, in the space below the last entry, under the watching washed-blue eyes of the thing in the bed, I began to write the discharges.
