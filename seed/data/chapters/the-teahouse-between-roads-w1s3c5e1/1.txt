Persi had inherited exactly three things in her life, and two of them had been disappointing, so she walked up to the teahouse with no expectations at all, which was, as it turned out, the perfect frame of mind for what the teahouse was.

The lawyer's letter had been very brief. A great-aunt named Ottoline, whom Persi had never met and never heard her family mention, had died, and left to her great-niece Persi a going concern called the Teahouse Between Roads, together with all its stock, its furnishings, and — the letter was specific about this in a way Persi found slightly odd — its obligations.

The teahouse stood by itself at a crossroads.

That was the first thing that did not quite sit right. It was a proper crossroads, four good roads meeting, the sort of junction that ought to have grown a village around it the way crossroads do — and there was no village. There was no anything. There were four roads running off into four different kinds of country, which was the second thing that did not sit right, because Persi had walked in along the south road through ordinary green farmland, and the east road ran off into a desert, and the north road climbed into snow, and the west road went down into a forest so dark and so old that looking along it made the back of her neck feel watched. Four roads, four seasons, four entirely different parts of the world, all meeting at one signpost, with a teahouse on the corner.

Persi stood and looked at that for a while. Then she decided, the way she had taught herself to decide difficult things, that she would simply go inside and have a look, and let the difficulty wait until she had her boots off.

Inside, the teahouse was warm, and clean, and ready. That was the word that came to her — ready. A fire banked low. Tables wiped. A long counter with a hundred small drawers, each one labelled in a fine old hand with the name of a tea, and some of the names Persi knew, and some of them she very much did not, and one drawer near the end was labelled simply for the ones who are running.

And pinned above the counter, where the keeper of the teahouse could not fail to see it, was a card, and the card was the third thing the lawyer had mentioned. The obligations. It was written in the same fine hand as the drawers, and it said:

The Teahouse Between Roads stands where four worlds touch, and it has stood here longer than any of them have had names. It is not yours, keeper. It is only yours to keep. The rule of the house is one rule and it does not bend. Every traveller who stops here is owed a cup and a question. Pour the cup honest. Ask the question kind. Refuse no one who comes through the door — not the lost, not the hunted, not the dangerous, not the king, not the thing that is not quite a person. And remember everything they tell you, because a teahouse between roads is the only place in any world where all the roads can hear each other, and one day, keeper, what you remember will matter more than you can presently imagine.

Persi read it twice. She was a practical young woman, and a practical young woman knows the difference between a quaint old card and an instruction, and she understood, standing in the warm ready room, that this was an instruction, and that she had — without quite meaning to, by the simple act of walking in and reading it — accepted the job.

She put her bag behind the counter. She found an apron on a hook that was, she noticed, exactly her size, which she decided not to think too hard about. And she had just worked out the trick of the fire, and got the big kettle settled over it, when the west door opened — the west door, the one that gave onto the dark old forest — and the cold green smell of that forest came in, and with it came her first guest.

He was a knight. There was no mistaking it; he wore the war on him the way some men wear weather. Mud and worse than mud on good armour, a sword he kept one hand near without seeming to know he did it, and a face that had not slept properly in a long time and did not expect to soon. He stopped just inside the door and looked at the warm room and the banked fire and the girl in the apron, and Persi watched something in him flinch at the ordinariness of it, the way a person flinches at a kindness when they have been braced a long time for the opposite.

"You're open," he said. His voice was rough. "I didn't think anywhere out here would be open."

"We're always open," said Persi, and found, as she said it, that she somehow knew it was true. "Sit anywhere. The fire's better at that end." She reached for a cup, because the card had been very clear, a cup and a question, refuse no one. "You've come a long way, by the look of the road on you. Sit down, and I'll pour you something, and then —" she set the cup on the counter, and met his tired braced eyes, and asked the first question she ever asked a traveller in the Teahouse Between Roads — "and then you can tell me what it is you're running from. Because you are running. I keep a whole drawer of tea for it."

The knight stood in the doorway a moment longer. And then, very slowly, like a man setting down something he had been carrying for much too long, he let go of his sword, and came in, and sat down at the better end of the fire.

"It hasn't started yet," he said. "That's the thing nobody believes. The war I'm running from. It hasn't started yet. I'm the only one who's seen it coming, and I have run down four roads to find someone who would listen before it does." He looked up at her. "Your card. The one above the counter. Does it really mean it? Remember everything?"

Persi poured the cup. Honest, like the rule said.

"Try me," she said, and sat down across from him, and the Teahouse Between Roads began, after a long quiet age of waiting, to do once more the work it had always been built for.
