They put her in her mother's apartments before the ashes were cold, because a queen with no rooms is a queen who can be argued with, and the council wanted Naeve furnished with the trappings of power as fast as possible — not to honour her, but to make her a target standing still.

She understood that. She had been a bastard for twenty-six years and a bastard's whole education is understanding why people are kind to you. Nobody had ever been kind to Naeve without an invoice.

The apartments still smelled of her mother. That was the thing that nearly broke her — not the pyre, not the bowing dragon, but the small ordinary fact that the queen's dressing room held the scent of the queen, bergamot and iron-water and the particular soap she had used, and Naeve stood in it alone for the first quiet moment she had been granted all day and had to put her hand flat against the wall.

Twelve days. Her mother had been poisoned twelve days, dying slow and clear-eyed and refusing to name a suspicion she could not prove, because a queen who accuses without proof starts a war, and the old queen had spent her whole reign not starting wars. She had died protecting the peace from her own murder. Naeve had come up the headland tonight carrying that, and only that: a daughter's plain intention to do the thing her mother had been too responsible to do, and find the hand that held the cup.

Now she was queen, and being queen, she was already learning, was a set of walls built specifically to keep a person from doing exactly that.

Marshal Edrin found her there. He did not knock; kings' apartments have no doors that matter to a man who has served three monarchs. He came in, and looked at her hand flat against the wall, and had the grace to pretend he had not seen it.

"You'll want to sleep," he said. "You won't. So instead I'll give you the lie of the land, and you'll decide tonight, before the council decides for you, what kind of queen tomorrow finds." He lowered himself onto a bench with the care of an old man's knees. "You think you have one enemy. The hand with the cup. You're wrong, and the wrongness will kill you if you don't correct it fast. You have three problems and only one of them is your mother's murderer."

Naeve turned from the wall. "Tell me the three."

"Good. That's a queen's question; bastards ask 'what do I do,' queens ask 'what is true.' Keep doing that." He held up a finger. "First. Caran. Your half-brother had the robes cut, Your Grace, which means he had the votes counted, which means somewhere in this castle is a list of council members who promised Caran their voices for a succession that did not happen. That list is a loaded crossbow and tonight Caran is deciding where to point it. He is not your enemy because he hates you. He is your enemy because you cost him something and the ledger is open."

"Second?"

"Second. You think the dragon is your protection. It is your sentence." Edrin's old eyes were steady. "Sarrowmund chose you, and nobody can unchoose you while it lives — which means everyone who wanted a different queen now has, very simply, a different problem to solve first. The dragon is three hundred years old, girl, and old things can be made to sleep, and old things can be made to grieve, and old things can be fed. There are people who went up that headland tonight expecting a tame succession and came down it understanding that the only door between them and the throne they wanted is a beast on the rocks. You should be more afraid for the dragon than for yourself. It is the lock. You are merely what's behind it."

Naeve sat down, slowly, across from him. Outside the high window the headland was dark and somewhere below it Sarrowmund lay in the night, vast and ancient and, she realised with a cold turn of the stomach, mortal — a thing that could be reached.

"And the third problem," she said.

The old marshal was quiet a moment. When he spoke it was gentler, and gentleness from Edrin was its own kind of warning.

"The third problem is the one you carried up the headland yourself. You want your mother's murderer, and you want them with the whole of your heart, and Your Grace — that wanting is the most dangerous thing in this castle, more dangerous than Caran's list, more dangerous than whoever would harm the dragon. Because a queen who is hunting can be steered. Show me a monarch consumed by a single private vengeance and I will show you a court that learns, very quickly, to hand her the wrong man — a tidy man, a convenient man, a man someone powerful needed gone anyway — and watch her execute him with her own grief and call it justice." He leaned forward. "They poisoned your mother to get a quiet succession. They failed; the dragon saw to that. So now the cleverest of them will do the next best thing. They will let you find a murderer. They will simply make very sure it is the murderer that suits them."

The room was silent. The scent of bergamot and iron-water hung in it like the queen herself, listening.

"Then what do I do," Naeve said — and heard, too late, that she had asked the bastard's question and not the queen's.

Edrin did not correct her this time. He only stood, with his old man's care, and looked down at the young woman the pyre had cursed with a crown, and gave her the second true thing of the night.

"You trust the dragon," he said, "and you trust me, and you trust no third thing, and you understand from this hour forward that everyone who weeps for your mother in front of you might be the one who emptied the cup — including, Your Grace, the brother who weeps best of all. Sleep if you can. Tomorrow they crown you, and a crowning is just a room full of your enemies watching to see what you've decided to become."

He left. Naeve sat alone in her dead mother's rooms, queen of a kingdom that had not wanted her, and did not sleep, and somewhere far below in the dark the dragon Sarrowmund shifted its ancient bulk against the rocks — the only thing in the world that was wholly on her side, and the easiest thing in the world, she now understood, for her enemies to take away first.
