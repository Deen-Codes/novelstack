They burned the queen at dusk, and they made her bastard watch from the back, behind the legitimate grief, where a bastard belonged.

Naeve stood among the under-stewards and the kennel-masters and the people who counted the silver, and she watched her mother's body go up, and she did not weep, because weeping was a currency and she had learned young not to spend currency in front of people who would price it. The legitimate children wept at the front in good black wool. Her half-brother Caran wept the most. Caran wept beautifully. Caran had a gift for it.

The pyre was built in the old way, on the headland over the sea, because the House of Vael had always burned its queens where the dragon could see. The dragon was the whole point of the headland. Sarrowmund — older than the kingdom, older than the name of the kingdom — lay coiled on the black rocks below like a range of hills that had decided to have opinions, and when a Vael queen died, her dragon climbed the headland, and lay beside the pyre, and when the fire had done its work the dragon chose. That was the succession. Not the council, not the crown, not the eldest legitimate son in his good black wool. The dragon walked to one of the dead queen's children and lowered its head, and that child was the monarch, and Sarrowmund was that child's, and the kingdom held its breath for the length of one beast's slow consideration and then had a ruler.

It had always chosen the obvious one. That was the comfortable secret of the whole rite — in three hundred years Sarrowmund had never once surprised anybody. It went to the heir the council had already picked. The dragon was a formality wearing teeth.

Everyone expected Caran. Caran expected Caran. Caran had, Naeve happened to know, already had the accession robes cut.

The fire burned down. The sea worked at the rocks. And Sarrowmund uncoiled from the headland's edge and climbed, slow as weather, into the firelight, and the whole gathered House of Vael went still in the particular way of people watching a formality they have watched all their lives.

The dragon walked past Caran.

It was not a small thing, the way it walked past him. It did not hesitate at him and move on. It simply did not see him, the way you do not see a chair, and Caran's beautiful weeping stopped as though a hand had been clapped over it, and the silence that came down over the headland was the silence of three hundred years of certainty cracking like ice on a pond.

Sarrowmund came through the legitimate children. It came through the council. It came through the good black wool and the people who counted the silver, and it came to the back, to where the bastard stood among the under-stewards, and Naeve looked up into an eye the size of a shield and old as grief and she thought, with a clarity that frightened her more than fear would have:

No. I came here to find out who killed her. Not to be given her chair.

The dragon lowered its head.

It lowered its head to her, the great horned skull coming down through the smoke until it was level with her, until the heat of its breath moved her hair, and Naeve understood that every plan she had carried up the headland — the quiet questions, the watching, the long patient bastard's work of learning which of these grieving people had put the poison in her mother — every plan had just been taken out of her hands and replaced with a crown.

She did not kneel. She would think about that, later, for years: that in the one moment the whole kingdom was watching her, she had been too stunned to perform the humility that might have softened them. She simply stood, a bastard in plain mourning, with a dragon's head bowed to her and four hundred faces turned to her white with shock, and her half-brother Caran staring at her over the dead fire with an expression she had never seen him wear and would come to know very well.

It was not grief. Caran had a gift for grief and this was not it.

It was arithmetic.

"Well," said a voice beside her — old Marshal Edrin, who had served three queens and buried two, the only person on the headland who did not look afraid. He said it very quietly, under the rising noise, for her alone. "The pyre's chosen, girl, and the pyre doesn't argue. So here is the first true thing anyone will tell you as queen, and you should hold it close, because it may be the last true thing for a while." He looked at the bowed head of the dragon, and then at the white shocked court, and then at Caran doing his arithmetic over the ashes of their mother.

"They wanted a quiet succession," the old marshal said. "You've just made it a loud one. Everyone on this headland who had a plan for tonight has watched it burn with the queen. And people, Your Grace, do not grieve a burnt plan. They avenge it." He inclined his head — the first bow she ever got, and an honest one. "Welcome to the worst night of your life. It will have a great deal of competition."
