Renata Vidal had played in front of sixty thousand people, and none of those crowds had ever frightened her the way fourteen twelve-year-olds did on a cold Tuesday.

"Again," she called across the pitch, and the girls groaned, and ran the drill again, badly, the way they had run it the first six times. The Eastfield Girls' Academy did not have a good pitch, or good lights, or good anything; it had a chain-link fence, a portacabin, and a coach who had until eight months ago been someone else entirely.

Eight months ago Renata had been a footballer. Not a coach. A footballer — left back for the national team, ninety-one caps, the words rising star printed under her photograph in newspapers that had since stopped printing her photograph. And then there had been a Tuesday much like this one, a routine challenge in a routine match, and her knee had done something that knees are not built to come back from, and the surgeon had been very kind and very clear, and that had been the end of the only life Renata had ever planned.

She did not, mostly, let herself think about it. Thinking about it was a room she kept the door shut on. She had found this academy, this struggling little academy with its chain-link fence, and she had decided that if she could not play the game then she would at least stay near enough to hear it, and she would build something for fourteen girls who reminded her, on the good days, of herself at twelve — all hunger and bad first touches.

The bad days she did not talk about.

"Renata." Marcus, who ran the academy on goodwill and a permanently failing budget, came across the grass with the particular walk of a man bringing news. "We've got a sponsor. An actual one. Enough to fix the lights and the changing rooms and maybe, maybe, get us into the regional league properly."

"That's —" Renata felt something genuine and bright go through her, the first in a while. "Marcus, that's everything. Who is it?"

"Local business. Construction firm, doing well, the owner wants to put money into youth sport, says it's about giving something back." Marcus was not quite meeting her eyes, she noticed. "He's coming down this afternoon. To see the place. To meet the coach."

"Good. I'll meet him."

"Renata." Marcus stopped walking. "The firm's called Vidal — no. Sorry. It's called Costa Construction. The owner's name is Diego Costa."

The whistle was still in Renata's hand. She felt her fingers close around it.

There were a lot of men in the world called Diego. It was not an uncommon name. She told herself that, in the half-second she had, the way you tell yourself a thing you already know is not going to hold.

"Diego Costa," she said.

"You know him." It was not a question. Marcus had clearly already worked out that she knew him, from her face, which was doing something she had not authorised.

She knew him. Of course she knew him. Diego Costa had been nineteen years old the last time she had stood close enough to say his name, and she had been nineteen too, and they had been the sort of in love that you only manage before the world has properly started charging you for things. And then the call had come — the under-21 squad, the academy in another city, the beginning of the only life Renata had ever planned — and she had taken it, because of course she had taken it, you do not say no to the thing you have wanted since you were six years old.

She had said no to Diego instead. On a doorstep, in the rain, badly. She had chosen the game, out loud, to his face, and she had been right to, she had told herself for eleven years that she had been right to, because the game had given her ninety-one caps and a stadium that roared her name.

The game had also, eight months ago, taken all of it back, and left her on a chain-link pitch with a whistle.

"We were together," Renata said. "A long time ago. Before any of it. He'll have a wife and kids and a construction firm now and he will not want to —"

The gate at the far end of the pitch opened.

The man who came through it was thirty, broad, in a good coat that did not belong on this pitch, and he stopped just inside the gate when he saw her, the way you stop when the ground is not where you left it. Eleven years had changed his face the way eleven years change a face — but Renata would have known the way he stood in a crowd of thousands, and there was no crowd here, there was only a portacabin and fourteen girls and the cold, and Diego Costa looked across the worst pitch in the county at the woman who had chosen football over him, and football had clearly, visibly, not chosen her back.

"Ren," he said. Just that. The old short version of her name, the one nobody had used since she was nineteen.

And Renata Vidal, who had been roared at by sixty thousand strangers, found that she had absolutely nothing to say to the one person who had ever known her before the noise.
