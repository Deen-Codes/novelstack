She kept the torch on him, and she kept the width of a fern bank between them, and she made him talk from there.

His name, he said, was Lucian Vane. He had been, when the house was young, its only son. He did not insult her by easing her toward the rest of it — June would think of that later, too, and grudgingly respect it. He simply told her, plainly, standing in her torchlight with his hands open at his sides: that he had not aged since 1903, that he could not leave the house, that the sun was closed to him and so, in a quieter way, was the dark beyond these walls. That he was, by every old word for it, a thing that should be in the ground and was not.

"You're telling me," June said, "that you're a vampire."

"I'm telling you what I am. The word is yours to choose." His mouth moved, not quite a smile. "I find most people prefer to choose it themselves. It lets them feel they worked it out."

"And the doors lock at dusk because of you."

"The doors lock at dusk because of the curse," Lucian said, "which is not the same thing, though I understand why you'd assume it. I did not make the house a cage, Ms. Mercer. The house and I were sentenced together. We have been keeping each other company in here for ninety years, and we are both, I think, extremely tired of it."

June was aware that she should be more frightened than she was. She catalogued that fact alongside the others. The man — the thing — had made no move toward her. He had stayed exactly where her light first found him. And there was something in the careful stillness of him, the deliberate harmlessness, that reminded her, absurdly, of the way one stands very still near a nervous animal so as not to frighten it.

He was, she realized, trying not to frighten her.

"The library," she said. "The agency told me a collector named Aldous Vane died and left it. Six weeks of work, they said. Generous terms."

"Aldous Vane was my father. He has been dead for ninety years." Lucian's voice did not change, but something behind it did. "There is no agency, Ms. Mercer, not really — or rather, there is, but it has been bringing cataloguers to this door for ninety years without ever once wondering why the library is never finished. The house calls them. It has been calling, all this time, for the one who could read the books that matter. They always leave before dusk. They always feel, around four in the afternoon, an overwhelming need to be gone." He looked at her steadily. "You felt it. I know you felt it. And you stayed anyway."

June thought of the agent stopping the car at the gate. The light's tricky out here. She thought of the four o'clock pull she had noticed and overruled because she was methodical and tired and wanted the contract done.

"The books that matter," she said slowly. "You said the curse is mine to break. In the conservatory. You said that before I'd told you my name."

"I said it because it is true, and because I have learned not to waste the rare nights when someone stays." For the first time the careful stillness of him cracked, just slightly, and what showed underneath was not hunger and not threat. It was something far more dangerous to her resolve. It was hope, ninety years deep. "There is a book in this library, Ms. Mercer, that holds the unmaking of all of this. I cannot find it. I have had ninety years and I cannot find it, because the curse that keeps me also keeps me from it — I cannot read the one thing that would free me. But a cataloguer can. A cataloguer's whole art is finding the one book in a thousand."

The torch was growing warm in June's hand. Outside the filthy glass, the moon had cleared whatever held it, and the conservatory filled, very faintly, with grey light, and she could see him properly now without her little beam: a tired man in old clothes in a wild garden, asking.

"That's why the house wants me," June said.

"That is why the house wants you." Lucian inclined his head. "I will not pretend the wanting is gentle. But I will promise you this, here, before anything else is said between us — I have not fed on a living soul in ninety years, and I do not intend to begin with the first person in a century to look me in the eye. You are safer in this house, tonight, than the house would like you to believe."

June Mercer stood in a locked conservatory with a dead man and a jungle and a curse, and did the only thing her nature had ever really allowed her to do.

She set down her satchel, took out her notebook, and said, "Then you had better tell me everything you know about the book."
