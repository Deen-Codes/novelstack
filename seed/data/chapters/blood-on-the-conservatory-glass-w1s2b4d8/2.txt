June was a methodical person, and methodical people do not panic in the dark. They make lists.

She made one now, standing in the locked hall, speaking it under her breath the way she spoke inventory. One: the front door would not open, and neither would the two side doors she found by feeling along the walls. Two: the ground-floor windows, when she tried them, did not open either, and the glass — she rapped it with a knuckle — was thicker than glass had any business being. Three: her phone had a torch, which was working, and no signal, which was not. Four: it was not yet fully night, and she was not yet truly afraid, and she intended to use the gap between those two facts to be sensible.

She would find a room with a working lamp, or a fireplace, or at minimum a comfortable chair, and she would wait out the night, and in the morning the doors would presumably unlock the way they had locked, and she would walk out and never take a rural contract again.

The torch beam swung ahead of her down a corridor. Portraits watched her pass — Vanes, she assumed, generations of them, pale narrow faces above dark collars. They were not, she told herself firmly, watching her. Painted eyes simply did that.

The corridor ended at another set of double doors, and these stood open, and beyond them the darkness changed quality. It got larger. It got colder, and wetter, and it smelled, astonishingly, green — of earth and leaves and growing things.

The conservatory.

June stepped through, and her torch beam climbed and climbed and lost itself in the vast vault of glass overhead. The grime on the panes had turned the moon to a smear of grey. All around her, in the dark, the long-dead garden of the Vanes had not died at all. It had run wild. Ferns stood shoulder-high. Vines had swallowed the iron pillars. Something with white flowers, luminous and faintly sweet, climbed toward the broken light.

It should have been impossible. There was no one to water it, no one to warm it; the panes were filthy and half the glass was cracked. A conservatory left ninety years to itself should be a graveyard of brown sticks.

This was a jungle, and it was thriving, and June stood at its edge with her small light and felt, for the first time, the fear arrive properly — not as panic, but as a cold and certain knowledge that the rules she had brought into this house were not the rules the house was using.

That was when the voice spoke, out of the dark, from somewhere among the ferns.

It was a man's voice, low and unhurried, and it sounded — this was the detail that would not leave her afterward — it sounded relieved.

"You came after dusk," the voice said. "They never come after dusk. Ninety years, and not one of them ever stayed for the dark."

June did not scream. She was proud of that, later. She turned her torch toward the sound, and the beam found, between two great arching ferns, the shape of a man.

"My name is June Mercer," she said, and was glad her voice held. "I was hired to catalogue this library. The doors have locked me in, and I would like, very much, for you to explain that to me."

The man stepped forward into her light. He was tall, and dressed in clothes that belonged to no decade she could name, and his face was the pale narrow face of every portrait in the corridor she had just walked down.

"I would like that too," he said. "I have wanted, for a very long time, for someone to ask."
