The estate agent would not come past the gate.

June noticed it the way she noticed everything — quietly, professionally, filing it for later. The woman had driven her the forty minutes out from the city, had talked brightly the entire way about the late Mr. Aldous Vane and his "really very significant" private library, and had then stopped the car at the foot of the long gravel drive, handed June a ring of cold iron keys, and declined, with a smile gone suddenly thin, to go any further.

"The light's tricky out here," the agent said. "Easy to lose track of the hour. You'll want to be done well before dark, Ms. Mercer. The house — settles, after dark. Old houses do."

Then she had turned the car around and driven away, and June had stood alone in the October afternoon with a satchel of archival supplies and a key ring heavier than her own hand, and looked up at Vane House.

It was beautiful in the way that frightened people. Three storeys of dark brick wrapped in ivy gone red with the season, a roofline jagged with chimneys, and along the entire east wing a conservatory — a vast cathedral of iron and glass, blind with grime, its panes catching the low sun and throwing it back in broken pieces.

June had spent eleven years cataloguing the libraries of the dead. She did not, as a rule, find houses frightening. Houses were containers; she liked containers; she liked the work of imposing order on what the dead left disordered behind them. The agency had described this one as a six-week contract and a generous one, and June, who was thirty-four and tired and saving with grim determination toward a flat that would finally be only hers, had said yes before the sentence was finished.

The front door took three keys and a shoulder.

Inside, the air was cold and still and smelled of paper — that particular sweetish dust of a great many old books breathing slowly in the dark. June stood in the hall and felt the familiar small lift of professional pleasure. A staircase curved up into shadow. Doorways opened left and right. And directly ahead, through a set of double doors standing open, she could see the library, and her breath caught a little, because it was magnificent.

It rose two full storeys. A gallery ran around the upper level. Books stood in their thousands, leather and cloth and vellum, and a rolling ladder waited on its brass rail like a patient animal.

"All right, Mr. Vane," June said aloud, to the cold and the dust and the dead collector. "Let's see what you've left me."

She worked until the light began to go.

It went quickly, out here — the agent had been right about that much. One moment the tall windows were full of grey afternoon, and the next the gold had drained out of the world and the library was filling with a blue dimness that made the spines of the books hard to read.

June straightened from the shelf she'd been listing, pressed her hands into the small of her back, and decided she had done a fair first day. She would find the lights, gather her things, and call the agency about a taxi.

She did not find the lights.

She found switches, and flipped them, and nothing happened — and she was still standing in the dimming hall with her thumb on a dead switch when, all through the house, one after another, she heard the doors begin to lock.

It was not a slam. That was the worst of it, somehow. It was a soft, deliberate, traveling sound — a click, and then a click further off, and then another, the bolts of Vane House sliding home in sequence as the last light failed, like a creature closing its eyes for the night.

June stood very still in the dark with the iron keys in her hand, and understood that the front door, when she reached it, was not going to open.
