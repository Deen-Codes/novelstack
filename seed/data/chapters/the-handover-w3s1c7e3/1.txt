Priya had worked nights on Ward Six for nine years, and she could tell within one shift whether a new doctor would last, and she had decided about the new one before he had finished taking his coat off.

He wouldn't last. That was her professional assessment, formed at 8pm on a Tuesday in the depth of a hard winter. He was too gentle. You could see it on him like a coat he hadn't learned to take off — the new agency doctor, Sam something, standing in the doorway of the staff room looking at the whiteboard full of names with an expression that was already, before a single one of those names had become a hard night, too soft for the work.

Ward Six did not reward gentle. Priya had learned that the slow way. Ward Six was a medical ward in a hospital that was always one nurse short and always two beds over, and nights on Ward Six were a long quiet war of small emergencies, and the people who lasted were the people who learned to hold their feelings somewhere a patient couldn't reach them. Priya had got very good at that. It was, she would have told you, simply part of being good at the job. You could not be useful to a frightened person at 3am if you were busy being frightened with them.

So she watched the gentle new doctor and she thought: a month. Maybe less. He'll go somewhere with daylight in it.

She gave him the handover the way she gave everyone the handover, fast and complete and without warmth, because warmth was not what a handover was for. Bed three, the gentleman in bed three, his sats, his history, the thing to watch for. Bed nine, the lady in bed nine, her family, the conversation that was coming and was not going to be an easy one. Sam listened. She noticed that he listened properly — no notes, just listening, the way you listen when you intend to actually remember a person rather than a chart — and she filed that away as a point in his favour and then filed the point in his favour somewhere she wouldn't have to look at it.

The night went the way the bad ones go.

It went fine until about 1am and then it stopped going fine. The gentleman in bed three, the one she'd flagged, turned in the way she'd known he might, and for forty minutes Ward Six was all hands and fast quiet voices and the particular focused calm that is the opposite of panic, and Priya did what she always did, which was run it — steady, certain, the fixed point the night turned around.

And Sam was good. That surprised her, a little, and she would think about it later. The gentle new doctor, in the actual emergency, was not gentle at all; he was quick and exact and unflustered, and he did the work, and the gentleman in bed three was, by 2am, stable, and the night exhaled.

It was after, that was the thing. It was the after that Priya did not have a category for.

Because at half past two she went to find Sam to sign something, and she found him not in the staff room but standing in the dark by the window at the end of the corridor, the long window that looked out over the car park and the city's low orange glow. And he was not on his phone. He was just standing there, and his shoulders were doing a thing, a small thing, and Priya understood, with a strange lurch, that the gentle new doctor had walked away from the ward to stand alone in the dark and feel the thing she had taught herself not to feel.

She should have left him to it. That was the correct, professional, Ward Six thing to do. Everybody had their corner of the corridor. You did not intrude on a person's corner of the corridor.

She went and stood next to him instead. She didn't plan to. Her feet just did it.

"It got hard for a minute back there," she said, looking out at the orange glow, not at him, because not-at-him was easier for both of them. "Bed three."

"Yeah." Sam's voice was steady but only just. "Sorry. I'm — I'm fine. I just needed a minute. I haven't learned the trick yet."

"What trick."

"The one all of you have." He gestured, vaguely, back towards the lit ward. "Where it doesn't get in. I watched you run that and it didn't touch you, and I don't — I can't do that yet, and I keep thinking I should be able to, and then I think maybe the day I can do it is the day I should worry." He pressed the heel of his hand briefly against one eye. "Don't write me off, charge nurse. I saw you do it at the handover. I saw you decide I wouldn't last."

Priya was quiet for a moment.

She had decided that. He was right. And standing here at the dark window at half past two with this man who felt everything, she found that her certainty had developed a crack in it — because the trick, the one she was so proud of, the not letting it in — she had never once, in nine years, asked herself what it had cost her to learn it. She had only ever counted what it saved.

"It's not a trick," she said, finally. "It's a wall. And walls keep things out and they also — they don't only keep out the bad nights, Sam. They're not clever like that. They keep out everything." She looked at him then, properly, the gentle doctor she had written off six hours ago. "Maybe don't be in a hurry to build one. That's all. Maybe the corridor and the dark window is a better system than mine, and I've just never let myself find out."

Sam looked back at her. The orange light was on both their tired faces.

"Stand here a minute, then," he said. "If your better system's a wall. Try mine. Just a minute. The ward will hold."

And Priya, who had spent nine years being the fixed point the night turned around, stood at the dark window next to the new doctor and let herself, for one entire minute, simply feel the weight of the night she had just carried — and to her enormous surprise the wall did not fall down, and the ward did hold, and something in her chest that had been clenched for the better part of a decade loosened, very slightly, in the dark.
