In Ashcombe the lamps were lit in a particular order, and the order was older than anyone living, and Nell Whitcombe had never once asked why, because in Ashcombe asking why was a thing that marked you, and Nell had spent her whole life trying not to be marked.

The town sat in a hollow, and the fog came into the hollow every evening the way water comes into a cupped hand — slowly, and then all at once, and then to the brim. By full dark the fog stood in the streets of Ashcombe higher than a tall man's head, and a person caught out in it could lose the road, the houses, the sound of their own footsteps, the whole solid world, in the space of a held breath. So the lamps were lit. Forty-one lamps on iron posts, threaded through the town on a route that Nell's father had walked at dusk for thirty years, and his mother before him, and her father before her, back and back into a past Ashcombe did not write down.

Nell had walked the route for two years now. She had walked it since the morning they found her father's lamplighting pole leaning against the first post on the route, and the lamp above it lit, and her father nowhere — not in the fog, not in the hollow, not anywhere a search could reach. The town had not called it strange. That was the thing about Ashcombe that Nell had only understood after; the town had not been surprised. The town had simply looked at sixteen-year-old Nell, and at her father's pole, and decided, in its quiet collective way, that the lamps would now be hers.

She had taught herself the order from her father's book. It was the only thing he had left that explained anything, a small black book of lamp numbers in a long sequence, and the sequence was not the sequence of the streets. That was the first thing Nell had learned and the strangest. If you simply walked Ashcombe and lit each lamp as you reached it, you would light them in the wrong order entirely. The book's sequence sent the lamplighter doubling back, crossing the town, lighting the lamp at the foot of Cooper's Lane before the lamp at the top of it, lighting the four lamps of the square not together but scattered through the route like beads spaced on a string. It made the walk longer. It made no sense. Nell had followed it exactly, every dusk, for two years, because her father's book did not explain and her father was not there to ask and following exactly was the only safety she had.

The night it went wrong was an ordinary night. The fog came into the hollow as it always did. Nell took up the pole and the book and the small jar of matches, and she began the route, and she was thirty-one lamps into the forty-one when the boy ran into her in the murk and made her drop the book.

He was a Ashcombe boy, frightened, lost in the thickening fog the way the fog liked to make children lost, and Nell did the kind thing — she steadied him, and turned him toward the lit lamp behind her, and sent him running home along the line of light. It cost her perhaps a minute. It cost her, also, her place in the book, because when she picked the book up out of the wet cobbles the pages had fallen open and she could not, in the failing light and the rising fog, be certain which lamp was next.

She thought it was lamp thirty-two. She was almost certain it was thirty-two. And lamp thirty-two stood right there, an arm's reach away, glassy with fog-damp, unlit.

Nell lit it.

It was, she would understand later, lamp thirty-five. She had lit lamp thirty-five in the place of lamp thirty-two; she had put one bead on the string out of its order, a small thing, a minute's mistake, the kind of mistake the book had spent two years and thirty years and longer warning her wordlessly never to make.

For a moment nothing happened. The lamp caught, and burned, and the fog stood around its light the way fog always stood.

And then, all through Ashcombe, in the deep grey blind heart of the fog, Nell heard the lamps she had already lit — the thirty-one behind her, the careful patient sequence — begin, one by one, in their correct order, to go out. And in the dark that came rushing back up the route toward her, something that had waited a very long time to be let in began, without any hurry at all, to come.
