In the morning the power was back and the festival was the day after and everything was different and nothing had changed, which is the particular vertigo of the morning after.

Nora got to the bakery at five. Caleb was already there. He had been already there every morning for two months, so this proved nothing, except that when she came through the door he looked up from the proofing rack and they both stopped, and the looking-up had a new weight in it, and the not-saying-anything went on a beat too long, and then they both, at the exact same moment, decided to be cowards about it and talk about bread.

"Crust on the festival loaves came out clean," Caleb said.

"Good," Nora said.

"The candlelight bake. The color's actually better than the electric. Might be the slower —"

"Caleb."

"— heat curve, the —"

"*Caleb.*"

He stopped. Set down the loaf. The kitchen at five in the morning had that blue pre-dawn light in it, the ovens warming, the radio off because neither of them had reached for it.

"I'm not good at this part," he said. It cost him something to say; she could see it cost him. "The line after the line. I've spent fifteen years being good at the work and bad at the rest. I burnt a marriage about as thoroughly as I burnt that service, and for worse reasons, and I came to Hartley Creek genuinely believing the smartest thing I could do with the back half of my life was keep my head down and weigh the flour and not — reach for anything." He looked at her. "And then I reached for you in a power cut like a man who'd learned nothing. And I've been standing here since four o'clock terrified you'd come in and tell me it was the storm. People do storm things. I'd understand it. I'd —"

"It wasn't the storm," Nora said.

The kitchen held very still around the sentence.

"I have wanted to do that," she said, "since the night you broke a croissant in half and handed it across the slab and asked me for a Tuesday. I've wanted it through every gram you've weighed and every customer you've terrified and the entire stupid argument about the rye. It was not the storm, Caleb. The storm just turned the lights off so I'd stop being able to see all the reasons I'd lined up for not."

He crossed the kitchen. This time he did it fast — fast as dough, faster — and the second kiss was not candlelit or storm-struck or accidental, it was five o'clock in the morning and entirely on purpose, and when it ended he kept his forehead against hers and they breathed in the warming-oven air, and for once Caleb Ros did not have a number for anything.

"There's a problem," Nora said into the small space between them.

"Mm."

"You work for me."

"I'm aware." She felt rather than saw him almost smile. "I've thought about it. I think the honest version is: I'd like to not work *for* you. I'd like to work *with* you. There's a man in Carver County retiring out of a pastry-equipment lease and — no. Not now. Not at five in the morning two days before the festival." He pulled back enough to look at her properly. "But I have ideas, Nora. Steady ones. About this place not closing. About it being something we built. If you'd want to hear them. After."

*After.* It was the second time a word had landed in her chest and refused to leave. *Not with you. After.* Small words, both of them, the kind you'd never weigh — and she was learning, from a man who weighed everything, that those were the ones that turned out to mass the most.

"I'd want to hear them," she said.

Then the bell over the door went, and it was old Sam Quist forty minutes early for a coffee that wasn't ready, and the ordinary day came down over the morning like flour through a sieve, even and forgiving. Caleb stepped back to the proofing rack. Nora went to start the urn. They moved around each other in the small kitchen the way they had for two months, except that now every pass was a sentence, every handed-over loaf a held hand, and the festival was tomorrow, and somewhere under all of it the rye — both ryes, hers and his — waited side by side in the cold case for a Tuesday that was going to have to wait its turn.

They had, after all, a town to feed first.
