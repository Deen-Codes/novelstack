One year later, on the morning of the Harvest Festival, Nora Whitfield got up at four o'clock and decided, fresh, to feed the town.

She did it gladly. That was the part that still surprised her, some mornings — that the four o'clock alarm no longer felt like a stone swallowed but like a door opening onto something warm. She dressed in the dark. She did not wake Caleb, because Caleb had been at the bakery since half past three, the way he was most festival mornings, the difficult man having turned out, underneath the difficulty, to be the most reliable person she had ever known.

The walk to the bakery took six minutes. Hartley Creek at four a.m. was doing its held-breath thing, porch lights and the diner's neon and somebody's screen door, and Nora walked through it the way you walk through a song you have finally, fully, learned.

The sign out front said WHITFIELD'S, because some things do not change and should not. But the window beside it had a second card now, hand-lettered, hung where BACK SOON had been for fourteen months and a lifetime: WHITFIELD'S BAKERY — EST. 1968 — APPRENTICE KITCHEN. The first apprentice, a serious seventeen-year-old named Priya who reminded Caleb so much of the Copenhagen kid that he'd had to leave the room the day he hired her, was already inside, weighing flour. She had been taught to weigh it. She had also been taught, by Nora, that you count twice and believe the second number, and that a thing tastes like a day of the week, and that the soul of a kitchen is not a recipe but a person choosing.

The cold case held both ryes. It had held both ryes for a year. Her mother's, dense and caraway-forward and unchanged since 1971, sold out by ten o'clock every single day. And beside it the *Ros Rye*, longer-fermented, deeper, which Caleb had wanted to name with a hydration percentage in it and which Nora had named instead, over his objections, after the man who'd walked into her kitchen on a Thursday without knocking, as if the room owed him an apology, and had stayed to learn that it didn't, and had given the room far more than he ever took.

He was at the marble slab when she came in. He looked up — sleeves rolled, flour to the forearm, no pencil mark today because she had finally cured him of writing on himself — and the looking-up still had its weight, a year on, the good weight, the checked-twice kind.

"Festival rye's resting," he said. "Forty loaves. Her way."

"Croissants?"

"Shattering as we speak."

Nora crossed the kitchen and stood beside him at the slab — beside, the way you stand next to someone for the things too big for across — and looked at the day's work coming up around them, the proofing rack full, the apprentice weighing, the ovens warm, the town beyond the glass waiting to be fed.

"We should tell them," Caleb said. "Today. At the table. Before someone notices the ring and Marjorie Pell finds out from the wrong direction and never forgives either of us."

Nora looked down at her left hand, at the small plain band that had been there since a Tuesday in spring — of course a Tuesday — when Caleb had broken a croissant in half over this same slab and asked her, instead of for a Tuesday, for all of them.

"At the table," she agreed. "Hers and yours, both ryes, the whole street watching. She'd have liked that. A thing announced honestly, in the middle of the work, with bread."

"A thing isn't finished until it's honest," Caleb said.

"You stole that from your mother."

"I'm aware. She'd have liked you. She weighed nothing in her life and she'd still have liked you." He brushed a streak of flour from Nora's cheekbone with his thumb, slow, the way he had in a power cut a year and a season ago, and then he went back to his croissants, because the festival opened at nine and the town was leaning, gently, on a promise.

Outside, the clean rinsed light came up over Hartley Creek. Inside the bakery her mother had built and the two of them had not let close, the ovens did their warm and honest work, and the second loaf — every second loaf, the one you bake after the first, the one that proves the recipe was never the point — rose exactly as it should.
