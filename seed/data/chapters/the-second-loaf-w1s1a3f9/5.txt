They fought about the rye in October, and it was, Nora understood even while it was happening, not really about the rye.

The Hartley Creek Harvest Festival was three weeks out. It was the town's biggest day — a Main Street closed to cars, a fiddle band, the historical society's apple pressing, and, traditionally, Whitfield's at the center of it with a long table of bread. Her mother had built the festival presence over decades. The dark seeded rye, in particular, was the thing people drove in from neighboring counties for. It was on the recipe cards in that looping certain hand. It had not changed since 1971.

Caleb wanted to change it.

"The crumb is heavy," he said. They were doing inventory after close, the ledger open between them on the marble slab, and he said it the way he said all true things, plainly, as if plainness were a courtesy. "It's a good loaf. It would be a better loaf with a longer ferment and less caraway. I could —"

"No."

"Nora —"

"That's my mother's rye." She heard her voice climb and didn't stop it. "That recipe is in her handwriting. People have eaten that bread at that festival for fifty years. It is not *heavy*, it is *traditional*, and you do not get to —" She put her hand flat on the recipe card, covering the looping ink, a gesture she recognized as childish and made anyway. "We agreed. You touch the pastry. The bread is the soul of the place and the soul of the place is hers."

The kitchen was very quiet. Caleb looked at her hand on the card.

"Okay," he said.

It was too easy. She had braced for the argument and he had simply set it down, and the lack of resistance left her off balance, swinging at air.

"That's it? Okay?"

"You drew a line on the first day," he said. "Pastry mine, bread yours. I've kept to it. I'll keep to it now." He closed the ledger. "But I'm going to say one thing, and then I'll never say it again, and you can throw it out."

Nora folded her arms. "Say it."

"The recipe isn't her." Caleb said it very gently, more gently than she had known he had in him. "I've watched you for two months now. You touch that card like it's going to stop breathing if you put it down. And I understand it — I do, I had a roll of my mother's pencils for years, I couldn't sharpen them, I couldn't lose them, they just sat in a drawer being a wound. But the rye isn't your mother. Your mother was the woman who got up at four every morning for thirty years and decided, fresh, every single day, to feed this town. *That's* the soul of the place. The deciding. Not the caraway."

Nora's eyes had gone hot. She hated that. She turned slightly toward the cold cases so he wouldn't have all of her face.

"She would have hated you," she said thickly. "You weigh the flour."

"She might have," Caleb agreed. "Or she might have weighed it too, eventually, if someone she trusted had shown her why. People who get up at four for thirty years are usually willing to learn. That's the same muscle." A pause. "I'm not asking to change the festival rye, Nora. I withdrew that. I'm asking whether you'd let me, just once, on an ordinary nothing Tuesday, with nobody watching, bake her rye and bake my rye, and you taste them side by side. And whatever you decide after that, I'll never raise it again. If you want the caraway, we keep the caraway forever. I'll make it your mother's way until the day this oven dies."

She didn't answer for a long moment. Outside, the festival posters were already up in the windows along Main Street, and the air had that turned, woodsmoke smell that meant the year was tipping over.

"Tuesday," Nora said at last, to the cold cases. "Nobody watching. And if hers is better, you never say the word *ferment* to me again."

"Deal."

She heard him gather his knives, the small clean sounds of him rolling them away. At the door he stopped.

"For what it's worth," Caleb said, "I think hers might win. Fifty years of a town loving a loaf isn't nothing. That's data too. You taught me that — you and your Tuesdays. I don't think a thing is right just because the grams are right anymore." A beat. "You did that. I came here to be steady and you went and made me honest instead."

The bell over the door went, and he was gone into the October dark, and Nora stood alone in the bakery with her hand still resting on her mother's handwriting. She did not lift it for a long time. But when she finally did, it was not a wound she was setting down. It felt, for the first time, more like a hand she was finally able to let go of, gently, the way you release someone at the end of a dance — not because the dance meant nothing, but because it was over, and there was the rest of the evening still to be lived.
