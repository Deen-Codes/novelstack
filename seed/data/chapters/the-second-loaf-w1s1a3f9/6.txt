The storm came in two days before the festival and took the power with it.

It was the kind of October storm Hartley Creek got once or twice a year — wind that came down off the ridge and leaned on the town like it had a grudge, rain going sideways, and then, at half past nine in the evening, every light on Main Street going dark at once with a sound like the whole world clearing its throat.

Nora was at the bakery. She had been at the bakery far too late, far too often, lately, and she had stopped examining why, because the why had a name and the name rolled knives away into canvas every evening before he left.

He had not left. That was the thing. The storm had come up fast while they were both still there finishing the festival prep — forty loaves' worth of dough, resting and proofing for the morning bake — and now the power was gone, and the proofing was a problem.

"The dough's fine for an hour," Caleb said in the dark. He had found the emergency candles in the drawer where her mother had always kept them; he knew where things were in this kitchen now, knew it the way you know a house. "Two, if the temperature holds. After that the proof goes long and the festival bread is ruined and we've got nothing for the table."

"The oven's gas." Nora's mind was moving fast. "If the power's out but the gas holds we can still bake — but the deck oven's electronic ignition, we'd have to —"

"Match. Manually." Caleb was already moving. "I've done it. It's stupid and it works."

So they baked through the storm.

It was the strangest night of Nora's life and, she would think later, one of the truest. The two of them in a candlelit kitchen with the wind shouldering the windows, lighting a sixty-year-old deck oven by hand, loading it by feel and firelight, judging the bake by smell and the color the crust took in the orange flicker. Caleb worked the way he always worked — clean, fast, no waste — but in the dark something in him had loosened. He talked. He told her about Copenhagen, the kid sending photos of his bread. He told her about the pencils in the drawer. He asked about her eleven years away and listened, actually listened, in the particular way of a man for whom listening had once been a skill that mattered on a line.

"You never said why you came back," he said, sliding a loaf out, the crust dark and singing faintly as it cooled. "Eleven years. Then home. People don't usually."

Nora pushed her hair off her face with the back of a floury wrist. The candle between them threw both their shadows huge and trembling on the wall.

"She got sick in the spring," she said. "And I came for what everyone thought would be a visit. And then I stood in this kitchen one morning while she slept, and I made the cardamom buns from her card, and the whole shop filled up with the smell of them, and a woman I didn't know came in and said *oh, thank God, I thought for a second you'd closed.*" Nora's voice did something and she let it. "And I understood that this place wasn't a building. It was a promise the town was leaning on. And I couldn't be the one who broke it. So I stayed." She looked at him across the candle. "I stayed for a promise. And then she died, and the promise was the only piece of her I had a job description for, so I held onto it like — like you and the pencils, I suppose. Too hard."

Caleb had gone still. The storm was easing; she could hear it pulling back off the ridge, the wind losing its argument.

"And now?" he said.

"Now," Nora said slowly, "I think I stay because I want to. Which is different. It took me four months and a difficult pastry chef and a power cut to tell the difference." She managed a small wet laugh. "Don't let it go to your head."

He came around the slab. Not fast — Caleb did nothing fast that wasn't dough — but deliberately, the way he crossed a kitchen, every step earning its place, until he was standing close in the candlelight with flour on his forearms and the smell of dark crust all around them and the town dark and quiet beyond the glass.

"Nora." Just her name. He said it like it was a number he'd checked twice and believed.

"I know," she said. Because she did. Because it had been there for weeks, in the half croissant across the slab, in *not smooth, not with you*, in every evening she found a reason to keep him later.

He lifted one hand and brushed a streak of flour from her cheekbone with his thumb, slow, careful, and Nora turned her face very slightly into the warmth of his palm, and the last candle guttered, and in the dark of the bakery her mother had built they finally, finally stopped counting — grams and Tuesdays and ninety days and eleven years — and the kiss, when it came, tasted like woodsmoke and dark rye and the particular relief of a long-held breath let go.

Outside, the storm moved off the town entirely. And somewhere under the candlelit quiet, forty loaves of festival bread went on rising, exactly as they should.
