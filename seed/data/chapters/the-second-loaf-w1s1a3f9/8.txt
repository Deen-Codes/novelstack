The Hartley Creek Harvest Festival opened under a clean, rinsed sky, the storm having scrubbed the air bright, and by nine o'clock Main Street was closed to cars and loud with fiddle music and the long Whitfield's table stood at the heart of it, exactly where her mother had always stood it.

Nora had not slept much. She did not feel tired. She felt like a struck bell.

The table was a thing of glory. Caleb had not changed the festival rye — he had kept his word, every loaf of it baked her mother's way, caraway and all — but he had built around it. A tier of croissants with that honest shatter. The plum tarts. A dark seeded boule and a soft braided loaf and the cardamom buns stacked in a golden pyramid that drew children like a spell. Two months ago Whitfield's had been a half-empty cold case and a missing eleven dollars. Today it was the brightest table on the street, and the town came to it in a steady current, and Nora stood behind it handing over bread and change and could not stop a smile that felt almost unmanageable.

Marjorie Pell bought a boule and announced to three people that she'd *always known the young Whitfield girl had it in her.* Old Sam Quist bought a cardamom bun and ate it with his eyes closed. The teachers came, and the fiddle band's bass player, and a woman from two counties over who said *I drove in for the rye, I drive in every year,* and Nora wrapped the rye for her in waxed paper and felt her mother standing, briefly and warmly, at her shoulder.

And then, a little after eleven, Bryce Halloran's too-clean car turned onto a street it was not allowed to be on.

He didn't get far — a festival marshal waved him off Main — but he parked and walked in, and Nora watched him scan the crowd and find the Whitfield's table and find Caleb behind it, sleeves rolled, restocking croissants, entirely in his element. She felt the old smooth-water dread rise in her chest.

Halloran came over. He picked up a croissant without asking and turned it in his fingers in front of the table, in front of the customers, performing again, always performing.

"Caleb Ros," he said. "Selling pastry off a folding table at a county fair. How the mighty —"

"Bryce." Caleb didn't go smooth this time. Nora noticed that first, before anything else: his face stayed open, stayed his. He set down the tray. "You drove a long way to watch me be happy. That seems like a poor use of a Saturday, but I won't tell you how to spend it."

"Happy." Halloran laughed his thin laugh. "You torched a Lemière service. You'll always be the man who —"

"He's the man," said Nora, stepping up beside Caleb, into it, where she belonged, "who put himself between your chef de cuisine and a nineteen-year-old kid that man was breaking on purpose. That's the service he burnt. I'd burn it again. I'd light the match for him."

A small silence opened in the festival noise. Marjorie Pell, holding her boule, had stopped to listen. So had the woman from two counties over. So had old Sam.

"And as for the folding table," Nora went on, and she was not loud, she had learned not to be loud, "this folding table fed half of Carver County its breakfast this morning. This town drove in for it. So I think what you're actually looking at, Mr. Halloran, is a man who walked away from a kitchen that broke people and found one that doesn't. If that looks like falling to you, I'd take a hard look at where you're standing."

Bryce Halloran put the croissant down. He had no audience anymore — the audience had quietly, entirely, become Nora's — and a performer with no audience is just a man holding a pastry he didn't pay for. He left. The festival folded back over the gap, fiddle and woodsmoke and children, and the current of customers resumed as if nothing had interrupted it.

Caleb was looking at her. They were shoulder to shoulder behind her mother's table in the middle of her mother's festival, and his face was doing the thing it had done at the marble slab on the very first morning — recognition, but warmer now, infinitely warmer.

"You said you'd light the match for me," he said quietly, under the music.

"I did." Nora handed a cardamom bun to a small boy and took his sticky coins. "I meant it. Don't make it weird."

"I'm not making it weird." But he was almost smiling, the rare full thing, and he picked up the croissant tray again, and just before he turned away to restock he said, low, only for her: "After the festival. The Tuesday. The two ryes. And then the other thing — the lease, the ideas, the *with* not *for.*" A beat. "I've checked the numbers twice, Nora. Both times they say stay."

The fiddle band swung into something bright. The town moved past the table in the clean rinsed light. And Nora Whitfield, who had driven home from a funeral with a stone in her throat and a dare in her chest, stood in the middle of Main Street and let herself believe the second number.
