The cloud arrived on a Tuesday, which Nora would later consider unfair, given everything Tuesdays had come to mean.

It arrived in the person of a man named Bryce Halloran, who drove in from the city in a car too clean for the season and stood in the middle of Whitfield's at the busy part of the morning and said, in a voice pitched for an audience, "I heard a rumor and I had to see it. Caleb Ros. Slumming."

Nora was at the register. She felt the room change temperature.

Caleb came out of the kitchen wiping his hands, and his face when he saw Bryce Halloran did something she had not witnessed it do before. It went smooth. Not calm — smooth, the way water goes smooth right before it goes over an edge.

"Bryce."

"The great Caleb Ros." Halloran turned to the room, to Marjorie Pell and the two teachers and old Sam Quist with his coffee, inviting them in. "Did he tell you? When he came begging for this job? Did he tell you he torched a service at the Lemière — full dining room, investors in the house — walked off the line mid-service and let the whole pastry section collapse? Eleven thousand dollars of comped tables. They had to —"

"That's enough," Nora said.

She did not say it loudly. She had learned, from a man who never raised his voice, that you did not need to. But she said it with the whole weight of the counter behind her, the worn pale patch, the BACK SOON card, her mother's recipes in every loaf on the shelf, and the room turned to her instead of to Bryce Halloran, and Halloran's performance lost its floor.

"This is my bakery," Nora said. "Mr. Ros works here. If you came forty minutes to gossip, you can drive forty minutes back, and you can buy something on the way out, because we are a business, and I have a town to feed."

For a moment Halloran's mouth worked. Then he laughed, a thin laugh, and the laugh told everyone watching exactly who had won, and he left without buying anything, and the bell over the door jangled too hard behind him.

The shop exhaled. Marjorie Pell, loyal in the way of small towns once a side has been chosen, said loudly that some people had no manners, and old Sam Quist said something about the city that made the teachers laugh, and the morning closed back over the moment like water.

But Caleb had gone back into the kitchen, and Nora found him there twenty minutes later, not working. Standing at the cold deck oven with his hands flat on the steel.

"It's true," he said, before she could speak. "Most of it. I walked off a service. I let the section go down."

"Okay."

"You should hear why." He didn't turn around. "There was a kid on my line. Nineteen. Talented — genuinely, the real thing. And the chef de cuisine had a habit, with the young ones, of —" Caleb's hands pressed flatter against the steel. "Of finding the soft place and pushing on it. Quietly. For years nobody named it. That night he had the kid in tears over a quenelle, in front of the room, and I watched it happen the way I'd watched it happen a hundred times, and something in me just —" He stopped. "I told the kid to put his knife down and go home. And then I followed him out. And I didn't go back."

The deck oven ticked, cooling, though it had not been on.

"Eleven thousand dollars of comped tables," Caleb said. "That's the number that travels. Bryce likes that number. The other number — the kid, who's working in Copenhagen now and sends me a photo of his bread every few months — that number doesn't travel. It's not as fun."

Nora thought about her own numbers. Eleven dollars short in a drawer. Ninety days. Count twice; believe the second.

"You burnt a service to put yourself between a bully and a nineteen-year-old," she said.

"When you say it like that it sounds —"

"It sounds like the most honest thing I've heard about you." Nora crossed the kitchen and stood beside him at the cold oven, both of them facing the steel, the way you stand next to someone rather than across from them when the thing being said is too big for eye contact. "A thing isn't finished until it's honest. You told me that. I've been thinking it was about croissants."

Caleb let out a breath that was almost, not quite, a laugh.

"I don't burn services anymore," he said. "I want you to know that. I came here to be — steady. Quiet. To not be a man with a cloud."

"I don't want you quiet." The words were out before Nora had cleared them with the cautious part of herself, and she heard them land, and she did not take them back. "I want you honest. There's a difference. My mother kept this place alive on honest. You can be difficult here, Caleb. You've earned difficult. Just don't be smooth at me. I saw your face go smooth at that man and I didn't like it."

He turned, finally, away from the oven. They were standing close — closer than the conversation strictly required, close enough that she could see the faint pencil mark still ghosting on his forearm, a hydration number from three days ago he hadn't washed off.

"All right," Caleb said. Low. Careful. Like a man weighing something and finding it heavier and better than he'd expected. "Not smooth. Not with you."

Out front the bell over the door went, an ordinary customer, an ordinary Tuesday resuming, and Nora stepped back before she did something that the cautious part of herself would have a great deal to say about. But she carried it with her into the rest of the day — *not with you* — and it sat in her chest all afternoon like a coal that refused, pleasantly, to go out.
