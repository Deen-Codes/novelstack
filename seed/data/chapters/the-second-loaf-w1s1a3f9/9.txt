The Tuesday came, as Tuesdays do, the week after the festival, when Main Street had been handed back to the cars and the posters had come down and the town had settled again into its held breath.

Caleb baked both ryes. He did it the way they'd agreed — nobody watching, an ordinary nothing morning — and at midday he set two slices on the marble slab, dark and dense and seeded, and stood back, and for once put his hands behind him like a man who had decided not to weigh the outcome.

"Hers on the left," he said. "Mine on the right. Don't tell me which is which while you eat. I genuinely don't want to know what you decide until you've decided it."

Nora ate the left first. It was her mother's rye, and she would have known it blindfolded in a hurricane — the caraway forward, the crumb close and serious, fifty years of a town's mornings folded into it. It tasted like four a.m. and a looping certain hand and the smell that had once made a stranger say *thank God, I thought you'd closed.* She ate it slowly. She let it be the whole of her mouth.

Then she ate the right.

It was, she understood immediately, a better loaf. Caleb had been correct in October the way he was correct about most things — the longer ferment had given it depth, a faint sourness like good news arriving late, the crumb open enough to hold butter properly, the caraway pulled back so the actual grain could speak. It was, by every measure Caleb owned, the superior bread.

She stood at the slab for a long moment with both tastes in her mouth and the whole question of her life, it felt like, balanced on the marble between them.

"Tell me," Caleb said. Quiet. Letting her have the time.

"Hers," Nora said.

She watched him take it — watched him receive the loss of his own better loaf without a flicker of the difficult man he'd advertised on the first day, just a small nod, *all right, the caraway forever, I'll make it her way until the oven dies.* And because he took it like that — because he was, in the end, exactly as honest as he had once promised a thing had to be before it was finished — she found she could say the rest.

"Hers for the festival," Nora said. "Hers on the recipe card, in her hand, always. That loaf is a promise the town leans on and we don't break promises here." She picked up the slice from the right, his slice, the better one. "But I want this one in the case. Every day. Under its own name. *Ros Rye*, or — I don't know, we'll argue about the name, you'll want something with a gram count in it." A breath. "Because you were right about the other thing too. The soul of this place was never the caraway. It was a woman deciding, fresh, every single morning, to feed the people who walked in. And deciding isn't the same as repeating. She'd have wanted the case to grow. She'd have wanted —" Nora's voice caught and she let it, she was done being dry-faced about the things that deserved water — "she'd have wanted somebody to stand at this oven after she couldn't, and decide. You decided. So we keep both. Her rye and your rye, side by side, the way they've been sitting in that cold case for a week waiting for me to be brave enough."

Caleb came around the slab. He took the slice of his own rye out of her hand and set it down, and took the hand instead, flour and all, and held it like the most checked-twice number he had ever believed.

"With, then," he said. "Not for."

"With," Nora agreed. "Tell me about the lease. Tell me all the steady ideas. I've got nothing but Tuesdays from here to Christmas."

So he told her — the equipment lease from the retiring man in Carver County, the second oven, the apprentice program he wanted to build because of a kid in Copenhagen and a kid he hadn't been able to save fast enough, the slow honest plan for a bakery that did not close, that grew, that became a thing the two of them had built and not merely inherited. He told her standing in the warm kitchen with her hand in his, and the cold case out front held both ryes, and the BACK SOON card —

The BACK SOON card, Nora realized, looking through the kitchen doorway to the corkboard by the front door, had finally become true. Her mother had hung it for a soon that never came. But the bakery had been back. It had been back for months, fuller and brighter and more alive than it had been in years, and it was going to go on being back, and there was no longer any reason to leave the card up as a wound.

"After we close today," she said, "I'm taking the card down. The BACK SOON one. It's not a wound anymore. It just turned out to be true."

Caleb followed her gaze to the corkboard, and understood, the way he understood the things that mattered, and squeezed her floury hand once.

"I'll hold the ladder," he said.

It was, Nora thought, the most romantic sentence anyone had ever offered her. She did not tell him so. She'd save it. She had, after all, every Tuesday from here on out.
