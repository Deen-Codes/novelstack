The man in the long coat was on it.

Maddox tapped his card and moved down the swaying aisle and the bus was exactly as it always was — the Nurse asleep against the glass, the Arguer near the front already murmuring into his phone, the diesel smell, the windows holding the city back as black mirrors. And near the back, upright, patient, hands flat on his knees, the man.

There was an empty double seat across the aisle from him. Maddox took it.

He did not look at the man. He had not survived eleven years of interview rooms by looking at people before he was ready, and he was not ready; he had nothing yet, only a route and a habit and a body in the reeds, and an experienced man could feel the difference between being studied and being noticed. So Maddox sat with his own notebook closed on his knee and watched the dark go past, the same dark the man watched, and the bus carried the two of them and the city's other leftovers out toward the river.

It was the man who spoke.

"You've changed your nights," he said. His voice was quiet and even, pitched to carry the few feet across the aisle and no further. "You used to be Mondays, Wednesdays, Fridays. Now it's Thursdays. Since the woman."

Maddox turned his head, then. Slowly. The man was looking at him with mild, unhurried interest, the way you might look at a change in the weather.

"You knew her," Maddox said. It was not a question. He had decided, in the half-second of turning, that there was no point spending a card he no longer held; the man had just told him, plainly, that he had been watched as thoroughly as he had watched.

"I knew all of you." The man returned his gaze to the window. "The nurse. The angry one. You. Her. You ride a thing like this long enough, three nights a week, you stop being strangers whether you like it or not. You become — " he searched, without effort, for the word " — regulars. To each other. Eileen used to count her rows out loud when she lost her place. Did you ever notice that? Very softly. *Forty-one, forty-two.* She thought no one could hear."

The bus slowed for Tannery Lane. The doors hissed. Nobody got on; nobody got off. Eileen Verrall's stop, four lit minutes from a flat she would not see again, slid backward into the dark, and Maddox felt the cold of it move through him.

"You get off at Carlow Reach," he said.

"I do."

"There's nothing at Carlow Reach."

"No." The man said it almost gently, as though Maddox were the one who needed comforting. "There isn't. That's rather the point of it, Sergeant. You're a man who goes home to an empty flat and you've worked out that it's easier to ride a bus through the dark than to sit in the quiet of the place you're meant to be. I understood that about you months ago. I go to Carlow Reach for the same reason. There's the river, and the path, and no one, and a man can stand by the water at one in the morning and not have to be anything at all." He looked at Maddox again, and his expression had not changed, and that was somehow the worst of it. "We are not so different, you and I. We are both people who would rather be carried through the night than arrive at the end of it."

The next stop was Carlow Reach. Maddox could see the timetable's sour glow ahead, hanging over the broken ground, and beyond it the lower darkness that was the river.

"The post-mortem found bruising," Maddox said. "Her arms. Consistent with a grip."

The man stood. He did it the way he did everything, without haste, gathering nothing because he carried nothing, and he reached up and pulled the cord, and the bell sounded its small flat note through the bus, and not one of the city's leftovers stirred.

"Then you should ask," the man said, looking down at him, "who else rode this bus with her, three nights a week, for long enough to stop being a stranger. Because that's a short list, Sergeant, and you've spent eleven months memorising it, and you're on it yourself." The bus was slowing. "Carlow Reach. This is me."

The doors opened onto the cold and the river smell. The man stepped down, unhurried, and the doors hissed shut behind him, and through the black mirror of the window Maddox watched the tall narrow shape walk away from the light, toward the water, until the dark took it the way the dark took everything on Route 9.

The bus pulled away. Maddox sat very still, his closed notebook on his knee, and understood that he had just been interviewed — that the man had used the eleven minutes to learn precisely how much Maddox had, which was nothing, and to leave him with a single true and poisonous fact arranged like a gift.

It was a short list. The regulars of the last bus. And Maddox was on it, and he could not yet prove he did not belong at the top.
