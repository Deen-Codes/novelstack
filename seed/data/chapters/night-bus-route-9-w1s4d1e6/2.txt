The dead woman's name was Eileen Verrall, and once she had a name she stopped being the Knitter and became a person, which was the part of the job Maddox had never learned to do without flinching.

She had been sixty-three. She had worked evenings cleaning the offices of an insurance firm in the centre, which explained the last bus — she finished at midnight, she caught the twelve-forty, she rode it home like Maddox did, like all of them did, the city's leftovers carried through the dark together. She had a daughter in another city and a flat she had lived in for thirty years and a habit, the daughter said on the phone, voice gone thin and careful, of knitting on the bus because the rocking of it helped her hands.

She had been reported missing after four days. Nobody had reported it sooner because nobody had been close enough to notice sooner, and Maddox wrote that down in his notebook and then sat looking at it, because it was the truest and the saddest sentence in the file and it described, near enough, his own life.

The post-mortem said drowning. It also said a number of other things, in the careful hedged language of a pathologist who has learned not to hand detectives their conclusions for them, and the other things were what kept Maddox at his desk long after the late shift had emptied the building around him. There was bruising. There was bruising to the upper arms, the kind the pathologist would only describe as *consistent with* a list of possibilities, and at the bottom of the list, unemphasised, sat the possibility of a grip.

A drowning, with bruises shaped like a grip, in the reeds below the only bus stop in the city that led nowhere.

Maddox did the proper thing first. He always did the proper thing first; eleven years and a failed marriage had not cured him of it. He pulled the transport records. Route 9, the last bus, the night Eileen Verrall was last seen alive — and the records confirmed what he already knew in his body, which was that she had boarded at her usual stop at twelve-fifty-one, tapping her card, a small electronic ghost of a living woman.

The records did not say where she got off. The system logged boardings, not exits; the city had never thought it worth the cost of knowing where its leftovers went.

But Maddox had been on that bus. Not that night — he had worked a day shift that week, one of the rare ones — but he had been on it a hundred other nights, and he knew its rhythm the way you know a song, and he knew that Eileen Verrall got off at Tannery Lane, two stops before Carlow Reach. He had watched her do it dozens of times. She gathered her grey knitting into a cloth bag, and she stood while the bus was still slowing because older people did not trust their balance to the lurch of the stop, and she got off at Tannery Lane and walked the four lit minutes to her flat.

Tannery Lane. Not Carlow Reach. She had never, in all the nights Maddox had watched her, got off at Carlow Reach.

So either Eileen Verrall had, on the last night of her life, broken thirty years of habit and ridden two stops past her home to a place with nothing in it — or she had got off where she always got off, at Tannery Lane, into the four lit minutes that should have carried her safely home, and something had happened in those four minutes, and her body had ended up in the river anyway.

And then there was the man in the long coat.

Maddox had not put him in the file. That was the thing he sat with, alone, in the emptied building, the desk lamp making its small island of light. He had not put the man in the file because the man was not evidence; the man was a thing Maddox had noticed, a private named ghost from eleven months of insomniac commuting, and a detective did not get to write *there is a man I find unsettling* into the murder book and call it a line of inquiry. He needed the man to become a fact before the man could become a name.

But he could not stop seeing him. The flat patient hands on the knees. The way he never read, never slept, never reached for a phone — the way he simply watched the dark go past, like a man riding the bus not to get somewhere but to be near something. Eleven or twelve minutes, and then the cord, and then Carlow Reach, and then the dark.

Maddox checked the duty roster for the week ahead. He was on lates again from Thursday.

He told himself it was the job. He told himself a detective sergeant riding the last bus on Route 9, three nights a week, with a notebook and eleven months of accumulated, unwritten observation, was simply doing the unglamorous patient work that murder books were actually made of. He told himself he was not a lonely man going back to the only company he had, on a route that had now turned out to run, all this time, straight through the middle of a killing.

He believed about half of it.

On Thursday night Maddox stood at the third stop, in the cold, with the timetable glowing its sour orange above him, and waited for the last bus on Route 9. And he wondered, as the headlights swung at last around the distant corner, whether the man in the long coat would be on it — and whether the man, who never read and never slept and only ever watched, had spent those same eleven months noticing Maddox right back.
