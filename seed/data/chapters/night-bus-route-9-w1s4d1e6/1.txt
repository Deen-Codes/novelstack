The last bus on Route 9 left the depot at twelve-forty and did not get anywhere worth getting to until it was nearly one, and Cole Maddox had been riding it three nights a week for eleven months because his car had died in the spring and he had not yet found the money or the will to replace it.

A detective sergeant should not, by rights, have been on a night bus at all. But Maddox worked the late shift more often than the roster strictly required, partly because the late shift suited the particular shape of his insomnia and partly because going home early meant going home, and the flat he went home to had been emptier by one person since March, and the bus was, in its rattling diesel way, a kind of company.

He knew the regulars now. That was the thing about the last bus — it carried the same drowned-looking handful of people every night, the city's leftovers, the ones whose lives ran on a clock the rest of the city had agreed to ignore. Maddox had named them privately, the way you name things you see often and never speak to. There was the Nurse, who got on at the hospital stop in scrubs and slept with her head against the glass. There was the Arguer, a young man who conducted long furious phone calls with someone who was apparently never, ever in the wrong. There was the woman Maddox thought of as the Knitter, sixtyish, who worked at something soft and grey on circular needles and never once looked up.

And there was the man who got off at Carlow Reach.

Maddox had noticed him the way a detective notices things, which is to say without deciding to. The man got on at the third stop, always, a tall narrow figure in a long dark coat, and he sat near the back, and he never read anything and never slept and never looked at a phone. He simply sat, upright, patient, his hands flat on his knees, and watched the dark go past. And then, eleven or twelve minutes later, he stood, and pulled the cord, and got off at Carlow Reach.

There was nothing at Carlow Reach.

Maddox had grown up in this city and he could have drawn its bones in the dark, and he knew that Carlow Reach was a stop the council had simply forgotten to remove. It served a stretch of the old river path, a span of broken ground between the closed tannery and the water, and there were no houses there, no flats, no all-night anything. There was the river, low and brown and edged with reeds, and there was the path, and there was the dark.

For eleven months Maddox had watched a man in a long coat get off the last bus, three nights a week, at a stop that led to nothing but the river, and for eleven months Maddox had told himself a story about it, because that was what the mind did with a loose thread — it tied it off. The man worked a night job at one of the units past the tannery; there were a few still limping along down there. The man liked to walk by the water; some people did, even at one in the morning, even there. The man was simply another of the city's leftovers, and his life ran on a clock Maddox had no right to question.

The story held until a Tuesday in October, when a dog walker found a body in the reeds forty yards downstream of Carlow Reach, and the body had been in the water four or five days, and it was a woman, and it was the Knitter.

Maddox stood on the cold ground above the river while the tent went up and the lights came on, and he did not say anything to anyone, because what he was thinking did not yet have the shape of a thing you could say. He was thinking about the last bus. He was thinking about a grey-haired woman who worked at something soft on circular needles and never looked up, and a man in a long coat who got off, three nights a week, at the only stop in the city that led to the place where she had been found.

He had been riding a bus with both of them. For eleven months. The victim and — Maddox made himself finish the thought, standing in the river wind with the water moving black and patient below — and very possibly the last person to see her alive, and possibly a great deal more than that, and Maddox had sat ten feet away night after night and tied the thread off and called it nothing.

A uniformed constable came up the bank toward him, young, pale, holding a notebook he hadn't written anything in yet.

"Sarge? CID's asking who caught it."

"I did," Maddox heard himself say. "I caught it."

It was not true, not yet, not in any way the roster would recognise. But he was already turning it over, the route, the stops, the eleven minutes, the man's flat patient hands on his knees — and Maddox understood, with the particular sinking clarity of a man who has just realised he is not a witness to a case but standing in the middle of one, that whether he had caught it or not, it had very much caught him.
