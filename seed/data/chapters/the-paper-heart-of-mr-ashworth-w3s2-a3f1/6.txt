He did not manage well without her. That was rather the point.

Mr. Ashworth's letter to Miss Pellinore was a thing Catherine never read, because he wrote it alone, at his own desk, and sealed it before the clerk could see it, and posted it himself. But she heard of its effects, because three days later a reply came addressed not to him but to *the offices of Ashworth & Crale*, and the clerk, not knowing better, brought it to the back room.

Catherine knew the hand. She had been answering it for six weeks.

She ought to have left it sealed. Instead she carried it up to Mr. Ashworth's office and set it on the bare desk and said, "From Miss Pellinore. I have not opened it."

He looked at the letter, and then at her, and then he did a thing that undid her a little: he pushed it across the desk to her, unread.

"You answered her longer than I courted her," he said. "Read it. If it is a wound, I would rather you took the first of it. You have a kinder hand for wounds than I do."

So Catherine read Honoria Pellinore's last letter aloud, in the office on Lombard Street, with the noise of the city coming up through the window.

It was not a wound. It was, characteristically, far cleverer than that.

Miss Pellinore wrote that she had read his confession twice — once in anger and once in something closer to relief. She wrote that she had suspected, somewhere about the third letter, that two different men were writing to her, because the warm one noticed everything and the dry one signed the bottom, and that she had let herself enjoy the warm one anyway because no one had ever troubled to notice her before and she had been greedy for it. She wrote that she was not heartbroken. She wrote that she was *grateful* — that he had told her the truth before a church and a settlement made the truth unspeakable, and that this single honest act had told her more about his character than six weeks of borrowed poetry. She wrote that she hoped he would find the person who had actually been writing to her, because that person plainly existed, and plainly was not him, and plainly mattered to him a great deal, since a man does not hire a voice and then fall silent in his own. She signed it *your friend, if you will have one, Honoria.*

Catherine lowered the letter. The room was very still. The cat was not in the courtyard; it had found, she supposed, something better to do.

"She knew," Mr. Ashworth said. "From the third letter. She knew there were two of us."

"She is the cleverest person either of us has corresponded with," said Catherine, "and you very nearly married her, and she has just, in the kindest possible terms, told you to go and find me."

She had not meant to say *me*. She had meant to say *the writer*. The word had simply arrived, uninvited, the way the right word always did.

Mr. Ashworth came round the desk. He did it slowly, the way he did everything, a man checking each step against an inner ledger and finding, for once, that the sums came out in favour of recklessness.

"I have a confession of my own to make," he said, "and I must make it badly, because I have sent away the only person who could have arranged it for me. So you will have to take it as it is." He stopped in front of her. "Catherine. I did not fail at those four letters because I lack the words. I have words enough for a contract of forty pages. I failed because I did not feel, for Miss Pellinore, the thing the letters were pretending. And I could not write a feeling I did not have." He drew a breath. "That is no longer my difficulty."

The letter from Honoria Pellinore lay between them, having done its last and kindest work.

"Then write it," Catherine said, very softly. "Now. To me. Badly. In your own dry hand. I have read enough beautiful letters this season. I should like, for once, to be told a thing plainly by the man who means it."
