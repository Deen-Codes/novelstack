"My name is Catherine Vane," she said, "and I write a good hand, and I have been ashamed of why. I have told myself the shame was modesty. It was not. It was a way of believing I was worth less than the people I wrote letters for, so that it would not hurt so much when the world agreed."

Mr. Ashworth did not interrupt. He had learned, somewhere in the last two months, the difference between hearing a person and merely letting them speak, and Catherine watched him hear her, and loved him for it without arrangement.

"You told me I costed myself at nothing," she went on. "You were right, and it made me angry, because the truth usually does. So this weekend I did the sum again. I wrote to my sister and told her everything — the house, the wages, the back room, you. I have stopped protecting the people I love by lying to them, because Mr. Crale showed me what that particular kindness looks like from the outside, and it looks exactly like contempt." She drew a breath. "And then I did the last figure. The one I had been leaving out. What *I* want. Not what is sensible for the firm, or safe for me, or kind to everyone else. What I want."

"And?" His voice was not quite steady. She found she was glad of that; she had spent a season among careful men, and an unsteady one was worth a great deal more.

"I want the back room," Catherine said. "I want the courtyard cat and the bad dry letters and a man who rearranges his whole week so that a lease requires attention it does not need. I want to be the truest entry in someone's book and to have him be the truest in mine. I want, Mr. Ashworth, to stop deciding alone. So my answer is yes. Yes, if Crale ruins us. Yes, if the firm survives. Yes, badly, in my own voice, and meaning every word of it."

He came round the desk. He did not check the step against any inner ledger; she watched him not do it, watched a careful man be, for the first time in his life, entirely reckless, and he took her hands — ink-stained, both of them, hers and his — and held them.

"There is one matter outstanding," he said. "Crale."

"There is no matter outstanding with Crale." Catherine smiled, and it was the smile of a woman who had posted three honest letters and feared none of them. "His daughter Jane wrote to me. Yesterday. It seems Miss Pellinore is not the only clever young woman in London, and it seems Jane Crale has no more wish to marry you than you have to marry her, and a great deal of wish to marry a music master her father has not been told about. She and I are going to be of considerable use to one another. I have, you understand, a persuasive hand."

Mr. Ashworth looked at her for a long moment, and then he did the thing she had been waiting two months to see complete — the expression that had no name arrived, all of it, at last, and resolved itself into a smile, plain and astonished and entirely his own.

"You arranged it," he said.

"I listened," said Catherine. "On everyone's behalf. It is the only thing I have ever been truly good at. The difference is that this time I remembered to listen on mine."

They were married in the autumn, quietly, with Honoria Pellinore in the second pew, because she had taken up the friendship and proved as good a friend as she had been a correspondent. Jane Crale married her music master a month later, and old Mr. Crale, presented with two settled daughters and a firm that had not, after all, burned down, discovered that he had been in favour of everything all along, as men of business often do once the figures come out.

Catherine kept the back room. She set a cushion in the window for the cat, and a desk for herself, and she went on writing — not love for strangers now, but letters of her own, to a sister in Hampshire who wrote back as one writes to an equal, and to a friend named Honoria, and, now and then, just for the pleasure of it, to her husband, who answered every one badly, in his own dry hand, and meant, she knew beyond any doubt, every single word.
