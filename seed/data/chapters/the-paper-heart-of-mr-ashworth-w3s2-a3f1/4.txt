Miss Pellinore replied within the week, and her letter was a small disaster, because it was charming.

Catherine had not allowed for that. She had pictured a vapid girl, easily managed, the sort of correspondent who would coo and gush and require nothing. Instead the letter that arrived at Lombard Street was funny. Honoria Pellinore wrote that she had read his letter twice — once for politeness and once for pleasure — and that she had decided the second reading did not count against her dignity since no one had witnessed it. She wrote that she had told her mother nothing, because her mother would *organise* it, and a thing once organised by her mother had never again been enjoyable. She wrote that she also had spent evenings being neither heard nor admired, and that she had not expected a gentleman of business to know what those evenings were like, and that she was, cautiously, prepared to be surprised.

Mr. Ashworth brought the letter to the back room himself, which he had not done before; usually the clerk left the correspondence on the table. He set it down in front of Catherine and stood with his hands behind his back like a man awaiting a verdict.

"Read it," he said.

She read it. She felt, absurdly, a flush of something she was reluctant to examine, and named it pride — pride of craft, the satisfaction of a good letter answered in kind — because the alternative names were not permissible.

"She is clever," Catherine said. "I told you she was clever. You are not marrying a mild girl, Mr. Ashworth, whatever her mother advertised. You are marrying a quick one. That is a different proposition."

"Is it a worse one?"

"It is a more *interesting* one. Whether it is worse depends entirely on whether you intend to keep up." She tapped the letter. "She has just done a brave thing, you understand. She has answered you honestly instead of correctly. She has told you about her mother. She is testing whether the man who wrote that first letter is real, or whether he was — a performance."

He was silent. The word hung in the room. *Performance.* Catherine had chosen it deliberately and regretted it at once, because his face had gone still in the particular way that meant she had struck something true.

"And he was a performance," said Mr. Ashworth quietly. "He was you."

"He was your facts and my arrangement. You agreed to the bargain."

"I did." He pulled out the chair across from her and sat, which he did not usually do until invited, and Catherine understood that the conversation had slipped its harness. "Miss Vane. I find I must say a thing plainly, because I am bad at saying things any other way. When that letter came I was glad. Not relieved — glad. And then I read it again and I realised I was not glad because a sensible match was progressing. I was glad because a clever woman had been delighted by something true that had been said to her. And the truth is that I did not say it. You did."

"You felt it," Catherine said. "I only —"

"You only listened on my behalf. Yes. You have said." He looked at the courtyard window, at the cat, at anything but her. "I am beginning to wonder, Miss Vane, what becomes of a courtship in which the lady falls in love with a correspondent, and the correspondent is not, in any honest sense, the man she will be asked to marry."

Catherine set down her pen. Her hand was not quite steady, and she disliked her hand for it.

"Then you must learn to write your own letters," she said, "and quickly, before there is anything to confess. That is the only honest road, and you know it."

"I know it." He rose. At the door he paused, and did not turn, and said the thing that would keep her awake that night and several nights after. "But I do not think it is the road I have been on since Thursday. And I do not think, Miss Vane, that you have been on it either."

He left. The letter from Honoria Pellinore lay between them on the table, warm and clever and addressed, in the end, to nobody who existed.
