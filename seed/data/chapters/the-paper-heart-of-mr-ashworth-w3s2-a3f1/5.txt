For a fortnight they were both very correct.

Catherine arrived punctually. Mr. Ashworth supplied his facts. The letters went out and came back, and Honoria Pellinore grew warmer with each one, and the warmer she grew the more carefully Catherine and Mr. Ashworth avoided looking at each other while they composed her happiness.

It could not hold. Such arrangements never do.

It broke, in the end, over a walk in Berkeley Square — not a walk in a letter, but an actual one, because Mr. Ashworth had a question about a lease near Catherine's lodgings, and it was on his way, and the afternoon was fine, and a dozen small reasonable things conspired the way they do when two people have stopped wanting to be sensible.

They walked the square twice without discussing the lease.

"Miss Pellinore writes," Catherine said, because the silence had become a third person walking with them, "that she would like to meet you again. Properly. A dinner. Her mother proposes it."

"I had the same letter. From her mother." He matched his stride to hers, which she noticed, and wished she had not noticed. "Miss Vane. May I ask you a question that is not about the work?"

"You may ask. I do not promise to answer."

"How did you come to write a good hand and own a turned dress?"

She had not expected that one. She had braced for something sentimental and been handed something far more dangerous, which was interest.

"My father was a man of business," she said, after a moment. "Not unlike you. Cleverer than he was lucky. He had me taught everything a gentleman's daughter is taught, on the understanding that I would never need to use any of it for wages. Then he died, and it transpired that the understanding had been the only thing holding the house up." She kept her voice even. The evenness was its own kind of confession. "I have a sister. She believes I keep a respectable companion's position with a quiet widow. She believes it because I write her letters in which it is true."

Mr. Ashworth stopped walking. They had reached the iron rail at the square's edge, and beyond it the plane trees moved, and he stood looking at her with an expression she had no trouble at all naming, because she had spent a fortnight refusing to name it.

"You write letters that are kind, and not quite true, to spare someone you love," he said. "To a sister who must never know what it cost you."

"Yes."

"And you have spent six weeks scolding me for doing the same thing to Miss Pellinore."

The square went very quiet. A nursemaid passed with two children. Catherine watched them go because it was easier than watching him.

"It is not the same," she said. "My sister loses nothing by my letters. They cost only me. Miss Pellinore stands to marry a stranger on the strength of mine. You know it is not the same."

"I know," he said, "that you understood me from the first morning. On Lombard Street. When you said you would not yet decide whether you accepted *me*. I have thought about that sentence more than a man of business ought to think about anything that does not bear interest." He turned, so that the rail was at his back and there was nothing between them but the small honest distance two people leave when they are about to make it smaller. "Catherine. I am going to write the next letter to Miss Pellinore myself. Badly. In my own dry hand. And I am going to tell her the truth — that I respect her, that I have enjoyed her wit, and that I cannot in conscience continue a courtship I have conducted by proxy. She is too clever to be married into a lie. You said so yourself."

Catherine's heart was doing something disorderly. "And then?"

"And then I will have no further professional need of you." He said it steadily, and she understood it was the bravest sentence he had ever constructed, because it set fire to the only roof under which they were permitted to meet. "Which means that anything I say to you after that letter is sent will be said by me. Not arranged. Not on anyone's behalf. Mine."

The plane trees moved. Somewhere a clock that did not concern them struck the hour.

"Send the letter," said Catherine Vane, "and we shall see, Mr. Ashworth, how you manage without me."
