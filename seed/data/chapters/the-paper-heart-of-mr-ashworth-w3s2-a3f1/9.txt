She did not say yes that night. She said, "Give me until Monday," which was not a refusal, and they both knew it was not a refusal, and he kissed her hand at the door in a manner so correct and so unsteady that Catherine stood at the top of the creaking stair afterwards and laughed and cried in the same disordered breath.

But there remained the letter she had not written, and over that weekend Catherine Vane finally wrote it — not to Mr. Ashworth, and not to herself, but to her sister in Hampshire.

She had owed the truth to Hampshire for two years. She had paid the debt, instead, in cheerful counterfeit — a quiet widow, a respectable position, a Catherine who was managing splendidly. She had told herself the lies were a kindness. Crale had shown her, with his gentle *correspondence clerk*, exactly what such kindnesses were worth: they were a way of deciding alone. They were a way of refusing to let the people who loved you carry any of it.

So on Sunday she sat at the narrow table and wrote the hardest letter of her life, and it was honest in every facet. She wrote what the house had really been when their father died. She wrote what she had really been doing for wages — the refined hand, the advertisements, the back room on Lombard Street. She wrote, because it was true and because the letter would have known if she left it out, that she had been hired to invent love for a stranger and had instead found it, inconveniently and entirely, for the man who hired her. She wrote that he had asked to marry her and that she was afraid, and that she was telling her sister all of this *before* she answered him, because she had decided to stop being the only person in her own life who knew anything.

She did not make it kind. She made it true. And she discovered, sealing it, that the truth was a kindness of a higher order than the counterfeit had ever been — that it treated her sister as a person strong enough to be trusted, which was the greatest compliment Catherine knew how to pay.

Then, because she had found her courage in one direction, she used the last of it in another. She wrote a second letter that Sunday. To Miss Honoria Pellinore. She had no professional reason to; the correspondence was long closed. But Honoria had ended her last letter *your friend, if you will have one*, and Catherine had been too proud, or too frightened, to take it up.

She took it up now. She wrote that she was the warm one. That she had been the second man at the desk all along, and that Honoria had been right about everything, and that she was grateful, and that she hoped — cautiously, in Honoria's own word — that the friendship offered to a banker might be extended to the woman who had actually written to her.

She posted both letters on Monday morning, walking to the receiving office through a fine cold rain, and she felt, with each one that left her hand, a little lighter, the way one does on setting down a thing one has carried so long the carrying has become a posture.

Then she walked to Lombard Street.

The staircase did not creak. It never had. But Catherine climbed it that morning as a different woman from the one who had climbed it first — not a person of refined hand applying for the privilege of being insulted, but Catherine Vane, who had told her sister the truth, and offered her friendship plainly, and decided, somewhere on a wet street between two receiving offices, that she was done costing herself at nothing.

Mr. Ashworth was at the bare desk. He looked up when she came in, and did not pretend the look was about business.

"It is Monday," he said.

"It is," said Catherine. "And I have come to give you my answer. But first, Mr. Ashworth, you must let me say a thing badly, in my own voice, because I have spent two years writing everyone's truth but my own, and I find I should like, just this once, to say mine aloud."
