He did not write it that afternoon. He told her, instead, in person, which was braver and clumsier and which Catherine would not have exchanged for the finest letter ever sealed.

He said: "I think of the back room when I am not in it. I have rearranged my week so that the lease near your lodgings requires a great deal more attention than any lease has ever required. I did not know what to do with that, so I treated it as an irregularity in the accounts. It is not an irregularity. It is the truest entry in the book."

It was, as confessions went, distinctly the work of a man of business. Catherine loved it without reservation.

But love, in Catherine Vane's experience, did not pay the quarter's rent, and it did not unmake the facts of a turned dress, and it did not change what the world would say of a banker who married the woman he had hired. And so, three days into being cautiously, dizzily happy, she did what she had trained herself to do whenever happiness arrived: she examined it for the catch.

There was a catch. There was always a catch. It was named Mr. Crale.

Crale was the *& Crale* of Ashworth & Crale, a heavier, older, smiling man who had built half the firm and intended to die richer than he was born, and who had a daughter. Catherine learned all of this in a single quarter of an hour, because Mr. Crale chose to deliver it to her himself, in the back room, on a morning he knew Mr. Ashworth would be at the Exchange.

"You will not have heard," Mr. Crale said, lowering himself into the chair across from her with the ease of a man who owned it, "that there has long been an understanding between this firm and my family. Ashworth is to marry my Jane. It was settled when he was a junior partner with nothing but a good head, and I lifted him. He owes the firm. He owes me. A man does not unpick a thing like that because a — correspondence clerk — has caught his fancy."

He said *correspondence clerk* gently. That was the cruelty of it. He said it the way one might say *weather*.

Catherine kept her hands folded. She had folded them on Lombard Street on the first morning, waiting to hear how she would be insulted, and here, after everything, was the insult, only later than billed.

"Mr. Ashworth has made me no offer," she said evenly. "There is nothing to unpick."

"He will make you one. I have watched him for fifteen years. He has the look of a man about to do something foolish for the first time in his life, and foolishness, in my experience, is most thoroughly cured before it is committed." Mr. Crale rose. He was not unkind, exactly; he was simply a man of business, and Catherine recognised the species, because she had loved one as a father and was learning to love another as something else entirely. "I am told you write a persuasive letter, Miss Vane. I would take it as a great favour if you would write yourself one. Persuade yourself out of this. It is the sensible match — for him, for the firm, and, if you are honest, for you, since I will see you comfortably placed elsewhere and no one need ever speak of the back room again."

He left her the way he had found her: folded, still, and very quietly furious.

Catherine sat for a long while after the door closed. Then she drew a clean sheet toward her, and uncapped the ink, because it was the only thing in the world she had ever been able to do when she did not know what to feel.

But for the first time in two years, the words would not arrange themselves. She sat with the pen above the page and found she could not write herself out of it, because the letter would have known it was a lie, and Catherine Vane did not write lies — only kind, true things, to people she loved.

She put the pen down. The page stayed white. And she understood, with a clarity that frightened her, that the only honest letter left to write was the one she had been most afraid of all along.
