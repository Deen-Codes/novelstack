She did not tell him about Crale. That was her mistake, and she knew it was a mistake even as she made it, and made it anyway, because she had spent two years protecting the people she loved by carrying things alone.

So when Mr. Ashworth came to her lodgings that Friday evening — properly, with the landlady's knowledge, and a small parcel of books he had decided she ought to own — he found a Catherine who was distant, and brisk, and full of sudden reasonable objections.

"It would be the ruin of the firm's reputation," she said. "Crale's daughter, the understanding, fifteen years of —"

"You have spoken to Crale."

"That is not the point."

"It is precisely the point." He set the books down on the narrow table with a care that was worse than anger. "You have spoken to Crale, and he has frightened you, and rather than tell me, you have spent three days quietly composing my retreat for me. You are doing it now. You are writing me a kind letter out loud, Catherine, in which it is all very sensible and no one is to blame and we both agree it cannot be."

"Because it cannot be." Her voice did not break, which she was proud of and ashamed of in equal measure. "Be a man of business for one moment and look at the ledger. You have a partner who lifted you, an understanding fifteen years old, a daughter expecting a settlement, and against all of that you have a correspondence clerk in a turned dress. I have *done the sum*, Mr. Ashworth. I did it before you ever could."

"You did the sum," he said, "and you left out the only figure that matters, which is what I want. You are very good, Catherine, at calculating other people's happiness. Your sister's. Miss Pellinore's. Mine. You have spent your whole ruined life arranging warm true letters for everyone but yourself, and now, when one is owed to you, you would rather post a cold one in your own name than let me write you the warm one I have been trying to say for a week."

The candle between them guttered. Outside, the stair creaked, because in this house every stair announced its traveller like a herald, and she thought of the silent staircase on Lombard Street, the clean expensive house where she had first added him up, and she felt the whole distance between his world and hers.

"I cannot let you set fire to your firm for me," she whispered.

"You do not get to decide that alone." He came closer, and the small honest distance grew smaller, and he did not touch her, because he was a careful man, but his voice was not careful at all. "That is what a marriage is, Catherine. It is the end of deciding alone. You have been deciding alone since your father died, and you have done it bravely, and I love you for it, and I am asking you — badly, in my own dry voice — to stop. Crale does not own my conscience. The understanding was made between two men over a girl who, I happen to know, cares for me no more than I care for her and will be glad of her freedom. The ledger you have done is wrong because you costed yourself at nothing. And you are not nothing. You are the truest entry in the book."

He had said that to her once before, across a desk. He said it now as a man with everything to lose, and Catherine, who had armoured herself in sense, felt the armour fail at exactly the seam she had known it would.

"I am frightened," she said. It was not an argument. It was just true, and she had run out of anything else.

"I know," said Mr. Ashworth. "So am I. I have never once in my life done a thing without the sums in front of me, and I am about to. Marry me, Catherine. Let the worst of it happen. We will face the worst of it the way you have faced everything — except, this once, not alone."

The candle steadied. And Catherine Vane discovered that there was, after all, one letter left in her that she had never learned to write, and that it could only be said aloud.
