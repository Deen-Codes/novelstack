The auction floor of Asterhold smelled of cold stone and warm fear, and Lysa Morrow had decided, somewhere around the third hour of waiting, that she would not give them the fear.

It was the only thing she had left to decide. Everything else had been decided for her — by her father's debts, by the court that converted those debts into a body when there was no coin, by the long iron-railed pen where the season's debtors stood in their numbered rows waiting to be looked at. A human in Asterhold did not own her own freedom. Lysa had grown up knowing that the way you know the weather. What she had not known, until this morning, was the exact price of her: four hundred marks, the clerk had said, reading it off a ledger, the way you'd price a quantity of wheat.

So the fear was all that was hers to keep or spend, and Lysa had made up her mind to keep it. Let them buy the body. They were not getting the rest.

The Houses came through at midday.

There were seven of them, the great vampire Houses of Asterhold, and they moved along the iron rail like a slow cold front. Lysa watched them and catalogued them, because cataloguing was a thing the mind could do that was not afraid. House Calvaire, in grey, buying strong young men for its mines. House Oresi, buying the pretty ones, and Lysa was glad, fiercely glad, that she had never been called pretty. House after House, and the debtors flinched down the line ahead of her like wheat under wind.

The seventh House came last, and the floor went quiet for it.

She knew which House it was before anyone said the name, because the iron rail itself seemed to get colder. House Vane. The oldest of the seven, and by every account whispered down the debtor pen, the cruelest — the House that bought servants and did not always trouble to explain, afterward, where the previous ones had gone.

The man who walked the rail for House Vane was not what the whispers had built. The whispers had built a monster. What walked the rail was a tall, unhurried figure in unrelieved black, with a pale narrow face and dark eyes that held a very specific and very tired expression, and it took Lysa a moment to place the expression because it was so out of keeping with the cold of the room.

He was bored. Profoundly, anciently bored. He walked the rows of terrified human beings the way a man walks a gallery of paintings he has seen a thousand times.

He stopped in front of Lysa.

She understood why. She had felt it happen — felt her own face do the thing she had promised it would do, which was nothing. The debtor to her left was weeping. The debtor to her right was shaking so hard the iron rail hummed with it. And Lysa Morrow stood in her numbered place and looked back at the vampire of House Vane with an expression she had spent three hours building, an expression that said, as clearly as she could make it: *you may have the four hundred marks' worth. You will not have one copper more.*

The bored dark eyes sharpened. Just slightly. The way still water sharpens when something moves under it.

"You're not afraid," he said. His voice was quiet, and unhurried, and it had the faint dust of an accent no longer spoken anywhere.

"I'm extremely afraid," Lysa said. "I've simply decided you're not entitled to watch."

For a long moment the vampire of House Vane did not move at all. Up and down the iron rail the auction went on — the clerks calling numbers, the Houses buying wheat — but in the small cold space between Lysa and the man in black, something had stopped.

And then he did a thing that frightened her far more than any monster from the whispers could have. The boredom went out of his face. All of it, at once, like a lamp being uncovered — and what was underneath the boredom was *interest*, sudden and total and centuries deep, the interest of a creature who had not been surprised by anything in a very, very long time and had just, against all odds, been surprised.

He turned to the clerk without looking away from her.

"This one," said the vampire of House Vane. "Whatever the price. House Vane will pay it now."

The clerk stammered something about the four hundred marks.

"I heard the number," the vampire said. "I'm telling you it's wrong." His dark eyes stayed on Lysa, and the faint dust of his old accent curled around the words like cold smoke. "She has been priced as a debt. She should have been priced as the only genuinely interesting thing to enter Asterhold in three hundred years — and House Vane, clerk, has always been willing to pay a great deal for that."
