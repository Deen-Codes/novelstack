The canal gives up its dead slowly. Femi Bankole had learned that the hard way, years back, and she thought of it again now, standing on the towpath at twenty past six with her coffee going cold in her hand and the body still face-down in the brown water like something the city had decided not to finish.

It was March and Manchester was doing what Manchester did in March, which was rain without commitment — not a downpour, just a fine grey insistence that got into everything and apologised for nothing. The towpath smelled of wet brick and diesel and the particular green rot of standing water. Behind her, the new flats went up forty storeys into the cloud, all glass and ambition, and in front of her a man floated in a canal that had been carrying coal before any of those flats were a drawing, and Femi thought, not for the first time, that this city kept all its centuries in the same square mile and never tidied up.

"You'll want to come round this side, Sarge." That was Pollard, the constable, young enough that the body still meant something to his face. He'd cordoned it well, she'd give him that. Tape strung neat between a bollard and the railing, the dog walkers herded back, a tent already going up over the recovery point. "Diver says forty minutes. Lock-keeper found him. Reckons he wasn't there at five."

"Reckons, or knows?"

"Does this walk every morning. Same time. Says the water was clear at five."

Femi nodded and drank the cold coffee anyway, because her mother had raised her not to waste, and looked at the dead man's back. Good coat. That was the first true thing. A coat like that did not end up in the Rochdale Canal by accident, and it did not belong to anyone the city would let drift for a week before the paperwork got urgent. Whoever he was, there'd be a phone ringing somewhere by nine, a personal assistant, a wife, a solicitor — somebody whose morning was about to stop.

"We got a name?"

"Wallet's still on him. Diver saw it. Didn't want to disturb." Pollard hesitated, and she watched him decide whether to say the next part, and watched him lose. "There's a card holder. Top of it's a business card. Sarge — it's Aldous Crane."

Femi did not say anything for a moment. The rain kept up its fine grey apology. Forty storeys above the towpath the Crane Regeneration hoarding ran the length of the site, a happy computer-rendered street full of computer-rendered people, and across the top of it in letters two metres high it said A BETTER ANCOATS, BUILT BY CRANE.

"Right," she said eventually, and it came out flatter than she meant. "Well. There goes everybody's quiet week."

Because she knew the name the way the whole city knew the name. Aldous Crane bought streets. He had bought a good deal of this one — bought the leases, bought the planning permissions, bought, by most accounts, a councillor or two along the way, though nothing had ever been made to stick. There were four terraced rows behind the canal that Crane Regeneration had a demolition order on, and there were two hundred-odd people in those rows who had spent the last year fighting it in church halls and comment forms and one memorable shouting match outside the town hall that Femi had personally helped police. Aldous Crane was the most hated man in three postcodes. And now he was face-down in the canal, in a good coat, with the rain not caring either way.

"Sarge?" Pollard again. "Who do I ring?"

Femi looked at the hoarding, at the happy rendered street, at the forty storeys of glass disappearing up into the cloud, and she thought about how many people in this city had wished Aldous Crane dead in the last twelve months, out loud, in writing, in church halls, on the record.

"Ring everyone," she said. "And tell DCI Soyinka I want this one before anyone upstairs decides they want it instead." She finished the coffee. It was foul. "Because they will, Pollard. Give it an hour. They will."
