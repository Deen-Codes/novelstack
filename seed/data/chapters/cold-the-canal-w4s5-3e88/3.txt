The planning file on the Ancoats canal site was four hundred pages long, and Femi read it the way she read everything, which was at her own kitchen table, late, with the radio low and a pen she chewed instead of smoking.

DCI Soyinka had given her the case. That part had gone better than she'd feared — Soyinka was old enough and tired enough to want the right detective on a hard one rather than the convenient one, and he'd looked at her across his desk and said, do it properly, Femi, and quickly, before properly and quickly become a choice. She'd taken that as the warning it was.

The file told a story, if you knew how to read a file, and Femi did. Crane Regeneration had wanted the four terraced rows behind the canal for six years. For six years they had not been able to have them, because the rows were a conservation area, and a conservation area cannot simply be knocked down by a man with a hard hat and a grin. And then, eighteen months ago, the conservation status had been reviewed. And the review had found — Femi read the paragraph three times — that the rows possessed "limited surviving historic fabric" and were "not of a character meriting continued designation," and the status had been lifted, and ninety days later the demolition order had been granted, and the happy rendered street had gone up on the hoarding.

She knew those rows. She had policed a shouting match outside the town hall over those rows. They had as much surviving historic fabric as anything else in Ancoats, which was to say a great deal, which was to say that someone had written "limited" and someone had signed it.

She turned to the back of the file, to the part most people never reached, the appendix of names — the planning officers, the reviewing surveyor, the councillors who had sat on the committee, the consultant who had authored the conservation review. She wrote them out on a fresh page, all of them, in her own hand, because writing a thing made it real to her in a way reading it never did.

Twelve names. She looked at the twelve names for a long time.

Della Crane's voice came back to her, steady and sharp over the long wet garden. Some of those people work in the same buildings you do. She had meant it as a warning and Femi had taken it as one, but sitting here now, with the radio low and the file open and twelve names in her own handwriting, she understood that Della had also, without quite meaning to, told her how to run the case. Aldous Crane had not floated up out of the canal because a city hated him. Cities hated developers every day of the week and the developers went on cutting ribbons. Aldous Crane had died because of something specific, something with a date and a paper trail, and the paper trail was on her kitchen table, four hundred pages of it, and somewhere in those twelve names was a person who had needed a hated man to stay quiet.

She underlined one name. Then, after a moment, she underlined a second, and sat back, and looked at what she had done.

Both of them were people she had met. One of them she had, in the loosest possible sense, considered a friend.

The radio finished its programme and started another. Femi made fresh tea she did not really want, because the night was going to be long now, and properly and quickly were already, she could feel it, starting to become a choice.
