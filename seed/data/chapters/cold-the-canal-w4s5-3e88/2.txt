Aldous Crane's widow lived in Bowdon, in a house that had a name instead of a number, and she answered her own door, which surprised Femi more than the house did.

"You'll be the police." Della Crane was sixty, maybe more, dressed for a day she was now not going to have — good wool, real jewellery, hair done. She had been crying, or had stopped recently, and she held the doorframe the way the recently bereaved hold furniture, as a fact to lean on. "They telephoned. They wouldn't tell me how. On the telephone they wouldn't tell me how."

"DS Bankole. This is DC Pollard. Mrs Crane, I'm very sorry. May we come in?"

The room she took them to looked out over a long wet garden, and there were photographs on every surface, and Femi did the thing she always did, which was to read a life off its mantelpiece while its owner put the kettle on out of habit. Aldous Crane at a ribbon-cutting. Aldous Crane younger, in a hard hat, grinning. Aldous Crane and Della on a beach somewhere the sun was real. A boy, then a young man, then the young man gone from the later pictures the way people go when something has happened, and Femi filed that and said nothing.

"When did you last see your husband, Mrs Crane?"

"Yesterday. Morning." Della set the tea down without offering milk, then noticed, and apologised, and Femi told her not to worry. "He had meetings. He always had meetings. He said he'd be late and I shouldn't wait, and I didn't wait, because after forty years you stop waiting, you just — leave a light on." Her voice did something then, and she steadied it the way the lock-keeper had steadied the tape, by main force. "He didn't come home and I thought, well. He's done that. He's slept at the office before. He has a sofa there. I rang it this morning and a girl answered and — and then you telephoned."

"Did he seem worried, lately? Frightened? Anything different?"

"Aldous didn't get frightened." She said it with something that was almost pride and almost grief and was probably, Femi thought, just forty years. "He got busy. When something was wrong he got busy. And he'd been very busy. The Ancoats thing — the canal site — that had been hard. People say things, you know. They wrote things. There was a letter."

Femi kept her voice level. "What letter, Mrs Crane?"

"To the house. Three weeks ago, perhaps four. Not signed. It said — " She stopped. She looked at the long wet garden. "It said he should think about what he was knocking down. That a street is people. That there'd be a reckoning. Aldous laughed at it. He put it in the kitchen drawer with the takeaway menus and he laughed, and he said every developer in England gets that letter, it's practically a trade certificate." She turned back, and now there was something underneath the grief, something sharper, and Femi recognised it because she had it herself most mornings. "But he kept it. He laughed at it and he kept it. He didn't keep things, my husband. He kept that."

"We'd like to take that letter, if we may."

"You'd like to take that letter," Della Crane repeated, slowly, as though hearing how it sounded. "Yes. The kitchen drawer. With the menus." She did not get up. She looked at the photograph of the young man who had left the later pictures, and then she looked at Femi, and she said the thing that Femi would carry out of that house and around the case for a long time after.

"He wasn't a good man, Sergeant. I'd like you not to waste my morning pretending you don't already know that. He was my husband and I loved him and he was not a good man, and a great many people had reasons. So you'll want to be careful." She finally picked up her own tea, and her hand was steady now, dead steady. "Because if you go looking for someone with a reason, you'll find this whole city had one. And some of those people work in the same buildings you do."
