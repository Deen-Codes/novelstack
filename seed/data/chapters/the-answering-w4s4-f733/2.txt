They began at dusk, as Felix had asked, and for the first hours it was only what it was: four tired people praying in a cold and beautiful room.

The chapel had been stripped of its movable holiness already and so they prayed in a sort of ruin — bare altar, the relics gone, the walls showing pale rectangles where pictures had hung for a century. But the proportions of the place were still devout, and the four of them knelt in the front pew with a single candle, and they prayed the relay as Felix had designed it. One voice always carrying. When that voice tired, the next took up the prayer mid-line, seamlessly, so that the words never stopped, only changed mouths. Felix, then Aaron, then Marisol, then Daniel, then back to Felix. A wheel of voices, turning slow through the dark.

It was, for a while, the most peaceful thing Aaron had done in months. There is a stage of tiredness, in the small hours, where the self goes thin and quiet, and the prayer seemed to pray itself through them, and he understood, kneeling there, a little of what Felix had always understood and Aaron never had.

The change, when it came, was not dramatic. That was the thing Aaron most wanted people to understand afterward. Horror, he had always assumed, announced itself. This did not. It arrived as a small administrative error, the kind you correct without alarm.

It was somewhere past three in the morning, deep in the cold heart of the night, and the prayer had been running unbroken for seven hours, and it was Marisol's turn to carry it while the other three rested with their eyes closed. Aaron was listening, half-asleep, to Marisol's voice going through the familiar words. And then he noticed, drowsily, without alarm, that Marisol's voice had stopped.

The prayer had not stopped. The words were still going, in their proper rhythm, in the proper place in the cycle — but Marisol was silent. Aaron opened his eyes. Marisol knelt beside him with her lips closed and her face slack with exhaustion, plainly resting, plainly not speaking. And the prayer continued, unbroken, in the room.

He looked at Felix. Felix's eyes were shut, his mouth still. Daniel the same. Four people kneeling in a stripped chapel with their mouths closed, and the prayer going on around them in a voice that belonged to none of them — a voice that had their cadence, that took the words up exactly where the cycle required, that was carrying the relay faithfully, exactly as Felix had asked, so that the prayer would never, ever break.

Aaron did the only thing his thinning, terrified mind could produce. He spoke the next line himself, loudly, to take the prayer back. His voice went out into the dark and joined the words — and the other voice did not stop. It simply continued alongside his, patient, in step, and Aaron understood with a horror that started in the soles of his feet that it had no intention of yielding its turn, because it had been waiting a hundred and forty years to be given a turn, and Brother Felix in his terrible sincerity had built it a wheel of voices and promised it the prayer would never stop.

Across the pew, Marisol's eyes came open. She was looking at Aaron, and her face was the face of someone who had heard it too, and her closed mouth had gone the white of pressed paper. Felix was still kneeling with his eyes shut and an expression of deep and dreadful peace, as though he, of all of them, had finally been answered. And the prayer went on, in the fifth voice, the one nobody had assigned, carrying the cycle forward into the last black hour before a dawn that Aaron was no longer at all certain the four of them were going to be allowed to see.
