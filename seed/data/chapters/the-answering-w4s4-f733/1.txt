St. Bonaventure's had taught and housed and shaped young men for a hundred and forty years, and it would close on the last day of the month, and there were four of them left to turn out the lights.

The diocese had been kind about it, in the bloodless way of institutions. The seminary was no longer viable; there were nine students where once there had been ninety; the building would be sold. The other students had already gone, scattered to parishes and to other seminaries and, in two cases, quietly out of the priesthood altogether. Only four remained, and they remained because the deconsecration of the chapel required hands, and because none of them, for reasons each held privately, had anywhere they were in a hurry to be.

There was Brother Felix, who was not yet a brother of anything but had been called Brother Felix since his first week for his terrible sincerity, and who had wanted to be a priest since he was six years old and now had nowhere to put the wanting. There was Daniel, older than the rest, a former soldier with a soldier's flat way of looking at a problem. There was Marisol, one of the lay students, sharp and tired and the only one of the four who had said aloud that she was relieved the place was closing. And there was the narrator of much of what follows, a doubt-ridden young man named Aaron who had come to St. Bonaventure's looking for certainty and was leaving it with less than he had arrived with.

Their task was simple and sad. A chapel that is to be sold must first be deconsecrated — its holiness formally, liturgically withdrawn, so that the building can become a restaurant or a private house or whatever the market decides, without sacrilege. The bishop would come for the rite itself. But the chapel had to be made ready: the altar stripped, the relics removed, the sanctuary lamp extinguished, a hundred years of accumulated devotional clutter boxed and catalogued.

They worked through a long grey week at it, the four of them, and the building emptied around them, and it was Marisol who said what they were all feeling, on the Thursday, standing in the stripped sanctuary.

"It feels like a wake," she said. "Except the body's still in the room."

That evening Brother Felix made his proposal.

They were eating together in the vast cold refectory, four people at a table built for sixty, and Felix put down his fork and said, in his terrible sincere way, that he wanted to do something for the place. Not the rite — the rite belonged to the bishop. But before the bishop came, before the holiness was formally withdrawn, Felix wanted the four of them to keep one last vigil. To pray through the night in the chapel, all four together, without stopping. A relay, he said. One voice always carrying the prayer while the others rested, so that the prayer itself never broke, from dusk until dawn — so that St. Bonaventure's chapel, after a hundred and forty years, would go to its deconsecration having been prayed in, continuously, lovingly, right up to the end.

Daniel said it sounded like a long night. Marisol said nothing, which from Marisol was a kind of objection. Aaron, who did not know what he believed anymore, found that he could not think of a single honest reason to refuse a man the chance to pray in his own chapel one last time.

So they agreed. They agreed to the vigil, the four of them, the last four of St. Bonaventure's, and they set it for the following night, and not one of them thought to ask the question that Aaron would turn over for the rest of his life: whether a prayer that is never allowed to stop is still, properly speaking, a prayer — or whether, somewhere in the long dark hours, it becomes a door that has been propped open and forgotten, and whether a door like that is a thing that only people walk through.
