WHAT THE PHONE KNOWS

It lies face down on the table
the way a person lies who has done
something they have not decided
whether to mention.
I do not turn it over.
We have an understanding.


THE LAST MESSAGE

It is still there, near the top,
the way a chipped step is still there,
and I have learned the route around it,
the scroll that does not quite reach,
the thumb that knows to stop.
But tonight the thumb is tired
and slips, and there it is —
your name, the small grey time-stamp,
the words, which were ordinary,
which were about a train,
which neither of us could have known
were going to have to last.


TYPING

Three dots, once, from someone.
They appear and I sit forward
like a dog at a gate,
and then they vanish,
and whoever it was
thought better of whatever it was,
and the screen goes back to black,
and I am left holding the particular ache
of an almost — the sentence
someone built, in the dark, for me,
and then, in the dark, took down.


DO NOT DISTURB

There is a setting for it now.
A little moon. You tap the moon
and the world agrees to wait.
And it works — the phone goes quiet —
and that is the terrible discovery:
that the world will, in fact, wait,
that it was never pounding at the door,
that the moon was always available,
that the noise I have been bracing against
for years, the urgent, the incoming,
was a thing I could have asked to stop
and it would simply, mildly, have stopped.


CHARGING

At least this is honest work.
At least overnight, while I do not sleep,
one small thing in the house
is filling slowly back to full,
and will be ready in the morning,
and will not, when morning asks it,
have any reason to say
that it is tired, that it cannot,
that it gave what it had
to a long dark it never agreed to.
