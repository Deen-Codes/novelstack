THE PORCH LIGHT

I leave it on for nobody.
Let me be honest about that first.
There is no one on the road tonight,
no headlights swinging in,
no key fumbling at the lock,
no voice about to call my name
the careless, lovely, ordinary way
a voice calls a name it owns.

I leave it on for nobody.
But nobody is a large country
and a man can stand a long time
at its border, in a doorway,
keeping one small light burning
in case the geography is wrong,
in case nobody, after all this,
turns out to have an address.


THE MOTH

Of course there is a moth.
There is always a moth,
and it has decided the bulb is the moon
or the sun or the one warm thing
worth ruining itself against.
I understand the moth completely.
I have done the moth's whole career.
I have flung my soft and only body
at lit windows belonging to other lives
and called the bruise devotion.


WHAT THE LIGHT TOUCHES

Three feet of cracked path.
The top of the gate. A circle of grass
gone the wrong shade of green,
too bright, electric, false —
the grass equivalent of a smile
held far too long at a leaving.

Past that, the dark does its honest work.
And I think: maybe that is the kindness.
Maybe a light is not a promise
that someone will come.
Maybe it is only a way
of saying to the whole indifferent night,
this much. I can vouch for this much.
A gate. A path. A patch of green.
Come this far and I can show you
where the ground is.


TURNING IT OFF

In the end I turn it off myself.
I always do. The switch is by the coats.
And the dark comes up over the path
like water filling a footprint,
patient, total, and not unkind,
and I stand for a moment in my own hallway
having, at last, let the night
have everything it asked for.
