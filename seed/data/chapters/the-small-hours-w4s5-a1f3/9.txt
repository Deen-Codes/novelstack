THE SMALL HOURS

So this is the last of them, the night-poems,
the ones I wrote standing up,
in the kitchen, in your old shirt,
with a cooling mug for a witness.

I want to say a thing about the hours
themselves — the two, the three, the four —
the ones we call small, as though they were
lesser, as though they were children
the day had sent up early to bed.

They are not small. I have lived in them.
I have walked their long municipal streets.
I have learned their weather and their birds,
their cats, their unlatched doors,
their single distant determined car.
They are a country, fully sized,
and most people are only ever
tourists there, passing through asleep,
and never see the porch lights on,
the kettles waited on, the moths,
the whole soft economy of the awake.


FOR THE OTHERS UP

And if you are reading this at the hour
it was written for — if it is dark,
if the fridge is keeping its one note,
if your phone is face down keeping its one secret —
then I wrote it, in the end, for you.

Not to fix the night. I cannot fix the night.
Only to sit a while on the page
the way a light sits on a path:
this much. I can vouch for this much.
You are awake. So am I, or was.
The birds are coming. They are always coming.
The grey will get its rumour.
The spoon will be visible again.


LAST POEM, ALMOST DAWN

I am going to try to sleep now.
I am going to put the cup in the sink
and not wash it, and climb the stair,
and step hard, on purpose, on the fourth one,
so it keeps its word and says nothing,
so I do not wake the version of me
who has finally, somewhere up there,
in the grey, in the safety of the nearly-morning,
managed it — managed the small,
enormous, ordinary thing
of closing both eyes
and trusting the house to settle
without my listening.

Goodnight. Or good morning.
At this hour they are, I have found,
the same kindness, said two ways.
Thank you for sitting up with me.
Sleep, if you can. The light will keep.
