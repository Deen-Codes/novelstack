WEATHER REPORTS

The forecast says tomorrow will be fine.
I distrust the forecast the way I distrust
anyone who is certain about tomorrow
while standing comfortably inside today.


RAIN AT FOUR

It starts the way confessions start —
a few words, then a few more,
then all of it at once,
the gutter overwhelmed,
the downpipe doing its frantic best,
the whole street saying everything
it held in all through the dry afternoon
when there were people about
and it had to seem all right.

I love the rain for this.
It has no pride. It does not wait
until it can do the thing gracefully.
It simply, suddenly, lets go,
and the morning, later, will be washed,
and nobody will mention
how loud it got, how late,
how thoroughly it came apart.


FOG

Some mornings the world arrives
unfinished, the far houses
sketched and then abandoned,
the tree a rumour of a tree.
I find I am calmer on these mornings.
It is restful, being asked to believe
only in what is near:
the wet rail, my own breath,
the six clear feet of path
the fog has generously agreed to.


THE BAROMETER

My grandmother had one in the hall,
brass, ticking, taller than me then.
You tapped it. That was the ritual.
You tapped the glass and the needle
shifted or did not, and either way
she would nod, as though the house
had told her something private,
and go on with the day informed.

I have her tapping in my hands still.
I do it to phones, to dark windows,
to the side of the kettle, to my own chest
some nights — a knuckle, twice, listening
for the needle of myself to swing
toward fair, toward change, toward anything
a person could plan a morning around.
