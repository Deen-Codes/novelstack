ALMOST MORNING

There is a colour the window does
that is not yet light and is no longer dark —
a grey with a rumour in it,
a grey that has heard something.
I have waited up, more nights than I will say,
just to be present when the glass
changes its mind.


THE STREET, FIVE-FORTY

A single car. A real one, with a person in it,
a person dressed and combed and going somewhere,
a person for whom this hour is a beginning
and not the frayed, unravelled end
of a rope I have been holding since midnight.
I watch the tail lights take the corner
and I bear that stranger no ill will.
I hope the shift is easy. I hope
there is coffee where they are going,
and someone who says their name
the careless, lovely, ordinary way.


THE KETTLE, AGAIN

And now, at last, I want the tea.
Now, with the grey arriving,
with the birds fully committed,
with the night demonstrably survived,
the wanting comes — plain and small
and possible. I fill the kettle.
I listen for the sound it makes
before the sound it makes.
Some errands, it turns out,
you have to walk all the way to morning
before they finally become real.


WHAT THE NIGHT WAS FOR

I will not pretend I solved anything.
The grief is the same size.
The list of what I gave away
is still folded in the same small square.
But the dark is nearly over,
and I stood in it, the whole length of it,
and did not — this is the only news —
did not, in the end, come to harm.

And maybe that is what the night was for.
Not answers. Just the slow accumulation
of evidence, hour over hour,
that a person can be awake inside the dark
and still, somehow, by morning,
be a person, and be standing,
and be filling a kettle,
and be here.


THE LIGHT COMES UP

It does not announce itself.
That is the thing I keep relearning.
It does not crest the roofs in glory.
It just — at some point you notice
that you can see the spoon,
that the room has quietly been given back to you,
that the worst of it is, again,
behind you, and was always going to be,
and that you can, if you like,
now, finally, put down the cup.
