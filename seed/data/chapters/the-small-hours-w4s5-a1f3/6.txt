SMALL ANIMALS

The mouse and I keep different hours
and have agreed, without discussion,
not to meet. I hear it in the wall
doing its brave, electric errands,
and I think: good. Someone in this house
has somewhere to be.


THE NEIGHBOUR'S CAT

It comes to my step at the worst hour
and looks at me through the glass
with the flat, unbothered patience
of a creature that has never once
lain awake wondering if it was loved.
I envy it the way you envy
anyone fluent in a language
you studied hard and failed.


SPIDER IN THE BATH

I should move it. I know the cup trick,
the postcard, the gentle export
of the small life to the garden.
But it is four in the morning
and we are both, clearly, insomniacs,
and it seems unkind to interrupt
a fellow citizen of the small hours
in the middle of whatever
it has come all this way down the pipe
to think about.


BIRDS, FIRST

Then the first bird, and I could weep.
Not at the beauty. At the timing.
At the nerve of it — to begin singing
while it is still completely dark,
to stake that claim, to insist,
against all the visible evidence,
that morning is a thing that happens
and is, in fact, already
on its way, already owed,
already, somewhere past the roofs,
being unwrapped.

And then a second bird, agreeing.
And then, the way it always goes,
the dark begins to lose the argument
it was never going to win,
and I, who sided with the dark all night,
find I am glad, after all,
to have been wrong in such good company.


THE DOG THREE GARDENS DOWN

It barks once, at nothing, at five,
the way a man clears his throat
before saying the thing.
Then it thinks better of it.
Then the street is quiet again,
and we all go on
pretending we did not hear
how badly someone wanted
to be the one who broke the silence first.
