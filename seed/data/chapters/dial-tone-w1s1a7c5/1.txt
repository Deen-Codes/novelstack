The overnight shift on the helpline has a sound, and the sound is mostly the absence of one.

Reza Daneshvar had been working the line for two years, the eleven-to-seven, and he had come to know that absence the way a sailor knows water. The call centre — though *centre* was generous for a converted office above a carpet shop, four desks and a kettle and a window that looked at a brick wall — went very quiet between calls. The phone did not ring continuously. It rang in clusters, around the bad hours, the two a.m. and the four a.m., when the world's loneliness reached its tide line, and between those clusters there was just Reza and the hum of the strip light and the wait.

He was good at the wait. He had learned that the wait was the job too. When the phone did ring, you had to be a person who had been quietly, patiently ready, not a person startled out of boredom — because the voice on the other end had usually spent a long time deciding to dial, and they could hear, instantly, whether you had been waiting for them or merely sitting there.

Most callers, Reza never heard from again. That was the nature of it, and he had made his peace with the nature of it. You were a stranger's voice in their worst hour. You held the line, you said the true useful things, you stayed until the worst of the tide had gone out, and then you hung up and never knew. Did it help. Did they sleep. Were they all right, in a month, in a year. You did not get to know. The not-knowing had nearly broken him in the first six months and now it was simply the shape of the work, a grief he had folded small and learned to carry.

And then there was the Thursday caller.

She had first called on a Thursday in February, fourteen months ago, and Reza remembered it because it had not been a crisis call, not really, and he had not at first known what to do with that. She had not been at any edge. She had called the overnight line, she said, because it was the only number she could think of where someone would definitely, certainly pick up, and she had simply not wanted, that night, to be the only person awake in her flat.

"I'm not going to do anything," she had said, fast, at the start, the way people said it. "I want to be clear. You don't have to do the — the assessment thing. I can hear you about to do the assessment thing. I'm not in danger. I'm just awake, and it's a bad kind of awake, and I thought — is it allowed? To just call and not be in danger?"

It was, Reza had told her, allowed. The line was for whoever needed the line.

So she had talked. Not about anything heavy. She had talked about a documentary she'd watched, about deep-sea fish, about the ones that made their own light, and she had been funny about it, unexpectedly, dryly funny in a way that had surprised a real laugh out of Reza at three in the morning above a carpet shop. And at the end she had said *thank you, sorry, this was strange of me,* and hung up, and Reza had assumed, the way you assumed, that he would never hear that voice again.

She called the next Thursday. And the Thursday after that.

It was fourteen months now. She called most Thursdays, somewhere after two, and Reza — against a guideline he was aware of and had decided, privately, to interpret generously — had stopped doing the assessment thing with her, because she was right, she was never in danger, she was just a woman who got a bad kind of awake on Thursday nights and had found, in a crisis line, the one reliable lit window in her week. They talked about deep-sea fish and bad films and her impossible upstairs neighbour and the allotment she was thinking of getting and never got. He knew her voice better than he knew the voices of people he saw in daylight. He did not know her name. The line did not ask for names, and she had never offered one, and Reza had come to understand that the namelessness was load-bearing — that she could be this honest, this light, this *herself* precisely because he was a voice in the dark with no way to ever become real.

He told himself the same was true in reverse. He told himself that for fourteen months.

But tonight was Thursday, and it was twenty past two, and Reza was sitting in the hum of the strip light not waiting for callers in general the way the job required, but waiting — he could no longer honestly pretend otherwise — for one. For her. For the particular cluster of rings that would be hers, and the *hi, it's me, sorry, is it a quiet night,* and the hour of ordinary things that had become, he was finally admitting to himself in the brick-walled quiet, the part of the week he steered the rest of his days around.

Which was a problem. It was a genuine, professional, several-different-kinds-of problem, and Reza knew it, and he sat with it under the strip light, and at twenty-three minutes past two the phone began to ring in the pattern that the rational part of him insisted no phone could really have, and his heart did the thing it was not supposed to do, and he let it ring exactly once — long enough for her to know she'd been waited for — and picked up.

"Crisis support, this is Reza."

"Hi," said the voice he had organised fourteen months of Thursdays around. "It's me. Sorry. Is it a quiet night?"

"It is now," Reza said, and meant far more by it than the line allowed, and settled in to listen.
