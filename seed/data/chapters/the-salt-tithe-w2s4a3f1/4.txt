I went down at dusk, the next day, because my grandmother said the asking should be done in the between-light, when the day is neither one thing nor the other and the water is most willing to talk.

I carried the bowl. We had argued about that too, briefly, but it was no argument really. The bowl was the thing that had carried the message up to us. It would carry my answer back down. I did not refill it. I carried it empty, and that emptiness felt louder in my arms than the salt ever had.

The village walked me to the top of the dunes and there it stopped, all of it, every soul in Carrow standing in the marram grass with the wind pulling at their coats, and not one of them came down onto the sand. They were not allowed to. The sea knew my hands, and a crowd at the water would be a crowd of strangers, and the water does not answer a crowd. So I went down the last slope alone, and the sound of the village fell away behind me until there was only the sea, breathing.

The tide was low and going lower. The whole grey breadth of the beach lay open, ribbed and shining, and the water was a long way out, a thin bright line under a sky going the colour of an old bruise. I walked toward it. The sand was cold through my boots. The bowl was very light.

I had been told what to do, more or less. My grandmother had told me, and my father, and even the inland priest had offered a few careful words. But none of them had been down here to do it, and so all their telling was guesswork, and I understood, walking out across that ribbed sand, that I was the first person in Carrow in a hundred years who actually knew what the asking would be. Everyone else only knew the carrying. I was about to know the rest.

I reached the water. The little waves were tired, barely turning over, hardly louder than a sleeper. I crouched down and I set the empty bowl in the wet sand where the next wave would just reach it. Then I stayed crouched, with my arms around my knees, and I said what I had come to say.

"You sent it back," I said. My voice did not shake, which surprised me. "We left the measure and you sent it back, and Carrow does not know why. I have come to be told. I am Sala, the miller's daughter, and these are the hands that carry the tithe, and I am asking. What have we failed to pay?"

For a long moment nothing happened, and the not-happening was so complete that I felt foolish, a girl talking to the weather, and I almost stood up and went home.

Then the wave that should have touched the bowl did not touch the bowl. It drew back instead. It drew back further than a wave draws, and further, and the water went on retreating down the sand in a long smooth withdrawal, the way a sheet is drawn back from a bed, and it did not stop. The sea pulled away from me down the whole length of the beach, quietly, without foam, and where it had been there was wet sand, and then there was darker sand, and then there were things standing on the sand that the water had been covering, and I understood that I was being shown a path, and that the asking was not finished. It had only just been answered with a single word, and the word was: come.
