I walked out along the seabed.

I want to be careful, here, telling it, because this is the part the songs get most wrong. They have me walking down through green halls with fish for candles, and it was not like that. It was the most ordinary frightening thing I have ever done. It was simply the beach, going on too far. The same ribbed sand, the same scatter of weed and shell, the same cold light from the bruise-coloured sky. Only the water had stepped aside, and stood up in two long walls to the left and right of me, grey and quiet and patient, near enough to touch and not touching.

I picked up the bowl, because I would not leave it, and I walked the path the sea had opened, and the walls of water walked alongside me without a sound.

I passed the wreck of a boat I half remembered, a fishing boat lost when I was a child, with a man's name on it that the whole village had grieved. I passed the ribs of older boats, and older, going back, a whole drowned fleet lying on its side in the sand, and I understood that the sea had not destroyed these. It had kept them. They were on its account, written down, the way the salt years were written down in my father's cracked book.

And then, ahead of me, in the deepest part of the path, I saw the bell.

It was a church bell, vast, far larger than the small bright bell in Carrow's own tower, and it lay on its side half-sunk in the sand, green with the long sea years, and it was the source of everything. I knew that the way you know things in the between-light. Every story my grandmother had told me about the drowned bell of Carrow had been a true story and a wrong story at the same time, the way folktales are, and here was the true part of it lying in front of me, and I was going to be told the wrong part properly at last.

There had been another village. Before Carrow. Older than the mill, older than the bargain. A village that had stood where the bay was now, and it had a church, and the church had this bell, and the village had done something — paid something wrong, refused something, looked back when it should not have — and the sea had come and taken the whole of it, the houses and the people and the church and the bell, and made the bay.

Carrow was the village built afterward. Built carefully. Built by people who had watched the water take their neighbours and had gone, trembling, to the river mouth, and asked, please, what must we pay so that you do not take us too.

And the sea had told them. A measure of salt. Freely given. Every autumn.

I stood in front of the green bell with the empty bowl in my arms, and the two walls of water leaned a little closer, and from inside the bell, or from under it, or from the whole grey patient weight of the sea around me, a voice that was not quite a voice said the thing I had walked down to be told.

It said: the measure was never the salt.
