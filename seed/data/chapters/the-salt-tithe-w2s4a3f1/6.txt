The measure was never the salt.

I stood with that, in the deep place, between the walls of water, and I felt the whole shape of a hundred autumns shift around me like sand under a wave.

"Then what was it," I said. "What have we been paying, all this time, if not the salt?"

The voice that was not a voice did not hurry. The sea is very old and has never once been in a hurry, and I think that is the most frightening thing about it, more than its size or its cold. It will outwait anything.

It told me. Not in words exactly. It told me the way a tide tells you the moon, by simply doing the thing until you understand it. And what I understood, standing there, was this.

When the first village drowned, the sea had not wanted the houses or the boats or even the bell. The sea has all the houses it wants. What the sea had taken, and kept, and could not make more of, was attention. The drowned village had stopped paying attention to the water. It had built its church with its back to the bay and rung its bell for its own affairs and forgotten, in the comfortable way that villages forget, that it lived on the edge of something vast that had not agreed to be ignored.

So the sea took it. And then it made a bargain with the next village, with frightened careful Carrow, and the bargain was never really about salt. The salt was just the shape the attention took. Once a year the whole village had to stop. Stop its harvests and its arguments and its small warm indoor lives, and walk down to the water together, and stand on the shore, and remember. The salt was only the receipt. The payment was the remembering.

And we had grown careless. Not cruel, not proud, only careless, in the slow way of a hundred safe years. Fewer of us walked down each autumn. The priest had stopped coming. The children were not taught the words. I had carried the tithe alone for three years and thought nothing of it, and three years is nothing to me and nothing to Carrow but it is a long quiet drifting to the sea, which counts in tides.

The salt had come back because the salt had become a lie. We were setting down a measure of salt and calling it the tithe, and keeping the real measure — the stopping, the gathering, the remembering — for ourselves, spending less of it every year.

"What do you want," I said, and my voice did shake now, finally, here at the end. "Tell me the new measure. I will carry it up. Carrow will pay it. Only tell me."

And the sea, between its two grey walls, with the green bell of a drowned village lying witness in the sand, told me what it would cost to keep the bay from rising. It was not salt. It was three things, and a name given freely, and I would have to agree to all of it alone, here, with no one from the village to tell me whether I had the right.

I was seventeen. The water was waiting.

I said, "Tell me the three things."
