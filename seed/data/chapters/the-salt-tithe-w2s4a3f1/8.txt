The first thing the sea asked for was the bell.

Not the green drowned bell lying in the sand before me. That one was the sea's already. It asked for Carrow's bell — the small bright bell in our own tower, the one that rang for births and burials and the turning of the year. The sea wanted it taken down and carried to the shore and set at the tideline, and from then on rung there, on the beach, facing the water, every autumn when the geese went over.

I understood the sense of it at once. A bell rung in a tower faces inward, over the village, for the village. A bell rung on the shore faces the sea. It is the village turning around. It is a hundred years of building with our backs to the bay, undone. It was a hard thing to ask, because a village loves its bell, but it was not a cruel thing, and I said yes, the bell, Carrow will give the bell.

The second thing was the words.

The sea asked that the words of the tithe — take the measure, keep the year, we remember — be taught again. Not kept by the miller, not carried by one girl alone, but taught to every child in Carrow as soon as the child could speak, the way you teach a child its own name. It asked that no one in the village should ever again be able to say they had not been told. This cost nothing but trouble, and trouble is the cheapest coin there is, and I said yes, the words, Carrow will teach the words.

The third thing was the walk.

And the third thing was the heart of it, and the hardest, and I knew it would be before the sea finished asking. The tithe could no longer be carried by one. Every autumn, the whole of Carrow — every soul who could stand, the old on the arms of the young, the babies carried — every one of them must walk down to the shore together, and stand at the water while the shore-bell was rung, and stay there, doing nothing, achieving nothing, simply standing and remembering, until the tide had come in and gone out once. A whole tide. Hours, in the cold and the dark.

That is a great deal to ask of busy frightened people. It is far more than a measure of salt. The salt cost a village nothing but salt. This would cost it a night, every year, forever, and the comfortable forgetting that a village most wants to keep. I stood there and I knew that some in Carrow would hate me for bringing this price up the beach. Henrik would hate me. He would say the sea had cheated, raised the rent on a debt we thought was small.

But it was not raising the rent. I saw that clearly, between the walls of water, with the drowned watching. It was correcting a lie. The price had always been the night and the remembering. We had simply been paying it in salt and pretending. The sea was only asking, at last, to be paid in the true coin.

"Yes," I said. "All three. The bell, the words, the walk. Carrow will pay them."

The walls of water did not fall. The bell in the sand did not ring. The sea only waited, because there was a fourth thing, and we both knew it, and it had been standing between us since I first crouched at the tideline and said my own name out loud.

A name, given freely. The sea had been told my name. Now it wanted to know if I would give it.
