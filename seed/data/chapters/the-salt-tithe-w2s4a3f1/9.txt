Here is what it means to give your name freely to the sea. My grandmother had told me a version of it, and the version was frightening, and the truth was both less and more than the version.

It does not mean you drown. The songs say the girl gave her name and walked into the green and was never seen, and the songs are wrong, and I am writing this account precisely so that the wrongness does not outlive me. I did not drown. I am old now, writing at the mill table, and I have grandchildren of my own who can say the tithe words before they can tie a knot.

To give your name to the sea is to become the one who answers. That is all, and it is everything. The bargain needs a party on the village's side — a single person whose name the sea holds, so that there is always a door for the message to come through. The first miller gave his name. That is why the mill kept the book. The name had passed down the miller's line for a hundred years, quietly, in the cracked ledger, and not one of those millers ever knew, because the bargain had run so smoothly that the door was never knocked on.

The door had been knocked on now. And the name on it — the name the sea had been doing business with for three autumns of salt — was not my father's. It was mine. I had carried the tithe. I had said my name at the water. Without ever being told, without ever agreeing, I had become the door, and the sea was now, at the very end, doing me the courtesy it had done the village: asking. Asking me to give freely the thing it could simply have kept.

If I gave it, I would be Carrow's answer for the rest of my life. Every autumn, when the whole village walked down and the shore-bell rang, I would be the one who stood closest to the water. If the sea ever had a message again, it would come to me. I would carry, always, a small cold weight that was not salt — the knowledge that I was the place where the village touched the thing that could end it. And when I grew old I would have to teach some child to carry it after me, and watch them take it, and know what I was handing over.

If I did not give it, the sea would let me walk back up the open path. It said so. It would let me go home unharmed. But then the door would stay where it was, half-open, knocked on and unanswered, and the bargain would have no party on the living side, and a bargain with only one party is not a bargain. It is a flood, delayed.

I thought about the woman in the shawl. I thought about my father's hand over mine, and the wheel turning in the river it was permitted to use, and the children of Carrow who did not yet know the words and would, now, if I let them have an autumn to be taught in.

I set the bowl down in the sand. I did not need it anymore. The empty bowl had carried the question down and the question was answered, and what I had to carry back up was not a thing that fit in a bowl.

I stood up straight in the deep place, between the grey walls, and I said it the way my grandmother taught me to say the tithe words: not as a prayer, and not quite as a bargain, but as a fact being entered, at last, into the true account.

"My name is Sala," I said, "and I give it freely. I will be Carrow's answer. Knock, and I will come down."

The sea took my name. I felt it the way you feel a tide turn — a vast quiet shift, far out, that changes everything slowly. And then the walls of water began, gently, with no anger at all, to come home.
