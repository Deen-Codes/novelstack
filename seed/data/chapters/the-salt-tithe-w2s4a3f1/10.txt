I walked back up the path with the water coming in behind me, unhurried, keeping pace, never once touching my heels. When I came up out of the deep place onto the ordinary beach, the ordinary tide was already there waiting, and it covered the seabed I had walked, and the wreck of the boat with the man's name on it, and the long drowned fleet, and the green bell, all of it gone under the grey again as if I had dreamed it. But I had not dreamed it. My boots were wet to the knee with the cold that is not the cold of weather, and that cold never entirely left me, all my life. It is the small weight I spoke of. It is the door, kept warm.

Carrow was still on the dunes. They had been there the whole time, hours, in the dark and the wind, and when they saw me come up out of the water alive a sound went through the crowd that I have never heard before or since, and I will not try to write it, because it was not made of words.

I told them everything. I told them at the mill, with the empty bowl on the table where the refused salt had sat, and it took most of the night, and there was an argument, of course, because villages are made of arguments. Henrik said the sea had cheated. He said it exactly as I had known he would. And my grandmother said the thing that ended it, the thing I have thought about every autumn since.

She said: "It did not raise the price. It only stopped letting us lie about it."

We took the bell down. It was hard, and it took three days, and a man broke his wrist, and when it finally stood at the tideline facing the water the whole village felt the wrongness of it and then, slowly, over years, felt the rightness. We taught the words. My own children learned them at my knee, and theirs at mine. And every autumn, when the geese go over, the whole of Carrow walks down to the shore, every soul of us, the old carried, the babies carried, and we stand at the water while the shore-bell rings, and we stay there a whole tide, in the cold and the dark, doing nothing, achieving nothing.

It is not nothing. That is the thing I most want written down, here at the end, for whoever keeps this book after me. It looks like nothing. It is hours of cold for no harvest and no profit, and every year there are young people who grumble that it is a waste, and every year I tell them what the sea told me between its grey walls. The measure was never the salt. The measure is the standing here. The measure is the whole village stopping, together, to remember that it lives on the edge of something vast that did not agree to be ignored.

The bay has not risen. The boats come home. The wheel turns in the river it is permitted to use. And once a year I walk down closest to the water, an old woman now, and I stand where the wet sand begins, and I wait, in case the door is knocked on. It has not been knocked on again in my lifetime. The bargain runs smoothly when it is paid in the true coin.

But I have taught my granddaughter the words, and last autumn I let her carry the shore-bell's rope, and watched her ring it out over the dark water with her small serious face, and I knew she would be the next answer, and that she would have to give her name freely the way I gave mine.

I am sorry for that, the way the woman in the shawl was sorry for me. But it is not a cruel thing. It is only the price of living, awake and remembering, at the edge of a patient sea.

Take the measure. Keep the year. We remember.

This account is true. I am Sala, the miller's daughter, and these are the hands that carried the tithe, and now you have read the letter too.
