Before it would name the three things, the sea showed me a council, and I think it showed me the council so that I would understand I was not the first to stand here bargaining, and would not be the last, and that the bargain was a real thing with real other parties and not a story I was telling myself.

The walls of water grew crowded.

That is the simplest way I can say it. They had been grey and smooth and empty, and now there were shapes standing in them, in the water, the way you might stand in a doorway. Drowned people. The lost village. Not skeletons, not ghosts as the inland priest would mean the word — they were simply the people the sea had kept, the way it had kept the boats, present and accounted for and not at all gone. A woman in an old-fashioned shawl. A man with a net still over his shoulder. Children. They did not look angry and they did not look sad. They looked the way Carrow had looked, standing in the dune grass, watching me go down. They looked like people waiting to see what someone braver would decide.

This was the drowned council. These were the others the bargain was made on behalf of — not against. I had come down thinking of the sea as a creditor and the village as a debtor, two parties, simple. But here was the third party, the one the songs never mention. The drowned were not the sea's prisoners. They were its memory. They were what happened to a village that forgot, kept forever as the reason the next village must not.

The woman in the shawl looked at me, and the bowl in my arms, and I felt — not heard, felt — a thing pass from her to me, gentle, the way my grandmother's hand was gentle. It was not a warning. It was almost an apology. As if she were sorry that the cost of remembering had to be paid by the living, over and over, and sorry that it had fallen tonight to a girl of seventeen with cold feet.

"Did you have a choice?" I asked her. "When the water came. Did anyone walk down, the way I have, and try to ask?"

The sea answered for her, because she could not, because she had nothing to answer with anymore. It said: no one walked down. That was the whole of it. No one walked down. The village rang its bell and barred its doors and waited for the danger to pass the way bad weather passes, and the danger was not weather, and it did not pass.

And I understood, then, why the sea had not simply risen over Carrow when the salt went stale. Why it had sent the bowl back instead, full, like a letter, and waited for someone to read it. The sea did not want another drowned village. It had one. It did not need two. What it wanted — what it had wanted for a hundred years and gone on patiently wanting — was to be remembered by a village that was still alive to do the remembering. It had sent the bowl back as a last courtesy. A creditor's final letter before the door is broken down.

I had been given the courtesy. I was the reading of the letter. And if I walked back up the open path now and told Carrow that I was afraid, that the price was too strange, that we should bar our doors and pray — then I would be the woman in the shawl, a hundred years from now, standing in a wall of water watching some other frightened child come down.

I turned back to the bell.

"Name the three things," I said. "I am still here. I am still asking."
