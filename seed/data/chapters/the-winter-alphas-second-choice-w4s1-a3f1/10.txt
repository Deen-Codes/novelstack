They buried Bricken on the high ridge, where the snow shed first and the spring would reach him soonest. The whole pack climbed up to do it — all sixteen of them, every one alive, which was a sentence Mara would not have believed possible three days before.

Greythorn grieved the way Greythorn did everything: plainly, together, and without pretending. And then Greythorn did the other thing, the thing Mara had not expected, which was that it began, very quietly, to mend.

The Karrholt stores were claimed under the old law of the defending pack — the strong wolf's cellar carried down the pass to the poor wolf's valley, the long terrible winter arithmetic rewritten in a single morning. The cold smithy was lit again. Wolves from two scattered packs nearby, hearing how seventeen had broken forty-four on the eastern road, came down through the snow asking, for the first time in eight years, whether Greythorn might have room.

It had room.

It was a week after the burial that Mara climbed the path to the alpha's house, in the blue evening, and found Garrick waiting on the step as he always somehow was.

"You never told me after," he said.

"You never asked."

"I'm asking."

She came up the last of the path and stood in front of him, and the wound in her chest — the severed place, the rejected place — was not a wound anymore. It had healed into something. She had been afraid, for a long time, of what that something would turn out to be.

She was not afraid now.

"When Callum rejected me," she said, "I thought the worst thing that could happen to a person was to be a second choice. To be the one the moon picked and the wolf didn't. I came here believing I'd been broken in a way that doesn't fix." She looked up at him, at the scar, at the grey at his temple, at the steady dark eyes that had counted every soul in this valley and let not one of them go. "And then I came to a valley full of people every other pack had thrown back. A whole pack of second choices. And I watched it become the bravest thing in these mountains."

Garrick was very still.

"You're not my fated mate, Garrick. The moon didn't tie us. There's no bond in my chest pulling me up this path." She took his hand, and this time neither of them flinched, and this time it meant everything. "I'm here because I chose it. Because I looked at every road I had left and I walked up this one with my eyes open. That's not a second choice. That's the only kind of choosing that was ever worth anything — and I would make it again, and again, until the snow stops falling on these mountains for good."

For a long moment the winter alpha of Greythorn did not move at all.

Then Garrick Vane smiled — the whole smile, the unguarded one, the one Senna had sworn he'd lost eight years ago — and it changed his scarred face into something Mara knew, with absolute certainty, she would spend the rest of her life climbing this path to see.

"Mara Voss," he said. "Rejected by a pack too small to keep you. Chosen by a valley too stubborn to let you go." He drew her in, out of the cold, across the threshold of the house that had let no one in. "Come inside. You've been standing in the snow long enough."

And she went, gladly, into the warm — and behind them the windows of the alpha's house, which had been dark for eight long winters, burned gold into the dusk, and the valley below saw the light, and understood that Greythorn, against every count, had come home.
