The winter stores were kept in a stone cellar under the longhouse, and on her second week Mara was sent down to count them.

She came back up grey-faced.

"That's all of it?" she asked Senna. "That's everything?"

The beta did not pretend otherwise. "That's everything. Salt meat, root crop, the grain we traded for. Enough for the pack we have, if the cold breaks early and nobody gets greedy and the hunting holds."

"And if the cold doesn't break early?"

Senna's silence was its own answer.

Mara had grown up in Ashfall, a pack that had never in her lifetime worried about a cellar. She had thought she understood what it meant for Greythorn to be *diminished*. She had not. Diminished meant arithmetic. It meant a beta who could tell you, to the week, when the food would run out.

She started paying attention after that. She watched the hunting parties come back and counted what they carried. She learned which of the old wolves ate less than their share so the others would not notice the shortfall — and she learned that Garrick Vane was first among them.

She noticed it because she had taken to watching him. She told herself it was strategic. The alpha was the pack, and the pack was now, for better or worse, the only thing standing between Mara and the long cold death of a rogue. It made sense to study him.

It made less sense how often she found her eyes going to him at the common fire, or how the wound in her chest had started to ache in a different register — not the raw tear of the rejection, but something slower, something that felt almost like the opposite of being torn.

She did not have a word for it and she did not want one.

One evening she carried a bowl up the path to the alpha's house, because he had not come down to eat and Senna was too proud to send anyone and Mara, it turned out, was not.

He answered the door before she knocked. Of course he did.

"You skipped the fire," she said, and held out the bowl.

Garrick looked at it. The scar caught the lamplight. "I'm not hungry."

"You're the alpha of a starving pack and you're giving your share away every night, and you think nobody's counting." She did not lower the bowl. "I'm counting. Eat, or I stand here until the stew freezes and then we've wasted it, and Senna can tell me to my face that wasting food in this valley is a sin."

A long silence. The wind worked at the eaves.

And then Garrick Vane did something she had not seen him do in two weeks. The corner of his mouth — the unscarred corner — moved. It was not a smile. It was the distant cousin of a smile, the rumour of one.

"Senna's right," he said. "You do talk too much."

But he took the bowl. And he stepped back from the door, which was not an invitation, except that it was, and Mara found herself stepping over the threshold of the house that let no one in.

The room beyond was spare and warm and lined with books, and there was a second chair by the fire that did not look as though it had been used in eight years.

"Sit," Garrick said, "before you decide that talking me into eating gives you the run of the place."

She sat. He ate. Neither of them spoke for a long while, and the silence was not the cold kind. Outside the snow kept falling on the valley, on the thin stores, on the long hard arithmetic of the winter — and inside, for the length of one bowl of stew, two people who had each been taught they were no longer wanted sat by a fire and were, very quietly, not alone.
