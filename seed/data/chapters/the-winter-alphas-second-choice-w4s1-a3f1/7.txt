The terms came two days later, carried by a single wolf under the grey truce-flag of parley.

He was sleek and well-fed and he wore his health like an insult. He stood in the middle of Greythorn's yard and looked around at the sagging roofs and the cold smithy with an expression of frank, comfortable contempt, and he delivered his alpha's offer in a bored voice, as though the outcome were already decided.

The valley. The stores. The dissolution of the pack. In exchange, Greythorn's wolves would be permitted — *permitted*, the messenger said, savouring it — to enter the Karrholt pack at the lowest rank, provided they were young enough or strong enough to be worth feeding.

"And the rest?" Garrick asked. His voice gave away nothing.

The messenger shrugged. "The old ones. The sick ones." He glanced at Bricken, at the elders, and did not bother to lower his voice. "Karrholt is not a charity. They'll be released."

*Released.* Mara stood at Garrick's shoulder and felt her wolf rise hot and snarling under her skin. *Released* meant turned out. *Released* meant the long cold and the law of rogues. It was the word the Ashfall enforcers had used over her, dressed up clean, and hearing it again she finally understood it was never anything but a sentence.

Garrick was silent for a long moment. The whole pack was silent, watching him, and Mara realized they were not waiting to be saved. They were waiting to be told who they were going to be.

"You've made an error," Garrick said at last. "A common one. You looked at us and you saw what we've lost. Let me tell you what you missed."

He took a step toward the messenger. Just one. The sleek wolf's confidence flickered.

"You're offering to take the strong and discard the weak, and you think that's a generous bargain because that is the only way your pack knows how to count. But I have buried half this valley with my own hands, and I learned something doing it that Karrholt has never had to learn." Another step. "A pack is not its strongest wolves. A pack is the promise that nobody gets *released*. Break that promise and you don't have a pack. You have a number of wolves standing near each other, waiting their turn to be the weak one."

The messenger had stopped looking bored.

"Tell your alpha this," Garrick said. "Greythorn declines. Every wolf here, the young and the old and the sick and the rejected, has been told once already by some other pack that they were not worth keeping. We have heard the offer. We are not interested in hearing it twice." He smiled then, and it was not the rumour of a smile, it was the whole terrible thing. "Tell him if he wants the valley he can come down into it. And tell him to count us again on his way out — because the number he'll be fighting is every single one."

The messenger left faster than he had come.

When the yard had emptied, Senna let out a long breath. "You've just told a pack of forty to come and finish us."

"I've told a pack of forty to come and fight a pack of seventeen that won't break and won't fold and won't run," Garrick said. "Karrholt has never fought wolves like that. They've only ever fought wolves who still had somewhere else to be." He looked at Mara, and the exhaustion in his face had been joined by something fiercer. "Now we have three days. Let's spend them making sure that when they count us again, the number costs them everything they have."
