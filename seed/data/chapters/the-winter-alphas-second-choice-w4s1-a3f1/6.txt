The pack that gathered in the longhouse that night was not an army. Mara made herself look at it clearly. Eleven wolves of true fighting age. Six elders who would fight anyway and shouldn't. Senna, worth three of anyone. Garrick, worth more than that and aging himself a year for every hour of it.

Against them, by the tracks, a scouting party that meant a pack of forty or more.

"They'll send terms first," Senna said. "That's how the strong ones do it. They'll offer us the chance to fold in — give up the valley, give up the stores, scatter into their pack as low wolves or starve as rogues. They'll call it mercy."

"It isn't mercy," said one of the elders, an old man named Bricken whose hands shook but whose voice didn't. "It's a wolf eating another wolf slowly enough to call it kindness."

Garrick stood at the head of the room and let them talk. He was good at that, Mara had learned — he let the pack empty itself of fear before he spoke, so that what he said landed in the quiet.

"They've misjudged us," he said, when the room had run down. "They've counted our numbers and our stores and they think they know what we are. They've counted everything except the one thing that doesn't show in a tally." He looked around the room, face by face. "We have nowhere to go. Every wolf here was put down by some other pack as not worth keeping — too old, too sick, too rejected, too poor. They think that makes us weak."

His eyes found Mara's, and held them, and did not let go.

"It makes us the most dangerous pack in these mountains," he said, "because there is not one soul in this room with anything left to lose."

Something moved through the longhouse then — not a cheer, Greythorn did not have cheers in it anymore, but a stiffening. A straightening of backs. Mara felt it go through her own body like the first warmth after a long cold, and she understood, suddenly and completely, what Senna had meant on her first day. *He'll keep you alive. That's what he does.*

She had thought it sounded grim. Standing in the longhouse, watching a broken pack remember it was a pack, she understood it was the opposite of grim. It was the whole of what love could look like, in a place that had run out of the easy kinds.

The plans went late into the night. Mara stayed for all of it. When the meeting finally broke and the wolves drifted out into the snow, Garrick caught her arm at the door — the first time he had touched her since the day with the axe.

"You don't have to be in this," he said, low, so only she could hear. "You've been here a month. This isn't your war."

Mara looked at his hand on her arm. She looked at the scar, and the grey at his temple, and the exhaustion he carried like a second pelt. And she felt the old wound in her chest — the severed place, the rejected place — and found, to her astonishment, that it had stopped aching.

It had filled with something else.

"Garrick," she said. "I crossed forty miles of frozen forest to get away from a pack that decided I wasn't worth keeping. And I walked straight into the one place that's never once made me feel that way." She covered his hand with hers. "This is the first thing in my whole life that's been mine to fight for. Don't you dare take it from me."

He looked at her for a long, long moment. The fire burned down behind them.

"No," he said softly. "I don't suppose I dare."
