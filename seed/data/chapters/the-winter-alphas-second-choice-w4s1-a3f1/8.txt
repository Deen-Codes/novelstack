They did not spend the three days waiting.

It was Mara, in the end, who said the thing aloud that changed the shape of the fight. They were bent over a crude map of the valley scratched into the longhouse table, and Garrick was speaking of where to make the stand, and Mara looked at the eastern ridge — the high pass, the only road a pack of forty could come down — and said:

"Why are we letting them choose the ground?"

The table went quiet.

"They're stronger," she said. "Everyone keeps saying it like it's the end of the conversation. They're stronger, so we wait, so we defend, so we let them walk down into our valley with the high ground and the numbers and the full bellies. But Garrick — you taught me this land. You taught me where the snow goes deep and where the slopes shed their ice and where the old denning sites are. We know the eastern pass the way they will never know it." She put her finger on the narrow throat of the high road. "We don't have to win a war. We have to make one road too expensive."

Garrick looked at the map for a long time.

"Ambush," Senna said slowly. "Up high. Where forty wolves have to come down single file and seventeen wolves who know every rock can make them pay for each step."

"It won't beat them," Mara said. "Nothing beats them. But it doesn't have to. It has to cost them so much, so fast, that their alpha does the same arithmetic you've been doing all winter — and decides a half-empty cellar in a poor valley is not worth what it's costing him to take it."

It was, Garrick admitted later, the best plan in the room. It was also the kind of plan that only worked once, and only worked if every wolf held their nerve, and Mara — who had been a pack member for exactly one month and a half — was the one who had thought of it.

They moved up to the pass on the second night.

Garrick set the line himself, wolf by wolf, the strong ones at the choke points and the elders further back where age mattered less than knowing the ground. He set Mara high on the eastern shoulder, with Bricken and two of the young wolves, watching the road.

Before he left her to take his own position, he stopped. The snow was coming down in slow fat flakes, settling on his dark hair, on the silvered scar.

"When this started," he said, "I told you it wasn't your war."

"I remember."

"I was wrong. It was always going to be your war. You just hadn't found it yet." He reached out — slowly, the way you reach for a wild thing — and brushed the snow from her shoulder, and let his hand rest there a moment. "Whatever happens on that road tomorrow, Mara — Ashfall threw away the best wolf in these mountains. I want you to know that somebody finally noticed."

The wound in her chest, the old severed place, did something then that it had not done since the night of the bonfire.

It went quiet. Completely, finally quiet — not torn, not aching, just still, the way a thing is still when it has at last been set down.

"Garrick," she said.

"I know," he said. "Tell me after. Tell me when the road's behind us." He stepped back into the falling snow. "Hold the shoulder. Trust the ground. And when it's over — come and find me."

Then he was gone down the line, and Mara turned her face to the eastern road, and waited for the dawn, and the count, and the war that her own clear eyes had made.
