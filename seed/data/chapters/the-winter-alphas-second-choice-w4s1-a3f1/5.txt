The border patrol was Garrick's idea, and Mara's punishment for arguing with him about it.

"You said everyone works," she pointed out. "I want to walk the borders. You walk them alone and that's reckless, and a reckless alpha is a pack's worst problem."

"You've been here a month."

"And in that month I've split four cords of wood, counted the stores twice, and learned the valley better than half the wolves who were born in it. Test me."

So he tested her. He took her out at first light, when the snow was blue and the air was sharp enough to cut, and he walked her the whole eastern line — every boundary stone, every scent-marker, every place where the trees thinned and a stranger might slip through.

He talked more than she expected. Not about himself; never about himself. But about the land he talked freely, and Mara understood, listening, that this was how Garrick Vane loved a thing. Not in words about feeling. In words about *care* — where the deer would yard up when the snow got deep, which slope shed its ice first, where the old denning sites were that a clever wolf could use in a hard night.

"You know all of this," she said, "and there's no one to give it to."

He stopped. They were on a ridge with the whole valley spread below them, Greythorn small and grey and stubborn in the white.

"My sister knew it," he said. The scar was very pale in the morning light. "Renna. She'd have made a better alpha than me. She had the — " he searched for it " — the warmth of it. I have the rest."

It was the most he had ever told her. Mara held very still, the way you held still when a wild thing came close, so as not to frighten it off.

"The sickness," she said quietly.

"The sickness." He started walking again. "Eight years. You'd think it would dull. It doesn't dull. It just — moves over. Makes room for the next thing."

They walked the rest of the line in a silence that had stopped being empty a long time ago.

It was on the way back that they found the tracks.

Garrick saw them first and went still all over, the way a wolf goes still, and Mara felt her own hackles rise before she even understood why. The prints were large. They crossed the boundary line and did not bother to hide it. Three sets — no, four — coming down from the high passes into Greythorn land.

"Rogues?" she breathed.

"No." Garrick crouched, touched the snow at the edge of a print, brought his fingers to his face. When he straightened his expression had changed into something Mara had not seen on him before, and did not like.

"Not rogues. Rogues hide. These didn't hide because they don't think they need to." He looked at the tracks, at the direction they led — down, toward the valley, toward the houses, toward the cellar with its terrible arithmetic. "These are a pack scouting weakness. They've smelled what we are. They've smelled the stores."

The wind came down off the pass, and it carried, very faintly, the scent of strangers.

"What do we do?" Mara asked.

Garrick was quiet for a moment. Then he turned to her, and for the first time he looked at her not as the rejected one, not as the wolf who talked too much, but as something he had decided to count on.

"We get back," he said. "And we wake the pack. And we find out how much fight is left in a place that's run out of everything else."
