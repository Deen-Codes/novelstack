Karrholt came down the pass at first light, and they came arrogant.

Mara watched them from the eastern shoulder, flat against the cold rock, and counted. Forty-four. Sleek and certain, strung out along the narrow road exactly as the ground forced them to be — single file at the throat of the pass, unable to bring their numbers to bear, unable to do anything but come down the one road into the trap that the valley itself had laid for them.

She did not give the signal. That was Garrick's. She waited, and the waiting was the hardest thing she had ever done, harder than the rejection, harder than the forty miles of frozen forest.

The Karrholt column reached the choke point.

Garrick's howl went up from the rocks below — and Greythorn fell on them.

It was not a battle the way the old stories told battles. It was fast and ugly and entirely about the ground. Greythorn's wolves struck from above and behind and from cover, struck hard, and slipped back into rock and snow before Karrholt could close. The valley fought for Greythorn. The deep drifts swallowed the strangers to the chest; the iced slopes took their footing; every place Garrick had ever taught Mara turned out to be a place a Greythorn wolf could use and a Karrholt wolf could only fall.

Mara fought. She had thought, in some quiet frightened part of herself, that she would freeze when it came to it. She did not. She came down off the eastern shoulder with Bricken and the young wolves and she fought for the valley, for the cellar, for the elders who would not be *released*, and her wolf — the wolf that had been born in pain on the night of a rejection — finally, fully, became hers.

But arithmetic is arithmetic, and forty-four is a great deal more than seventeen.

She saw it turn. She saw Karrholt's wolves stop falling for the tricks of the ground, saw them push through the choke point into the wider road where their numbers could finally breathe. She saw the Greythorn line bend.

And she saw, on the road below, the Karrholt alpha himself drive straight at Garrick.

Mara was too far. She knew it even as she ran — knew the distance, knew the snow, knew she could not cover the ground in time. She ran anyway, screaming his name in a voice gone all wolf, and below her Garrick met the Karrholt alpha on the bloodied road and the two of them went down together in a snarl of fang and snow.

It was Bricken who turned it. Old shaking Bricken, who should not have been on that road at all, threw himself sideways into the Karrholt alpha's flank — bought one heartbeat, one, with the last of a long life — and that single heartbeat was all Garrick needed.

When Mara reached the road the Karrholt alpha was down and not getting up, and the strangers at the choke point had seen their alpha fall, and the count had finally come due.

A pack that fights for a valley fights to the death. A pack that fights for a cellar fights only until the price gets too high. Karrholt broke. They broke and they ran back up the pass they had come down, and Greythorn — bleeding, gasping, impossibly still standing — held the road.

Mara found Garrick on his knees in the red snow, alive, gripping old Bricken's body, and she dropped down beside him and did not say any of the things there were to say, because some things the body says better.

She put her arms around the winter alpha of Greythorn, and he let her, and the two of them knelt in the dawn on the road they had held, and the valley below them was still theirs.
