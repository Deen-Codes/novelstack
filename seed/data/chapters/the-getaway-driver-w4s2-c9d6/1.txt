The driver took the long way round, and Camila noticed, and said nothing, and that was the first thing that ever passed between them — a noticing, in silence, in a moving car.

His name was Reuben. That was all the household had told her, and the household told her everything else in exhausting detail: the new driver was approved, vetted, contracted, his references confirmed by people whose job was confirming references. He would take her where she needed to go. He did not socialize with the family. He did not, the household added, with the particular delicacy they reserved for the family's questions about staff, have a past they intended to discuss.

Camila had run his name herself, that first night, because she was twenty-six years old and tired of being the only person in her own life who wasn't allowed access to it. It hadn't taken long. Reuben Castellan had been, for three years, the kind of racing driver whose name people knew. And then there had been a conviction — something to do with the people who'd funded the team, money that had turned out to be the wrong money — and there had been time served, and there had been, after it, a man who drove other people's cars for a living and did not, the search results agreed, give interviews anymore.

She got into the back of the car the next morning and looked at the back of his head and decided not to mention any of it.

"You took Ferris Street," she said instead, a week later. "There's a faster way to the gallery. There's been a faster way every day this week and you haven't taken it once."

In the mirror, his eyes found hers. He had steady eyes. That was a strange thing to notice about a person and she noticed it anyway.

"There's a grey sedan," Reuben said, "that's been three cars back of us every morning since Monday. Different drivers. Same car. The fast way is a straight road and a straight road makes it easy for them. I take the turns so I can see who keeps turning." He said it the way other people read out a weather forecast. "I wasn't going to mention it until I was sure. I'm sure now. Someone is following you, Miss Vasquez, and they're not very good at hiding it, which honestly worries me more than if they were."

Camila sat very still in the back of the moving car.

"You should have told me."

"I'm telling you. I have a rule about not asking the family questions, and a habit of not volunteering things I can't back up. But you asked me a direct one just now — about Ferris Street — and I don't lie to a direct question. That's a different rule." He took another turn, unhurried, watching the mirror. "I've told the household. They've told me to keep to the routes and let their security handle it. I'm going to do that. I just thought the person actually sitting in the car had a right to know what the car was doing."

She studied the back of his head, this quiet man with the convicted past and the steady eyes and the rule about direct questions, and she understood something that the household, with all its vetting, had entirely missed. They had hired him because his references were clean and his record made him cheap and grateful. They had not noticed that they had also, by accident, hired the only person in Camila Vasquez's carefully managed life who had so far told her one single thing that was completely true.

"Reuben," she said. "If the household's security is wrong — if the grey car turns out to be something real — what would you do? Not what would you be told to do. What would you actually do."

The car slowed at a light. He was quiet for a moment, and outside the window the ordinary city went about the ordinary business of not being followed.

"I'd stop taking the routes," he said finally. "I'd take you somewhere that isn't on anyone's map, and I wouldn't tell the household where, because a thing that's known to a household is known to everyone the household talks to." His eyes met hers in the mirror again, level, certain. "But you didn't hire me, Miss Vasquez. Your family did. So that's not my call to make. It'd have to be yours."

The light turned green. The car moved. Three cars back, grey and patient, something turned with them.

And Camila Vasquez, heiress, watched and protected and managed her whole life, felt the route of it begin, very quietly, to come off the map.
