Riven Marsh had three rules, and she had built a livable life out of refusing, ever, to bend them.

Rule one: the blood was always paid for, always consensual, always logged. Her donors came in clean and sober and walked out the same way with money in their pockets, and the registry on her counter recorded every drop, because the treaty lived or died on registries, and she was not going to be the blood-house owner whose ledger started a war.

Rule two: no names. The vampires who came through her door on Nightshade Strip were customers, and customers got a number and a curtained booth and a paid donor and nothing else. She did not learn their names. She did not let them learn hers. A name was a hook, and hooks were how careful people got pulled out of their careful lives.

Rule three, and the one that mattered most: Riven herself was never on the menu. She owned the house. She did not work the booths. She had not given a vampire so much as a paper cut in the six years since she'd hung the lantern over the door, and she intended to keep that record until the day she sold the place and moved somewhere the sun was allowed to mean something.

It was a Tuesday, slow and wet, the rain coming down on the Strip in that fine grey way that made the neon bleed, when the man came in and broke all three of her rules inside of a minute.

She knew what he was before the door finished closing. You learned to know, running a house like hers — something in the stillness of them, the way they took up space without seeming to displace any air. He was tall and rain-dark and unhurried, and he crossed the room to her counter and did not look at the curtained booths or the chalkboard of rates, which was the first wrong thing, because vampires who came to Nightshade always looked at the rates.

"I'd like a donor," he said.

"Then you've come to the right place." Riven slid the registry toward him and uncapped the pen. "Number, not a name. Pick a booth that's lit green. The board has the rates by volume. My donors are all —"

"I'd like you."

The rain ticked against the window. Somewhere down the Strip a tram groaned around its curve.

Riven set the pen down very precisely on the open registry, and looked up, and let him see her face do absolutely nothing at all, because a blood-house owner who let her face move was a blood-house owner who got eaten.

"The owner isn't a donor," she said. "That's a house rule. It's the house rule. You can have any donor on the green board, and they're good, they're the best on the Strip, or you can have the door. Those are the options."

"I know your rules." He said it without heat, almost gently, and that was the second wrong thing, because he said it as though he had read them, as though her three private rules were a document he had studied. "Rule one, paid and logged. Rule two, no names. Rule three, never you." He tilted his head. The light caught his eyes and they were the wrong color, that deep arterial red that meant old, that meant powerful, that meant get this one out of my house. "I'm not breaking rule one. I'll pay, and you can log it, log every word of this if you like. I'm not asking you to break rule two — I won't give you my name and I don't want yours. But rule three I'm afraid I have to break, Riven Marsh, and not because I want your blood."

The fact that he knew her name went through her like cold water. Nobody on the Strip knew her name. She had spent six years making sure of it.

"Then we're done," she said, and her hand was already moving under the counter toward the silver-loaded crossbow she had never once had to use. "Whatever this is —"

"There's a vampire coming to this district," the man said, "in eleven days, and when he gets here the treaty on the Strip is going to end. Not strain. End. And every blood-house owner with a clean registry and a paid-and-consensual ledger is going to be the first thing the new order burns, because clean registries are evidence, and the people coming don't want evidence." He had not moved. His hands were flat and open on her counter, well away from his body, the deliberate stillness of a man showing a frightened person he held no weapon. "I came to you first. Out of every house on this Strip, I came to yours first, because yours is the cleanest, and the cleanest house has the most to lose, and the most reason to listen."

Riven's hand was on the crossbow. She did not draw it. She hated, in that moment, that she did not draw it — that some cold practical animal in the back of her skull had heard eleven days and the treaty is going to end and had decided, against six years of hard-won caution, to keep listening.

"Why," she said, "would you tell me this. What's the donor for, then, if you don't want to feed."

For the first time something crossed the man's still face — and it was not hunger, and it was not threat. It was something older and more tired, something that looked almost like grief.

"The donor's for the registry," he said. "I need to be a logged customer in your ledger, Riven. I need a reason to be able to walk back through that door. Because eleven days isn't long, and I cannot fix what's coming alone, and you are the only person on Nightshade Strip with the cleanest hands and the most to lose — which makes you, whether either of us likes it, the only person worth trusting."

The rain came down on the Strip. The neon bled in the window.

And Riven Marsh, who had three rules, picked up the pen.
