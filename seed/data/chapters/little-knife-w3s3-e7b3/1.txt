The first thing my daughter said that frightened me, she said over breakfast, with jam on her chin, in the ordinary voice she used for everything.

"You shouldn't have moved the car, Mummy."

I was at the counter with my back to her, buttering toast, and I remember my hand stopped. Just for a second. Just long enough that if anyone had been watching me they would have seen it stop and start again, and the only person in the room was Pia, who was eight, and who was not watching me, who was lining her cereal up by colour the way she did, absorbed, content, a small girl on an ordinary Tuesday.

"Which car, sweetheart?" I said. I kept my voice the way you keep a cup level when you carry it.

"The blue car. From the end of the road." She put a yellow cereal piece in her mouth. "You moved it after. That was a silly thing to do."

I turned around. I made myself turn around slowly. Pia was still arranging her cereal, the yellows in a curve now, and she did not look up, and there was jam on her chin, and she was, by every visible measure, exactly what she had been thirty seconds earlier — my daughter, eight years old, eating breakfast.

"I don't know what you mean, Pia."

"Yes you do," she said. Mildly. Not unkindly. The way she might tell me I'd forgotten her water bottle. And then she did look up, and she smiled at me, a normal smile, gap where the front tooth had come out, and she said, "Can I have more toast," and the moment closed over like water, and breakfast went on.

I want to be precise about why this frightened me, because precision is the only tool I have left and I have decided, writing this down, that I am going to use it properly.

It did not frighten me because children say strange things. Children say strange things constantly; it is half of what they are for. If Pia had told me there was a tiger in the garden or that her teacher was secretly a robot I would have laughed and loved her and forgotten it by lunch.

It frightened me because of the blue car.

There had been a blue car, at the end of our road, eleven months ago. It had belonged to a man named Stellan Brandt, who lived in the last house, who I had not liked, for reasons I had told no one, reasons that were mine and stayed mine. And eleven months ago something had happened with that blue car, late, on a wet night, something I had been part of and had never spoken of to a single living person — not to my husband, not to the police who came round afterward asking their gentle door-to-door questions, not to my own reflection. I had moved the car. Afterward. It had been, as my eight-year-old daughter had just observed over her cereal, a silly thing to do, and I had known it was silly while I was doing it, and I had done it anyway, because fear does not consult you first.

Nobody knew. That is the load-bearing fact of my life, the one everything else rests on. Nobody knew, because I had been careful, and because I had been alone, and because the only witness to any of it had been the dark and the rain.

And Pia had been four years old and asleep in her bed three miles away.

I cleared the breakfast things. I gave her more toast. I drove her to school and I watched her run in through the gates with her bag bouncing, an ordinary child among ordinary children, and I sat in the car in the school car park for a long time with my hands at ten and two, not driving anywhere.

There were, I told myself, explanations. I am a rational woman; I built a whole adult life on being the rational one. She had overheard something. Children overhear everything; they are small and quiet and we forget they are in the room. She had pieced it together from fragments, the way children do, and landed on a sentence that only sounded like knowledge. That was possible. That was the explanation a sensible mother would hold on to with both hands.

But I kept coming back to the words she had used. Not after the accident. Not after the man got hurt. After. Just after. The exact, bare, careful word I used in my own head, the word I had never said aloud, because saying more than after would have meant admitting what came before it.

She hadn't heard that anywhere. There was nowhere to hear it. It lived in one place only, and that place was inside me, locked, and my daughter had reached in over the breakfast cereal and taken it out and set it on the table between us, gently, with jam on her chin.

And she had smiled. That was the part I could not put down, sitting in the school car park with my hands at ten and two. When she said you shouldn't have moved the car, she had not been frightened, and she had not been confused, and she had not been guessing.

She had been telling me she knew. And she had wanted, very much, for me to understand that she knew.
