Vex stole an hour from a stranger's head on a Tuesday and it ruined her whole year, which was about par for a Tuesday.

The job came in clean. They always came in clean; that was the trade. A broker she half-trusted, a meeting in a noodle bar in the wet under-levels where the city's drainage made its own weather, a card slid across the table with a face on it and a price under the face. The face was a man, mid-fifties, soft, the kind of soft that money makes. The price was enough to keep Vex in clean memory-stock and dirty rent for six months.

"One hour," the broker said. "His last one. You know the work."

She knew the work. Vex was a tithe-runner, which was a polite word, and a memory thief, which was the honest one. She did not steal jewels or data or any of the old reliable things. She stole the last hour of a person's life — the final sixty minutes before some event the client wanted erased from a head. A confession. A betrayal. A face seen that should not have been seen. You went in through the mark's feed-implant, you found the hour, you lifted it clean, and the mark woke up with a soft grey gap where the hour had been and no idea anything was missing. That was the cruelty and the genius of it. You cannot mourn an hour you do not remember living.

The rule of the trade, the only rule, the rule Vex had kept for eleven years the way a sailor keeps clear of rocks, was this: you never keep a memory. You lift it, you deliver it, you wipe your own buffer clean. A runner who keeps memories goes wrong. Everyone knew a story. The hour belongs to someone else; carry it long enough and it starts to feel like it belongs to you, and a head with two pasts in it does not stay a head for long.

Vex had never broken the rule. Vex was very good, and very careful, and she intended to retire one day to somewhere with actual weather and die with only her own memories rattling around in her skull.

The job went wrong forty seconds in.

She found the mark — alone, as promised, slumped in a transit pod in a parking stack, sedated by her own broker's people, as promised. She jacked into his feed-implant, found the hour, the last hour, glowing in his recent-stack like a coal. She reached for it.

And the hour reached back.

That should not happen. A memory is not alive. It does not have intentions. But the instant Vex's runner-software touched that hour, something in it latched onto her like a hand closing on a wrist, and instead of lifting the hour clean out of his head it poured the hour into hers, all sixty minutes of it, fast and total and unwiped, straight past every safeguard she had, and Vex sat down hard on the floor of the parking stack with a stranger's last hour burning behind her eyes and her own name nowhere in it.

She knew, instantly, three things, and none of them were good.

She knew that the hour she had just inhaled was not a confession or a betrayal. It was a recording — a deliberate one. The soft moneyed man had not been a mark. He had been a courier, and the hour had been the package, and someone had built it as a trap for exactly the runner sent to lift it.

She knew, because the hour told her, in its last burning minutes, what it contained: a name, a location, and a number, and the number was the kind of number that ends cities.

And she knew — because the moment the hour finished pouring into her she heard, through the parking stack, the soft synchronized tread of a corporate kill-team coming up the ramp — that the trap had a second half, and that the second half was tracing the leak, and that the leak was now sitting on the floor of a parking stack with someone else's catastrophe in her skull and exactly one rule of her trade already broken.

Vex got up. Her knees did not entirely agree to it. The neon from the stack's signage washed over her, pink and blue and indifferent, and somewhere below her the kill-team climbed, and she did the math that runners do, the cold fast math of corridors and exits and how long a head has before it becomes a liability that someone closes.

"Okay," she said, out loud, to the empty pod and the burning hour and the city that had just sold her. "Okay. New plan. Survive the Tuesday."

And she ran.
