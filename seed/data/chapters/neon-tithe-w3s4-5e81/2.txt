There was exactly one person in the city Vex could go to with a head full of someone else's apocalypse, and that was the worst possible part of the worst possible Tuesday, because the person was Sable Roe, and Sable Roe had spent two years wanting Vex either in her bed or in a shallow grave and had never been entirely clear which.

Sable was a memory-cutter — a surgeon of the illegal kind, the kind who could go into a head and trim what should not be there. She was also a former runner, a current cynic, and the only ex-anything Vex had ever managed to keep alive in the same city as herself. They had worked one job together, two years ago. The job had gone beautifully and the aftermath had gone to pieces, the way things did when two people who solved problems for a living tried to solve each other. Vex still did not know whose fault it had been. Vex suspected, in the parts of herself she did not visit often, that it had been mostly hers.

She came up the back stair of Sable's clinic at three in the morning with a kill-team somewhere behind her in the city and the borrowed hour still smoldering behind her eyes, and she did not knock, because Sable's locks had never quite forgotten her, and because knocking felt like asking permission and Vex had run out of the kind of pride that asks.

Sable was awake. Sable was always awake; it was one of the eleven things about her that Vex had told herself she did not miss. She was sitting at her cutting bench in the blue light of her instruments, and she looked up when the door opened, and her face did the whole journey in about a second and a half — surprise, then something warmer that she clearly hated, then the cold professional flatness she put on the way other people put on gloves.

"No," Sable said.

"You don't know what I'm going to ask."

"I know it's three in the morning and you came up my back stair and you have the look. You always get the look right before you make my life worse. So. No." But she did not get up, and she did not reach for the panic-stud under her bench, and Vex had been reading Sable Roe for two years and knew that not reaching for the panic-stud was the closest thing to yes that this conversation was going to produce.

"A job went wrong," Vex said. She came into the blue light. She let Sable see how badly her hands were doing. "Not wrong like a lift that slipped. Wrong like a trap. I went in for an hour and the hour came into me — all of it, unwiped, latched on and poured. It's still in here." She tapped her temple, and regretted it, because it made the hour flare. "And it's not a confession, Sable. It's a weaponized recording. There's a name in it and a place and a number, and the number is — it's bad, it's the kind of bad that doesn't fit in one head safely, and there is a corporate kill-team in this city right now tracing the leak, and the leak is me, and you are the only cutter I trust enough to let inside my skull."

Sable was very still. The blue light moved on her face.

"You kept a memory," she said. Quiet. Almost gentle, which was worse than shouting. "Eleven years you held that one rule over the rest of us like you were better made. And you kept a memory."

"I didn't keep it. It kept me. There's a difference and I need you to believe the difference, because if you think I went soft and greedy you'll cut wrong."

"If I cut at all."

"Sable."

And here it was, the thing Vex had carried up the back stair underneath the catastrophe, the small private thing that had nothing to do with kill-teams. She made herself say it, because there was no version of the next hour that worked if she did not.

"I came to you," Vex said, "not because you're the best cutter in the city, though you are. I came to you because two years ago I let a good thing fall apart rather than admit I needed it, and I have spent two years being very proud of how little I need anyone, and tonight that pride is going to get me killed and possibly the city with me. So I'm here. With the look. Asking. And I know exactly how much you get to enjoy this."

Sable stared at her for a long moment. Then she stood up, and crossed the clinic, and shot the heavy bolt on the door Vex had not bothered to lock behind her, and turned, and her face had given up on the professional gloves entirely and just looked tired and furious and something else that Vex did not let herself name yet.

"Sit on the bench," Sable said. "If there's a kill-team tracing you, they're tracing the implant signature, which means I have maybe an hour before they're on this street. So we are not going to have the conversation we should have had two years ago. We are going to have it later, if there is a later, and you are going to be there for all of it." She pulled a tray of instruments toward her. "Now lie down, shut up, and let me see what somebody poured into your stupid, brave, infuriating head."
