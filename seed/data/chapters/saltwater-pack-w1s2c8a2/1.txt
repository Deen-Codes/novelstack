The ferry to Aldermere ran twice a day in summer and once a day in winter, and it was the once-a-day season when Tessa arrived, which told her something about the island before she'd set a boot on it.

She stood at the rail with her gear bagged at her feet and watched the place resolve itself out of the morning haze — a low green hump of land ringed in black rock, a single huddled village at the harbour, and beyond the village nothing but moor and cliff and the long pale crescents of beach where, the grant paperwork promised, the largest undisturbed grey seal colony on the coast hauled out to pup each autumn.

Tessa loved seals the way other people loved their families: completely, and with a willingness to be inconvenienced. Six years of fieldwork, two of them spent writing the funding bid that had finally, finally come through, and here she was, and the cold salt wind was in her face, and she was as happy as she knew how to be.

The man waiting on the quay did not look happy at all.

She knew he was waiting for her because he was the only person on the quay not doing something else, and because he watched the ferry come in with the particular flat attention of someone counting an unwelcome thing toward shore. He was big, and weathered, and somewhere in his thirties, with a fisherman's forearms and a face that had clearly decided about her hours ago.

"You'll be the seal woman," he said, when she came down the ramp. Not a question.

"Tessa Hale. Marine Mammal Survey." She put her hand out. He looked at it long enough to make a point, then shook it, once, briefly. His grip was startlingly warm — almost feverish, she thought, surprised, on a quay this cold. "And you are?"

"Callum." He picked up her heaviest bag without being asked, which she would have appreciated more if he hadn't done it with the air of a man removing an obstacle. "I keep the harbour. I'll take you up to the cottage. The Survey arranged it through the council and the council arranged it through me, so." He started up the quay without finishing the sentence, which seemed to be a habit of his.

Tessa followed, hauling the rest of her gear, and got her first proper look at Aldermere.

It was beautiful, and it was watching her.

She didn't mean that the way it sounded — she was a scientist, she did not, as a rule, indulge in the feeling. But there was no other honest word for the way the village received her. Doors did not slam. People did not stare. It was subtler and stranger than that: a woman pegging out washing went still as Tessa passed, the way a deer goes still; two men mending a net by the slipway stopped talking, not rudely, just — completely, the way a room of people stops talking when a stranger's name has just been mentioned. An old woman in a doorway looked at Tessa, and then looked, deliberately, at Callum, and something passed between them that Tessa was not meant to catch and caught anyway.

"Friendly place," she said, to see what he'd do with it.

"It's a small island." Callum didn't slow down. "Small islands count their strangers. You'll do your work and the seals will do theirs and in three months the ferry'll take you off again, and that's the whole of it." He glanced back at her, and his eyes in the grey morning light were an unusual colour, a pale amber she didn't have a name for. "Best thing you can do here, Miss Hale, is keep to the beaches and the daylight. The colony's all south of the headland. There's no reason for your work to take you north of it, and no reason for you to be out past dusk, and if you stick to those two things we'll get along."

It was, very politely, a warning. Tessa had been doing fieldwork in remote places long enough to know a warning when she was handed one, and long enough to know that a warning is also a map — that the thing a place tells you not to do is always, exactly, the shape of the thing it is hiding.

North of the headland. Past dusk.

"Noted," she said pleasantly, and meant nothing by it at all.

The cottage sat alone above a curve of grey beach, whitewashed and snug, and Callum dumped her bag inside the door and gave her a tide table and a list of phone numbers and left without taking the tea she offered, all in under five minutes, like a man discharging a duty he found faintly distasteful.

Tessa watched him walk back toward the village from her new front step, and then she turned to look at the sea.

The tide was low. The grey beach ran out long and shining, and the seals were there, dozens of them, dark commas on the wet sand, and Tessa felt the old happiness lift in her again at the sight.

But it was not the seals that made her stand very still on the step with her tea going cold in her hand.

It was that one of them, far out on the tide line, had lifted its head from the sand — and across two hundred metres of beach, unmistakably, deliberately, it had turned to look at her.

Seals were curious. She knew that. Seals watched people all the time; it meant nothing.

This did not feel like nothing. This felt, in a way that lifted the hair on her arms and that she would spend a long time refusing to think about, like being recognized.

The seal held her gaze for a long moment. Then it turned, and humped its slow way down the wet sand, and slipped into the grey water without a splash — and Tessa stood on the step of the cottage she had wanted for two years, and felt the island close quietly around her, and counting.
