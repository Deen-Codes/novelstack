There remained the matter of her father, and the matter of the name.

Honora travelled back to London alone — she had insisted on alone; some conversations a person must carry without an escort — and she found the workshop much as she had left it, smelling of ink and wood-shavings, and her father at the wide table with a glass clamped to his eye, working a coastline she could see, from the door, was slightly wrong.

She told him everything. She had decided on the coach that she would tell him everything, in order, holding nothing back, because she was about to marry into a life built on a closed room and she would not begin her own marriage by opening a new one in her father's house.

He heard her out. He set down the glass. He was quiet a long while, her father, looking at the half-drawn map, and Honora waited, braced once more against something, for the objection — the difference in their stations, the speed of it, the impossibility.

"Forty pounds," he said at last. "That was the commission. Forty pounds for a survey of an estate." He looked up at her, and his eyes were wet, and he was — she realized with a lurch — not grieved at all, but trying not to laugh and not to weep at the same time, and losing at both. "Honora. I sent my daughter west to chain some hedgerows for forty pounds, and she has come back an earl's betrothed. Your mother would never have let me hear the end of it. Never. She always said I undercharged."

And then he did laugh, and she crossed the workshop and put her arms around him, and for a while neither of them said anything sensible at all.

But there was the name. She made herself come back to it, later, over the cold supper they always ate when work ran long. The name was the thing that had brought her to Wexcombe in the first place — the Vane name, the master's name, the name the world hung on its walls and believed a man had drawn.

"I am to be a countess, Papa," she said. "Crispin knows what I am. He knows every map that has left this shop these two years was mine. He knows I signed your name to them, and he does not care, and he has said — " she steadied herself, " — he has said that the Wexcombe estate plan, the fair copy, the one I have spent the winter drawing, will hang in the great hall under my own name. Honora Aldous. Cartographer. He says a house should not have a lie on its wall."

Her father was quiet again. She watched him understand it — that the long, useful, shameful fiction was ending; that the daughter who had carried his failing craft would now carry her own name on the work, and that the world would, in at least one great hall, be made to know it.

"Good," he said. Just that, at first. Then, gruffly, looking at the slightly wrong coastline under his hand: "It was always a lie I hated, you know. Putting my name on your maps. I told myself it was for the family, for the bread, and that was true, but it was also — Honora, it was also a small daily cowardice, and I have known it for a small daily cowardice for two years, and I have not had the courage to do what your earl has done in a single sentence." He pushed the unfinished map an inch away from himself. "Sign your own name, girl. From this day. On everything. I should have made you do it long ago, and the only honest thing I can do now is tell you so, plainly, before you go."

It was, Honora thought afterward, on the coach rolling west again toward Wexcombe and Crispin and her whole reckless unmeasured future — it was a season of shut rooms being opened. The east wing. Lady Margaret's barred gate. And now this, the oldest and quietest closed door of her own life: the name she had not been allowed to own.

She watched the country open up around her, bare hedgerows softening now toward spring, and she took out her field-book, and on the inside cover, where for two years she had written nothing at all, she wrote her name. Her own. In ink, in her own clear cartographer's hand, with no one's leave but her own.
