She did not sleep well that night, and so she was awake to hear him.

The west wing where they had lodged her was a place of small noises — the settle and tick of old timber, the wind finding its way through casements that no longer met true. Honora had grown used to all of them. What she was not used to, an hour past midnight, was the sound of a man's footstep in the gallery below her, slow and even, going somewhere with purpose.

She told herself it was none of her affair. She told herself a great many sensible things, and then she lit her candle and went down anyway, because she was a cartographer's daughter and a footstep in the dark was, to her, simply another blank space asking to be filled.

She found him in the long gallery. He had not heard her. The Earl of Wexcombe stood at the window — the same window he always stood at — and he was holding something small in both his hands and looking down at it, and the moonlight had taken the colour out of him and left only the shape: a tall man, bowed at the shoulders, entirely alone in a house he had emptied on purpose.

"My lord."

He did not startle. He simply closed his hands around the small object and did not turn.

"You should be asleep, Miss Vane."

"So should you." She came no closer. She set her candle on a side table so that the light made a small warm room of the two of them in all that dark. "What is it you are holding?"

For a long moment she thought he would not answer. Then he opened his hands. It was a child's toy — a little wooden horse, the paint worn from its flanks by years of a small thumb passing over the same place.

"Her name was Beatrice," he said. "My sister. She was eleven years younger than I, which is to say she was, in every way that signifies, more my child than my sister. Our parents were not — " he searched for the word and discarded several, " — present. So she was mine to raise, and I raised her, and I was, I think, not bad at it." The corner of his mouth moved, not into a smile. "She was clever like you. Impertinent like you. She could not leave a question alone, either."

Honora stood very still. The fire she had been wanting, all those weeks — the door opening — and now that it had opened she found she was almost afraid to go through.

"The year of the scar," she said.

"The year of the scar." He turned at last, and the moonlight caught the silvered line of it, and he let her look. "There was a fire, Miss Vane. In the east wing. Six years ago. I went in for her. I did not — " His voice did the thing a hinge does when it has rusted past use. He stopped. He began again, more carefully, laying the words down like a man crossing rotten ice. "I came out, and she did not. That is the whole of it. That is the entire history of this house, and of me, and of every shut and shuttered room in it. I came out, and she did not, and I have spent six years deciding that the correct response to that is to make myself as small and quiet and useless as a man can be. You named it this afternoon. You named it exactly. I have been haunting my own life."

The little wooden horse sat in his open palm between them.

Honora did not say she was sorry. She had buried her own mother; she knew what the word was worth, which was nothing, and she respected him too much to hand him nothing.

Instead she said, very quietly, "Show me her room."

And the Earl of Wexcombe — who had refused the world for six years, who had let forty rooms go dark sooner than let one stranger near the one that mattered — looked at the cartographer's daughter standing in her candlelight, and closed his hand around the horse, and said, "Not tonight. But soon. I find — Miss Vane, I find I want to show you. I had not thought I would ever want that again."

It was not a declaration. It was nothing she could have written down or measured. But Honora carried it back up the cold stairs as though it were a lit thing, and she held it all the way into a sleep that came, this time, easily.
