She might have left then. A prouder woman would have. Honora lay awake that night and assembled the prouder woman's speech — dignified, brief, edged — and found she did not want to give it, because under the hurt was a thing she trusted more than her pride, and the thing was this: she did not believe he had finished speaking.

He had said he was good at giving up what he wanted. He had not said he was going to.

So in the morning she did not call for a coach. She put on her heaviest wool and her good boots, and she took her field-book, and she went and stood in the cold east lawn — the one that drained badly, the one where the old culvert had collapsed under the yew walk — directly beneath the single window of the east wing whose shutter was never quite closed. And she waited, the way she had waited on a hundred fields, for the land, or the man, to tell her the truth.

It was not long. He came out to her bare-headed in the rain, and Lady Margaret's grey shape watched from a window of the house, and the Earl of Wexcombe walked the whole length of his ruined lawn in full view of his aunt to stand in front of a tradesman's daughter, and Honora understood, before he said one word, that the giving-up was over.

"Come inside," he said. "I am going to show you the hole in the map."

He took her up through the west wing and through a door Mrs. Pell unlocked without being asked — so that Honora understood this had been arranged, decided, that he had lain awake too — and into the east wing, which was not, as she had half imagined, a ruin of char and ash. The fire had been six years ago. The wing had been cleaned, repaired, and then simply shut, room after room of furniture under white holland sheets like a household of ghosts sitting very still.

All but one room.

He stopped at its door. He did not open it at once. "I have not let anyone in here," he said, "since I had it set right. Not my aunt. Not Mrs. Pell, beyond the dusting. No one. I told myself it was a kind of faithfulness. I have come to think it was only another shut door, and that Beatrice — who could not bear a shut door in her life — would have been ashamed of me for it." He put his hand to the latch. "She would have liked you enormously, Honora. That is the thought that finally made me able to do this. She would have liked you, and she would never have forgiven me for sending you away."

He opened the door.

It was a child's room, and a girl's — kept, exactly as he had said, as it should be. A small bed neatly made. A bookshelf. A window seat with a cushion worn pale in one spot. On the sill, where the light would catch it, the little wooden horse, returned to its proper place. The room was not a shrine. That was what undid Honora, standing in the doorway with her field-book forgotten in her hand. It was not a shrine and it was not a wound. It was simply a room where someone beloved had lived, kept warm and dusted and aired by a man who had not known any other way to say I have not stopped.

"Thank you," she said, and her voice was not steady at all now, and she let it not be. "For showing me. The map is true now, my lord. There is no hole in it anywhere."

He turned from the window seat. The grey light was on the scar and she had long since stopped seeing it as anything but part of his face, part of the whole map of him, every line of which she had now been given.

"Crispin," he said. "I would like, very much, for you to call me Crispin. And then I would like to ask you the thing I should have asked you in the library, before my aunt and her miniature, before I let six years of cowardice answer for me." He crossed the small room. This time he did take her hands, ink-stained cuffs and all. "Stay. Not as a surveyor. As my wife, Honora — and let Hampshire keep its complexion, and let Bath say what it likes, and let every drawing-room in England measure us and find us wanting. I have done the sensible thing. It cost me everything. I am asking you, instead, to do the reckless one, with me, for the rest of our lives."

Outside, very faintly, Honora heard the wheels of Lady Margaret's carriage. But she was not listening to the carriage. She was looking at the true and complete and unhidden map of the man in front of her, and she was already, in the part of her that drew the fair copy, beginning to letter in the legend.

"Yes," she said. "Crispin. Yes."
