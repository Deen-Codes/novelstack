But tomorrow brought, instead, the carriage of Lady Margaret Aldous.

Honora heard it before she understood it — wheels on the gravel, brisk and certain, the sound of arrival in a house that had forgotten the meaning of the word. By the time she came down to the hall, the visitor had already shed her travelling cloak into the arms of a startled Mrs. Pell and was surveying the dim, half-shuttered entrance with the expression of a general assessing a defeated country.

She was perhaps sixty, upright as a fence-post, dressed in expensive grey, and she had the Aldous bones — Honora saw it at once — the same long face, made handsome in the Earl and formidable in the aunt.

"You," said Lady Margaret, "are not a servant."

"No, my lady. Honora Vane. I have been engaged to survey the estate."

"A surveyor." The pale eyes went over her once, swift and total, the way Honora's own eyes went over a field — and missed, she suspected, nothing. "A surveyor who has been in residence eleven weeks, by the housekeeper's account, for a survey of a property this size. And who comes down to the hall, in the middle of the morning, with ink on her cuff and a colour in her face, when an Aldous carriage arrives." She let that sit. "I did not become an old woman by being a stupid one, Miss Vane. Where is my nephew?"

He came then — drawn by the same wheels — and Honora watched something in him close like a shutter. The man who had smiled at her in the library yesterday was gone; in his place stood the careful, diminished earl of her first week, and she understood, with a cold drop of the stomach, that this woman had known him in the years of his shutting, and that he had no defence against her except the old one of making himself small.

"Aunt."

"Crispin." Lady Margaret kissed the air beside his scarred cheek with brisk affection. "I have come because you do not answer letters, and because a man who does not answer letters must be visited, and because — " her glance went, deliberate as a placed pin, to Honora, " — I begin to hear things, even buried in Bath, that a careful aunt cannot ignore. We will speak privately. Miss Vane will excuse us."

Miss Vane excused them. She went back up to the cold library and sat with her unfinished fair copy and did not draw a single line.

She knew what was being said below her. She had known it would be said eventually; she had said most of it herself, to him, weeks ago, in this very room. A tradesman's daughter. No fortune. No name. The kind of attachment that, allowed to ripen, would draw the sneer of every drawing-room from here to London and hang it round the neck of the last Earl of Wexcombe like a stone. Lady Margaret was not cruel. Honora understood that, even unable to hear a word. Lady Margaret was something far harder to argue with: she was correct, in the world's own grammar, and she had come a long way in a cold carriage to be correct out loud.

It was an hour before Mrs. Pell came to fetch her down, and the housekeeper's face — that narrow, guarded face that had warned her off the east wing on the very first day — wore, now, something almost like sympathy, which frightened Honora more than open dislike could have.

She found the Earl alone in the gallery. Lady Margaret had withdrawn to rest from her journey. He stood at his window. He did not turn.

"She wishes me to marry a Miss Carstairs of Hampshire," he said, to the glass. "Eighteen thousand pounds and a complexion. She has, in fact, brought a miniature of the young lady in her reticule. She is very thorough, my aunt."

Honora's hands were quite steady. She made them be.

"And you?" she said. "What do you wish, my lord?"

For a long moment the rain was the only voice in the room. Then the Earl of Wexcombe turned from the window, and his face was not closed, after all — she had been wrong about that — it was simply a man's face holding too much at once.

"I have spent the morning," he said, "discovering exactly how much practice I have had at giving up the things I want. It is a great deal of practice, Honora. It is six years of practice. And I find — God help me — I find I am very, very good at it."
