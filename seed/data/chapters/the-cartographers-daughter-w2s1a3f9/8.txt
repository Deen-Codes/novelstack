Lady Margaret did not, in the event, leave that morning.

She had been halfway into her travelling cloak, the miniature of Miss Carstairs no doubt still warm in her reticule, when her nephew came down into the hall with Honora Vane's hand on his arm and informed his aunt, in a voice Honora had not heard him use before — level, unhurried, entirely a peer's — that he was going to be married, and that he would be glad of her blessing, and that he would proceed without it if he must.

Honora had braced for a battle. She had, in fact, rather looked forward to one; she did her best thinking with her back against something.

What she had not braced for was Lady Margaret Aldous slowly taking the cloak off again.

The old woman stood a long moment in the dim hall, looking at the two of them — at her nephew standing upright in a coat he had, Honora noticed, troubled to have brushed; at the ink on Honora's cuff; at the simple unmistakable fact of the thing. And whatever Lady Margaret saw, it was not what she had driven from Bath expecting to fight.

"Sit down, the both of you," she said at last. "I am too old to conduct a family's entire future standing in a cold hall."

In the library — the warm one, the one Crispin kept for Honora now — Lady Margaret arranged herself in the best chair and was silent for the length of time it took to make two younger people thoroughly uncomfortable, which Honora recognized as a deliberate art and privately admired.

"I came here," the old woman said, "to rescue my nephew. I had it on excellent authority that some adventuress had got her hooks into a broken man in a closed-up house, and I came to do my duty, which I have always done, whether or not it made me beloved. I expected to find a fool and a schemer." She looked at Honora. "I have found, instead, a man who walked the length of a wet lawn in front of my window — do not imagine I did not see it, Crispin, you did it precisely so that I would — and a young woman who, I am given to understand by a housekeeper who has served this family thirty years and never once volunteered an opinion to me before, opened the east wing."

Honora said nothing. Some silences were not hers to fill.

"Six years," said Lady Margaret, and for the first time the formidable voice had a crack of frost-thaw in it. "Six years I have written to that wing and had no answer. I buried Beatrice too, Miss Vane. She was my brother's child as much as Crispin's. And I have grieved her every day, and I have also, every day, grieved her brother, who was alive and would not let me near enough to know it." She drew a slow breath and put her formidability back on like a shawl. "You did in eleven weeks what I could not do in six years. I do not entirely forgive you for that. But I am not such a fool, at my age, as to mistake my own wounded pride for my nephew's welfare."

She rose. She crossed to Honora, and she took the ink-stained hand and turned it over and looked at it — the calluses, the chain-marks, the working hand of a working woman — and she did not let it go.

"You will be sneered at," she said, plainly. "I will not pretend otherwise; I have never lied to this family and I shall not begin with its newest member. There will be drawing-rooms closed to you. There will be a great deal of measuring, and you will be found wanting, because you were not born to it and they will never let you forget the trespass."

"I am a surveyor, my lady," said Honora. "I am not afraid of being measured. I have only ever been afraid of being lied to."

Something moved across Lady Margaret's long Aldous face — and Honora saw, astonished, that it was the beginning of respect.

"Then we shall manage," the old woman said. "I am not without weight in those drawing-rooms, Miss Vane. I have spent sixty years acquiring it for no very good reason; it will be a pleasure, at last, to spend it. They will sneer. And they will, every one of them, attend the wedding, because they will not dare to be the family that quarrelled with me." She released Honora's hand. "Welcome, child. God help you. You are marrying the most stubborn man in three counties."

"I am aware, my lady," said Honora gravely. "I surveyed him thoroughly before I agreed to it."

And Lady Margaret Aldous laughed — a short, surprised, genuine bark of a laugh, the sound of a fortress unbarring one small gate — and Crispin, by the fire, looked at the two women who had each, in her own way, refused to let him stay buried, and did not trust himself to say anything at all.
