They were married in the spring, in the small church on the Wexcombe land, and Lady Margaret had been as good as her word: the drawing-rooms sneered, and the drawing-rooms came, every one of them, because not one dared be the family that had quarrelled with the old woman in grey who sat in the front pew like a fortress that had decided, late in life, to defend something worth the trouble.

Honora wore no great gown. She had been offered one and had declined it, gently, the way she declined most things she did not need, and she was married in good plain silver-grey, and her father gave her away with his shaking hands quite steady for the length of time that mattered, and the Earl of Wexcombe waited for her at the altar and did not, when she reached him, trouble to hide a single thing his face was doing.

The estate plan hung in the great hall by midsummer.

It was, Honora thought — and she allowed herself the thought, because false modesty was a kind of lie and she had sworn off lies — the best work she had ever done. Every field true. Every boundary chained and checked. The collapsed culvert marked, and beside it, in a small neat hand, the note recommending its repair, which had been carried out that very spring so that the east lawn now drained sweet and the sour patch was gone. The folly, the orchard, the lodge, the long beech avenue. The house itself, west wing and east, every room of it drawn open and entire.

There was no hole in the map anywhere.

And in the lower corner, in the cartouche where for two years another name had gone, the legend read, in her own clear lettering: Surveyed and drawn by Honora Aldous, Countess of Wexcombe, in the year of her marriage.

Crispin found her in front of it one evening, late, when the hall was empty and the long light came gold and slantwise through windows that were all of them, now, unshuttered.

"You are admiring your own work," he said. "I have told you that is impertinent."

"I am a cartographer, my lord. I admire accuracy wherever I find it." But she leaned back against him when he came to stand behind her, and his arms came round her, ink-stained cuffs and all, and they looked together at the map of everything they had — the house no longer closing, the land no longer wasted, the whole estate drawn true and warm and lived-in.

"You said once," he murmured, "that a blank space on a map is not an absence. That it is a question."

"I did. It is the first principle of the craft and I stand by it."

"Then look." He turned her, gently, by the shoulders, away from the wall and toward the long gold windows and the green park beyond them, going on and on into the soft evening. "There is no blank space left in this whole house, Honora. You walked into a man with every room of himself shut and shuttered, and you would not leave a single question alone, and now — " his voice did its old hinge-thing, the rust of feeling, but he did not stop this time; he had stopped stopping, " — now there is nothing in me that is not surveyed, and chained, and drawn, and known. You have mapped me entirely."

Honora Aldous, who had spent her life translating the patient memory of the land into ink, who had at last been permitted to sign her own name to her own true work, stood in the warm unshuttered hall of her own reckless and unmeasurable happiness, and reached up, and laid her hand along the silvered scar of his face — the part of the map she had been given last, and trusted most.

"Not entirely," she said. "A good cartographer never calls a map finished, Crispin. She only calls it true. And then — " she smiled, and it was the smile of a woman with the whole rest of a life still blank and bright before her, " — and then she keeps walking the ground, every season, for the pleasure of finding what has changed."

The light went gold, and then soft, and then blue. And the house — every window of it open to the warm evening — kept none of its rooms dark.
