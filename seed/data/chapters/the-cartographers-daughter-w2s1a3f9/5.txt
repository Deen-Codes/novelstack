The survey of the grounds was, in the plain mechanical sense, finished within the week. Honora had walked every boundary, chained every field, taken the height of every folly and the depth of every ha-ha. Her field-book bulged. By any honest reckoning her work at Wexcombe was done, and the correct and professional thing to do was to write to her father, draw the fair copy, take the forty pounds, and go.

She did not write to her father.

She told herself it was the weather — and the weather did, conveniently, oblige her, the sky settling into days of low grey rain that made the roads a misery. She told herself it was the fair copy, that a plan of this size was better drawn on site where the land could correct her. Both things were true. Neither was the reason, and she was too honest a person, in the end, to pretend the reason was anything but the man.

She had begun to be necessary to him. She could see it, and worse, she could feel the answering thing in herself — the way her whole day now bent quietly toward the hour she knew he would find some excuse to seek her out. They had moved their conversations indoors with the rain. He brought her books. She corrected his opinions. He had taken, with a dryness she suspected was the nearest he came to tenderness, to calling her "my impertinent cartographer," and she had taken, with a private alarm she examined every night and resolved every morning to stop examining, to liking it very much.

"You ought to send me away," she told him.

They were in the library, which was the one room of the west wing he kept properly warm now — and she did not let herself dwell on when, exactly, that had started. Rain ran down the tall windows. He was in the chair opposite, a book unread on his knee.

"I am aware," he said, "that I ought to do a great many things."

"I am a tradesman's daughter, my lord. I sign another man's name to my own work because the world will not buy a map drawn by a woman. I have no fortune, no family of note, no — " she gestured at the room, the house, the whole weight of his name, " — none of this. Whatever it is we are doing, by sitting in this room every afternoon, it is the kind of thing that ends one of two ways, and one of those ways ruins me and the other ruins you, and a sensible man would have seen that a fortnight ago and put me in a coach."

The Earl of Wexcombe set down his book.

"I spent six years," he said slowly, "being the most sensible man in England. I shut every door. I refused every invitation. I made myself so small and so careful that nothing could ever again be taken from me, because I had nothing left worth the taking. It was flawless reasoning, Miss Vane. It was the soundest, most sensible plan a grieving man ever made. And it cost me six years of my one life, and it would have cost me the rest, and I knew none of that — not one word of it — until a stranger walked into my gallery and told me to my face that I was haunting my own house."

He stood. He crossed the small distance between their chairs, and he did not touch her, but he stood close enough that she had to tip her head back to hold his eyes, and the scar was very near, and she did not look away from it and he saw that she did not.

"I am done being sensible," he said. "It is the most expensive thing I have ever owned. So I will not put you in a coach, Honora. I will instead do the reckless thing, and the ruinous thing, and ask you to stay — not as a surveyor. You know I am not speaking of the survey."

The rain came down. The fire spoke to itself in the grate.

"Ask me properly," said Honora Vane, whose voice was not as steady as she would have liked, "when you have shown me her room. I will not have you give me your future, my lord, until you have let me see your past. A map with a hole in it is still a lie. Even now. Even this one."

He looked at her for a long moment. And then, for the first time since she had known him, the Earl of Wexcombe smiled — fully, the scar and all, a smile that changed the entire weather of his face.

"Tomorrow," he said. "Tomorrow I will show you the hole in the map."
