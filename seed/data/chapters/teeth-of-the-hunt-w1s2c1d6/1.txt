Mara had been hunting things with teeth for nine years, and the first rule she'd learned — the rule that had kept her alive past every hunter who'd started when she did — was that the hunt is not about the kill. The hunt is about the waiting.

She was very good at waiting.

She had been in the blind above the Vanmoor treeline since before dawn, still as the cold stump she was crouched against, rifle balanced, breathing slow. The contract in her coat pocket was the most money anyone had ever offered her for a single name, and the name was not a name at all, which was the part that should have warned her. Hunter, the contract said. The Vanmoor pack had put a price on whoever was thinning their borders, picking off their outrunners one clean shot at a time — and they did not know it was Mara, because Mara was good, because Mara did the work and left no face behind it.

So the Vanmoor pack had hired a hunter to kill their hunter. They'd hired her to kill herself, essentially, and paid handsomely for it, and Mara had taken the contract with a flat private amusement and decided to use it the simplest way there was: she'd take the pack's money, sit in this blind, and shoot whatever poor enforcer the alpha sent up the mountain to find the killer the killer had been hired to find.

It was elegant. It was the kind of solution that made her good at her job and bad at sleeping.

The light came up grey over the Vanmoor valley. Mara waited.

She heard him before the light was strong enough to show him — a wrongness in the ordinary sounds of the wood, a hush where there should have been birds. Something was moving up the slope toward her blind, careful, unhurried, and large. Pack enforcer. Maybe the alpha's second. She settled the rifle, found her breath, found the cold patient nothing at the center of herself that the kill lived in.

A shape resolved out of the trees forty yards downslope.

Not a wolf — a man, on two legs, which was its own kind of message. He was not hiding. He had come up the mountain into the territory of a hunter who'd been dropping his packmates for a month, and he had come on two legs and in the open, hands loose at his sides, and that took either a fool or an alpha, and the breadth of the shoulders and the way the wood went quiet around him told Mara which.

The alpha himself. They had sent the alpha.

She put the crosshairs on his chest. Nine years of the work made it nothing; the work was nothing, that was the secret nobody wanted, the work was just geometry and a slow breath. She had the shot. She had paid for the right to take it with a month of cold mornings in this blind. Her finger found the trigger's first pressure.

The alpha lifted his head, and looked directly at the blind — directly at her, as though the dawn light meant nothing to him, as though her month of leaving no face behind had been a child's game — and Mara saw his face.

And the cold patient nothing at the center of her came apart like wet paper.

Because she knew that face. She had known it before it was a man's face. She had known it across a different treeline ten years ago, when she was sixteen and angry and newly orphaned and a Vanmoor boy two years older had found her trying to teach herself to shoot in the woods her family had once owned. He had not turned her in. He had not laughed. He had crouched in the leaves beside a furious grieving girl and corrected her grip, and her breathing, and the angle of her shoulder, and he had come back the next week, and the week after that, and for one strange green summer the boy who would be alpha of Vanmoor had taught the girl who would hunt his kind how to do the thing she would spend her life doing.

His name was Cael. She had spent ten years and a great deal of effort not thinking it.

Down the slope, forty yards away, in her crosshairs, Cael of Vanmoor went very still — and Mara understood, with a lurch that had nothing to do with the rifle, that he had not come up the mountain on two legs out of arrogance.

He had come because the bond had told him who the hunter was.

She felt it now herself, the thing she had outrun for ten years, the thing she had told herself the work had killed in her — a low hard pull under her ribs, pointed straight down the slope at him, answering. It had not died. It had only been waiting, the way she waited, the way the hunt waits, and it had chosen the worst possible clearing in the world to wake up in.

One of them was going to have to walk out of this. The contract in her pocket said so. The Vanmoor border full of his dead outrunners said so.

Cael spread his empty hands a little wider, slow, deliberate, the way you show a thing with teeth that you mean it no harm.

"Mara," he called up the grey slope, to the blind, to the rifle, to the girl he'd taught to use it. "I know it's you. I've known for a week." His voice carried, even and unafraid and unbearably familiar. "So either take the shot — you've got it, I'm not going to pretend you don't — or come down here and let's both find out what we became."

Mara crouched in the cold with her finger on the trigger and the bond pulling like a hook, and for the first time in nine years, she did not know what the waiting was for.
