Marek's first rule, the rule the shop was built on, the rule his uncle had taught him the same way other men's uncles taught them to drive, was simple: you do not ask where it came from.

A pawn shop on Halloran Street did not survive on curiosity. It survived on a kind of deliberate, professional blindness, a blindness Marek had spent fifteen years perfecting until it was less a choice than a reflex. A man came in with a thing. The thing had a value. You named a sum against the thing, and you wrote a ticket, and you put the thing in the back, and at no point in the transaction did the question of the thing's history cross the counter between you. People brought Marek their grandmother's rings and their dead brother's watches and, yes, sometimes things that had been somebody's that morning and were nobody's by lunchtime, and Marek's blindness made no distinction, because the distinction was exactly the thing that got pawnbrokers hurt.

So when the kid came in on the cold Tuesday — and he was a kid, nineteen maybe, jittery, wrong around the eyes in a way Marek had seen ten thousand times — Marek did not ask. The kid put a man's wedding ring on the counter, a good ring, heavy, old gold, and Marek picked it up and turned it to the light and did the arithmetic and named a sum, and the kid took the sum, all of it, fast, the way the desperate take things, and he was out of the door and gone into the grey afternoon before the bell over it had finished ringing.

Marek wrote the ticket. He put the ring in the till tray for the day's small valuables, behind the glass, and he thought no more about it, because thinking more about it was precisely the thing the rule existed to prevent.

He should have looked at the inside of the ring.

He understood this later, much later, but he understood it completely: the whole of the winter that followed turned on the fact that Marek, for once, did not turn a thing to the light long enough to read it. Because the inside of the ring was engraved, the way old wedding rings are, and the engraving was a name and a date, and the name was a name that Marek — and a great many other people on this side of the city — would have recognised, because the man it belonged to had been, until two days before that cold Tuesday, a man you did not take things from. A man with people. A man whose ring did not simply walk into a pawn shop on the wrist of a jittery nineteen-year-old unless something very large and very bad had happened to the hand it belonged on.

Marek learned the name the way you learn these things on Halloran Street, which is to say it came to him sideways and too late.

It came to him on the Thursday, in the form of two men who walked into the shop twenty minutes apart and were very obviously not there to pawn anything. The first was older, calm, expensive in the understated way that meant real money rather than the loud kind, and he stood at the counter and looked around the shop with an assessor's slowness and then asked Marek, pleasantly, whether anything *interesting* had come in this week. He did not say the word ring. He did not need to. The second man, the one twenty minutes later, was younger and harder and asked Marek the same question with none of the first man's pleasantness, and let his coat fall open enough, while he asked it, to make the conversation's stakes clear.

Two sets of people. Wanting the same ring. And Marek, standing behind his own counter in his own shop, the shop his uncle had left him, did the arithmetic he was actually good at and arrived at a cold and simple total: the ring in his till tray belonged to a dead man, and the dead man had been important enough that his death had two factions already moving against each other over the pieces of it, and one of those factions had killed him, and the other faction wanted to know which — and both of them now knew that the answer, or a thread leading straight to it, was sitting behind glass on Halloran Street in the keeping of a pawnbroker who asked no questions.

Marek closed the shop early that Thursday. He had not closed early in nine years.

He stood in the back room with the lights off and the ring in his palm, and he turned it, finally, to what little light came through the grille over the window, and he read the name and the date on the inside of it, and the cold went all the way through him. His uncle's rule had kept him alive and solvent for fifteen years, and the rule had a flaw, and Marek was holding the flaw in his hand. *You do not ask where it came from.* But a thing that comes from somewhere is also a thing that is going somewhere, and the ring was going to be the death of him, or the saving of him, depending entirely on what he did in the next few days — and Marek, who had spent his whole adult life perfecting the art of not deciding things, understood that the winter was going to make him decide this one.
