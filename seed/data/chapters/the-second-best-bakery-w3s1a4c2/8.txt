Festival Sunday came up hot and gold and cloudless, the kind of day Marrow Lane only got three or four of a year, and the whole town spilled out into the street to spend it.

The bunting had been found — in the charity shop loft, mildewed at one end, hung anyway. The brass band played at the memorial. The river walk had been swept. And on a long trestle table outside the Anchor, under a banner that said SPRING BAKE-OFF in letters someone's child had painted, the contest waited.

The rules were simple and cruel. One bake each. Same brief: a thing that tastes like this town. The committee would judge, and then the town would, by raising hands, and Mr. Penhallow would count.

Nora and Theo had not spoken since Thursday. That had been the agreement — bake separate, bake honest, no conferring — and Nora had spent two days alone in her grandmother's kitchen, and her grandmother's kitchen had felt, for the first time since April, like a kitchen with someone missing from it.

She knew what Theo would make. He would make the croquembouche, scaled down, a perfect small tower — the thing he was best in the world at, the thing no one in this town could touch. He would bake to win because she'd told him to, and he would win, and she found she was glad. Let him have it. Let the town love him properly at last. He had only ever wanted to bake on this street.

So Nora did not make the cardamom bun.

She had meant to. It was her best card, her grandmother's card, the thing that tasted like the town because it had fed the town for sixty years. It would have given her a fighting chance. Instead, on Saturday night, she had taken down the tin and gone through it card by card until she found the one she wanted, in the oldest hand, her great-grandmother's: a plain seed loaf, the everyday loaf, the bread Hartley's had sold for tuppence in 1939 and the bread her grandmother had been scoring the morning she went up to bed and didn't come down.

It was not a showstopper. It was just bread. But it was the truest thing she owned.

At noon they laid their bakes on the trestle table. Theo's tower was a small marvel, caramel-gold, spun sugar catching the light like something a spider had done on a good day. The town breathed out at the sight of it. Nora set her seed loaf down beside it, brown and blunt and honest, and Theo looked at it, and then he looked at her, and she watched him understand.

She watched him understand that she had not baked to win.

His face did a complicated thing. The committee was already moving down the table with their little forks and their serious faces, and there was no time, but Theo crossed the small distance between their two bakes anyway, and he stood close to her in front of the entire watching town, and he said, low, just for her:

"You magnificent coward."

"I'm not throwing it," Nora said. "I'm telling the truth. The truth is your tower's better. The truth is also that this loaf is what the town tastes like, and I'd rather lose with it than win without it." She lifted her chin. "Go and win, Theo."

And Theo Marchetti, who had come to this street two months ago in a charcoal coat to put her out of business, looked from the seed loaf to the woman who'd baked it, and made, in front of God and the brass band and the entire Marrow Lane Spring Festival Committee, a decision that no rule had prepared anyone for.
