They knocked the wall through in October.

Not the wall of either shop — those stayed, HARTLEY'S in flaking gold and MARCHETTI in fine grey-green, because the town would not have stood for losing either sign and neither, in the end, would Nora. What they knocked through was smaller and more sensible: the back of Hartley's storeroom met the back of the building behind it, and the building behind it had come up for rent, and so the two shops on opposite sides of Marrow Lane were joined, not across the street where everyone could see, but behind it, where the work was done.

One kitchen. Two shopfronts. It was, the town agreed, the most sensible mad thing anyone had done on Marrow Lane in living memory.

They were married in the spring, a year almost to the day from the morning Theo had walked into a cold floury dark with a chocolate dome and a gold leaf and got told to get out. The festival committee, who had never forgiven them for the bake-off and had entirely forgiven them for everything else, did the bunting. Mrs. Okafor cried so hard she had to be fanned with an order pad.

The cardamom bun went on sale in both windows. So did the seed loaf. Customers learned to come in one door and leave by the other, threading through the joined kitchen in between, and nobody minded, because the joined kitchen was warm and smelled of forty years of somebody's grandmother and was, on any given morning, the happiest room on the street.

The tin lived on the shelf above the proofing cupboard, the rubber band long gone, the lid loose. Nora had added a card of her own to the top of the stack — her grandmother's hand, then her great-grandmother's, then hers, and now a fourth, Theo's, his recipes in their careful slanted script filed in among the women's like a man who had finally found the kitchen he was looking for.

On the last page of the tin, after all the recipes, Nora had copied out the line her grandmother left her, the one that wasn't a recipe at all, so that whoever opened the box next — and they both quietly hoped there would be a next, a small one, with flour-rough hands of their own one day — would find it first.

Don't bake alone, child. It comes out tasting like it.

They opened early every morning, both doors at once, Theo to the grey-green side and Nora to the gold. The proof box did not leak anymore; he'd fixed it the first winter, without being asked, the way he couldn't watch a crooked thing and not set it straight. The bell over the Hartley's door still didn't lock unless you lifted it. They had decided, together, to never fix that one.

Some mornings, before the light, Nora would stand in the middle of the joined kitchen with a coffee Theo had pulled — no fern; the machine still didn't do ferns; she had grown to love it for that — and she would look at the two doorways, the two streams of grey dawn, the two signs of the two bakeries that had stopped being a contest the day a tired man told the truth about a loaf of bread.

The second-best bakery on Marrow Lane, the old joke went, was always whichever one you weren't standing in.

It was, everyone agreed, the best joke in town.
