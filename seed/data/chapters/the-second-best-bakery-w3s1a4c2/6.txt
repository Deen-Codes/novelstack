They built the Penhallow wedding tower over three nights, because the days belonged to their two shops and only the dark belonged to them.

It was the strangest, best work Nora had done since her grandmother died. They had agreed, without ever quite saying it, to do the wedding in her kitchen — Hartley's was bigger, and the proofing cupboard was warmer, and the radio played the old songs. Theo brought his piping bags and his thermometer and his terrifying patience. Nora brought the cardamom, and the tin, and a lifetime of hands.

At two in the morning on the second night the tower was half-built, a slope of choux glued with caramel, and they were both past the point of pretending to be careful with each other.

"You hold your spoon like a weapon," Theo said. He was behind her, watching her fold the crème into the next batch. "You attack the bowl."

"I get results."

"You'd get the same results in half the time without the rage." He reached around her — to take the spoon, to demonstrate, that was all it was meant to be — and his hand closed over hers on the handle, and the demonstrating stopped.

Neither of them moved. The oven light was on, that low amber square, and the radio was playing something slow from before either of them was born, and Theo's hand was warm over Nora's, and the bowl of crème sat between them entirely forgotten.

"Nora," he said. Just her name. He said it the careful way, the way he'd said it opens when it opens, like a thing he didn't want to break.

She turned around. It was a small kitchen and turning around put very little space between them, and she used up most of what was left, and looked up at him — at the flour on his jaw, the tired dark eyes, the man who fixed her shelves without asking and had taught her not to bake alone.

"If you say one word about my glaze," she said unsteadily, "I will end you."

"I wasn't going to say anything about your glaze," said Theo.

He kissed her, or she kissed him; afterward neither of them could fairly claim it, and they argued about it for years, happily, the way they argued about everything. It was not a careful kiss. It had three nights and four weeks and a great deal of dislike-turned-inside-out behind it, and when it ended Nora had flour in her hair and Theo's hands were framing her face like something he'd built and was afraid to set down.

"The tower's going to collapse," she whispered.

"Let it."

"It's a four-hundred-guest wedding, Theo."

"Let it collapse," said Theo, and kissed her again, and the tower, propped and patient on the counter, did not collapse at all — it held, the caramel set hard and sweet in the cool kitchen air, the way things do when they're built right, slowly, by two people in the dark who have finally stopped working alone.
