Theo turned to the committee, who were two bakes away and closing, and said, in a voice pitched to carry all the way down the table:

"I'd like to withdraw."

The brass band did not stop, because brass bands never do, but everything else on Marrow Lane stopped. Mr. Penhallow looked up with a fork halfway to a stranger's lemon cake.

"You'd like to what?"

"Withdraw. Concede. I'm out." Theo put his hands in his apron pockets, perfectly calm, the calmest Nora had ever seen him. "My tower's very good. It's the best tower I've ever made, and I made it to win, exactly as we agreed. But I've been standing here looking at it, and I've worked something out." He looked at the tower, the gold spun-sugar marvel, with something almost like fondness and entirely like farewell. "It doesn't taste like this town. It tastes like every town. It tastes like Paris and Lyon and a dozen kitchens that aren't here. That was the brief, and I've failed it, and I'd rather say so than win on a technicality."

A sound went through the crowd, the low collective mm, but it had wonder in it now.

"This loaf," said Theo, and laid two fingers on Nora's blunt brown bread, "is the answer to the question you asked. It's a Hartley's seed loaf and they've baked it on this street since before the war and it is the single most this-town thing on this table. I've eaten it. I ate it this morning, I bought a slice off her counter like a coward, the same as I did in March." He found Nora's eyes. "It's not pretty. The crust's a shade dark. If I served it I'd be ashamed of it. And it's the best thing I've eaten since I came to this place, and the town should give it the prize, because the town would only be telling the truth."

Nobody moved. Then Mrs. Okafor, near the front, began to clap — slowly, deliberately, the clap of a woman who has been proven right about everything — and the street took it up, and the brass band, hearing applause and being a brass band, swung gamely into something celebratory and only slightly wrong.

Mr. Penhallow counted the show of hands anyway, because there were rules, and the rules said a contest had to have an end. The seed loaf won it forty to nothing, because Theo had withdrawn and you cannot vote for a tower that has gone home.

Afterward, in the gold afternoon, with a paper rosette pinned crooked to her apron, Nora found Theo sitting on the low wall by the memorial, dismantling his own perfect tower with his fingers and eating it, piece by piece, with the unhurried pleasure of a man no longer performing.

She sat down beside him. He handed her a choux bun off the ruins.

"You lost on purpose," she said.

"So did you. We're well matched." He chewed. "Penhallow's furious. He wanted a winner and he got two losers in love, which is the one ending a festival committee can't put on a banner."

"You called your own tower a failure in front of the whole town."

"It was the truth. You taught me to like the truth." Theo turned, and the afternoon was all over his face, and he wasn't smiling the magazine smile, he was smiling the other one, the real one, the one she had decided weeks ago she would be doing a great deal of work to keep on his face. "Marry me, Nora."

The brass band, mercifully, had stopped.

"It's two months," Nora said. "It's barely two months, Theo, people will say —"

"People will say mm," said Theo, "and they'll be right, and they've been right since April. Marry me. Bake with me. I'm tired of working alone on the opposite side of a street that's only four blocks long." He took her flour-rough hand in his. "Don't bake alone, Nora. You told me what was written in the tin. It comes out tasting like it."

And Nora Hartley, sitting on a sun-warmed wall in the middle of her own town with a crooked rosette on her chest and the ruins of a beautiful tower in her lap, said yes — and the only witness was the war memorial, and a passing dog, and Mrs. Okafor, who had of course been watching the entire time, and who went mm so loudly that they heard it clear across the square.
