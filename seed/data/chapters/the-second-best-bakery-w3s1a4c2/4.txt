The tin opened on a Tuesday, and it was Theo's fault, though he never meant it to be.

He had come over before either shop was light, the way he did now, with two coffees and a problem. The problem was a wedding. A big one — the Penhallow girl, marrying in June, four hundred guests, and the bride wanted a croquembouche, that absurd tower of choux and spun sugar, and she also wanted, the bride had said, with the particular cruelty of the very young, something that tastes like this town.

"She means childhood," Theo said, leaning on Nora's counter. "She means the thing you can't buy in. And I can build her a tower that'll make people weep, but I can't make it taste like a place I've lived in for two months." He turned his cup on the counter, around and around. "I came to ask if you'd do the bones of it. The flavor. A partnership. Your taste, my structure."

Nora was already shaking her head. "I don't do weddings that size. I haven't the hands for it. It was always Gran and me, and now it's just —"

"Just you," Theo said. "I know. That's the other half of why I'm asking."

And Nora stood there in the dark shop with the offer hanging in the air, and she understood that what he was really doing was a kindness dressed up as business, because Theo Marchetti had worked out that she was lonely in her own kitchen, and this was the only way he knew to say so without saying so.

"The flavor would have to be hers," she said at last. "Gran's. The cardamom thing. And I don't have it written. It's in the tin."

They both looked at the shelf.

"It opens when it opens," Theo said carefully. "I'll find another way. I'll —"

"No." Nora reached up and took the tin down. It was lighter than grief had made it in her memory. She set it on the counter between them, between his two cooling coffees, and worked the brittle rubber band off with her thumbs, and it snapped and fell, and she lifted the lid.

Cardamom. The smell came up first, and then the cards — dozens of them, in three different hands, because her grandmother had copied recipes from her own mother, and Nora had copied recipes from her grandmother, and the tin held all three of them at once, three generations of women in a stack the size of a sandwich. On top, in the shakiest hand, the last thing the old woman had written: a card that just said FOR NORA, when she's ready, and under it the cardamom bun, and under that, a line that was not a recipe at all.

Don't bake alone, child. It comes out tasting like it.

Nora made a sound she hadn't planned on. Theo did not say anything clever. He did not say anything at all. He came around the counter, and he stood next to her, close, and put one hand flat between her shoulder blades, the way you'd steady something you didn't want to fall — and they stood like that in the dark with the open tin and the smell of forty years of cardamom, and outside the first grey light came up over Marrow Lane, and neither of them moved to turn the shop sign round to OPEN for a long, long while.
