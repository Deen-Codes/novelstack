The Marrow Lane Spring Festival Committee met in the back room of the Anchor pub on the second Thursday of every month, and had done since before anyone living could remember. Nora's grandmother had chaired it for nineteen years. Nora had been avoiding it for three.

She went, that May, because Theo made her.

"You can't run a shop on a street," he said, "and not show up to decide what happens on the street. They'll resent you. Towns are long memories with shops attached." He was right, which she was getting tired of, and so she went, and he came with her, and they walked into the back room of the Anchor together and the entire committee stopped talking at once.

It was not a large committee. Mrs. Okafor. The butcher, Sallow. The two women who ran the charity shop and finished each other's sentences. Old Mr. Penhallow, the father of the bride, who had appointed himself chair the way water appoints itself the shape of a jug.

"Well," said Mrs. Okafor, into the silence, with enormous satisfaction. "The two of them. Together."

"We share a coffee machine," said Nora.

"Mm," said Mrs. Okafor, and the entire committee said mm with her, a low collective hum of a sound, and Nora felt her face go hot and did not dare look at Theo.

The meeting itself was an hour of magnificent triviality — the bunting, whether the bunting from last year was salvageable, whose loft the bunting was currently in. The brass band's fee. Whether the river walk should be swept. And then Mr. Penhallow, with the air of a man producing a rabbit, said, "And the bake-off. Festival Sunday. We'll want the bake-off."

The room changed. Nora felt it change before she understood why.

"There's always been a bake-off," Mr. Penhallow went on, looking at her, looking at Theo, looking between them like a man measuring a doorway for a coffin. "And there's always been the one bakery. Now there's two. So." He spread his hands. "It seems to me the festival has a contest on its hands whether it wants one or not. Hartley's against Marchetti. The town can judge. Healthy. Good for trade."

It was not, Nora understood, about trade. It was about theatre. The town had watched two bakers feud and then watched them stop feuding, had watched them walk into a pub together and go mm about it, and now the town wanted its story to have a shape, and the shape it wanted was a winner.

"We're not —" Nora started.

"We'd be glad to," said Theo.

She turned and stared at him. He was smiling his old smile, the magazine one, the one she hadn't seen in weeks, and it did not reach his eyes at all.

"Wonderful," said Mr. Penhallow.

They walked home down Marrow Lane in a silence with weather in it. At her door Nora stopped.

"Why did you say yes."

"Because if I'd said no," Theo said, looking not at her but at his own gold window across the street, "they'd have spent all summer deciding which of us was afraid. This way it's settled in one afternoon. You'll win, Nora. The town's hers, and you're hers, and they'll vote with their hearts. I've made my peace with it." He found a smile for her, smaller, truer. "I just wanted to bake on this street. I didn't need to be loved on it."

He went inside. The bell rang behind him.

Nora stood on the wet pavement between the two shops — HARTLEY'S behind her, MARCHETTI lit gold ahead — and discovered that the thing she wanted, suddenly and completely, was not to win.
