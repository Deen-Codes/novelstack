The town knew by Friday.

Nora never worked out how. She and Theo had said nothing, had been, by their own estimation, the souls of discretion — and yet she came down Marrow Lane on Friday morning and Mrs. Okafor was waiting outside Hartley's with a face like a sunrise.

"You look well," Mrs. Okafor said. "Doesn't she look well, Sallow."

"Blooming," said the butcher, from his own doorway, not even pretending to be passing.

"I haven't —" Nora began.

"The Penhallow tower," said Mrs. Okafor serenely, "was delivered this morning by the two of you in one van. Theo Marchetti drove and you held the box on your knees and when he took the corner by the memorial you both laughed. The whole bus queue saw. The whole bus queue, Nora." She patted Nora's cheek. "We've been waiting since April, dear. We'd practically given up."

There was no surviving it, so Nora didn't try. She unlocked the shop. She baked. And all morning the town came through her door and bought bread it didn't need so that it could look at her face and go, very softly, mm, and across the street the same thing was plainly happening to Theo, because every so often he would appear in his gold doorway and find her in her glass door and they would stand there, two streets apart, being looked at, and grinning like fools.

It should have been perfect. It was nearly perfect. It lasted until four o'clock, when Mr. Penhallow came in.

He did not buy anything. He stood by the door with his hat in his hands, and he was not theatrical now; he was an old man choosing his words.

"I'll come to it plain," he said. "The bake-off's Sunday. And the town's decided you're a couple, and the town's pleased, and that's all very nice. But a contest with a courting couple in it isn't a contest, Nora. It's a pantomime. People are saying you'll throw it for him. Or he'll throw it for you. Either way, nobody believes in it anymore." He turned the hat. "Half the committee wants it called off. The other half wants the two of you to — to declare yourselves separate. For the day. Bake like you mean it, like you did in March, so the result's honest."

"It is honest," Nora said. "Whatever happens Sunday will be —"

"It won't feel honest," said Mr. Penhallow, gently, and that was the trouble, because feeling honest and being honest were different things, and a town runs entirely on the first one.

He left. Nora stood with the question in her hands.

That evening Theo came across the road and she told him, and he listened, and at the end of it he was quiet for a long time, longer than she liked.

"So they want us to pretend we're enemies again," he said. "For one Sunday. So the town can enjoy the show."

"That's the size of it."

Theo looked at the tin on the shelf. At the radio. At her.

"And if we say no," he said slowly, "the festival loses its bake-off, and the committee blames us, and the town that's spent two months falling in love with us falling in love decides we spoiled the ending. We'd be the couple that broke the festival." He exhaled. "Or we say yes, and we spend Sunday pretending in front of everyone that I'm nothing to you. Bake against each other for real. Let them have their winner."

"Theo —"

"I think we should do it," he said. "I think we should bake to win, both of us, no mercy, and let the town have its honest contest." He met her eyes. "And I think it's a terrible idea, and I think one of us is going to lose in front of the entire town, and I think that's going to matter more than either of us is admitting tonight."

Nora opened her mouth to say it wouldn't matter, that it was only buns, only sugar, only theatre.

But she had grown up on this street, and she knew exactly how it worked, and she closed her mouth again, because Theo was right, and Sunday was coming, and somebody she loved was going to lose.
