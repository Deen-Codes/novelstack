They reached the toll on the fourth day.

It stood where the grey hills pinched the road into a single defile — a gatehouse of new-looking stone straddling the way, with a bar across it and a man beside the bar. Behind the gatehouse the road ran on toward Anvarre, and Elin could see the city clearly now, white walls shining on the plain, beautiful, and over it not one thread of smoke.

The toll-keeper was wrong in a way that took Elin a moment to name. He was dressed like a toll-keeper and stood like a toll-keeper and the bar was an honest bar of honest oak. But he did not breathe. His chest did not move, and the cold day did not fog his mouth, and when Oren drew the wagon up he turned his head to them with the smoothness of a weather-vane.

"Toll for the road to Anvarre," the toll-keeper said. His voice was the road's voice. Elin knew it the way you know your own name. It was the voice that had said come, made into a mouth. "The road was made. The making is owed. The toll is one."

"One copper?" said Oren, reaching for his purse. "One silver?"

"One," said the toll-keeper, "of you."

The silence after that was very long. The baby murmured against Hesta's chest.

"A road is not a gift," the toll-keeper said, patient as stone. "A road is a debt. The land dreamed forty leagues of road and a city with white walls and a fountain, and a dream so large is not paid in metal. It is paid in what walks it. One of you remains here. The others pass and reach Anvarre and the toll is met. This is the shape of the thing. I did not make the shape." It turned its smooth head toward Elin, and she understood that it knew her, that it was, in some sense, her own ink looking back at her. "She made the shape. Ask her."

They all looked at her then. Oren, bewildered. Hesta, her arms tightening on the baby. Tam, not understanding. And Pell — Pell looked at her with the flat terrible clarity of a girl who had said there is no smoke and been told perhaps the wind.

"You said you were a mapper," Pell said. "You drew this. Didn't you. You drew this road."

There was no lie left that was worth telling. "Yes," Elin said.

"And you knew there'd be a toll."

"No. I didn't know its shape. But I knew —" Her voice failed and she made it work again, because the girl had earned the truth and the boy was listening. "I knew the road would cost something. Sib told me. A road is an invitation. I drew it anyway. I drew it because a dying man offered me three hundred crowns and because — because it was beautiful, and I wanted to have drawn something beautiful. That is the whole of it. There is no better reason underneath."

She had thought confessing it would feel like a weight set down. It did not. It felt like a weight named, which is heavier.

Oren had gone grey. "Then it's owed against you," he said, and there was no cruelty in it, only a father doing sums. "You owe it. Not my children."

"Yes," Elin said. "It's owed against me."

She unslung her case. She set it on the road. She felt, distantly, almost relieved — there was a clean answer here, a way to pay for what she had made with the only coin that was honestly hers. She stepped toward the bar.

"It will not take her," the toll-keeper said.

Elin stopped.

"The maker cannot be the toll." The road's voice was not unkind. It was simply explaining its own arithmetic. "If the maker is unmade, the making unravels, and then there is no road, and then there is no debt, and then the toll is not paid — it is escaped. The dream does not permit the dreamer to be the price. The toll is one who walks the road. Not one who wrote it. Choose."

And Elin Sarr stood on her beautiful road between the grey hills, and understood that her gift had built one last cruelty into itself: it would not even let her pay her own debt.
