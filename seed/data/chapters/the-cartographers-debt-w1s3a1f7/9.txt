The duke died in autumn, in his bed at Hallow House, and he died unhappy.

He had been promised a city. He had paid three hundred crowns toward a city, and he had cleared a place in his will for the second three hundred, and he had told the household — over and over, in the last weeks, with the stubborn brightness of a man who has decided a thing is true — that when the mapper returned he would be carried west down a beautiful road and would sit at last by the fountain his mother had sung him.

But the mapper did not return. And there was no road. And when the duke's people, humouring him, finally rode out to the western gate to look, they found old Sib in her lean-to and a moor with sheep on it and nothing else at all, and they came back and did not know how to tell him, and in the end they simply did not, and he died still listening for wheels.

His heir, a practical nephew, found among the duke's papers a contract with one Elin Sarr, cartographer, of Hallowmere, for the charting of a road to the city of Anvarre. The nephew made enquiries. There was a cartographer of that name; she had a shop, modest, and a debt against it in her dead father's hand. She had taken a commission some seasons past and had not been seen since. Cartographers wandered; it was the trade. The nephew, who was tidy, paid off the debt against the shop out of the estate — it seemed the decent thing, the woman having died, presumably, in the duke's service — and had the shop shut up, and thought no more about it.

So the debt that had sat in a Hallowmere ledger under Elin Sarr's name, the debt that was a door she had walked a forty-league road to try to buy her way through, was cancelled in the end by a tidy stranger for reasons that had nothing to do with her. That is how it goes, mostly. The accounts of a life rarely balance in the columns you watched.

And Anvarre?

Anvarre was a song. It went back to being a song. Somewhere, perhaps, an old woman still sang it to a child — white walls, a fountain, a market square — not knowing that it had once, for the space of a few days, been forty leagues of real road and white stone, summoned up by a dying man's wanting and undrawn again by the steady hand of the woman who had drawn it. The song did not remember. Songs don't.

But this is not quite the end, because of the girl.

Years on — eleven of them, the same eleven Elin had once spent being careful — there was a woman in the river country who mended pots and traded down the towns, and who, in the evenings, had taken up an odd second trade. She drew maps. Not true maps, in the dangerous sense; she had no gift, and was glad of it. She drew honest, ordinary, foot-walked maps of the river country, accurate leagues and honest fords, and she sold them cheap, and on the back of every single one, in small plain letters, she wrote the same handful of words.

A road is an invitation. Be sure you can pay for who comes.

People asked her, sometimes, what it meant, and where she had learned her craft, since she had clearly learned it from someone. And Pell Coverdale — older now, sharp still, a frayed thread still — would look west for a moment, the way you look toward a thing you are the last on earth to remember, and she would say:

"From a woman who drew the truest map I ever saw. She drew herself out of the world so the rest of us could keep walking in it. I never knew anyone else who'd do that. So I write her down. On every map. It's the only way I've got to keep her real."

And then she would take the buyer's copper, and roll the map, and the name Elin Sarr — unmarked on any stone, uncarved, uncut, owed nothing and owing nothing now — would go out into the river country one more time, folded into the back of an honest map, true at last in the only ink that ever really holds: the ink of being remembered, on purpose, by someone who chose to carry the weight.
