They made camp that night in the shadow of the toll, because none of them could think what else to do, and nobody chose anything, because choosing was the unbearable thing.

Elin did not sleep. She sat with her draft unrolled across her knees by the last of the fire and she read her own road the way a physician reads a wound. There had to be a flaw in it. She had drawn it; she knew her own hand; every line a maker draws has a place where the pen lifted, a join, a weakness. If she could find the flaw she could pick at it.

Pell came and sat down across the fire from her. The girl had not slept either.

"You're looking for a way out of it," Pell said.

"I'm looking at my work."

"Same thing." Pell drew her knees up. In the firelight she looked older than fifteen and also exactly fifteen. "Tell me how it works. Not the comforting version. The true one. You owe me that and you know you owe me that."

So Elin told her. The bridge at Tarn Ford and the boy who went through the ice. The way the world had a secret true shape and her ink could coax it early onto the page. The way the duke had wanted a city the fast way. And the way a road, once drawn, was not a line but an invitation, and would always, always find someone to walk it, because that was what roads were for.

Pell listened with her chin on her knees. When Elin finished, the girl was quiet a while.

"So the road has to be walked," she said. "And someone who walks it has to stay. And you can't be that someone, because you made it." She turned a stick in the fire. "What if nobody walks it?"

"Then nobody reaches Anvarre."

"I didn't ask about Anvarre. I asked about the toll. The toll is on walking the road. If we turn around — all of us — if we walk back east, back to the gate, and nobody ever finishes the road to Anvarre — does the debt come due?"

Elin opened her mouth to say of course it does and found that she did not know. She looked down at her draft. At the pale confident line curving west off the edge of the true world. At the milestones her hand had not drawn — ANVARRE XL, XXXI — the road had added those itself, the dream finishing the dream.

A road with no end is not a road, she had thought, days ago, on the moor. The dream wanted to be a true thing.

"It isn't finished," she said slowly. "That's it. That's the flaw, Pell, that's the lifted pen. The road isn't a true road yet. It's a dream of a road, and a dream isn't owed anything — the toll-keeper said it himself, the making is owed, but a thing half-made is not yet made. The road only becomes a true and finished road, with a true and binding debt, when someone walks it all the way through. When someone reaches Anvarre. That's the moment the dream sets. That's the moment the toll falls due."

Pell's eyes were very bright. "So if we don't reach Anvarre —"

"If nobody ever reaches Anvarre, the road never finishes setting. It stays a dream. And dreams —" Elin's hands had started, very slightly, to shake. "Dreams you can wake from. Sib said it. A road half-dreamed can still be woken from. I have to unmake it before anyone reaches the city. I have to unmake it from inside, standing on it, with the ink that drew it." She looked up. "And to do that I have to go forward. Not back. I have to reach Anvarre — first, alone, ahead of all of you — and unmake it from the fountain, from the very heart of the dream, before it can set around me."

"That's mad," said Pell. "If you reach Anvarre, you've finished the road. You said it yourself. Reaching the city is the moment the toll falls due."

"Yes," Elin said softly. "It is."

And she watched the girl work it through — watched her arrive at the place Elin had already arrived at, the cold floor at the bottom of the arithmetic. The maker could not be the toll. But the maker, standing in the finished city in the instant the dream set, unmaking the road with her own ink — the maker could refuse to let the dream find anyone else to charge. She could be there, in Anvarre, at the moment the bill came due, with no one else inside the walls.

There was a price the road would not let her pay. And there was a price, she was beginning to see, that it could not stop her from paying, if she paid it fast enough and alone.

"Don't," Pell whispered.

"Sleep," Elin said. "We'll talk in the morning." It was the duke's trick, ending an audience, and she was ashamed to use it. But she needed the girl asleep. She had a long way to walk before dawn, and she meant to walk it by herself.
