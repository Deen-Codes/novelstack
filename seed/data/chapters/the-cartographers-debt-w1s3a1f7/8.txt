Pell woke at dawn to a moor with no road in it.

She would spend a long time, afterward, trying to hold onto what she knew. It was like trying to hold a dream after the eyes open. There had been a road — she was almost sure there had been a road — and a toll, and a thing at the toll that did not breathe. There had been a woman with a case of pens who drew true maps. Hadn't there? Her father did not remember her. Her mother did not. Tam, when she asked him, only said, "What mapper?" and went back to throwing stones at a milestone that was not there anymore either.

The wagon stood on open heather, west of a sorry grey gate. There was no Anvarre on the plain. There never had been. Oren scratched his head and said he could not for the life of him think why he had brought the family up onto the high moor with winter coming, and they turned the wagon around, and Hesta sang to the baby, and they went back the way they had not quite come.

Only Pell looked back.

And only Pell, because she had sat across a fire and demanded the true version and not the comforting one, kept anything at all. The road had rewritten everyone it had touched — except that it had been unmade before it could finish setting, and a thing unmade in haste leaves frayed threads, and Pell had always been a frayed-thread sort of girl. She remembered. Not all of it. But enough.

She remembered a woman saying there is no better reason underneath, and meaning it, and choosing anyway.

She remembered a woman saying I have to unmake it from inside.

When the wagon passed back through the western gate of Hallowmere, the old gatekeeper was sitting in her lean-to against the pillar, and Pell, on an impulse she could not have explained, jumped down and went to her.

"Was there a mapper?" Pell said. "A woman. She drew true maps. She went west through your gate, days ago, with a case of pens."

The old woman — Sib, some buried thread in Pell's mind supplied — went very still. Her eyes searched Pell's face for a long moment, and what she was looking for, Pell understood, was whether the girl was a frayed thread too. Whether it was safe to remember out loud.

"Elin Sarr," Sib said at last, quietly, like a word she had been keeping. "Aye. There was. She came through my gate to draw a dying man his city, and I told her a road is an invitation, and she went anyway." The old woman looked west, at the empty moor where no road ran. "She didn't come back."

"No," Pell said. "She didn't." And then, because somebody ought to say the rest of it, and there was no one else left who could: "She drew a road, and the road wanted someone to pay for it, and it wouldn't let her pay with herself. So she — she made herself into the road. So the road would be what got unmade. She paid it anyway. The thing it told her she couldn't pay, she found a way to pay it."

Sib was silent a while. Up the moor, the tinker's wagon was waiting, the baby crying, ordinary life calling its girl back.

"Most don't, you know," the old woman said. "Most who can make things — they never once think the cost is theirs to carry. The duke surely never did. He's still up at Hallow House, dying, waiting for his fountain, and he'll die not knowing it cost a thing." She reached out and, gently, with one finger, touched the back of Pell's hand. "But you'll remember. That's a weight, girl. You understand that? You'll carry her now. Nobody else can."

"I know," Pell said.

She went back to the wagon. She did not tell her father, who would not have kept it. She climbed up beside Tam and the wagon creaked east toward the river country, toward winter, toward an ordinary long life — and Pell Coverdale, fifteen years old, sat with her chin on her knees and carried, carefully, the whole true weight of a woman the world had agreed to forget.
