She slipped past the toll-keeper in the black hour before dawn.

It did not stop her. It turned its smooth head and watched her duck under the oak bar with her case on her back, and it said only, "The maker walks the road," in a voice that might have held the ghost of curiosity, and then she was through, and Anvarre was ahead of her, white under the setting moon.

She walked the last nine leagues alone and faster than she had ever walked in her life. The road was very finished now, this near the city — cobbled, kerbed, worn smooth, ancient. It had been waiting forty leagues to become a real road and here at its destination it had nearly succeeded. She could feel the dream straining toward setting under her boots, the way you feel a held breath in a room.

Anvarre's gate stood open. Of course it did. A dreamed city had no reason to fear anything; nothing had ever come to it but its own road.

Inside, it was beautiful, and it was empty, and the emptiness was the most frightening thing Elin had ever seen. White walls, just as the song promised. Clean swept streets. Shopfronts with their shutters open and their counters bare. Windowboxes with painted flowers, because the duke's mother's song had not specified real ones. A city built entirely of saying-so, holding its shape the way a word holds its shape in the instant after it is spoken, before the silence comes for it.

And at the heart of it, a market square. And in the square, a fountain.

It was not running. A dreamed fountain did not know how to run. It was a basin of white stone and a standing figure and a silence where water should be, and Elin walked to the lip of it and set down her case and understood that she had reached Anvarre. The road behind her had, in that step, become a true and finished road. Forty leagues of binding debt had just come due, and the toll-keeper in the hills was, at this very moment, turning its head to find the one who would pay.

She had perhaps the length of a held breath. She had to make Anvarre never have existed before the road could find anyone else inside its reach to charge.

Elin unrolled her draft on the dry lip of the fountain. There was her road. There, in her own hand, was the pale line that had started all of it, curving west off the edge of the true world — the lifted pen, the first untrue stroke, the join.

She uncapped her best pen. Her hand was steady now. It is a strange mercy that the hand goes steady at the end.

She did not draw. Drawing was what had made this. Instead she set the nib to the start of the pale line, to the very first false stroke, and she began — slowly, deliberately, with the whole weight of eleven honest years behind it — to cross it out.

Not scribble it. Unmake it. She drew the true line back over the false one: the cart track ending in the grass at Sib's gate, the heather closing, the moor with no road in it, the milestones gone, the toll gone, the white walls gone. She drew the world as it had been before a dying man liked a song. She drew the absence of Anvarre as carefully and as lovingly as she had ever drawn the presence of anything, because unmaking, done right, is just mapmaking aimed backwards, and she was very good at her craft. She had always been very good at her craft. That was the whole tragedy of it.

The white walls around her began to thin.

The fountain under her draft began to lose its certainty, the standing figure softening like a name half-forgotten. The light changed. And Elin Sarr kept her nib moving, crossing out her road league by league, drawing Anvarre back into the song it had come from, and she felt the toll — the unpaid, owed, enormous toll — turn its full attention onto the only living thing left inside the dream as the dream came apart.

The maker cannot be the toll, the road had said, because unmaking the maker unravels the making.

Yes, Elin thought, with the last clear corner of her mind. Yes. That is exactly the door. I will pay you by unravelling the making, and the making is the road, and the road and I will go out together, and there will be no one — no tinker, no father, no boy with his mouth open — left standing on a true road to be charged for it.

She finished the line. She crossed out the last false stroke, the join, the lifted pen.

The white city went out like a candle.
