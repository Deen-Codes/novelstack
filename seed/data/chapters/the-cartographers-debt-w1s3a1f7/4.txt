The wagon caught her by dusk, because she let it.

She had argued with herself for an hour about whether to let it. A part of her wanted to walk on alone, to keep the thing she had done a private sin. But the road was getting cold, and the grey hills were rising ahead, and a woman alone on a forty-league road with three hundred crowns in her case was a fool, gift or no gift. So she stood at a milestone — ANVARRE XXXI — and waited, and lifted her hand.

The wagon was a tinker's rig, hung with pots and bundled hides, and it was driven by a family. A man with a careful, tired face. A woman with a baby slung against her chest. A boy of nine or ten on the tailboard with his legs swinging, and an older girl, fifteen or so, walking alongside with a goad.

"Room for a footsore mapper?" Elin called.

The man drew up. He looked her over — the case, the good boots, the absence of obvious weapons — and something in him relaxed. "Climb up. Mind the kettles." He was called Oren, the woman Hesta, and they were tinkers out of the river country, and they were as glad of company as she was, which made her feel worse.

"Didn't expect anyone else on the Anvarre road," Oren said as the wagon creaked into motion. "It's not much travelled."

"No," Elin agreed. Her mouth was dry. "How did you come to be on it?"

"Same as everyone, I expect." He shrugged. "Heard there was custom in Anvarre. Tinkers go where the custom is." He said it the way a man says a thing he has always known. And there it was — the world finding its reasons. Oren did not think he had been summoned. He thought he had decided. He thought he had always meant to go to Anvarre, the way the masons had always been building the bridge. The road had reached back into his life and rewritten the part where he chose.

"Have you been before?" she asked. "To Anvarre."

Oren frowned, and for just a moment the frown was the frown of a man whose memory had handed him something that did not fit. "No," he said slowly. "No, I don't believe I have. Strange. I feel as though I have." He shook it off and clucked to the team.

The girl, walking alongside, had been listening. Her name was Pell. She was the only one of them, Elin would come to understand, who the road's small lie did not quite fit — too sharp, too watchful, fifteen and already disinclined to be told things.

"There's no smoke," Pell said.

"What's that?" said Oren.

"Ahead. We've come down off the high moor, you can see a long way west now, and there's a city forty leagues out and not one thread of smoke over it." She looked at Elin, not at her father. "Cities smoke. Even at this distance you'd see it. Hearth-smoke, smith-smoke, something. There's nothing. It's like looking at a painting of a city."

Elin made herself meet the girl's eyes. "Perhaps the wind carries it off."

"Perhaps," Pell said, in a voice that did not believe her, and walked on with her goad.

That night they camped at a milestone and Hesta shared out a stew, and the baby fussed and was sung to, and the boy — his name was Tam — fell asleep against the wheel with his mouth open. Elin sat a little apart with her case in her lap and did not open it. She had three hundred crowns and a draft of a road, and around the same fire sat five people who were walking, warm and trusting and entirely real, toward a market square with a fountain that existed only because a dying man had liked a song.

Everything true has a cost, the duke had said. Who pays for a city?

She looked at the sleeping boy with his mouth open, and the question stopped being a riddle.
