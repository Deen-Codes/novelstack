They finished it on a Tuesday, of course. It would have been wrong to finish it on any other day.

It was the second week of March, and outside the back-room window the snow had gone soft and grey and undecided, and inside, under the buzzing light, the Double Wedding Ring quilt lay complete across the long table — top, batting, backing, all three layers quilted through, the deep evening-blue border framing two hundred interlocking rings.

June's granddaughter would be married in five weeks. The quilt would be wrapped in tissue and given to her, and she would say it was beautiful, and she would never know about the curved seams that nearly caused a divorce in the circle, or the crooked accidental join Dorothy had sewn and been told to leave in, or the blue cloth that was really a goodbye, or the boy who fixed the table leg, or the empty chair.

That was all right. A quilt is not supposed to explain itself. It is only supposed to keep someone warm.

"Last stitch," Margaret said. "Who does it?"

They looked at each other. Dorothy in the rocker. June on the folding stool. Iris in the green armchair with the burn mark. Margaret at the church pew. And the fifth chair, Carl Hennessy's ugly estate-sale chair, still there, still empty, because they had never folded it up after all.

"Put the needle on the chair," Iris said.

So they did. Margaret threaded it one last time — Dorothy's brass thimble, Pauline's brass thimble, passed down the table — and she made the final stitch in the binding, the stitch that closed the ring, and then she laid the threaded needle gently on the seat of the empty chair, the way you might leave a light on for someone, and the four of them sat back and looked at what they had made.

"She'd say it was crooked," Dorothy said.

"It is crooked," said June.

"She'd say it anyway. And then she'd say it was the best thing we ever did."

The light buzzed. The snow went on being undecided. In a few minutes June would put the kettle on and they would have one more cup of tea in the warm back room of a hardware store in a small town, four women and an empty chair and a finished quilt, and next Tuesday they would come back and begin something new, because that is what the circle was for — not the quilt, never really the quilt, but the coming back.

They would need a new pattern. They would argue about it. Someone would bring cookies.

And the chair would stay where it was, holding nothing, holding everything, the way it had chosen to from the start.
