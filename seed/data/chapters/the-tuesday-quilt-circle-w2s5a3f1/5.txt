The pattern was called Double Wedding Ring, and Margaret had argued against it from the start.

"It's too hard," she had said back in September, before any of this, when Connie was still alive and still in the chair. "All those curved seams. We'll fight the whole time."

"That's rather the point of a quilt circle," Connie had said. "If it were easy we could do it at home alone, and then what would we have."

So they had chosen the wedding-ring pattern, and Connie had been right, they had fought the whole time, and now Connie was gone and the half-finished quilt of interlocking rings lay on the table like an argument she had won and could no longer enjoy.

Margaret thought about that a great deal. The quilt was for June's granddaughter, who was getting married in the spring to a young man none of them had met, and the four of them had quietly agreed to finish it in time no matter what, because Connie had wanted it finished and because a deadline was a kind of mercy. It gave the grief somewhere to stand.

The curved seams were every bit as wretched as Margaret had predicted. You could not simply run a straight line; you had to ease the fabric, coax it, persuade two stubborn arcs to agree to be one ring. June was good at it. Iris was patient. Dorothy swore softly in the rocker. Margaret herself was merely stubborn, which she had learned, over twenty years of Tuesdays, was eighty percent of what a quilt actually required.

"A wedding ring," Dorothy said, holding a finished block up to the light, "is a circle with no beginning. That's the whole symbolism. No beginning, no end."

"It's got plenty of beginning," June said. "It's got two hundred beginnings. Every one of these seams is somewhere I had to start the needle again."

Margaret laughed. She thought June had said something truer than the symbolism — that a marriage, a friendship, a Tuesday circle, was not one unbroken ring at all but hundreds of small restartings, the needle going back in, again and again, at the place where the last thread ran out.

They had restarted, hadn't they. After the phone call. They had come back to the back room and put the kettle on and picked up the quilt. That was a restart. That was a stitch begun again in the cold.

"I take it back," Margaret said. "About the pattern being too hard. It was the right one. Connie knew."

"Connie usually did," said Iris, and eased another curved seam into a circle that, from a distance, looked seamless, and was not, and never had been, and held anyway.
