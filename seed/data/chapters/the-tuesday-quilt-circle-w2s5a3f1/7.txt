They had their worst argument in years over the borders, and it was not really about the borders.

It was the first Tuesday of December. The wedding-ring blocks were all assembled, two hundred curved seams behind them, and now the quilt needed its outer border — the frame, the last band of cloth that would hold the whole thing in. And on the question of what color that border should be, the circle split clean in two.

"Cream," said Dorothy. "It has to be cream. You don't frame a quilt this soft with anything loud. Cream, and quiet, and done."

"Connie wanted blue," said Iris.

"Connie is not here to want anything."

It came out sharper than Dorothy meant it. The room went still. Margaret looked at her lap. June's hand tightened on the fabric.

"That was unkind," Iris said quietly.

"I know it was." Dorothy's voice shook. "I know. I'm sorry. But I am so tired, Iris, I am so tired of every single thing in this room being about Connie. The chair is about Connie. The pattern is about Connie. The seam Dorothy sewed wrong is about Connie. She has been dead two months and she is still running the meeting."

Nobody spoke. The kettle, which June had put on, began its slow climb toward boiling.

"You're allowed to miss her without missing her perfectly," Margaret said at last, carefully. "And Dorothy's allowed to be angry. Anger's just grief that hasn't found its chair yet."

"I'm not angry at Connie," Dorothy said, and then, because the room was safe, because it had always been safe, "I'm angry that she didn't say goodbye. She knew. The doctors had told her in August and she sat right there in that chair every Tuesday through September and she never once — she let us find out from a telephone. Thirty years and she let us find out from a telephone."

So that was it. That was the swallowed button, finally up and out.

Iris reached into her sewing bag and took out a folded square of cloth and laid it on the table. Blue. A deep, soft, evening blue.

"She gave me this in September," Iris said. "For the border. She said, Iris, don't make a fuss of it, just see it gets used. I think that was the goodbye, Dorothy. I think she was just too much of a coward to do it with words, like the rest of us. She did it with cloth."

The kettle reached its boil and shrieked, and June got up and silenced it, and the four of them looked at the deep blue square on the table.

"Blue," Dorothy said. "Fine. Blue. The stubborn woman gets her border."

And it was, somehow, the kindest thing she had said in weeks.
