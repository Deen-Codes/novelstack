There is a part of quilting that nobody photographs. The top is the part people love — the pattern, the color, the clever interlocking rings. But a quilt is three layers, and the other two do the quiet work: the batting, the soft hidden middle that makes it warm, and the backing, the plain underside that no one is meant to look at.

The third Tuesday of December was a backing-and-batting day, and Tobias the hardware store boy had asked, shyly, if he could help, and so there were five of them in the back room again, which none of them remarked on and all of them noticed.

You cannot baste a quilt alone. That is the practical truth of it. The three layers must be smoothed flat and pinned together across the whole table, and it takes hands at every edge, pulling gently, keeping the tension even, so that no wrinkle gets sewn permanently into the middle where it can never be reached again.

"Pull your side, Tobias. Gently. You're not landing a fish."

"Like this?"

"Like that. Now hold it."

They worked around the table, the four old women and the boy, smoothing the deep-blue-bordered top over the soft batting over the plain unshowy backing, and pinning as they went, hundreds of small safety pins winking under the buzzing light.

"My mother used to say," June said, "that the backing is the honest part of a quilt. The top is what you want people to think of you. The backing is what you actually are. Plain. A bit patched. Holding everything together where it doesn't show."

"Then Connie was backing," Dorothy said. "All those years. We thought she was just sitting in that chair. She was holding the tension."

Tobias, pulling his side gently, not landing a fish, did not know who Connie was, not really, only that she was the empty chair and the cold hands and the blue cloth and the reason these four women had been sad since April. But he understood the thing about the backing. He thought about his own grandmother, dead before he was old enough to know her, who was apparently the reason his mother could cook and the reason his mother was kind, a plain hidden layer holding a warmth he got to keep without ever seeing its source.

"Hold it," June said again, and they all held it, every edge, every hand, and the quilt lay flat and true with no wrinkle pinned into its heart, and that — though none of them said so — was the entire point of the afternoon, and of the circle, and possibly of the thirty years.
