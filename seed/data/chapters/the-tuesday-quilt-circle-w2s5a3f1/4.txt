The hardware store boy was nineteen and his name was Tobias and he did not understand the quilt circle at all.

He had worked at Hennessy's since April, mostly stacking bags of salt and cutting keys and telling people which aisle the wood screws were in, and every Tuesday afternoon the same five — no, four now, he had noticed it was four — old women filed past the register to the back room and stayed there until closing. He could hear them through the wall. Laughing, sometimes. Arguing about colors. The buzz of their voices under the buzz of the lights.

He thought it was a little sad, if he was honest. He thought a lot of things about old people were a little sad, in the unexamined way that nineteen thinks things.

Then, the second Tuesday of November, June caught him at the register.

"You," she said. "Tall one. We need you."

"I'm minding the front."

"The front will survive eleven minutes. Come."

He came, because she was the sort of woman it did not occur to you to refuse, and in the back room they had a problem: the long table had a leg gone wobbly and the quilt would not lie flat, and none of the four of them could get down on the floor to fix it and back up again without considerable production.

So Tobias got down on the floor.

It took him four minutes. A wood screw, the very kind he sent people to aisle six for, driven up at an angle into the soft old pine. While he was down there, on his back under the table, he could see the quilt from below — the underside of it, the knots and the loose threads and the place where two cream borders had been sewn together wrong and left that way on purpose. He did not know it was on purpose. He thought it was a mistake nobody had bothered to fix.

"There," he said, standing up. "It won't wobble now."

"Sit down a minute," Dorothy said. "You've earned a cookie."

He should have gone back to the front. He sat down. He ate a lemon cookie, and then another, and the four old women went back to their sewing and stopped paying him any attention at all, and Tobias found, to his genuine surprise, that this was restful. Nobody wanted anything from him. The light buzzed. The needles moved. Somewhere a long story was being told in pieces he could not follow, about somebody named Connie and her cold hands, and he understood none of it and did not need to.

He stayed eleven minutes. The front survived.

Walking home that evening he thought: it is not sad. I had it wrong. And he could not have explained, to anyone his own age, what exactly he had had wrong, only that the back room of the hardware store was warmer than the rest of his life and he would not mind being asked again.
