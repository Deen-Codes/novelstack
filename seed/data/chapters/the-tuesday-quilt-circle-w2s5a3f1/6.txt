June's daughter Renata drove up from the city on a Tuesday, which was either an accident or wasn't, and arrived at Hennessy's Hardware at twenty past two with her good coat on and her mouth already set for a difficult conversation.

She found her mother in the back room, sleeves pushed up, easing a curved seam.

"Mum," Renata said. "Can we talk."

"We can talk here," June said, without looking up. "These three have heard everything else."

The three others bent lower over the quilt, a performance of not-listening so transparent that Renata almost laughed, and didn't, because she had driven ninety minutes to say something hard and she wanted to get it said before the wanting wore off.

"It's too much for you," she said. "The store closing the back room evenings. The hip. The drive. I think it's time you thought about somewhere — somewhere with people, with help. I've looked at a place near us. It's nice, Mum. It has a garden."

The needles stopped. Even the light seemed to buzz quieter.

June set down her work. She looked at her daughter for a long moment, and Renata braced for the argument, the stubbornness, the whole familiar fortress.

"Sit down, love," June said instead. "In the chair."

"Which chair?"

"That one. The empty one."

Renata sat in Carl Hennessy's ugly estate-sale chair, and felt, without being told, that she had been let into something. The four old women looked at her sitting there and something passed between them that she could not read.

"This is Connie's chair," June said. "Connie sat there for thirty years. She died last month. And here is the thing I want you to understand, because you've come all this way and you mean it kindly. This room is not too much for me. This room is the thing that is exactly enough. You want to move me somewhere with a garden and people. I have people. I have had the same four people every Tuesday for half my life, and one of them is gone, and I am not — I am not going to spend whatever Tuesdays I have left in a garden I didn't choose with people I haven't earned."

Renata was quiet. The chair was, in fact, deeply uncomfortable.

"I'll think about the hip," June said, gentler now. "I'll let you drive me to the doctor. I'll let you fuss. But not this. Not the Tuesdays."

"Okay," Renata said, and was surprised to find she meant it. "Okay, Mum."

She stayed the afternoon. They put her to work on the easy straight borders, the ones with no curves, and at four o'clock she understood for the first time that the quilt with her own daughter's wedding in it was being sewn, in part, by a dead woman's instructions, and she did not find this morbid. She found it, sitting in the uncomfortable chair, something close to holy.
