The Saturday crowd came back the way water finds an old riverbed — slowly, and then all at once.

By day fifty-five the Tuesday classes had a waiting list, and the people from the classes brought their families on Saturdays, and the Saturday families told the rest of the block, and Orchard Street remembered. Owusu came every morning and sat at the end of the counter with a coffee and told anyone who'd listen that this was the real bakery, the same bakery, and his word carried because he had been buying bread there since before some of the customers were born.

Renata kept a different notebook now. Not the one for things she thought she couldn't do — that one was nearly full of crossed-out lines. The new one was the ledger, and on day fifty-five the ledger did a thing it had not done in two years: it showed a number in black.

A small number. Forty-one dollars, after everything. But black.

She showed Dani the page that night and Dani looked at it for a long moment and then laughed — a real laugh, the one from the photograph, the one Renata had been chasing since the funeral.

"Forty-one dollars," Dani said. "We saved a bakery for forty-one dollars."

"It's not over. There's a month left. Saturdays could —"

"Ren." Dani took the notebook out of her hands and closed it and set it on the steel table. "Let it be a good day. You're so afraid of the next bad thing that you keep skipping the good days. Your abuela's note didn't say *be vigilant.* It said *be brave.* Those are different. Brave includes letting yourself have the forty-one dollars."

They were standing in the warm kitchen, which had become the place where the true things got said, and Renata looked at this woman — back in her life by way of a deathbed phone call and a tin box of photographs — and felt the old reflex rise, the one that wanted to be careful, to keep the heart cool, to not want anything she might lose.

She let it go.

"Stay," she said. "After the ninety days. Not as my teacher and not as my employee. I'm not asking you to be smaller. I'm asking you to be exactly the size you are, here, with me. Quit the hotel or don't, keep both, I don't care about the logistics. I'm asking the actual question now, Dani. The one we never finished. Will you stay."

Dani didn't run her hands through her hair. Renata had learned to read that — the deciding gesture only came when the answer was still in doubt.

"I gave my notice at the hotel on Tuesday," Dani said. "I've been trying to find the right night to tell you. I kept being careful." She smiled, rueful, caught. "Turns out I burn the sugar too."

Renata kissed her over a ledger showing forty-one dollars of profit, on day fifty-five, with thirty-five days still to go and, she understood now, no real suspense left in any of them — only the good slow work of opening the gate each morning and letting the neighborhood in.
