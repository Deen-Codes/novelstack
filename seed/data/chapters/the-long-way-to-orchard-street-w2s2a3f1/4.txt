They opened the bakery on day twenty-two.

It was not a triumph. Renata had wanted a triumph — a line down the block, the neighborhood remembering all at once. Instead, eleven people came in over the course of the day, and most of them came in to ask if it was really open, and four of those bought nothing, and one woman stood at the case for a long time and then said, "It's not the same," and left.

Renata locked the door at closing time and sat down on the floor behind the counter, which was not a thing a business owner should do, and put her head against the cool wood of the case.

"It's not the same," she said. "She's right. It's not."

Dani came around the counter, looked at her on the floor, and — instead of pulling her up — sat down next to her. They sat with their backs against the display case, shoulder to shoulder, in the dim of the closed shop.

"Day twenty-two," Dani said. "You know what your abuela's bakery looked like on day twenty-two? I don't either. But I promise you it didn't look like a line down the block. Nothing does. You're comparing your first chapter to her last one."

"I have sixty-eight days. Eleven customers a day at this margin is not a profit, Dani, I did the math, I'm a teacher, math is the one thing —"

"Then we change something." Dani turned her head. In the low light her face was very close. "But not tonight. Tonight you're allowed to sit on the floor of your grandmother's bakery and feel like it's too big for you. That's allowed. Tomorrow we change something."

Renata was aware of every inch of the half-foot of air between them. She had been aware of it for two weeks now, in the evenings, in the careful choreography of two people sharing a narrow kitchen — the way Dani would put a hand briefly at the small of her back to move past her, the way they had both stopped pretending those touches were only logistics.

"You're going to leave in sixty-eight days," Renata said. It came out as barely a sound.

"I know what I said."

"I'm not — I'm not trying to make you stay. I learned that lesson. I'm just." She closed her eyes. "I'm sitting on the floor being honest because you said I was allowed to feel things tonight. So here's a thing I feel. I don't want you to go. And I have no right to that, and I know it, and I'm telling you anyway."

The refrigerator hummed. Outside, Orchard Street did its evening business without them. Dani was quiet for so long that Renata thought she'd ask to pretend the last minute hadn't happened.

Then Dani's hand found hers, on the floor, between them, and held it.

"Eleven years ago," Dani said, "you asked me to be smaller. Tonight you asked me to stay and told me you'd be okay if I didn't. Do you understand that those are not the same question?" Her thumb moved over Renata's knuckles. "I'm not answering it tonight. I want that on the record. But I noticed too, Ren. For two weeks. I've been noticing."

They stayed on the floor a while longer, not kissing, not saying anything else, just two women holding hands in a closed shop that wasn't a profit yet, and might not become one, and Renata found that for the length of that quiet she did not care, and that not-caring was its own kind of answer to a question nobody had finished asking.
