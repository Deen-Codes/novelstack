Day eighty arrived gray and raining, and the rain was a problem, because day eighty was also the day the inspector came.

Renata had forgotten about the inspector entirely. The city scheduled a health inspection for any business changing ownership, and the letter had come weeks ago and gone into the drawer with the things she was too busy to be afraid of, and now a man in a city windbreaker was standing in her kitchen with a clipboard and the rain was hammering the front windows and a failed inspection would close the bakery for as long as it took to fix whatever he found, and there were ten days left.

"Breathe," Dani said, under her breath, passing behind her with a tray. "You cleaned this kitchen like it owed you money. You're fine."

Renata was not sure she was fine. She watched the inspector run a thermometer into the proofing cabinet, swab the marble, crouch to look at the underside of the case the way Dani had on the first day. She watched him write things down. The notebook of things she thought she couldn't do had a line in it from week one — *I can't run a business, there are a hundred ways it can be taken from me and I won't see them coming* — and it had not been crossed out yet, and here, in a city windbreaker, was the hundred-and-first way.

The inspector finished. He clicked his pen.

"Floor drain in the back needs a cover plate," he said. "That's it. Minor. You've got fourteen days to fix it, and it's a twelve-dollar part. Otherwise —" he looked around the kitchen, the racks, the soft-covered notebook still on the steel table — "this is one of the cleaner kitchens I've seen this month. Lucia's place, right? My mother used to send me here for bread." He almost smiled. "Glad it's back."

He left. The door chimed. The rain kept on.

Renata sat down on the floor behind the counter, which was still not a thing a business owner should do, and Dani — who had learned the choreography of this too — came around and sat beside her without a word.

"Twelve-dollar part," Renata said. "I spent three weeks being afraid of a twelve-dollar part."

"You spent three weeks being afraid of losing this." Dani leaned her head against Renata's shoulder. "Which is different, and it's allowed, and you're going to keep doing it, probably, because you love it now and that's what loving something costs. But look at what you did with the fear, Ren. You didn't get smaller. You cleaned the whole kitchen. You found Carmen. You learned to seal a roll and teach a Tuesday and run a ledger. The fear didn't win. It just made you thorough."

Renata took out the old notebook from her apron pocket — the things-I-can't-do notebook, nearly full, nearly all crossed out. She found the line from week one. She crossed it out too, slowly, while Dani watched.

Then she wrote one new line at the bottom, the only thing on the last page.

*I can run this bakery.*

She did not cross it out, because it wasn't a fear anymore. It was just a fact, written down, in case — the way her grandmother had taught her, the way the whole long path had been pointing all along.

Outside, the rain began to ease. Ten days left. A twelve-dollar part to buy. And forty-one dollars, then more, then more, in black in the ledger, and a woman beside her on the floor who was not going anywhere.
