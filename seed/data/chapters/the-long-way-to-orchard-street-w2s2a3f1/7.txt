On day sixty-three, a woman Renata didn't know came into the bakery an hour before closing and stood at the case for a long time, the way the first woman had on opening day — *it's not the same* — and Renata braced for it.

"Are you Lucia's granddaughter?" the woman asked.

"I am."

"I'm Carmen. I worked here. 1988 to 1996, mornings, I did the bread." Carmen's eyes moved over the room, the marble counter, the hand-lettered signs Renata had learned to make in her grandmother's blocky style. "I heard it reopened. I didn't believe it. People said it closed for good."

"It almost did."

"I have something." Carmen set a paper bag on the counter and drew out a notebook — spiral-bound, the cover gone soft as cloth. "Your grandmother gave me this when I left. The morning recipes, in her hand. She said, *take it, in case.* I think she meant in case of exactly this." She slid it across the marble. "I don't bake anymore. My hands. But you should have it. It's yours."

Renata opened the notebook with the care of someone handling a relic and found her grandmother's fence-in-a-windstorm handwriting, page after page — the bread, the rolls, the conching of the chocolate, the proportions the whole neighborhood had mythologized. The actual secrets. Not in a tin box. In a person, exactly as Dani had said, carried out the door in 1996 and brought back thirty years later by hand.

That night she showed Dani.

"She wrote them down," Renata said. "She always said she didn't trust paper. But she did, once, for Carmen. *In case.*" She turned the soft pages. "She planned for the bakery to outlive her. She planned for someone to need these. She planned for me, Dani, years before she got sick. The ninety days, the note, sending me to find you — none of it was a test I might fail. It was a path she built so I couldn't get lost."

Dani came to stand beside her and read over her shoulder, and for a while neither of them spoke; there was only the soft turning of pages, the recipes coming home.

"Then you don't need me for the recipes anymore," Dani said. It wasn't a question, and there was a smile in it. "It's all right here."

"No," Renata agreed. "I don't need you for the recipes." She closed the notebook and turned around and put her hands flat against the navy apron, over Dani's heart. "I need you for the part that isn't in any notebook. The part that's just — you, in this kitchen, every evening, for the rest of it." She felt the steadiness of the heartbeat under her palms. "That part she couldn't plan for me. I had to be brave enough to find it myself."

Carmen's notebook sat on the steel table between the cooling racks, and the bakery on Orchard Street held its secrets again, all of them, the written ones and the human ones, ready for whatever came after day ninety.
