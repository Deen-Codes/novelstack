On day ninety, Mr. Adeyemi came to the bakery instead of summoning Renata to the office. She thought that was kind of him until she realized he had come because he wanted to see it.

He stood in the doorway in his good coat and looked at the full display case, the chalkboard listing the Tuesday class, Owusu at his usual seat with his coffee, two customers debating guava versus cheese. He took it all in the way he took everything in, softly, and then he set his briefcase on the marble counter and opened it.

"The terms required profitability over the ninety-day period," he said. "I've reviewed the ledger your accountant submitted." He looked up. "You were profitable in forty-six of the last sixty-eight operating days. The aggregate is comfortably positive. The condition is met. The bakery is yours, Ms. — it's yours. Free and clear."

Renata had thought she would cry. She had been bracing for the crying all morning. Instead what came was that low distant hum from the lawyer's office on the first day — the refrigerator in another room — except now she understood what it had always been. It was not numbness. It was the sound of something running, keeping things from spoiling, doing quiet work so the rest of the house could live. It was the sound of being relied upon.

"Thank you," she said.

"Don't thank me. I just read the page." Mr. Adeyemi closed the briefcase. "Your grandmother left one more thing. I was instructed to deliver it only if you succeeded." He took out an envelope, and this one was thicker, and he set it in her hands. "She seemed quite certain you would."

He bought a guava roll on his way out and ate it on the sidewalk in his good coat, and through the window Renata watched him stop chewing, surprised, the way people did.

She opened the envelope in the kitchen with Dani, because the kitchen was where the true things happened. Inside was a deed — the small apartment above the bakery, the one Renata had assumed was sold years ago — made over to her, with a note clipped to it.

*Mija. If you are reading this you did it, and you are not surprised, because by now you know you always could. The apartment is yours so you never have to choose between the work and a place to rest, the way I did. Live above the bakery. Wake up to the smell of it. And if you were brave enough to ask her, and she was wise enough to say yes — and I think she will be, I have spoken to her, she loves you, she always did — then there is room up there for two. Bake well. Love everyone who comes in. Take three peppermints whenever you like. The long way was always going to lead here.*

Renata read it aloud, and her voice held until the last line, and then it didn't, and Dani took the deed out of her hands the way she had taken the ledger weeks ago — closing it, setting it down, making room.

"She knew," Renata said. "She knew before I did. About you. About all of it."

"She called me six months ago and talked for two hours," Dani said softly. "She didn't only tell me the recipes, Ren. She told me to be patient with you. To wait for you to get brave. She built the path for both of us." She wiped Renata's face with her thumb. "So. Room up there for two?"

Renata looked around the kitchen — the racks, the steel table, Carmen's soft-covered notebook, the apron on the hook, ninety days made of flour and fear and forty-one dollars turned into a life.

"Room for two," she said. "And the long way home."
