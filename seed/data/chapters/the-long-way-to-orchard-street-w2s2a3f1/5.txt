The change they made was Dani's idea, and it was small, and it worked the way her grandmother's whole life had worked: through people.

"Stop selling pastries," Dani said over coffee on day twenty-four. "Start selling Tuesdays."

Renata waited.

"Tuesday afternoons, two o'clock. You teach a class. Six people, the counter, an hour. How to make the guava roll. Charge for it. People won't come for a pastry they can buy anywhere — but they'll come to *learn the famous one*, the one their grandmother used to bring home." Dani spread her hands. "And while they're here for an hour, learning, they smell the place. They talk. They come back on a Thursday because now it's *their* bakery. You're not selling rolls. You're selling belonging. That was always the product."

The first Tuesday, two people came. Renata taught them anyway, hands shaking, and they were patient with her, and one of them — an older man named Owusu who lived above the laundromat — said at the end, "Your grandmother taught me to make bread in 1994. I came to see if you'd do it the same way." He paused at the door. "You did."

The second Tuesday, five people came. The fourth, the counter was full and Renata had to turn two away and ask them to come the following week, and that night she found her grandmother's note in her coat pocket and read it again, *you always do, you just don't believe it yet,* and she believed it a little now.

Day thirty-eight. They had closed up and Dani was rinsing the last bowl, sleeves pushed up, and Renata stood in the doorway between the front room and the kitchen and watched her, and the wanting was so simple and so large that it stopped being something to manage.

"Dani."

Dani turned the tap off. She didn't turn around right away — she set the bowl on the rack with great care, and dried her hands on the faded navy apron, and only then faced Renata across the kitchen.

"I burned the sugar," Renata said, which was not what she meant. "Today. The caramel. I burned it because I wasn't watching the pot. I wasn't watching the pot because I was watching you."

"I know," Dani said. "I smelled it."

"That's my whole — that's the thing I'm trying to say." Renata crossed the kitchen. "I can't watch the pot anymore. I've been trying to be careful with this for sixteen days and I keep burning the sugar."

She stopped close. Close enough that the choreography had nowhere left to go.

Dani reached up and touched the gray streak in Renata's hair, the one that had been black in the photograph in the tin box, and her hand was warm and certain.

"Then stop being careful," she said.

Renata kissed her in the warm dark of the kitchen, eleven years and ninety days and one burned pot of caramel in the making, and Dani's arms came around her like something finally set down after being carried a long way. The bakery held them. It had been built, Renata thought distantly, for exactly this — for people, for belonging, for the long way around to the place you should have been all along.
