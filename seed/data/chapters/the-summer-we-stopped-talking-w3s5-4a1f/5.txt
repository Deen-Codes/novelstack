The Fourth of July arrived the way it always did, all at once and too hot.

Our town did fireworks over the lake. Everyone gathered on the public beach with folding chairs and citronella candles and coolers, and the fire department set the show off from a raft anchored in the middle, and the fireworks bloomed twice, once in the sky and once upside down on the water. When we were small, Caleb told me the lake had its own fireworks and the sky was just copying. I believed it until I was eleven, which is embarrassing, but in my defense he was very convincing and the water really did look like it was doing the work.

I had been counting on the Fourth of July all summer. I want to admit that, because it was a foolish thing to count on, and I had built more on it than it could hold. Somewhere in June I had decided that the fireworks would be the cure. I had told myself the story the way you tell yourself a story to get to sleep: Caleb would stand on the public beach, and the first shell would go up, and the boy who had narrated his own life like a sportscast, the boy who loved fireworks more than he loved most people, would simply be returned to us, restored, the way a borrowed thing is returned. I had no plan beyond that. The fireworks were the plan. That is how you think when you are sixteen and frightened — you pick a date on the calendar and you load it with everything you cannot carry yourself, and you call that hope.

Caleb didn't want to go.

"I'll stay back," he said, when Mom started loading the cooler. "You guys go."

And Mom, who had been so careful all summer, so soft, so willing to accept answers shaped like ducks, suddenly wasn't. She set the cooler down on the kitchen floor hard enough that the ice shifted.

"You love the fireworks," she said.

"I'm tired."

"You have been tired," my mother said, "since the second of June." Her voice didn't rise. It did the opposite. It got very level and very quiet, and that was so much worse, and Caleb and I both went still. "I have watched you be tired for five weeks, Caleb, and I have let you, because I thought you'd come up for air on your own. But you love the fireworks. You have loved the fireworks your entire life. And if you are too tired for the fireworks then I need you to look at me and tell me what is actually wrong, because I am done. I am done pretending the lasagna fixed it."

The kitchen held very still. The clock ticked. Somewhere a screen door, not ours.

I watched my brother stand at the exact center of the worst moment of the summer, and I watched him not be able to do it. His mouth opened. Nothing came. The truth was right there, the failed class, the six weeks of walking, the imaginary glaciers, and he could not lift it. It was too heavy and he had been carrying it alone for too long and his arms were just done.

I had never watched anyone fight themselves before. Not really, not the way I watched my brother fight himself in our kitchen that evening. It is a strange thing to witness, because almost nothing moves. From the outside it looked like a boy standing still beside a melting cooler. But I had spent a summer learning to read him, learning to fish, learning that you don't yank the line, and so I could see the whole struggle going on behind his stillness — the truth rising up toward the surface, and some old terrified part of him reaching down to hold it under. He had a chance, right then. The door was open a crack and the light was on inside and all he had to do was walk through it. I saw his shoulders start to come down from around his ears. I saw him almost do it.

And then I saw him not. I saw the old part win, the part that had folded a letter into eighths and carried it eight hundred miles in a glovebox rather than let it touch us, and his shoulders went back up around his ears, and the door swung quietly shut, and my mother's face did something I will never forget — not anger, nothing as simple as anger, just a terrible tired patience, the look of a woman who has been waiting at a door for five weeks and has just heard the lock turn again from the inside.

So I lifted the other end.

"He failed a class," I said. "In the spring. He stopped going. He's been scared to tell you because he thought you'd be—" I didn't have the word. "He thought it would cost something. It's been eating him the whole time. That's the tired."

The kitchen was so quiet I could hear the ice settling in the cooler.

Caleb looked at me. I had broken the not-promise, the one I'd never actually made, and I waited for his face to close against me like the screen door, like the dock, like June.

It didn't. It did the other thing. It came open. His eyes filled and he didn't wipe them and he looked, for the first time all summer, his actual age, which was nineteen, which is not very old at all.

"Yeah," he said, to Mom. "That's the tired."

And our mother crossed the kitchen and put her arms around her son, and the cooler sat melting on the floor, and outside the light was going gold, and we missed the start of the fireworks. We missed them entirely. We sat at the kitchen table instead while Caleb finally talked, and through the window the sky flashed and the lake flashed back, copying, and none of us got up to watch.
