On the last Saturday before he left, Caleb said he wanted to walk the long way around the lake.

The long way is exactly what it sounds like. Most of the year nobody does it. It's eleven miles of shore road and dirt path and one stretch where you have to climb over a fallen tree that has been fallen for so long it has a name, the Bridge, even though it bridges nothing. We packed sandwiches. Mom watched us go from the porch with an expression that was trying very hard not to be sentimental and failing in a way she'd have hated to know was visible.

There is a thing about the long way that you only learn by doing it, which is that it is not one walk. It is many walks, joined. There is the bright easy first stretch along the shore road, where you are fresh and the lake is on your left and you make jokes. There is the dull middle, the dirt-path miles, where your feet start to register a complaint and the lake disappears behind trees and you stop talking, not unhappily, just because talking, like everything else, gets rationed once you understand how far you still have to go. And there is the Bridge, the named tree, the fallen thing that bridges nothing, and after the Bridge there is a different walk again, a slower and more honest one, two people too tired now to perform anything for each other.

I had walked the long way exactly twice before in my life, both times as a much younger kid, both times with Caleb, and I understood without either of us saying it that he had chosen the long way on purpose, as a last thing, because the long way was a place that belonged only to the two of us and to the people we had been before any of this. He was not just taking a walk. He was going back to get something.

We walked. It took us all day. And somewhere in the eleven miles, my brother came all the way back.

It happened in pieces, the way it had all summer, but the pieces came faster now, like the lake when the wind picks up. He talked about the spring properly, finally, the whole shape of it. How it hadn't started with anything dramatic. How he'd just gotten a little behind, and then being behind had felt like a thing everyone could see, and so he'd skipped a class to avoid being seen behind, and skipping had put him further behind, and the further behind had made the being-seen worse, until walking into the building felt like walking on stage naked, and so he stopped, and the stopping was so much easier than the walking-in that for six weeks he just chose easy, over and over, every morning, until easy had built him a small terrible house with no doors.

"The whole time," he said, climbing over the Bridge, "I knew it was stupid. That's what nobody tells you. You're not confused. You can see exactly how stupid it is. You just can't make your legs do the other thing."

"That doesn't sound stupid," I said. "That sounds like being stuck."

"Yeah." He reached back and gave me a hand over the Bridge, which I didn't need, but I took it. "I think that's the word. I was so busy calling it stupid that I never tried the word stuck. Stuck you can do something about. Stuck has a way out. Stupid just makes you hate yourself in a circle."

We ate the sandwiches on a rock at mile seven. The lake from there looked enormous, a whole inland sea, our town a smudge on the far shore that you could cover with a thumb.

It is a strange feeling, covering your whole life with a thumb. I sat on the rock and held my thumb up and blotted out the town — the house, the dock, the bait shop, the school, the guardrail, all of it, the entire stage on which the worst summer of our family had been performed — and it vanished, just like that, behind one thumb. I don't fully know how to explain what that did to me. It did not make the summer feel small, exactly. It made it feel survivable. From mile seven, with the lake huge and the town thumb-sized, I could finally see that the silence Caleb and I had built was a thing with edges, a thing that fit inside a view, a thing that the world went on enormously around. In June, inside it, the silence had felt like the whole sky. From the rock I could see it was just weather over one small smudge of shore. And weather passes. Weather always, always passes. You just cannot believe that while you are standing in the rain.

"I'm scared to go back," Caleb said, with his mouth full, casually, the way you can finally say a thing once it's stopped being a secret. "Like, properly. What if I just do it again."

"Then you do it again," I said, "and we'll be here, and you'll try a third time." I shrugged. "Dad already wrote the line. I'm just saying it back to you."

Caleb laughed, a full one, the kind with a floor and walls and a roof, a whole house of a laugh, and the sound of it went out over the water and the water, as ever, copied it back.

We finished the long way as the light was going. Eleven miles. My feet were ruined. We came up the shore road in the gold-going-blue of late evening and there was our house with its lights on, and Mom on the porch where she'd been that morning as if she had simply waited there all day, and the dock going dark over the dark water.

"I used to think the lake smelled like a wet dog's opinion of itself," Caleb said.

"You said that constantly. For years."

"I was wrong," he said. "It smells like home. I think I just couldn't smell anything, for a while there." He looked at me sideways. "Thanks for walking it with me. The long way."

"It's the only way I know how to do anything," I said, and we went up the steps together, into the light, where dinner was waiting and the clock was ticking and there was, finally, enough noise in the house again to drown it out.
