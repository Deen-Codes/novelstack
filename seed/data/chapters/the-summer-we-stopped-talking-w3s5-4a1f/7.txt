In August the lake gets warm enough to swim out far.

Caleb asked me on a Tuesday, which I noticed, because he had come home on a Tuesday, and I had started keeping a private superstition about the day. He found me on the dock with my feet in the water, in his old spot, doing his old thing, and the symmetry of it was not lost on either of us.

"Swim out to the float?" he said.

The float was a wooden platform anchored a long way out, past where the green went dark. It had a ladder and a faded number painted on it and it bobbed all summer like a thought you keep meaning to have. We used to race to it when we were kids. He always won, until one year I won, and he claimed a cramp, and we never raced again because the truth had come out and ruined it.

That is the kind of thing you understand differently at sixteen than at ten. At ten I had been furious about the cramp, furious that he would not just let me have the one clean win, and the fury had soured the float for years, made it a place I associated with my brother's small ordinary cowardice. But sitting on the dock now, with everything I had learned that summer, I found I could see the whole memory from his side for the first time. He had been thirteen, maybe. He had lost a race to his little sister and he had not had, at thirteen, the equipment to simply lose it. So he had invented a cramp. He had built a small fort of a lie to live inside, the way you do, the way he had built a whole fake semester of glacier lectures six years later. My brother had always been a person who, when the true thing was too heavy, quietly manufactured a lighter thing and handed it over instead. The cramp was just the failed class in miniature. He had been telling me who he was since he was thirteen years old, on this exact stretch of water, and I had been too busy being ten and robbed to listen.

"It's far," I said.

"It's the same distance it's always been."

So we swam. The water near the dock was bath-warm and then, all at once, past a certain line, it turned cool and serious, the deep water reaching up. I am not a strong swimmer. I felt the cold take my feet and I felt my breath get short and stupid, and I almost turned back, and then Caleb, beside me, matched his stroke to mine. He slowed himself down to my speed without saying anything about it. The way our hands had found the rhythm at the sink. The body remembering.

We reached the float and hauled ourselves up the ladder and lay on the hot warped wood, breathing, the platform tipping gently under us, the shore a long way off and small.

"I keep thinking about June," Caleb said, to the sky. "When I couldn't talk. It wasn't that I didn't want to. I'd open my mouth and there'd just be this. Weight. Like the words were down at the bottom and I couldn't get a breath big enough to dive for them."

"I know," I said. "I mean. I didn't know then. I do now."

"You did the diving." He turned his head to look at me. Water ran out of his hair across the wood. "On the Fourth. You went down and got the words for me. I don't know if I ever. I should have said." He stopped. Started again. "Thank you. For getting the words."

The float tipped. The sun was very bright. I thought about all the things I could say back, and I understood that this was the deep water, that this was the line where the green went dark, and that I had a choice to make about whether to dive or turn back.

"You'd have done it for me," I said. "You did do it for me. Remember when I broke the Petrakis window and I couldn't tell Mom and you just. Told her. You said you did it. You took a week of dishes for a window you didn't break."

Caleb blinked. "I forgot about the window."

"I didn't," I said. "I never forgot the window. That's the whole point of a brother. Somebody who'll take the dishes." My voice did something embarrassing on the last word and I let it. We were out past where it was shallow enough to be careful.

And then I made myself dive, because I had decided, somewhere in the last stroke before the ladder, that I was done being a family that handed each other the lighter thing. "I have to tell you something," I said, to the sky, copying his trick, not looking at him. "In June. When you were so quiet. I didn't only feel sorry for you. I want that to be true between us, the real one, not the nice one. I also hated it. I hated you a little. I'd lie in my room and hear you not-talking through the wall and I'd think, he's doing this to us, he's choosing this, he could just be normal and he won't. And then I'd feel like the worst person alive for thinking it. I carried that around all summer and it was heavy and I never told anyone, and I think — I think if I don't tell you now, I'll be doing the exact thing you did. Folding it into eighths. Keeping it in a glovebox."

The float tipped. For a long moment Caleb didn't say anything, and I lay there with my heart going hard, certain I had wrecked it.

Then he said, "Good." And when I turned my head, startled, he was almost smiling. "I mean it. Good. Hate the June version of me a little. That guy deserves it." He looked back up at the sky. "What I couldn't have stood — what would've actually finished me — is if everyone had only ever felt sorry. Sorry's a thing you feel about a stranger. You hated me because you knew me. Because you remembered there was supposed to be a different brother in that room." He breathed out, long and slow, the deep water rocking us both. "Thank you for the hating. Genuinely. It's the most like family anyone's treated me since I got home."

We didn't race back. We swam the same speed, side by side, the cool deep water under us the whole way and neither of us pretending it wasn't, and that, I think, was the actual end of the summer we stopped talking, even though the calendar still had a month to go. The talking had already started again. It just hadn't gotten loud yet.
