After the bait shop, Caleb started coming to find me.

Not every day. Not in a way you could circle on a calendar. But there would be afternoons when I'd be walking the long way home along the shore road and a shadow would fall in beside mine, and it would be him, and we would walk. We didn't talk much even then. We had become two people who knew how to be quiet near each other without it being a wound, which is its own kind of skill, and which I would not have believed possible in June.

I want to try to explain the half-conversation thing properly, because it was the most useful thing I learned that whole summer, and I have used it many times since, on many people, and it has never once failed me.

What I had been doing wrong in June was asking. Asking is a door, and a door demands a yes or a no, and a person who is drowning does not have a free hand to work a doorknob. Every question I had asked Caleb in June — how was the year, are you okay, what's wrong — had been a door swung at his face, and he had done the only thing a person can do with a door swung at their face, which is flinch back from it.

A half-conversation is not a door. A half-conversation is a thing you set down on the ground between you and then look away from. You say a small true sentence about the world — the Hendersons got a new dog — and you make it complete, make it ask nothing, make it a thing he can pick up or step over entirely with no cost either way. And then you wait. You keep walking. You look at the road. And what I discovered, that summer, was that a person who will not walk through a door will sometimes, four steps later, quietly pick the thing up off the ground and turn it over, just to have something in his hands.

I learned to give half a conversation and let him decide whether to pick up the other half.

"The Hendersons got a new dog," I would say.

Sometimes nothing. Sometimes, four steps later, "What kind."

"Some kind of poodle thing. It has a haircut."

And sometimes, if the light was right and the day had been kind to him, he would say something like, "Dogs shouldn't have haircuts. It's undignified. For everyone," and there he would be, my brother, the boy who reviewed sandwiches, surfacing for air in the middle of a sentence about a poodle. Then he'd go back under. But I was learning to fish. I was learning that you don't yank the line. You let it run.

It was on one of those walks that he said the first true thing.

We were at the place where the road bends away from the water and a guardrail keeps cars from discovering the drop. Caleb stopped and put both hands on the rail, which was hot, and didn't take them off.

"I failed a class," he said, to the lake. "In the spring. I failed it."

I kept very still. A deer in the clearing.

"It wasn't even hard," he said. "That's the part I can't. It wasn't hard. I just stopped going. I'd walk to the building and then I'd just keep walking. I did that for like six weeks. I'd call Mom on those walks and tell her about classes I wasn't going to anymore." He laughed, a small terrible laugh with no floor under it. "I got really good at describing them. I made up a whole lecture about glaciers once."

I understood, standing there, that I was being handed something. Not a confession, exactly — it was bigger and stranger than a confession. My brother, who had said maybe two hundred words to me in five weeks, had just told me the load-bearing secret of his entire spring, the thing under all the tiredness, and he had done it not to me, really, but to the lake, with his back half-turned, the way you can sometimes only say the worst thing if you don't have to watch it land on a face. The phone calls were the part that broke me. I kept getting stuck on the phone calls. My brother, walking away from a building he could not make himself enter, calling our mother, and inventing — out of nothing, out of pure desperate love — entire lectures, entire classes, an architecture of a life he was no longer living, so that she would not have to know. He had built her a fake semester the way you build a child a fort. To keep her safe inside it.

I had a hundred things I could have said. I could have said it's okay, which would have been a lie shaped like comfort. I could have said why, which would have been a door slammed in his face — and I had finally, finally learned about doors. I could have said you should tell Mom, which was true and useless and not what the moment was for.

So I said, "Glaciers."

And Caleb said, "Glaciers."

And then, unbelievably, he laughed again, and this time the laugh had a floor, and I laughed too, the two of us cracking up against a hot guardrail about the imaginary glacier lecture my brother had delivered to our mother to keep her from knowing he was drowning. It was not a happy laugh. But it was real, and it was ours, and when it died down the silence that came after it was a different silence than before. It was the silence of two people who now shared something, instead of the silence of two people hiding the same thing in separate rooms.

"Don't tell her," he said.

I didn't answer that. I have never been a good liar, and I wasn't going to start with a promise. But I bumped his shoulder with mine, the way we used to in the back of the car, and we walked the rest of the way home, and the not-answering sat between us, patient, like a third walker who knew we'd have to deal with it eventually.
