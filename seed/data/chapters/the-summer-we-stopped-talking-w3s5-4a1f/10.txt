Caleb left for the spring semester on a Tuesday.

I had stopped, by then, keeping the superstition about Tuesdays. It had served its purpose. Some superstitions are just handrails you hold in the dark until the lights come back, and when the lights come back you can let go, and you should, or you'll spend your life gripping a wall in a lit room.

We loaded the truck in the cold. His college was eight hundred miles away and Dad was driving him, having traded a shift to do it, which was its own quiet sentence in the language our family spoke. Mom packed food for a journey that did not require that much food, because that was her sentence, and I stood in the driveway with my breath showing, working out what mine would be.

I had been thinking, all that winter, about the language. About how I'd spent June believing my family had simply stopped talking, gone broken and mute, when the truth was stranger and kinder than that. We had never stopped talking. We had only stopped talking in the obvious way, the with-mouths way. All summer, underneath the silence, the family had been saying enormous things to each other in the only currencies the silence left open. Mom had said I will not let you disappear in a pan of lasagna and then in a kitchen floor she set a cooler down hard on. Dad had said you will be okay in a thirty-year-old story kept clean for exactly this. Caleb had said I love you so much I would rather drown than splash you in a letter folded into eighths in a glovebox. And I — I had said it in warm root beer, and half-conversations, and a shoulder bumped against a shoulder. We had been fluent the whole time. I had just been too young, in June, to know that love has a great many alphabets, and that the loudest one is not always the one a family reaches for when the thing it needs to say is too big for the mouth.

"Text me your actual days," I said finally. "Not the sanded ones. I'll know. I learned to hear it from Mom."

Caleb leaned against the truck. He looked good. Not fixed, not finished, but good, the way a house looks good when the lights are on and you can see people moving around inside. "What if some of the days are bad."

"Then text me the bad ones," I said. "That's the deal. Bad days still count as days. You taught me that. Real days snag on things."

"That was Mom."

"It's all of us now," I said. "It's the family thing. We pass it around."

He pulled me into a hug, a real one, the kind that has weight, and over his shoulder I could see the lake, gray and flat under a winter sky, the dock empty, the float hauled in for the season. I thought about June, about the boy on the dock with his feet in cold water, wearing his own face a size too large. I thought about how I had spent a whole summer afraid he was gone for good, replaced, that the brother I knew had been left behind in some lecture hall eight hundred miles away.

He hadn't been gone. That was the thing I finally understood, with my chin on my brother's shoulder in a cold driveway. He had been right there the whole time, underwater, holding his breath, waiting for the family to learn how to dive. And we had learned. Slowly, badly, missing the fireworks, snapping beans, walking eleven miles. But we had learned.

"Drive safe," Mom said to Dad, and to Caleb she didn't say anything at all, she just held his face for a second between both her hands, the way you'd hold something you had spent a whole summer learning how to hold, and then she let go, because that is also a thing you have to learn, the letting go, and she had been practicing it since June.

Caleb climbed into the truck. Through the window he looked at me, and I looked at him, and we did not say anything, because by then we did not need to — we had spent a whole season getting fluent, and a fluent family can hold an entire conversation in a look across a cold driveway. His look said I'll text you the bad ones. My look said I'll know if you don't. And both of us, I think, were also saying the older thing, the under-thing, the sentence the whole summer had been a slow translation of: I am so glad it is you. I am so glad, of all the people I could have been assigned to drown next to, that it was you.

The truck pulled out. I watched it to the bend in the shore road, the place with the hot guardrail, cold now, and then it was gone, and the lake did nothing, because lakes don't, they just hold whatever weather you give them and wait.

I went back inside when the cold finally won. The house was very quiet without Caleb's bag by the door and without the truck in the drive, but it was not the June quiet, the held-breath quiet, the quiet with something hiding inside it. It was just the ordinary quiet of a house with two of its people out on a road. I went up to Caleb's room and stood in the doorway. He had made the bed. On the desk, where for one whole summer there had been nothing, he had left a single thing for me to find — a postcard, blank, of our own lake, the kind they sold at Mr. Petrakis's by the melted candy, and on the back, in my brother's handwriting, the handwriting I knew like a face, it said only: for the bad days. text me the bad ones too. I kept that postcard. I have it still. It is the most words my brother ever needed to say to me, and there are only nine of them, and I have built a fair amount of my life on what they meant.

That night at dinner there were three of us, and then Dad would be back and there would be four, and in the spring, for the summer, there would be five again, and the summer after that who knew. Families breathe. They get big and small. I know that now in a way I did not know it in June.

Mom said, "One good thing."

"Caleb texted from the first gas stop," I said. "He said the drive was good. No traffic." I paused. "And then he said the rest stop bathroom was a war crime and Dad wouldn't let him get the big slushie. So. A real day. It snagged."

And my mother laughed, the real way, the way that made her put her fork down, and I sat in the noise of it, the good ordinary noise, and I thought: this is the summer we started talking again. It just took until winter for me to be sure.
