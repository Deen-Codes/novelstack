By take eleven of the same vocal line, I know two things I didn't know yesterday.

The first is that Cole Vega can sing. Really sing — not the produced, stadium-sized thing the old Ravensdown records gave people, but the actual instrument under it, raw and exact, a voice that does what he tells it the way my faders do what I tell them. The label sent me a man with a reputation for being finished. They did not send me a finished man. They sent me someone who is, very privately, terrified that they're right.

The second thing I know is that he keeps stopping the take when it's good.

"Again," he says, into the talkback, from the other side of the glass.

"Cole. That was the one." I pull my hand off the faders so he can see I've stopped working. "Take eleven. That was the take. The pitch was honest, the breath was in the right place, you cracked a little on *under* and the crack is the best thing on the track. Come listen."

"It's not there yet." He's pacing the live room, a shadow behind the glass, hands in his hair. "Once more."

I've worked with perfectionists. The studio is full of them; perfectionism is practically the entry fee. But this isn't that. I've been doing this for nine years and I know the difference between a man chasing a better take and a man who has noticed the take is good and is frightened of what comes after good — which is finishing, and releasing, and finding out in public whether the world still wants him.

I press the talkback. I think about my four words taped above the board, and I decide, just this once, to do the thing the rule exists to prevent, which is to actually see him.

"Come into the control room," I say. "Not to listen back. Just come sit down for a minute. The clock's mine tonight; I'll burn it however I want."

He comes. He drops into the chair beside me, and the studio does its small trick of getting smaller, and he doesn't reach for the headphones.

"You don't want to finish this record," I say. It isn't a question.

He's quiet for a long moment. On the monitors, take eleven sits paused, his own voice frozen mid-word.

"Last time I finished something," he says, "I walked off a stage in the middle of it." He says it to the console, not to me. "Forty thousand people. I've never told anyone why. You want to know the truth, Reyna? There's no story. There's no scandal, no breakdown, nothing the headlines would've been satisfied by. I just stood up there in the middle of the song we'd closed every show with for two years, and I realized I couldn't remember the last time any of it felt like mine. So I stopped. Mid-line. And I haven't finished anything since, because finishing is the part where you find out if it was ever yours at all."

I should say something professional. I should steer us back to the track. The rule is right there above my head, nine years old, load-bearing.

Instead I turn my chair so I'm facing him, in the small bright room at midnight, and I say the truer thing.

"Take eleven is yours," I tell him. "I've heard the old files. I've heard the failed sessions, the producers who tried to make you sound like the band again. This isn't that. This take, tonight — the crack on *under*, the breath you didn't fix — that's a man, not a fact of the decade. That's the most yours anything's sounded in four years, and I'd put my name on that mix in front of anyone." I hold his eyes, which I should not do, which I am doing anyway. "You don't have to be afraid of finishing this one. This one's already yours. We're just going to let other people hear it."

Cole Vega looks at me for a long time. The monitors hum. The paused take waits.

"Play it back," he says, finally, and his voice is rough. "Take eleven. I want to hear what you hear."

I press play. And I do not look away from him while it runs, and somewhere around the crack on *under* I understand, with total clarity and no comfort at all, that the rule above my board is no longer load-bearing, and that I am going to have to find out, the hard way, what holds the roof up now.
