The talent is late, which is the first thing I learn about Cole Vega, and the second thing I learn is that he doesn't apologize for it.

I've been at the board for two hours already. Studio C is mine before anyone else arrives — that's how I like it, that's the deal I made when I took this job, the early dark hours when it's just me and the faders and the room learning my hands. I've got the monitors warmed up. I've got coffee that has stopped being coffee. I've got, taped above the console where only I can see it, the rule I have followed for nine years without a single exception: *Never get attached to the talent.*

It's not cynicism. It's survival. Musicians are weather. They roll in, they break your roof open, they roll out, and the smart engineer is the one who built indoors.

At eleven-forty the door opens and Cole Vega walks into my studio like he's checking whether it's still standing.

I know his face the way everyone knows his face. Four years ago he fronted Ravensdown, and Ravensdown was not a band so much as a fact of the decade — stadiums, the lot. And then one night, mid-tour, he walked off a stage in front of forty thousand people and didn't walk back on, and the band ended, and the headlines spent a year guessing why. He never said. That's the thing people can't forgive him for, I think. Not the leaving. The not explaining.

He's smaller in person and harder somehow, all sharp edges and a stillness that isn't calm. He looks at the room. He looks at me.

"You're the engineer."

"Reyna Marsh. I'm mixing the record." I don't get up. I learned a long time ago not to get up. "You're two hours late, Mr. Vega, and I've used them well, so we're actually fine. But I'll need you on time tomorrow, because the room sounds different at eleven at night than it does at nine, and I'd like your ears to learn one version of it."

Something moves across his face. Surprise, maybe. People don't usually open with the truth at him; I can tell, the way you can tell a dog's been shouted at. He was braced for charm or for fear, and he got a schedule note, and it's thrown him.

"They told me you were good," he says.

"They told me you were difficult." I turn back to the board. "So we both come well-reviewed. Sit down, put the cans on, and let me play you what eleven months of someone else's failed sessions actually sound like, because the label sent me the old files and you deserve to hear how far we're starting back."

He doesn't sit down right away. He stands behind me, and I feel him reading the room — the console, the coffee, the tape above the board with my four words on it. I watch his eyes find it. I watch him read it. *Never get attached to the talent.*

"That for me?" he says.

"That's for everyone. It's nine years old. It's load-bearing." I cue the first of the old files, the abandoned sessions, the comeback that's already failed once before I got here. "Headphones, Mr. Vega. This is going to hurt, and then we're going to fix it."

He puts the headphones on. He sits down at last, in the chair beside mine, close enough that the small studio gets smaller, and for the first time since he walked in he stops looking like a fact of the decade and starts looking like a man who walked off a stage four years ago and has not, I think, found anywhere good to stand since.

The music starts. I watch him listen. And I think — with the specific dread of someone who can already feel the weather changing — that nine years is a long time for a rule to hold, and that I have a bad, bad feeling about the next eleven tracks.
