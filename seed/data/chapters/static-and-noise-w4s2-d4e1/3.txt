There's a room at the back of the studio complex that nobody books. The label calls it the quiet room. It's a dead space — acoustically treated until sound just stops, no reflection, no life — and it exists so engineers can check a mix without the room lying to them. Most people hate it. It's unnerving, a room with no echo, a room that gives you nothing back.

I find Cole in there at two in the morning, sitting on the floor against the foam wall, and I understand at once that he didn't come here to check a mix.

"It's the only place in the building that's actually silent," he says, before I can ask. "I've been coming here between takes. Don't tell the label. They'll bill me for it."

I sit down against the wall too. Not close. The kind of not-close that I'm aware of, which is its own answer to a question I haven't let myself ask out loud.

We've been at the record for three weeks now. Eleven tracks, and we've finished four of them, really finished, and each time he's let one go I've watched it cost him and watched him survive the cost, and somewhere in those three weeks the studio stopped being a job site and started being the place where the realest hours of my week happen. I know how he takes his coffee. I know which of his own lyrics he can't sing without his jaw tightening. I know the rule above my board is dead, and I've stopped pretending the tape is still doing anything but holding a memory of who I used to be.

"Can I ask you something," he says, into the dead air, "that isn't about the record."

"You can ask."

"Why the rule. *Never get attached to the talent.* Nine years old, you said. Load-bearing." He turns his head against the foam to look at me. "Rules that specific aren't general. Somebody taught it to you."

The quiet room gives nothing back. No echo to hide a sentence in. Maybe that's why I tell him.

"His name was Daniel. Singer-songwriter, my second year doing this. I mixed his whole first record. I thought —" I stop, and the dead room lets the stop just sit there, no softening. "I thought we were building something. The record, and the other thing. And then the record came out, and it did well, and he got a bigger studio and a bigger engineer and I learned that to him I had been weather too. Just a season he passed through on the way to the next room." I pull my knees up. "So I made the rule. Build indoors. Don't get attached to people who are only ever going to roll out."

Cole is quiet for a moment.

"I'm going to roll out," he says. "When the record's done. That's true. I'm not going to insult you by pretending the studio's forever." He says it gently, but he says it, and I respect him for not lying in the one room in the building where a lie has nowhere to land. "But Reyna — I've been thinking about your rule wrong, and I think you have too. The problem with Daniel wasn't that he left. Everyone leaves a studio; the record ends, that's the job. The problem was that while he was here, you were a *room* to him. A space he used. He never once sat on the floor of the dead room at two in the morning and told you something true."

He shifts, and now the not-close has closed to almost nothing, and neither of us moves to fix it.

"I don't know what happens when track eleven is done," he says. "I won't pretend I do. But I know that you are not weather to me, and you are not a room I'm passing through, and whatever this is — it started before I should let it, and I'd rather say that out loud in here, where it can't echo into something I didn't mean, than keep being careful at you for eight more tracks."

The quiet room holds it. No reflection. Just his sentence and my breathing and the very small distance left between us, which is, I realize, entirely mine to close or keep.

I think about nine years of building indoors.

And then I think about how the dead room is the only place in this whole building that has never once lied to me — and I lean across the last of the quiet, and I close it.
