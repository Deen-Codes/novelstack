He told her she could not use it. She did not leave.

This, Sebastian was discovering, was the central fact of Iris Calloway: she received a refusal the way a river received a stone. She acknowledged it. She moved around it. She kept going.

"You don't understand what this collection is," he said. They had been arguing, in a slow and almost pleasant way, for the better part of an hour, and he had let it go on far longer than he should have, because the library's changed breathing was a kind of warmth and he had been cold for two hundred years. "These are not records, Miss Calloway. A record is something a person makes. These books make themselves. They are — closer to the dead than any source you have ever cited."

"I've worked with human remains," Iris said. "I've worked with the actual bones of the actual poor. I am not squeamish about closeness."

"That isn't the danger I'm describing."

"Then describe the danger you're describing, instead of just looming about it."

Sebastian looked at her for a long moment. The rain had stopped. In the new quiet the library's lamps had warmed without his asking them to, which the library did not do for strangers, and he noted that, and filed it, and chose for the moment not to read what it meant.

"There is one rule," he said at last. "Not mine. The collection's. The archivist may read any volume. A visitor may read one." He held up a single finger, old habit, old ceremony. "One. The library will choose. You do not get to choose. You ask for what you are looking for, and the collection brings you the single book it judges you are owed — and then the threshold closes to you forever. That is the bargain it offers the living. One answer. One door. Are you still interested in your sources now?"

He had expected the rule to frighten her off. It was, he had found over the decades, an efficient and merciful way of clearing his threshold. People wanted everything; they rarely wanted *one thing* badly enough to spend their only chance on it.

Iris Calloway went very still.

"One book," she said.

"One."

She set her heavy canvas bag down on the reading table, slowly, as though it had become breakable. When she looked up her exhausted brightness had gone somewhere underneath, and what was on her face now was older than twenty-eight.

"Then it isn't going to be for my dissertation," she said. "I want you to understand that I came here for the dissertation. Fourteen months. I came here for the unmourned poor of the eighteen-forties, plural, abstract, a thesis chapter." She drew a breath. "But if I only get one — then there's a name. A person. Not from the eighteen-forties. From — closer. There's a name I have been carrying around for a great deal longer than fourteen months, and if your library really keeps the forgotten, then it keeps her, and I would spend my one book on her without thinking about it for a second."

The cold pressure behind Sebastian's sternum had become something else. Something with an edge.

"Who," he said. It did not come out as a question. It came out flat, and careful, the way a man speaks when he is walking on a surface he is not sure will hold him.

And Iris Calloway said the name.

It landed in the long reading room and the library's lamps did not flicker, because the library was far too disciplined for that — but Sebastian felt every volume on every gallery turn its attention, all at once, toward a single shelf in a single cold room three floors down. A shelf he had not climbed to in ninety years. A shelf where, in the worst week of a very long life, he had filed a slim grey book in his own hand and then taught himself, deliberately and completely, to walk a route through the stacks that never passed it.

"You've heard of her," Iris said, watching his face. "You've — oh. You knew her."

"Miss Calloway." His voice was not entirely his own. "Why are you carrying that name?"

And Iris, who received refusals like a river received stones, who had walked past a wall for two years until it became a door, looked at the archivist of the forgotten and said the second thing that would change both their lives.

"Because she was my great-great-grandmother," she said. "And every record of her simply stops in the autumn of eighteen-ninety. No death. No grave. Nothing. As though the city forgot her in a single afternoon." Her voice did not shake, but it cost her not to let it. "And I have spent my whole adult life learning how to read forgotten people, Mr. Archivist — and I think I have just walked into the one building that knows what happened to mine."

Three floors down, in the dark, the slim grey book that Sebastian Vael had written and buried waited on its shelf to be read.

And for the first time in two hundred and six years, the keeper of the library was afraid of his own collection.
