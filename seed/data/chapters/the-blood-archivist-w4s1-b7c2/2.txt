She came in out of the rain at twenty minutes past four, and Sebastian knew before he saw her that she was alive, because the library changed its breathing.

It did this for the living. He had felt it perhaps four times in the last fifty years — a kind of attentiveness moving through the stacks, the particular alertness of a house that has remembered it can be visited. The dead did not visit. The dead arrived, slim and grey and finished. The living were a different and rarer weather.

He found her in the long reading room, dripping, looking up at the galleries with an expression he recognized and had not missed: the expression of someone deciding whether a place is wonderful or wrong.

"You're not open," he said, from the shadow of the stair. It was not quite a question.

She did not startle. He gave her a private point for that. "There wasn't a sign," she said. "Or a door, really. There was a — gap. And then I was inside." She frowned at the galleries again. "I've walked past this building for two years. I would have sworn it was a wall."

"It is a wall," Sebastian said, "to most people. Most people are not looking for anything badly enough."

She turned then and looked at him directly, and he saw that she was perhaps twenty-eight, with the specific tiredness of the overcommitted graduate student — the tiredness that has been spent rather than suffered. Wet dark hair. A canvas bag heavy enough to pull her shoulder down. And eyes that did the thing he had not braced for, which was to look at *him*, a too-pale man in a too-old coat standing in the dark of a library that should not exist, and decide, visibly, that he was the most interesting thing in the room rather than the most alarming.

"I'm looking for something badly enough," she said. "I've been looking for fourteen months. Are you the librarian?"

"I'm the archivist." The distinction mattered to him in a way he no longer expected anyone else to understand. "A librarian lends things. I only keep them."

"That's the most depressing job description I've ever heard."

"Yes," Sebastian said, and was faintly astonished to find that he had nearly smiled.

She crossed the reading room toward him, leaving wet footprints, entirely undeterred by the fact that he had not invited her to. She had the focus of a person who had learned that politeness cost time and time was the only currency she had left.

"My name is Iris Calloway," she said. "I'm a doctoral candidate in social history. My dissertation is about the institutionalized poor of this city in the eighteen-forties — the people the official record didn't bother with. The unmourned. The ones who just —" she opened one hand, a small gesture of vanishing — "stopped, and nobody wrote it down."

The cold pressure stirred behind Sebastian's sternum.

"And someone told me," Iris went on, "an archivist three institutions ago, a very old woman who I think was a little frightened of saying it — someone told me that if I was ever truly desperate, there was a collection in this city that *did* write them down. All of them. Every forgotten person. That there was a place that remembered exactly the people history threw away." She looked up at the galleries one more time, at the shelves climbing into dark, at two centuries of slim grey volumes. "I didn't believe her. I want you to know that. I came here to prove a sweet old woman had lost the thread."

The rain ran down the high windows. The library held its changed, attentive breath.

"And now?" Sebastian asked, very quietly.

Iris Calloway looked around the room that remembered the forgotten, and her exhausted face did something complicated and bright.

"Now," she said, "I think I've just found the only archive that matters, and I think you're going to tell me I can't use it."

She was right about that. Sebastian had every intention of telling her exactly that.

He had not yet learned that she was holding the one name in two centuries of shelves that he would do anything — anything — not to let her find.
