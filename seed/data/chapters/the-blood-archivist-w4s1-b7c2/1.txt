The library does not have a door, exactly. It has a threshold, and a threshold is a more honest thing — it admits that there is an inside and an outside and that the difference between them is a decision you make with your whole body.

Sebastian Vael has been making that decision for two hundred and six years.

He stands now at the foot of the central stair, in the blue hour before the streetlamps decide they are needed, and he watches a new volume settle itself onto the shelf marked with the current year. It arrives the way they always arrive — without sound, without ceremony, simply present where a moment before there had been a gap. A slim book, this one. Grey board covers. Its spine bears no title because the dead do not title themselves; they only stop, and the library remembers them, and the remembering takes the shape of a book.

He does not need to open it to know its weight. He has learned the weights. A long life thinly mourned makes a thick volume of thin pages. A short life mourned by no one at all makes this — a grey slim thing, dense as a stone, the whole of a person pressed down into the smallest space the library can manage.

Somewhere in the city tonight, someone has died, and not one living soul has noticed enough to grieve. And so the library has grown by one, the way it grows by one every time, and Sebastian Vael — who is its keeper, and its prisoner, and the only reader it has ever had — feels the new volume's arrival as a small cold pressure behind his sternum, like a held breath.

This is the work. This is the whole of the work, and the whole of the bargain, and the whole of what is left of him.

He climbs the stair. The library rearranges itself politely around his preferences, the way it has learned to over the centuries — the lamps warming as he passes, the colder rooms drawing back. It is fond of him, he thinks, in the way a very old house is fond of the last person who still lives in it. They are both, the library and Sebastian, in the business of keeping. Neither of them is in the business of letting go.

He takes the new grey volume down. He always reads them on the night they arrive; it is the closest thing to a funeral the forgotten ever get, and he has decided, across two centuries, that someone ought to attend.

He opens it.

A woman. Eighty-one years old. She had a cat, and a particular chair, and a way of folding bus tickets into small precise squares before she threw them away. She had once, sixty years gone, loved a man on a train platform for the length of a four-minute conversation and thought about him, on and off, for the rest of her life. She died on a Tuesday. The cat noticed first.

Sebastian reads all of it. He always reads all of it. When he closes the book the cold pressure behind his sternum eases, the way it eases when a thing has been properly witnessed, and he slides the grey volume back into the gap that has been waiting for it.

"There," he says, to the library, to the woman, to the two hundred years of dust. "Someone came."

It is not grief. Sebastian has not been able to grieve for a very long time — that, too, was part of the bargain, the part nobody warns you about, the part where they take the teeth out of your sorrow so that you can do this forever without it killing you. What he feels is something tidier than grief. Something shelved.

He does not know, standing there in the lamplight with the dust settling, that in nineteen hours a living woman will cross his threshold for the first time in a decade.

He does not know that she will be carrying a name.

He does not know that it will be a name he wrote into this library himself, with his own hand, in the worst week of a very long life — and that he filed it, as he files everything, where he would never have to read it again.

The library, perhaps, knows. The library has always been better than Sebastian at understanding that some books are not finished simply because you have closed them.

It rearranges one shelf, very slightly, in the dark. And it waits.
