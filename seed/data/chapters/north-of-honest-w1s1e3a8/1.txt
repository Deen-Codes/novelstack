The solicitor's office had a box of tissues on the desk, positioned with the discreet professionalism of a place where people came to receive news, and Dana Mercer looked at the box of tissues and thought, with the strange flat clarity that grief had been handing her in pieces all week, that she was not going to need it for the reason the solicitor expected.

She had already done the crying that mattered. Six days of it, since the phone call, since the words *there was an accident* and *both of them* and *I'm so sorry* had rearranged the furniture of her entire life. Cara and Joel — her oldest friend and the man Cara had married — gone in an afternoon, on a wet road, leaving behind a house, an estate, an unbearable absence, and a seven-year-old.

The seven-year-old was named Lily, and Lily was Dana's goddaughter, and Dana had loved her since the hour she was born, and that was why Dana was sitting in this office. That part she had understood. That part she had been braced for.

What she had not been braced for was the door opening, and Sam Okafor walking in.

Eight years. It had been eight years, and Dana's body knew the exact length of his absence before her mind had finished the arithmetic, the way you know a missing tooth with your tongue. He stopped in the doorway when he saw her, and she watched the same recalculation move across his face — the bracing for grief, and then the second, sharper bracing for *her* — and for a moment neither of them said anything at all, in a solicitor's office, beside a box of tissues, while the fact of the last eight years sat down in the room with them.

"Dana," Sam said.

"Sam."

He took the other chair. He sat carefully, the way you sit in a room that has already proven it can hurt you. He had grey in his hair now that hadn't been there, and he looked tired in a way that wasn't only this week, and Dana hated, with a small fierce private hatred, that she noticed these things, that eight years had not made her stop noticing things about Sam Okafor.

The solicitor, a kind, careful woman named Mrs. Adeyemi, looked between them, and Dana saw her register that there was a history here, and saw her decide, professionally, to proceed straight through it.

"Thank you both for coming," Mrs. Adeyemi said. "I'll be as clear as I can. Cara and Joel made their wills four years ago, and updated them once, two years ago, and they were — they were thorough people. They thought carefully about Lily." She opened the folder. "I need to tell you what they decided, and I want to give you a moment with it, because it will be a great deal."

Dana's hands were flat on her knees. Beside her, she was aware of Sam going very still.

"They named co-guardians for Lily," Mrs. Adeyemi said. "In the event that both of them — in this event. They named two people, jointly. They named you, Dana." A pause, and Dana felt the floor of the week tilt, because she had known this was possible, had braced for it, *I'm her godmother, of course, of course I'll —* "And they named you, Sam."

The room went absolutely silent.

Dana did not look at him. She could not, for a moment, do anything at all. *Co-guardians. Jointly.* Cara and Joel had known — of course they had known, Cara had been there for the whole of it, Cara had held Dana through the end of Sam and had also, Dana knew, never entirely stopped loving Sam herself, never entirely accepted that the breakup had to be permanent — Cara and Joel had sat down four years ago, and again two years ago, and had written into a legal document the two people they trusted most with their child, and had not flinched from the fact that those two people had not spoken in eight years.

"Together," Sam said. His voice came out rough. "They want us to raise her together."

"They've asked you to be co-guardians, yes." Mrs. Adeyemi's voice was very gentle. "The estate includes the house, which they intended Lily to remain in — there's provision for that, financially. The will expresses a clear wish that Lily not be moved from her home or her school, that the disruption be kept to the minimum a child can be given in these circumstances." She looked at them both. "I know this is enormous. I know there is — context, between the two of you, that I'm not party to. But I'd ask you to hear the shape of what they wanted before either of you responds. They wanted Lily kept safe, kept home, and kept with the two people who love her most. They believed those two people were you. Both of you. In the same house."

Dana finally turned and looked at Sam.

Eight years ago they had ended it badly — not with a single clean wound but with a hundred small ones, a slow accumulation of things unsaid until the unsaid things were the whole of the relationship, until there had been nothing left to do but stop. She had spent eight years certain she had been right to leave. She had spent eight years, if she was honest, somewhere north of honest, not letting herself examine it too closely.

And now Cara and Joel were dead on a wet road, and their little girl was at home with a babysitter not understanding where her parents had gone, and the two people who loved that little girl most in the world were the woman and the man who had not been able to be in a room together for eight years.

"Lily," Dana said. Her voice did the thing she'd been holding off all week, and she let it, because some things you could not say in a steady voice and should not try to. "Where's Lily right now. Today. While we sit in this office."

"With a neighbour," Mrs. Adeyemi said. "Asking, I'm told, every hour, when she can go home."

Sam looked at Dana. The grey in his hair, the tiredness that wasn't only this week, eight years of unsaid things stacked in the air between them like furniture in a house nobody had decided what to do with yet.

"Then I think," Sam said slowly, carefully, to Dana and not to the solicitor, "the first thing we have to do — before anything else, before us, before any of it — is take that child home. And work the rest out after. If you can stand to."

And Dana Mercer, who had been so sure, for eight years, that she and Sam Okafor were finished, heard herself say, "Yes," and understood that the word had just bound her, for the foreseeable and unimaginable future, to the one person on earth she had organised her whole life around avoiding — and that she had said it instantly, without hesitation, for the sake of a seven-year-old, and that somewhere underneath the grief and the shock a much older, much quieter part of her had said it for reasons she was absolutely not, not today, going to look at directly.
