The long hold of the Hesper had been built for cargo and Anneke Sol had made it a memorial.

Forty names. She had cut them into the bulkhead plating with a torch, one by one, over forty years, and the dates beside them ran from the second year of the voyage to a date nine years gone. Beside each name she had set something small: a cup, a folded shirt, a pair of spectacles, a child's carved animal. The structural lights washed the whole long room in patient amber, and the rings turned, and forty people who had asked for help and not received it kept their quiet rows.

"Read them back to the Adelaide," Wren said to Marrow, on the open channel. "Every one. I want them in the log."

Marrow read them. It took a long time. Ditta, on the bridge, entered each name as it came, and nobody on either ship said anything else while it was done, and when it was finished the Hesper's forty dead were, for the first time in forty years, recorded somewhere other than on a wall.

"Why the torch," Wren asked. "Why cut them into the hull?"

"Because the hull is what survives," Anneke said. "I always knew the ship would outlast me. I wanted whoever found her to find them too. A salvage crew strips a hull for scrap. I wanted them to have to read the names while they did it." She said it without accusation. "I'm sorry. That was unkind of me, to the salvage crew who never came. And then you came, and you read them anyway, and you weren't even angry."

"Why would I be angry?"

"People are, sometimes, when you make them look at the dead. It's easier to take a ship apart if it's only a ship."

Pell's voice came over the channel from the Adelaide, careful. "Captain. I've finished the structural read. The hull's sound — better than sound, she's a clean claim, and I won't pretend the numbers aren't good. But." He paused. "There's a thing you should know before you decide anything. The Hesper's reactor didn't fail. Not the way Anneke thinks."

Anneke went still.

"Say that again," Wren said.

"The reactor was shut down," Pell said. "Manually. Forty-one years ago, near as I can date the lockout. Someone scrammed it from the engine deck and pulled the restart keys. It never failed, Captain. It was switched off."

The amber light hummed. Anneke Sol stood among her forty names with her dirt-creased hands hanging open at her sides, and Wren watched forty years of certainty come apart behind the woman's eyes.

"That's not possible," Anneke said. "We lost the reactor in the second year. Everyone knew. Captain Halloran told us — he gathered the whole ship and he told us the reactor was gone and we'd have to ration for the long drift, and we believed him, of course we believed him, why would—" She stopped.

"Anneke," Wren said gently. "Who had the restart keys?"

"The captain," Anneke whispered. "The captain had the restart keys."

She sat down, slowly, on the deck of the long hold, among the cups and the folded shirts and the carved animals, and Wren let her sit, because there was nothing to say yet that would not be cruel. Forty years. Forty people who had starved by careful degrees through a long honest drift, and the drift had not been honest at all. Someone had switched off their future and told them it had failed.

"I need to see the engine deck," Wren said at last. "Anneke. I'm sorry. But I need to understand what happened on this ship, and so do you, and the only way is the engine deck."

Anneke looked up at her from the floor of the memorial she had spent her life carving.

"Halloran's quarters are aft of the engine deck," she said. Her voice had gone flat and strange. "He sealed them, the year the reactor failed. He said the seal was for safety. We never questioned it. We never questioned anything he told us." She got to her feet, and something in her had hardened into a terrible new shape. "Captain Calloway. I have waited forty years to be rescued. I find I would settle, instead, for the truth. Take me to the engine deck. And then take me to Halloran's door."
