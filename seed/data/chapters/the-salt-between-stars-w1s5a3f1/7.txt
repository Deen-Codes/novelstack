They argued it on the Adelaide's bridge, the four of them, with Anneke patched in from the Hesper's garden, and it was the hardest argument Wren had ever chaired because everyone in it was right.

Ditta was right. "We came out here on fuel we can't replace," she said. "The salvage claim on that hull is the only thing standing between us and a debt that follows us to every port we'll ever see. If we don't strip her, we don't just lose money, Captain. We lose the Adelaide. We lose ourselves. There is a version of being noble that is just a slower way of dying, and I won't have it said I didn't say so."

Pell was right too, from the other side. "If we strip her, we strip the names," he said. "I read the engine log. I had to sit down after. I'm an engineer, I deal in what's true, and the truth is that ship is not scrap, she's evidence and she's a grave, and I don't know how to put a torch to either."

Marrow said the thing nobody wanted said. "There's a third truth. The Hesper's reactor works. Restart her and you don't have a derelict, you have a ship. A sixty-year colony transport, sound hull, working reactor, decades of supplies aboard for the two thousand who aren't there to eat them. That's not salvage. That's a fortune. A bigger fortune than scrap, by an order of magnitude — if you're willing to be the people who profited off her."

The bridge went quiet.

"Say that last part again," Wren said.

"You heard it. A working colony transport is worth more whole than in pieces. Far more. We could claim her, restart her, sell her on at a colony port for enough to buy three Adelaides and never do a hard run again." Marrow shrugged, his usual economical shrug. "It's the smart claim. It's also the claim where forty-one people's grave gets sold to strangers who'll paint over the names. I'm not telling you to take it. I'm telling you it exists, because pretending it doesn't is just letting the hard choice make itself."

Anneke's voice came over the channel, quiet from the garden. "I won't pretend I have no stake. I told you what I want. But I'll say this for the captain to hear, since I seem to be the only one aboard who has watched arithmetic eat a ship." She paused. "Halloran's mistake was not that he did sums. It was that he let the sums decide, and then he hid from what they decided. Whatever you choose, Captain — choose it. Out loud. With your face showing. That is the whole of the difference between you and the man at that desk, and it is not a small difference. It is the only one that has ever mattered."

Wren stood at the forward port. The Hesper hung in it, her rings turning, her open airlock dark.

"Here's what we're going to do," she said, "and I'm going to do it with my face showing, so listen.

"We're not stripping her. Pell's right; I won't put a torch to a grave, and I'd think less of every one of us if we did, and a crew that thinks less of itself doesn't last anyway. So that's the scrap claim gone.

"And we're not selling her whole. Marrow's right that it's the fortune, and Marrow's also right that it's the fortune you can't spend, because the day we hand the Hesper to a stranger we become the next set of people who decided forty-one names were worth less than the arithmetic. I didn't burn eleven hours of fuel to do a quieter version of what Halloran did.

"Here is what the Hesper is worth, and I want it in the log. She's worth the truth. We restart her reactor. We take her engine log and Halloran's statement and the forty names off her wall, all of it, to the nearest registered port, and we file it — every word — as what it is. A colony transport's loss, fully investigated, fully recorded. There's a bounty on resolved missing-ship cases; it's not a fortune, but it's real, and it's clean, and it might just cover the fuel. Ditta, you'll hate it. It's thin."

"It's thin," Ditta agreed. "Go on."

"And the Hesper herself," Wren said, "we don't sell and we don't scrap. We restart her, and we crew her, properly, with people who know what she is. She's a working sixty-year transport with forty years of supplies in her holds. Anneke kept her alive for forty years to be a grave. I think the forty names on her wall would rather she went back to being a ship — a ship that takes people who can't pay for a fast crossing somewhere worth going. We register her as a memorial vessel. The names stay cut in the hull. And the first passenger she carries, when she carries anyone, is the woman who kept her warm."

She turned from the port and looked at her crew, and at the small dirt-handed figure on the channel screen.

"It's the thin claim and the slow claim and the claim that might still cost us the Adelaide," Wren said. "I know it. I'm choosing it anyway. Out loud. With my face showing. Anyone who wants to argue, the record's still open."

Nobody argued. Ditta, after a long moment, only said: "Put it in the log that I think it's a mistake, and that I'm proud of us, and that both of those are still true at once."

"They usually are," Wren said. "Pell. Let's go switch on a reactor."
