Tannhauser was a hard port, and it received the Adelaide and the Hesper the way hard ports receive everything: without sentiment, and with forms.

Wren had expected that. What she had not entirely expected was how long the forms took. The inspectorate kept the engine log for nineteen days. They kept Halloran's statement longer. They sent two officers across to the Hesper to read the forty names off the bulkhead and photograph each one, and one of those officers, a young woman with a very straight back, came back from the long hold and did not speak for some time, and Wren thought better of the inspectorate for it.

In the end the resolved-case bounty came through. Marrow worked the numbers at the bridge table and read them out, and they were exactly as thin as Ditta had promised: enough to cover the fuel burned in the eleven-hour run, and the fuel for the crossing to Tannhauser, and a little over. Not a fortune. Not three Adelaides. Enough to keep the one Adelaide flying and her crew fed and her honest, and not one credit more.

"So we're where we started," Ditta said, studying the figures. "Minus three weeks. Plus a working memorial ship we don't own and can't sell."

"That's about the shape of it," Wren said.

"I want it in the log," Ditta said, "that I think it was a mistake." She let the pause run exactly as long as it needed to. "And that I would do it again, on the same dead band, the same eleven hours, every time. Put both in. They're both still true."

The Hesper did not leave Tannhauser when the Adelaide did. She stayed in dock while the registry processed her new and unusual status — *memorial vessel, crewed, names not to be removed from the hull* — a status the registry had no proper form for and had to invent, grumbling, over the course of another month. Anneke stayed with her. Wren had, as promised, known one or two honest people; word of the Hesper had a way of traveling, and honest people, it turned out, were drawn to a ship like that the way cold hands are drawn to a fire. By the time the Adelaide was loading her next contract, the Hesper had four crew and a waiting list.

Wren went to see Anneke before the Adelaide burned out-system.

The garden ring was greener than it had been. Anneke had her hands in a grow-trough, as she nearly always did, and the white light was steady and strong, and she had set the child's carved animal on the trough's edge where she could see it while she worked.

"You'll want to know what she carries," Anneke said, without turning. "Now that she carries anyone."

"I was going to ask."

"The same thing she was built to carry. People who can't afford a fast ship. People who have somewhere to be and no quick way to get there." Anneke straightened, wiping her hands. "We're slow. We were always slow. But we have decades of supplies in the holds and a reactor that works and forty names on the wall to remind anyone aboard exactly what a captain's arithmetic is worth. I think she'll be a good ship to cross the dark in. I think the forty would think so too."

"They'd be proud of you," Wren said.

"No," Anneke said, and she said it gently, and with certainty. "I don't think it's pride. I kept them forty years because keeping was all I had. You came on the salvage band and you read their names and you wouldn't sell them, and that turned the keeping into something else. It turned it into a ship that's going somewhere." She looked around the green ring, the standing light, the slow turn of it. "That's not pride, Captain. That's just the difference between a grave and a journey. You gave me that. On a dead band, on fuel you couldn't spare, against the advice of a comms officer who was completely right. I won't forget it, and neither will the Hesper."

They said the rest of it the way Wren and Anneke said things — plainly, and not at length. Then Wren went back across to the Adelaide, and Pell warmed the engines, and Ditta plotted the honest unremarkable course to the next honest unremarkable contract, and the freighter burned out of Tannhauser into the salt-cold dark.

Behind them the Hesper stayed at dock a little longer, taking on passengers, her forty names cut bright into her flank, her reactor holding its low enormous note. A slow ship. A good hull. Forty-one souls, and a forty-second who had chosen, at last, to stop waiting and start carrying — out into the dark between the stars, where the lost are sometimes found, and where mercy, when it comes at all, comes with its face showing.
