Halloran's door took an hour to open, and what lay behind it ended the argument none of them had quite been having.

Marrow cut the seal. It was a good seal, a careful one, the work of a man with time and tools and a powerful wish not to be disturbed, and when the hatch finally swung the smell that came out was the smell of forty years of stillness and nothing worse, because the cold of the dead ship had kept everything the way the cold keeps everything.

Captain Halloran was at his desk.

He had been there a long time. He had arranged it, plainly — he had not died of the drift or the dark or the bad luck; he had sat down at his desk with the restart keys in front of him and a written statement under his folded hands, and he had taken something from the medical stores that had let him not get up again. The cold had made of him a quiet grey figure, almost peaceful, a man asleep over his paperwork.

Wren read the statement aloud, because Anneke asked her to, and because some things should be carried by a voice that is not breaking.

It was not a confession. That was the worst of it. Halloran had not written a confession. He had written a defense — a long, careful, reasonable defense, the defense of a man explaining a hard duty to a future he expected to be grateful. He wrote that the reactor failure had presented him with an impossible arithmetic. He wrote that a working reactor would have meant rationing so severe that the whole ship would have died slowly together, and that this was a sentimental death, an unserious death. He wrote that by shrinking the ship's numbers he had ensured that *some* would live, that the colony might still be founded, that the mission might still mean something. He wrote the word *triage* eleven times. He wrote that history would understand him even if his crew could not, and that he had sealed himself away because a captain must not let the people he is rationing see his face.

He had switched off the reactor so the deaths would not be his fault. And then, Wren understood, finishing the statement, he had switched off himself for exactly the same reason — because a man who stays alive to see the end of his arithmetic has to admit it was arithmetic, and a man who dies early gets to call it sacrifice.

"He left the keys," she said. "Anneke. Look. He left the restart keys on the desk."

Anneke looked. The keys lay where the grey quiet figure had set them, forty-one years ago, in plain sight, untouched.

"He could have written it down," she said. "One line. *The reactor can be restarted; the keys are here.* He could have left that for whoever survived. The recyclers, the hydroponics — we could have stopped dying the day he died. I was alive when he died, Captain. I was thirty-one. I had thirty good years of supplies behind that arithmetic that I never got, because he sealed the door and let us think the keys were lost."

"He couldn't write it down," Marrow said quietly. "If he wrote down that the reactor still worked, then everything after the second year was murder. As long as the keys were lost, it was a tragedy."

"Yes," Anneke said. "Yes. He needed it to be lost." She crossed the cold quiet room and she picked up the restart keys, and they were only keys, small and dull, and she closed her hand around them. "Forty people. My whole life. And it was three days' work and a switch the entire time."

She did not weep. Wren had stopped expecting her to. Anneke Sol had done all her weeping in the long hold, among the names; this was something else, something with no tears in it, the cold clean fury of a person who has finally been handed the truth and finds it heavier than the not-knowing ever was.

"Captain Calloway," Anneke said. "I want to ask you for something, and I want you to think about it before you answer, because it is not a small thing and you do not owe it to me."

"Ask."

"Don't strip her," Anneke said. "Don't take the Hesper apart for salvage. I know the hull is your fuel and your debt and your honest claim. I know what it costs you to even hear me ask. But she is forty-one graves and forty-one names cut into her plating, and I have spent my whole life keeping her, and I am asking you not to sell my whole life for scrap." She held out the restart keys on her open palm. "I'm asking you to help me switch the reactor back on instead. And then I'm asking you what a person does, with a working ship, and one passenger, and forty years of someone else's arithmetic to undo."
