It is a particular thing, to switch on a star that has been dark for forty-one years.

Pell did the work and Anneke held the keys and Wren stood in the reactor hall and watched, and it was not dramatic, in the end, because the great machines are never dramatic when they are working properly. There was a sequence. There were checks, and Pell ran every one of them twice, because Pell was the kind of man who would not be hurried into hope. And then there was a sound — low, building, a sound that came up through the deck plating and into the body like the first deep note of an enormous slow instrument — and the amber emergency lights all through the Hesper flickered, and steadied, and turned, one corridor at a time, to white.

The Hesper woke around them. Air systems Wren had not known were silent began, faintly, to breathe. Somewhere a long way off a recycler cleared its throat and got to work. In the garden ring the grow-lights, which Anneke had nursed for four decades on the barest trickle of structural power, came up to a strength they had not held since the second year of the voyage, and Wren — patched through on the channel — heard Anneke make a sound she had not made before, a small broken astonished sound, at the sight of her pale stretched plants standing in real light.

"Reactor's nominal," Pell said. He said it quietly, the way you speak in a place that has just become sacred. "She's a good ship, Captain. She was always a good ship. Nobody ever gave her the chance to prove it."

They spent two days aboard her after that, the Adelaide riding alongside, and they did the slow honest work the choice had bought them. Marrow recorded the engine log and Halloran's statement in full, every reasonable terrible word, and filed copies in three separate places because Marrow trusted no single place. Ditta plotted the course to Tannhauser, where there was a registered port and an inspectorate and a bounty office, and where the thin claim would either save the Adelaide or not, and there was no way to know which until they got there.

And Anneke walked the long hold one last time, alone, by her own request, among the forty names and the cups and the folded shirts and the carved animals, in the white light of a working ship. When she came out she was carrying the child's carved animal — only that, only the one small thing — and she did not explain it and Wren did not ask.

"I've decided something," Anneke said. They were in the garden, which was no longer dying. "You offered me passage. The first passenger the Hesper carries when she carries anyone — you said it would be me."

"I meant it."

"I know you did. That's why I have to tell you no." Anneke turned the carved animal over in her dirt-creased hands. "I am not a passenger on this ship, Captain. I never was. I am the last of her crew. Forty years I kept her warm, and I find I am not ready to be kept warm by someone else. The Hesper needs a steward — someone who knows which corridors lie and which grow-troughs sulk and where the structural power likes to wander. Someone who will not let her become scrap or a stranger's fortune the moment my back is turned. I would like that to be me. I would like to stay."

Wren looked at the small woman in the standing garden and understood that she had never, not once, been offering Anneke Sol a rescue. She had been offering her a choice, which was a different and better thing, and Anneke had taken it and made it her own.

"Then you'll need a crew," Wren said. "A memorial vessel still needs hands. You can't run a sixty-year transport alone, not even you."

"No," Anneke agreed. "I can't. I was rather hoping," and here something almost like humor crossed her face, the first Wren had seen, "that a freighter captain with a thin claim and an uncertain future might know some people. Honest people. The kind who answer calls on dead bands."

"I might," Wren said. "I might know one or two."

The rings turned. The reactor held its low enormous note. And in the garden of the Hesper, in real light at last, the two of them began the slow good work of deciding who the dead ship would carry now that she was alive.
