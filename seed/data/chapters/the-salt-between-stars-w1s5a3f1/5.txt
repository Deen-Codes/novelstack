The engine deck told its story plainly, the way machines do, because machines have no reason to lie and no way to learn how.

Pell came across in the work pod to read it properly, and he walked the dead reactor hall with Wren and Marrow and Anneke, and he showed them where the scram had been thrown and where the restart keys had been pulled from their sockets, and he showed them the maintenance log, which had kept itself faithfully on structural power for forty-one years because nobody had thought to tell it to stop.

The log was not kind to Captain Halloran.

The reactor had been sound. A coolant line had failed in the second year — a real failure, a frightening one, the kind of failure that floods a deck with alarms and sends a crew running. But it had been a repairable failure. The log showed the repair scheduled, parts allocated, a work crew assigned. And then the log showed the work order cancelled, by command authority, and the reactor scrammed by command authority, and the restart keys logged out to the captain's custody, and after that the log showed forty-one years of nothing, because there was nothing left to record.

"He could have fixed it," Pell said. He sounded ill. "Three days' work. He had the parts. He had the people. He could have fixed it and he chose not to, and I cannot for the life of me tell you why."

"I can," Anneke said.

She was standing at the reactor's cold flank with her hand flat against the metal, the way Wren had seen her touch the grow-troughs in the garden, the way you touch a thing you are responsible for.

"Supplies," she said. "We carried supplies for the crossing. The crossing was meant to take sixty years — we were a slow ship, a cheap ship, a ship for people who couldn't afford a fast one. Sixty years of supplies for two thousand people. But the reactor — a working reactor runs the recyclers, the hydroponics at full power, the medical bay, everything. A working reactor means two thousand mouths for sixty years and the sums are very tight. Halloran did the sums. We always knew Halloran did the sums; he was proud of it."

She turned around.

"If the reactor failed," she said, "then it was nobody's fault. And a captain whose reactor has failed can do the only thing left to him. He can ration. He can decide who is essential and who is not. He can shrink two thousand mouths down to a number the supplies will carry, and every death along the way is the cold and the dark and the bad luck of the drift, and not one of them is his."

The reactor hall was silent except for the structural hum.

"There were two thousand of us when we left," Anneke said. "By the second year there were two thousand still — we were a healthy ship, a hopeful ship. By the tenth year there were four hundred. By the twentieth, ninety. Forty-one when I made the recording. He told us it was the drift. He told us it was rationing fairly applied. He gathered us every year and he read the names of the dead and he grieved with us, and all the time the restart keys were in his quarters and the reactor was waiting to be switched back on." Her hand closed slowly into a fist against the cold metal. "He didn't kill us to survive, Captain. He killed us so the arithmetic would be clean. So that he could be a good captain doing a hard duty, instead of a frightened man who'd made a choice."

"Where is he now," Wren said. "Anneke. Where's Halloran?"

"His quarters are sealed," Anneke said. "Aft of this deck. He sealed them the year the reactor failed and I never once wondered why a man would seal himself away from the ship he was shepherding." She started toward the aft hatch, and her step was steady, and that steadiness was a more frightening thing than any amount of weeping. "I think I have always known, somewhere under the part of me that needed to keep the garden alive. I think I have known for forty years exactly what is behind that door. Come with me, Captain. I have waited long enough to stop being wrong."
