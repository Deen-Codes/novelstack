Theo Ashforth turned twenty-five on a Tuesday, and the man came for him on the Wednesday.

There was no party. The Ashforths did not, as a rule, do parties; they did old money and older silence, a tall narrow house full of clocks and a family that communicated chiefly through what it declined to say. Theo's grandmother had set out a single cake the evening of his birthday, and lit a single candle, and watched him blow it out with an expression he had spent his whole life failing to read — and had said, in the small voice she used for the few things she said directly, "There. Twenty-five. We did get you to twenty-five."

He had thought it a strange way to phrase a thing. He thought it stranger by Wednesday afternoon.

The knock came at four o'clock, when the November light was already going. Theo answered it himself — the house had no staff, only the three of them, Theo and his grandmother and the silence — and found a man on the step.

The man was beautifully dressed. That was the first thing, and it stayed with Theo absurdly, that the most frightening event of his life had begun with admiration of a suit: a grey suit, immaculate, cut close, of a cloth that did not look as though it belonged to this decade or quite to any other. The man wearing it was perhaps forty, or appeared to be; he was pale and composed and entirely unremarkable in the way that the most remarkable things often arrange to be, and he was holding a slim leather folio under one arm like a man arrived for a meeting that had been on both their calendars for years.

"Mr. Ashforth," the man said. "Mr. Theodore Ashforth. Twenty-five years old as of yesterday." He smiled, and it was a courteous smile, an apologetic one almost, the smile of someone delivering news he genuinely regretted. "My congratulations. I do mean that. Reaching twenty-five has not, historically, been a certainty for the men of your family, and I am always pleased when it is managed. It makes the rest of the conversation so much more civilised."

"I think," Theo said, "you have the wrong house."

"I have the only house," the man said gently. "May I come in? What I have to say is owed to you, properly speaking — it is, in the truest sense, your inheritance — and it would be unkind of me to deliver it on a doorstep in the cold."

Behind Theo, in the hall, he heard his grandmother's footstep stop.

He turned. She was standing at the foot of the stairs with one hand on the newel post, and her face — the face he had never once in twenty-five years been able to read — was, for the first time in his life, perfectly legible. It was holding grief. Old grief, deep and worn smooth, the grief of a woman who has known a date was coming for a very long time and has just heard it knock.

"Let him in, Theo," she said quietly. "I should have told you years ago. I told myself there was time, and that was a coward's arithmetic, and now there isn't, and he is owed his hearing." She looked at the man in the grey suit with something that was not quite hatred and not quite fear but was certainly older than both. "Hello, Mr. Vale. You've kept your appointments better than we kept ours."

"I always do," said the man called Vale, and stepped, uninvited and entirely welcome, across the Ashforth threshold.

He sat in the front parlour as though he had sat there before. He laid the leather folio on his knees and did not open it, and he waited, with the bottomless patience of a thing that had a great deal of time, until Theo had sat down too, because Theo's legs had stopped offering a reliable opinion on the matter.

"Your family," Vale said, "made a promise, four generations ago. It was made in a hard winter by a man named Edmund Ashforth, who had a dying child and no money and no faith, and who found, very late one night, that there was a fourth thing in the world a man could trade with when the first three had failed him." His voice was low and even and not unkind. "I am that fourth thing, Mr. Ashforth. Edmund's child lived. The Ashforths prospered — this house, the name, the long quiet comfort of you all. None of that was luck. It was lent. And the terms of the loan were very simple, and very old, and they have been coming due, one Ashforth at a time, for a hundred and forty years."

Theo's mouth was dry. "And what," he said, "is the payment."

Mr. Vale opened the leather folio at last. Inside, Theo saw, was not a contract.

It was a portrait. Small, and very old, and the face in it was a young man with the Ashforth jaw and the Ashforth brow — and standing behind that long-dead young man, one pale hand on his shoulder, unchanged, uncreased, wearing the same immaculate grey, was Mr. Vale.

"The payment," said Mr. Vale, "has never been money, Mr. Theodore. I have no use for money. The payment is time — a span of years from an Ashforth life, given freely, spent in my company, learning the things I have to teach. Your great-grandfather gave me eleven years and we parted, I will tell you honestly, as friends. Your grandfather's brother gave me rather more." He closed the folio gently. "I have come to discuss your figure. And I will tell you the one thing the stories always leave out, the one mercy in the whole arrangement, because I think you should hear it from me before the fear decides everything for you." He met Theo's eyes, and his own were patient, and old, and not, Theo realised with a lurch, entirely cold. "It is a conversation, Mr. Ashforth. Not a sentence. The debt is real and it will be paid — but how we spend the paying of it is, very largely, going to be up to the two of us."
