They did not burn it. The contract turned out to matter after all, though not in the way either of them had imagined when they signed it.

Eleanor — the woman in red, the lesson, the reason the document ran to forty pages — resurfaced in the eleventh month, and she did not come alone. She came with a lawyer of her own, and a theory, and a great deal of patience, and the theory was this: that the marriage of Julian Hartstone was a fraud constructed solely to satisfy a clause in a dead man's will, and that a fraud, properly exposed, could undo everything the clause was meant to grant.

She was not entirely wrong. That was the trouble. Eleven months ago she would have been precisely, devastatingly right.

"She can't prove a thing," Julian said. They were in the study, the curtains open now, because Iris had opened them in the spring and refused to let him close them again. "The marriage is legal. We cohabit. We appear together. There is no statute that requires a marriage to begin in love."

"No," Iris said. "But there's a clause." She had the contract open on the desk between them, all forty pages, and she was looking at it the way her father had looked at a frost warning. "Page nine. The one that says no physical relationship is expected or implied. The one that lists this as an arrangement. Julian, you didn't write a marriage contract. You wrote a confession. And it has both our signatures on it."

He went very still. She watched him see it — watched the financier in him do the cold arithmetic and arrive, for once, somewhere he did not want to be.

"If that document is ever read in a courtroom," he said slowly, "it ends me. The inheritance, the firm, the grandfather's clause — all of it gone, and the press would do the rest." He looked up at her, and there it was again, the tiredness, the young unguarded fear. "And I made you sign it. I sat you down and I made you put your name to the thing that can destroy us, and I called it protecting myself."

"You were protecting the man you were eleven months ago." Iris came round the desk and sat on the edge of it, near him, the way she had learned she was always allowed to now. "He needed it. He'd been hurt badly and he built himself a fortress out of paper. I'm not angry at him. I married him." She picked up the contract. Forty pages of a frightened man's architecture. "The question isn't whether she can use it. She can. The question is what we do."

"There's nothing to do. The document exists. It says what it says."

"It says what it said *then*." Iris turned to the last page, to the two signatures, to the date eleven months gone. "Julian. The contract has a term. Eighteen months, beginning to end. We're at month eleven. In seven months it expires — and on the day it expires, it stops being a marriage contract and becomes a piece of history. A frightened man's bad idea that two people grew out of." She set it down. "Eleanor isn't fast. Her lawyer isn't fast. If we can hold the line for seven months — if we can simply *be married*, the real way, in public and in private, with no false note for her to catch — then when the contract expires there is nothing left for her to expose. The fraud will have ended the only way a fraud truly ends. By becoming true before anyone could prove it false."

Julian Hartstone looked at his wife, sitting on the edge of his desk in the room that finally had its curtains open, and something in his face came loose.

"Seven months of being convincing," he said.

"No," Iris said. "Seven months of being us. The convincing part takes care of itself now. That's the whole thing she'll never understand, and the whole thing that saves us." She held out her hand. "Hold the line with me. Seven months. And then the eighteenth month comes, and the contract dies, and we find out what's still standing."

He took her hand. His grip was not careful, the way everything about him had once been careful. It was certain.

"I know what will be standing," he said.

"So do I," said Iris. "But let's make her watch us prove it anyway."
