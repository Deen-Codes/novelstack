Iris had assumed she would not see Julian at breakfast. The contract had said nothing about breakfast. The contract, in fact, had a great deal to say about galas and benefit dinners and the photographs that might be taken at them, and almost nothing about the ordinary hours, the unwatched ones, as though Julian Hartstone had simply not budgeted for a life that nobody was looking at.

So when she came down on the fourth morning and found him at the small dining table with a newspaper and a cup of black coffee, she stopped in the doorway like a woman who had walked into the wrong room.

"You're allowed to come in," he said, without lowering the paper. "It's also your house. For the next seventeen months and three weeks."

"You're counting."

"I count everything. It's a professional condition." He turned a page. "Mrs. Aldous tells me you've been taking your meals on a tray in your room."

"I didn't want to intrude on the treaty."

That made him lower the paper. He looked at her over the top of it, and there was something in his face she had not seen before — not warmth, exactly, but a kind of reluctant attention, the look of a man recalculating. "Sit down, Miss Calloway. Calloway. I find I can't make myself say the other name when there's no audience for it; I hope you'll forgive the lapse." He folded the paper with the same terrible neatness he brought to everything. "If we're going to be convincing in public, we should be at least functional in private. People can tell. The Pembertons especially. Mrs. Pemberton has buried two husbands and reads a marriage the way other women read a wine list."

Iris sat. A place had been laid for her, she noticed — toast, fruit, a pot of the tea she had mentioned, exactly once, to Mrs. Aldous. She wondered which of them had remembered.

"Then we should have rules," she said. "For breakfast. Since neither of us trusts anything that isn't in writing."

"Go on."

"No newspaper when the other person is speaking. No discussion of the vineyard note, the contract, or money before nine o'clock. And you call me Iris, in private, because *Miss Calloway* makes me feel like I'm being audited."

The corner of his mouth did the thing again, the distant cousin of a smile. "Those are acceptable. I'd add one. No questions about my grandfather. Not because I'm hiding anything. Because there's nothing in that subject that improves a morning."

"Agreed." She poured her tea. "That's a strange clause to need."

"Most of mine are." He picked up his coffee, and for a moment they sat in a silence that was, against all expectation, not unpleasant — two strangers learning the shape of a shared table. Outside, the disciplined garden was loosening in the spring, a few of the beds breaking ranks into colour, and Iris thought that the house was perhaps not as afraid of itself as it pretended.

"You set the table for me," she said. "Or you told someone to."

Julian Hartstone looked back at his coffee. "Mrs. Aldous," he said, "uses initiative." But he said it a beat too quickly, and Iris, who had spent four days learning to read the small print of him, made a note, and did not say anything, and let the man keep his secret along with all the others.
