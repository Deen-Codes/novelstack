The Pemberton dinner was a rehearsal. The charity gala, six weeks later, was the performance.

It was held in a museum, among marble emperors and glass cases of antique coins, and four hundred people had paid an obscene amount of money to stand among the artefacts and be seen standing among them. Iris wore a dress the colour of deep water, chosen by the woman who came on Thursdays, and when she had looked at herself in the long mirror of the east wing she had not entirely recognised the person looking back, and had not entirely minded.

Julian met her at the foot of the stairs. He was in black, severe and faultless, and he watched her descend with an expression she could not name, and when she reached the bottom step he said, quietly, "You look —" and then stopped, as though he had walked to the edge of a sentence and found no safe way down.

"Convincing?" she offered.

"Yes," he said, after a moment. "That."

In the car he briefed her like a general. The Hallorans were not speaking to the Wests. The man with the silver beard would try to discuss the valley development and she should plead ignorance, charmingly. There would be photographs near the Roman gallery, and they should be taken there, together, because the Roman gallery had the best light and the firm's communications office had requested it.

"You've turned our marriage into a press release," she said.

"Our marriage *is* a press release. That was always the product." But he said it without satisfaction, looking out the window at the lit city sliding past, and Iris had the strange sense that somewhere in the last six weeks the product had begun, very slightly, to disappoint him.

Inside, it was easier than she had feared. She had grown up in a vineyard; she knew how to make a stranger feel welcome on land that was not theirs. She drew the silver-bearded man into a long, harmless conversation about Roman viticulture and watched Julian, across the room, fail to suppress his surprise. She made Mrs. Pemberton laugh — a real laugh, sharp and pleased — and saw Julian register that, too.

And then, near the end of the evening, by the cases of antique coins, a woman in red touched Julian's sleeve and called him by a name from another life — *Jules* — and Iris watched her husband go entirely still.

"Eleanor," he said.

"You got married," the woman in red said, looking at Iris with frank, unhurried assessment. "Quietly. The Julian Hartstone I knew did nothing quietly."

"People change."

"No," the woman said, almost kindly. "They don't. But sometimes they get better at hiding." She smiled at Iris, a smile with a history folded inside it, and moved away into the crowd.

The drive home was silent for a long time. Iris watched the city give way to the dark of the beech road, and then she said, gently, breaking her own rule about questions, "Who was she?"

"Someone I was going to marry," Julian said, to the window. "Before I learned what I was actually worth to people. She taught me, in the end. It was a useful lesson. It's why the contract is forty pages long." He turned from the glass and looked at Iris in the dim moving light of the car, and for the first time since the lawyer's office he looked not cold but tired, and young, and unguarded. "I priced the inconvenience accurately with you because I have learned not to underpay. But you should know that you have done the job better than I asked you to. Tonight. With the coin man, and Mrs. Pemberton, and — all of it. I didn't budget for that."

It was not a romantic thing to say. It was, Iris thought, the most honest thing he had ever said to her, and it undid something in her chest that she had been very carefully keeping done up.

"It wasn't a job tonight," she said. "Not all of it."

Julian Hartstone did not answer. But he did not look away, either, and the car carried them home through the dark, and neither of them moved to turn on a light.
