After the gala, the frost came back.

Iris should have expected it. She had spent a season learning that Julian Hartstone retreated from anything that warmed him, the way a hand pulls back from a stove — fast, instinctive, before the heat could register as anything but danger. For three days after the museum he was simply not there. He left before breakfast and returned after midnight. The newspaper sat folded at his place, unread. When she did glimpse him, in a corridor, on the stairs, he was perfectly courteous and perfectly remote, a man conducting a treaty with great professionalism.

She told herself it did not matter. The contract had not changed. The vineyard would still be hers in twelve months, and a chilly housemate was a small price for a homecoming.

She told herself this in the window seat, with her father's photograph, and she found that she did not believe it, and that the not-believing had been going on for some time.

On the fourth day she stopped waiting and went to find him.

The study was in the west wing, behind a door she had never opened. She knocked, and when there was no answer she went in anyway, which was not in the contract, and found him at his desk in the lamp-light with the curtains drawn against the afternoon.

He looked up. He did not look surprised, only braced, as though he had known this conversation was somewhere in the building and had simply been hoping it would lose its way.

"You're avoiding me," she said. "I'd like to know which thing I did, so I can decide whether to apologise for it."

"You didn't do anything."

"Then it's something I am. That's worse, Julian. You can't return a thing someone is."

He set down his pen. The study, she saw now, was the only room in the house with a personality — books out of order, a photograph face-down, a chess set with a game abandoned mid-defeat. The room of the boy who had been worth staying for.

"You said something in the car," he said. "After the gala. You said it wasn't a job, not all of it." He spoke carefully, laying each word down like a man crossing ice. "I have spent eleven years building a life in which nothing can cost me what Eleanor cost me. The contract was the architecture of that. Forty pages, so that no part of this could ever become — unpriced. Unaccounted for. And then you said one sentence in a dark car, and I have not been able to put it anywhere in the ledger."

"So you stopped coming to breakfast."

"So I stopped coming to breakfast," he agreed. "It was cowardly. I'm aware. I'm very good at structures, Iris, and very bad at the thing that happens when a structure stops being enough."

The lamp ticked. Outside the drawn curtains the disciplined garden was doing whatever it did when no one watched it. Iris crossed the room — that was not in the contract either — and stopped on the other side of his desk, near enough now that he had to look up at her.

"Here is what I think," she said. "I think you didn't overpay for the inconvenience. I think you overpaid because some part of you wanted to be generous to someone and had nowhere to put it. I think you set the breakfast table that first morning. And I think you've spent six weeks finding out that the marriage worked, and you've never been more frightened of anything in your life."

Julian Hartstone looked at her for a long moment, in the lamplight, in the room with his name all over it.

"That," he said, very quietly, "was not in the contract."

"No," Iris said. "It wasn't. We're going to have to write a new one."
