The eighteenth month arrived the way a long-promised season arrives: slowly, and then all at once, and then as though it had always been here.

They had held the line. It had not, in the end, been difficult, which was the thing that astonished Iris most when she looked back. Eleanor had circled, and probed, and sent her patient lawyer to ask patient questions, and had found — nothing. There was nothing to find. There was a man who came to breakfast. There was a woman who left books with notes in the margins. There was a marriage so entirely unremarkable in its realness that the whole elaborate theory of fraud had simply starved for lack of evidence and quietly gone away.

On the morning the contract expired, Mrs. Aldous brought champagne to the small dining room without being asked, set it on the table, and left again with what was, for her, an extravagant display of emotion: she smiled.

"She's known for months," Julian said, watching the door close. "She knew before we did, I think. She always does."

"She stayed for the boy," Iris said. "She'd recognise the day the boy finally got out."

The contract lay between them on the table, all forty pages, eighteen months old to the day. By its own terms it was now expired. Inert. A piece of history, exactly as Iris had promised — a frightened man's bad idea that two people had grown all the way out of.

"We agreed we wouldn't burn it," Julian said. "I'd like to revisit that."

"We're not burning it." Iris put her hand flat on the cover. "We're keeping it. In a drawer, where we keep things that are over. Because one day, Julian Hartstone, a very long time from now, we are going to be old, and we are going to take this out and read page nine to each other, and we are going to laugh until we can't breathe at the two terrified people who thought a marriage could be kept inside forty pages." She slid it to the side of the table — and then, deliberately, all the way off the precise edge, the way he had once slid it toward her in a glass tower, a lifetime ago. "There. Now it's just furniture."

He laughed. The whole laugh. She would never get tired of it.

"The clause is satisfied," he said. "The firm passes to me next week. My grandfather's sentimental, irrational, entirely correct clause." He turned his coffee cup a slow quarter-turn — an old habit, a structure for a man who needed one, except that he didn't, anymore, not really. "He believed a man with something to lose behaves better. He was right. I have spent eighteen months becoming a man with everything to lose, and I have never in my life been less afraid."

"That's not the contract talking."

"No," Julian said. "There's no contract. There's just me. Sitting here. Telling you that I would like to be married to you on no terms whatsoever — no pages, no clauses, no expiry, nothing to hold the line against. Just the open kind. The kind that's only as strong as the two people in it." He reached across the table and took her hand, certain, uncareful. "Stay. Not because the document says so. Because it doesn't, and you would anyway."

Iris looked at her husband, in the small dining room, with the morning coming in through windows whose curtains would never be closed again, and the expired contract lying off the edge of the table like a thing they had both, finally, outgrown.

"I tore up the old one weeks ago," she said. "I keep telling you. You're always the last to notice." She lifted his hand and pressed it, briefly, to her cheek. "Yes. The open kind. Stay forever. Both of us."

Mrs. Aldous, listening shamelessly from the corridor — she would have admitted it; she had earned the right — went quietly back to the kitchen, and did not bring the tray, because for the first time in eighteen months nobody in that house was eating alone.
