They were married again in October, at the vineyard, in the long dark rows that had started all of it.

It was not a large wedding. Julian Hartstone, who had once turned his marriage into a press release, had discovered in himself a previously unsuspected appetite for privacy, and so there were perhaps forty people on the grass between the vines — and forty, Iris pointed out, was a number with a certain history between them, and they agreed it could stay.

Mrs. Aldous came. She wore a hat. She cried exactly once, briefly and furiously, and denied it afterward with such conviction that everyone agreed to believe her.

The vineyard was Iris's again — had been for months, the note forgiven, the land returned unencumbered, every clause of the original bargain honoured to the letter. That was the strange arithmetic of it, the part that Julian still turned over sometimes in the lamplight: the contract had done everything it promised. It had saved her home. It had unlocked his inheritance. By its own cold terms it had been a complete and total success.

It had simply also, against every projection, failed at the one thing it had been designed to do, which was to keep two people safely apart.

"You're doing the face," Iris said. They were standing at the end of the rows, in the gold October light, in the hour before the small ceremony. "The arithmetic face. We're getting married in forty minutes, Julian. What could possibly still need calculating?"

"I was thinking," he said, "that my grandfather would have liked you. He'd have been insufferable about it. He'd have said *I told you so* from his portrait on the wall every day for the rest of our lives."

"Then we'll turn the portrait to face the garden."

"He'd like the garden too." Julian looked out at the vines, at the valley going gold, at the stone house with its shutters open now, all of them, every window. "I keep waiting to find the catch," he said. "Eleven years of building a life where nothing could cost me anything — and then eighteen months of you, dismantling it, page by page, and I keep waiting for the bill. The hidden clause. The thing in the small print that takes it all back."

"There's no small print." Iris took both his hands. Behind them, somewhere, someone was beginning to gather the forty guests; the light was exactly right; her father's photograph stood on the windowsill of the stone house, squinting into a sun eighteen years gone, keeping an eye on things. "That was always your mistake, my love. You thought the protection was in the pages. It was never in the pages. It was in choosing someone who'd stay when the pages were gone. And then doing it again the next day. And the next." She smiled at him, the man who had not risen from his desk when she first walked into his life. "We'll sign nothing today. We'll just promise. Out loud, in front of forty people, with no expiry date and no exit clause and absolutely nothing to hold the line against — and then we'll do it again tomorrow, at breakfast, when no one's watching at all. That's the marriage, Julian. Not the wedding. The breakfast. Forever."

Julian Hartstone looked at his wife in the gold light at the end of the rows, and the arithmetic face finally, fully gave way — to the whole smile, the undisguised one, the one it had taken her eighteen months and forty pages to win.

"Forever," he said. "On no terms whatsoever."

And then someone called them, and the light was right, and they walked back together through the vines her father had planted, toward the forty waiting faces and the open house and the rest of the unwritten, unguarded, entirely unprotected, perfectly safe rest of their lives.
