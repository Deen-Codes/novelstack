They did not write a new contract. They did something far more dangerous, for two people like them: they left the old one alone, and simply stopped obeying it.

It happened in small mutinies. Julian began coming to breakfast again, and then began arriving early, and then began arriving before Iris, so that the tea was already steeping when she came down. She started leaving books in the west-wing study — her books, with her notes in the margins — and he started reading them, and arguing with the margins, in pencil, beneath her lines. The chess game on his desk, abandoned for years mid-defeat, acquired a second hand. It took them three weeks to finish it. He won, narrowly, and was insufferable about it, and Iris discovered that an insufferable Julian Hartstone was a man she would walk a long way to see.

In the eighth month, he took her to the vineyard.

It was his idea. He framed it as due diligence — the firm still technically held the note, the harvest projections required review, there were entirely sensible reasons for the trip — and Iris let him keep the fiction all the way down the valley road, because she had learned that he needed a structure to stand inside before he could feel anything at all.

It was raining when they arrived. The vines ran in long dark rows under a low grey sky, and the stone house sat at the end of them with its shutters closed, waiting, and Iris got out of the car and stood in the rain and felt her whole chest come apart and put itself back together in a different shape.

"This is it," she said. "This is the thing I signed forty pages to keep."

Julian came and stood beside her. He had a coat that cost more than the car they were standing next to and he let the rain ruin it without appearing to notice. He looked at the vines, and the closed house, and the grey weather coming down the valley, and Iris watched him try to see it the way she saw it — and fail — and keep trying anyway, which was the part that mattered.

"My grandfather," he said, "believed that marriage was a sign of stability. He put it in his will because he thought a man with something to lose behaves better than a man with nothing." The rain ran off his hair. "I always thought he was sentimental. I thought he didn't understand the arithmetic. But standing here — " He stopped. He looked at the house her father had walked out of every morning of her childhood. "I think he understood it perfectly. I think a man with something to lose *does* behave better. I think it's the only thing that has ever made me behave better. And I think I have been lying to you, and to myself, and to a forty-page document, about exactly how much I now have to lose."

Iris turned to face him in the rain.

"Say the rest of it," she said. "I know you have it costed somewhere. Say the rest."

"The rest of it," Julian Hartstone said, "is that I do not want the divorce. I have never, since roughly the third week, wanted the divorce. I want a marriage that no one is watching. I want to keep coming to breakfast for the next forty years and I want it to have nothing to do with my grandfather's will, and I am terrified, Iris, genuinely terrified, because there is no clause anywhere that protects me if you don't want the same thing."

The rain came down on the vines, on the stone house, on the two of them standing in the long dark rows.

"There's no clause," Iris agreed. "There's just me. Standing here. Telling you that I tore up the old contract weeks ago and you simply hadn't noticed." And then, because he still looked braced, still looked like a man waiting to be told the price — she stopped explaining, and crossed the last of the distance, and kissed him in the rain among her father's vines.

When she stepped back, Julian Hartstone was smiling. Not the distant cousin of a smile. The whole thing, at last, undisguised.

"That," he said, "was definitely not in the contract."

"Good," said Iris. "Burn it."
