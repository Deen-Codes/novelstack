On the Ambonel you learned to whisper before you learned to walk, and you learned why before you learned to whisper, because the why was the first thing every child on the ship was taught and the most important thing they would ever know.

Sefa had been an acoustics officer for two years, and a quiet child for sixteen before that, and she had never once in her life raised her voice. Nobody had. The Ambonel was a generation ship three generations into a crossing that would take nine, and for all three of those generations she had run silent — engines damped, hull dead, every conversation a murmur, every footstep soft-soled, every alarm a light and never a sound. The ship did not hum. The ship did not speak. The ship moved through the dark like a held breath, and the people inside her had made themselves into a population that knew how to hold one.

The why was simple, and it was the only piece of the founding logs that every citizen could recite from memory. The first captain, eighty years out, had run the Ambonel the way ships were always run — loud, alive, broadcasting, announcing herself to the void with engines and beacons and the ordinary racket of ten thousand people living. And something in the dark had heard her. The logs did not say what. The logs said only that it had answered, and that it had come, and that the first captain had done the one thing that made it lose interest: she had killed every system that made a sound, and ordered the ship to silence, and the Ambonel had slipped past the thing in the dark because a quiet ship, it turned out, was a ship that was not there.

Eighty years of silence since. It worked. That was the thing Sefa had grown up certain of: the silence worked, the silence was the whole reason any of them were alive, and you did not question a thing that had kept ten thousand people breathing for eighty years.

Her watch was the quiet watch — the long middle of the ship's night, when even the murmuring stopped and the Ambonel was as silent as a ship full of people could be made. Sefa's job in that watch was to listen. The acoustics station fed her every sensor on the hull, and she sat in the dark with the readouts and she made sure the ship was making no sound, because a generation ship is an old machine and old machines develop voices, and a voice was the one thing the Ambonel could not be allowed to have.

That night, in the third hour of the quiet watch, the hull made a sound.

Sefa caught it the way you catch a wrong note — before she understood it, her body already knew. It was faint, far down in the registers, a sound the human ear alone would never have found. But the sensors found it, and the sensors put it on her readout as a slow soft pulse, regular, patient, and Sefa sat very still and listened to it with every part of her training.

It was not the engines. It was not the structure flexing, not coolant, not any of the forty ship-sounds she had memorized as harmless. She ran it against all of them and it matched none.

And it was not coming from outside the hull.

That was the part that made the cold start in her hands. The sensors triangulate; that is what they are for. The slow soft patient pulse was not something in the dark answering the ship. It was inside the Ambonel. Deep inside, somewhere below the cargo decks, somewhere in the old sealed spaces no one had walked in three generations — something was making a sound, down in the body of the ship, and it had been quiet enough until now to never once be heard.

Sefa's hand hovered over the watch log. Protocol was absolute and she had known it her whole life: any unidentified sound, reported immediately, no exceptions, the silence is the why and the why is everything.

But she did not log it yet. She sat in the dark of the acoustics station and listened to the slow pulse, and she found she was thinking a thought that no one on the Ambonel was ever supposed to think, a thought as dangerous as a shout.

The pulse was regular. The pulse was patient. The pulse had the rhythm of something that was not broken machinery at all.

It had the rhythm of something keeping time. Of something that had been down there in the dark of the hull, being quiet, for a very long while — and had only now, on Sefa's watch, decided it was ready to be heard.
