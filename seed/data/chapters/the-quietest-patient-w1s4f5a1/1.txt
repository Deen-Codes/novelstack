My quietest patient never spoke, and for the first four months I considered that the most interesting professional problem I had.

His name was Martin Sole, and he had been referred to me with a diagnosis of selective mutism, which is a tidy clinical phrase for a thing that is rarely tidy. He could speak. There was no physical reason he could not; the referral was clear on that. He simply did not, had not for some years, and somewhere along the chain of professionals who had failed to reach him he had landed in the consulting room of Lena Marsh, which is me, on a wet Tuesday afternoon, and sat down in the chair opposite mine and looked at me with a patience I found, even then, slightly unnerving.

We developed a method. He wrote. I had thought, at first, that we would build toward speech the way you build toward anything in this work, slowly, with trust as the scaffolding — but Martin had no interest in being built toward speech, and after a few sessions I stopped pushing, because a therapist who insists on her own idea of progress is no longer listening. So he wrote. He brought a notebook, a cheap black notebook, and during our fifty minutes he would write, unhurried, in a small even hand, and then he would tear the page out and hand it to me, and I would read it while he watched my face.

For four months the notebook held the ordinary furniture of a damaged life. His childhood. A father I came to understand without ever being told the worst of it directly. The particular loneliness of a man who has decided that words cost more than they are worth. It was good material. He was, on the page, articulate and self-aware and even, in a bleached-out way, funny, and I began to think of Martin Sole as a quiet success forming slowly in the corner of my week.

The notebook changed in the fifth month.

I want to set down exactly how it changed, because I have gone over it many times since and the precision matters. It did not change all at once. The page he handed me that Tuesday began the way his pages always began, with the small even hand and the careful reflection — and then, halfway down, without any shift in the handwriting, without any signal at all, it stopped being about Martin.

It became about a woman.

He described her. He described a woman in her fifties with a particular coat, a particular route, a particular evening habit of walking a particular dog along a particular stretch of canal path. He described the path. He described the hour. He described, in the same small even unhurried hand he used for his childhood, the way the light failed along that stretch of water in the early evening, and the way the woman always paused at the third bench, and the way there was no camera between the second bridge and the lock.

I read it twice. Then I looked up at Martin Sole, and Martin Sole was watching my face with the patience I had always found unnerving and now, for the first time, found frightening, and I asked him — I said it lightly, I made my voice do the easy professional thing — I asked him who the woman was.

He took the notebook back. He wrote one line. He tore it out and handed it to me.

*No one yet.*

I told myself a great many things that week. I told myself it was fantasy, that disturbed and isolated men construct elaborate inner narratives and that the constructing of them is often the safest place for the disturbance to live. I told myself that to treat the notebook as a threat would be to break the trust that four months had built, and to break that trust over a piece of writing would be a failure of nerve and of craft. I told myself that I had a duty of confidentiality and that the duty was not lightly set aside, and that there was, in any case, nothing to set it aside *for* — a description of a canal path is not a crime, a woman who is *no one yet* is not a victim, and a therapist who runs to the police every time a patient writes something dark would have no patients and would deserve none.

I told myself all of that, and most of it was even true, and I said nothing, and I let Martin Sole walk out of my consulting room with his cheap black notebook under his arm.

Eleven days later a woman in her fifties was found dead on a stretch of canal path between the second bridge and the lock, where the light failed early and there was no camera, and her dog was found further along, still on its lead, waiting.

I read it in the local news on my phone, sitting in my own kitchen, and the kitchen went very quiet and very wide around me, and I understood that I was now a person who could not go to the police — because to go to the police was to explain that I had read the plan eleven days before the act and had said nothing, and had let him leave, and the notebook was his and not mine, and I had no proof of anything except my own silence.

Martin Sole had his next appointment with me on the Tuesday. And I knew, sitting in my kitchen with the news still bright on the phone, that he would come, and that he would sit down in the chair opposite mine with his patient unnerving calm, and that he would open the notebook — and that whatever he wrote next, I had already, by my silence, agreed to be the only person in the world who would read it in time.
