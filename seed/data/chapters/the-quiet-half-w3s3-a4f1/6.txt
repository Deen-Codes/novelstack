I did not sleep that night, and Gerald did not come home, and the two facts kept each other company.

In the morning I telephoned my sister, Marion, and asked if I might come for the afternoon, and she said of course, in the voice she keeps for when she has heard something in mine. Marion is two years older than me and has spent those two years, by her own account, being right about people. She had been right about Gerald, in a small way, at the start. She had said, the once, "He watches you the way a man watches a clock he isn't sure he can trust," and I had laughed, and she had let it go, and we had neither of us mentioned it for nine years.

I did not tell her about the room. I had decided that on the drive over, with the poplars going by and the field beyond them. The room was too large a thing to set down on a sister's kitchen table; it would not fit, and Marion would want to telephone people, and people meant the question of the trapdoor would stop being mine. So I told her something smaller and true: that I had begun to think I did not know my husband, and that I wanted to ask her what she remembered.

"Remembered of what?"

"Of before. Of his first wife. You met her once."

Marion put the teapot down. "Once," she said. "At the Aldous wedding. Before your time. Tall woman. Nervy." She sat. "Why."

"He says she was unwell in her thinking."

"Does he." Marion has a way of returning a sentence to you slightly heavier than you handed it over. "She wasn't nervy at the wedding because she was unwell, Eleanor. She was nervy because of him. I remember it perfectly because I remember thinking, that woman is frightened of her own husband and is being very brave about it. And then I forgot, because it wasn't my business, and then you married him, and by then it felt unkind to say."

"Say it now."

"I'm saying it now." She looked at me across nine years of not saying it. "I don't know what happened to her. People said she left. People always say she left. Where does a frightened woman go, Eleanor, when she leaves a careful man? Has anyone ever had a letter?"

I drove home in the early dark and I did not turn at the poplars toward the house. I went on, another mile, to the public box outside the shut chemist's — a Sutton had owned that chemist, I remembered, and there was a file in the room with SUTTON on its spine — and I stood in the cold lit box and I did not, in the end, telephone the police.

I have asked myself a great deal, since, why not. The honest answer, the one that costs me, is this. I did not yet know what was at the bottom of my own stairs, and as long as I did not know, I was a wife with a strange husband and a curious afternoon behind her. The moment I telephoned anyone, I would become a wife who had known, and waited, and the waiting would have to be explained. I told myself I needed proof. What I needed was a few more hours of being a woman to whom this had not, quite, yet happened.

I drove home. The house was dark, and Gerald's car was in front of it, and there was a light on, high up, in the quiet half of the house.
