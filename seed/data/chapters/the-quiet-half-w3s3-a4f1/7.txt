He was in the kitchen when I came in, in his shirtsleeves, making tea, and he turned and smiled at me and said, "There you are. I was beginning to think I'd have to send out a party." And the ordinariness of it was the worst thing that had happened to me yet, worse than the files, worse than the trapdoor, because it meant he did not know that I knew, and that meant the marriage was still running, like a clock that has lost its hands but goes on keeping the time inside itself.

"I was at Marion's," I said. "We lost track."

"Good," he said. "Good. You should see her more." He brought the tea to the table. He had laid two cups. He had, while I was learning what my husband was, set out two cups and warmed the pot, and I sat down opposite him and drank my tea because not drinking it would have been a sentence, and I was not ready to say a sentence.

I watched him over the cup. I had spent nine years not watching him and I made up a portion of it in that one quarter of an hour. He was tired; the city had tired him. There was ink on the side of his right hand, fresh, and I thought of the fountain pen lying parallel to the blotter, and I understood that he had gone up to the room before I got home, that the light I had seen high in the east wing was Gerald adding to a file, and that the file might have been mine, and that the entry might have read: Sat — sister's, returned after dark, agitated.

He had built a room to hold his knowledge of three hundred people, and he had built, without ever raising his voice, a wife who could be summarised in a margin.

"You're quiet," he said.

"I'm tired too." That, at least, was true.

He nodded, satisfied, and that was when I learned the last thing I needed to know about Gerald, and I learned it not from a file but from his face. He was satisfied. He had a wife who was quiet and tired and accounted for, and three hundred people who were afraid of him in a manner so refined it never had to speak its own name, and a house with a quiet half that no one entered, and he was, sitting there in his shirtsleeves with ink on his hand, a contented man. Whatever was at the bottom of the stairs did not trouble his sleep. It had not troubled it for years.

That night I lay beside him and listened to him breathe, slow and even, the breathing of a man who has never once lain awake over what he is, and I made my decision in the dark, fully formed, the way the smell had come fully formed under the door. I would go down the stairs. I had to know the whole of it before I did anything, because a woman who knows half a thing can be talked out of the half; a woman who has seen the whole cannot be talked out of anything. I would wait for the next concern, the next city night, and I would take the green lamp down into the cold, and I would look.

And whatever I found, I understood — lying still, matching my breathing to his so that even my breath would not be a sentence — there would be no afterward in which Gerald and I went on drinking tea from two warmed cups. The marriage was already over. It had ended the afternoon I turned the key. All that remained was to find out which of us was going to be allowed to leave the house.
