The key turned without complaint. That surprised me; I had expected resistance, some final mechanical objection on the marriage's behalf. But the lock was new and well made, and it gave with a single soft sound, and the oak door swung in on a darkness that smelled of cold stone and, underneath that, very faintly, of ink.

I had braced myself, in the four days of the shoe, for a number of things. A woman. That was the obvious dread, the cliché I had rehearsed and half hoped for, because a woman would at least have been a thing I understood. I had braced for clutter, for shame of the ordinary kind — bottles, debts, the soft secret failures men hide. I had not braced for what was actually there, because what was actually there belonged to no category I owned.

It was a room of paper.

The first of the two rooms had been fitted, floor to ceiling, with shelves, and the shelves were full, and they were not full of books. They were full of box files, hundreds of them, the stiff cardboard kind with a metal clip on the spine, each one labelled in Gerald's small upright hand. Not titles. Names. I walked the length of the shelves with my own breath very loud, reading. SUTTON, R. PLEAVANCE, M. CARROW, D. and DAUGHTER. Some names I half knew — a Sutton had owned the chemist's in the town, years back, before it closed. Most meant nothing. There were perhaps three hundred names, and three hundred files, and the room was as orderly as a chapel.

In the centre of the room was a desk, and on the desk a green-shaded lamp, a blotter, a fountain pen lying exactly parallel to the blotter's edge, and a single open file. He had left it open. I understood, looking at it, that he had not expected anyone ever to stand where I was standing, and so leaving it open had cost him no thought at all. The file was labelled, in the upright hand: HALLORAN, E.

E. for Eleanor. Halloran was my name before I married him.

I did not touch it for a long moment. I stood over my own file in my husband's locked room and I felt the floor of nine years tilt very gently, the way the deck of a ship tilts before you have understood that the ship is moving. Then I sat down in his chair, because my legs had stopped being reliable, and I read the first page, and the first page was a list of every place I had been in the previous month, with times, and small dry observations in the margin. Tues — chemist, 11 min, bought nothing. Thurs — sister's, returned 4.40, agitated.

He had been keeping a file on me. He had been keeping a file on me, and on three hundred other people, in a locked room he had hired quiet men to build, and somewhere in this calm and careful house was the reason, and I was going to have to find it before his car came back down the drive and turned at the poplars and brought him home to the wife who now, at last, had started to know him.
