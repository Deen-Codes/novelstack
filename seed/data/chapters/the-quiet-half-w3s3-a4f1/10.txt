They found her in the second week. The newer brick gave the panel away within an hour of the first constable going down the stone stair, and after that it was no longer my account to tell; it became the county's, and then the country's, for a while, in the way these things do. The woman before me had a name — she had always had a name, it was only Gerald who had filed her under a category — and the name was Vivienne, and she had been thirty-eight years old, and she had not, after all, left him. Marion had been right at the Aldous wedding. She had been a frightened woman being very brave about it, and the bravery had not been enough, because bravery rarely is, against the careful kind.

The room of paper did the rest. Three hundred files, three hundred names, three hundred small economies of fear, and every one of them a door that the law could now open. Some of the people in those files wept, I am told, when the constables came — not from fear, that time, but from the particular relief of a thing finally being said aloud after years of paying to keep it silent. A Sutton was among them. The chemist's might even reopen. I like to think about that more than I like to think about the cellar.

Gerald did not run. I had half expected him to; I had pictured the car not turning at the poplars at all, going straight on, a careful man becoming a small grey dot on a road. But he came home. He saw the lamp burning in the window of the quiet half — a neighbour, walking a dog, saw him stop the car in the drive and simply sit in it, for a long time, with the engine off, looking up at the light. And then he got out, and went into the house, and was sitting in his own kitchen, quite composed, with the kettle filled for two, when they came for him in the morning. He had warmed the pot. I find I cannot stop returning to that. To the end, two cups.

They asked me, the patient men who asked me things for many weeks afterward, why I had not telephoned them the first afternoon, the afternoon of the key. Why I had waited. Why I had driven to my sister's and hidden documents in a coal scuttle and lit a lamp in a window like a woman in a story rather than a woman with a telephone. I told them the truth, which was that I had needed to know the whole of it first, because a woman who knows half a thing can be managed, and I had read the word manage in my own husband's hand and I had decided, kneeling on his cold cellar floor, that I would not be.

I do not live at Wraydon now. The house was sold; I could no more have slept in it than slept in a courtroom. I have a flat, small, with three good windows and one door, and the door has a lock, and the key to that lock hangs on a hook by the door where anyone who visits me can see it and take it down and let themselves out whenever they please. I find I need that. I need a house with no quiet half.

People who hear the story — and people do still hear it — almost always ask the same thing last. They ask whether I ever truly knew him at all. And I have learned to give them the answer that is kind to them, which is no, of course not, how could I, he was a monster wearing a husband.

But that is not the true answer, and you have had the true account from me, so you may as well have the true answer to finish it. I did know him. That is the thing I have had to carry. I knew he was careful, and I knew he kept things in case, and I knew his first wife had been unwell in her thinking, and I knew there was a smell under a door he had hired quiet men to build. I knew all of it. I simply, for nine years, declined to look at what I knew, because looking is work, and the not-looking had a garden and a warmed pot and a man who held a coat as though the woman in it mattered.

So when you find your own locked door — and most houses have one, most marriages, most lives — I will not tell you to be brave. Bravery is Vivienne's, and look. I will tell you only this, which is smaller and harder and the single thing I am now certain of: look early. Look while the smell is still faint and the file is still thin and the brick is not yet laid. Look while looking is only curiosity and not yet survival.

That is the whole of it. The lamp is out now. The house is someone else's. And I keep my own key, as you see, on a hook, in the light, where it belongs.
