The list, when I finished it, had four items, and I am not going to pretend to you that they were the items an innocent woman writes.

The first was: telephone Sergeant Pell from the box, not the house. The second was: take the file marked HALLORAN, and three others at random, to Marion's and leave them there. The third was: do not be in the house when the car turns at the poplars. The fourth I have never told anyone, and I will tell you now only because this account is worth nothing if it is not the whole of it, and the fourth item said: be ready to be disbelieved.

Because that was the trap of him, you see. That was the genius of the careful man. Gerald had spent nine years building me into a wife who could be summarised, and a summarised wife is a wife nobody quite hears. He had a first wife who had been "unwell in her thinking" — and if I went to the police with a story about files and a bricked cellar and a smell, what was I but a second wife who had become unwell in hers? He had laid that brick too. He had been laying it across two marriages. The cellar was only the part of it you could touch.

So I did not do the brave clean thing, the thing the law would have wished of me. I did not telephone Sergeant Pell that afternoon and stand in my kitchen waiting to be believed. I did the careful thing, because nine years married to a careful man teaches you, against your will, to be careful. I drove the four files to Marion's and I put them in her hands and I said, "Do not open these, and do not lose them, and if anything happens to me, give them to a solicitor and not to a policeman, and tell him to start with the cellar at Wraydon and the woman before me." And Marion, who had spent two years being right about people and nine years being silent about it, did not ask a single question. She took the files. She held my face in her two hands for a moment. Then she put the files in her own coal scuttle, under the coal, and we did not speak of them again.

I telephoned the police from the box outside the shut chemist's, in the last of the light, and I told a young constable, plainly and slowly, that I believed there were human remains bricked into the cellar of my house, and that my husband had put them there, and that he would be home by morning, and that I would be at my sister's, and that there were documents. I made him read it back to me. I made him say the word documents twice.

And then I did the thing I am least proud of and least sorry for. I went home, to the empty house, alone, in the dark, and I went up to the quiet half, and I unlocked the oak door, and I took the green-shaded lamp off the desk, and I set it burning in the window of the east wing where it could be seen from the drive — so that when Gerald's car came back, and turned at the poplars, the careful man would see, before anything else, before he was even out of the car, a light on in the room that nobody was ever to enter.

I wanted him to know, in the length of the drive, that it was over. I wanted him to have those few hundred yards of knowing. It was the only cruelty available to me and I took it, and I have decided to forgive myself for it, because a woman is allowed one.

Then I drove to Marion's, and I sat in her kitchen, and we waited for the morning, and the morning was a long time coming.
