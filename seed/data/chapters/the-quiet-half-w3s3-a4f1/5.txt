I gave myself rules, sitting in his chair. I have always worked better inside rules. I would touch nothing I could not return to its exact position. I would not take anything out of the room. I would read until I understood, or until the light began to go, whichever came first, and then I would lock the door and put the key in the shoe and be, by the time he came home, a wife again.

The rules held for about forty minutes.

The files were not, as I had first wildly assumed, simple records of where people went. They were thicker than that. Each one began with a sheet of facts — a name, an address, a marriage, children, a profession — and then ran on into pages of observation, and the observations circled, always, the same drain. Money. Who had it. Who had recently lost it. Who had a weakness, a debt, a habit, a child in trouble, a reputation that would not survive daylight. Gerald's margins were full of small assessments, and the assessments were the coldest writing I have ever read, because there was no cruelty in them. Cruelty would have been a kind of company. There was only accountancy. Vulnerable from spring. Wife's illness — leverage. Will pay to keep son's matter quiet, est. considerable.

I understood, somewhere in the second hour, what my husband did. He had told me, over nine years, that he had investments and concerns. He did. His investment was other people's fear, and his concern was making sure it kept its value. He found people at the worst hinge of their lives and he learned the one fact they could not afford to have known, and then he did not, usually, even have to say anything. The knowing was enough. The knowing, gently demonstrated, was the whole transaction. The trips to the city were collections. The petrol receipts, kept in case, were the careful man building his alibi for journeys he might one day need to account for.

And the smell. I found the smell in the second room.

The second room was smaller and colder and had no shelves. It had a trapdoor. Gerald had told me, nine years ago, that the cellar was unsafe and the door to it had been boarded over downstairs — and so it had, the door I knew. But a man who builds a private kingdom builds himself a private stair into its lower floor. The trapdoor was in the corner under a rug, and it was not locked, because he had not imagined a need, and when I lifted it the cold well-smell came up around me like a held breath finally let go.

I did not go down. I want to be clear about that, because of everything that came after, and because I have had to live inside this account ever since. I knelt at the edge of the open trapdoor with the green lamp's light not reaching the bottom of the stair, and I looked down into the dark of my own house, and I smelled stone and cold and, threading through it now, faint and unmistakable and entirely new, something that was not stone and not cold and not ink.

I closed the trapdoor. I put the rug back, corner to corner, exactly. And I went and sat in his chair again, and I found that my hands had stopped shaking, which frightened me more than the shaking had, because steady hands meant some part of me had already moved past surprise and into the next thing, and the next thing was a question with only ugly answers. Whose smell was that. And how long had my husband kept it.
