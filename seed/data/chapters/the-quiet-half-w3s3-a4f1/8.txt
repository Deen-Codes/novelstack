The concern came nine days later. He told me at breakfast, folding the newspaper along its crease. Two nights this time, possibly three; a matter in the city that had grown legs. He kissed my cheek in the hall. The car went down the drive and turned at the poplars, and I stood and counted to two hundred after the sound of it had gone, because I no longer trusted that a thing was over simply because I could not hear it.

Then I fetched the green lamp's spare bulb, and a torch, and Gerald's own gardening gloves, and I went up to the quiet half of the house.

I had thought, in the nine days, that I had prepared myself. I had not. You cannot prepare for a cellar; you can only enter one. The stair was stone and turned once, and the cold came up to meet me step by step, and the torchlight went ahead of me into a low brick room that was larger than I had expected and emptier, and for one swung moment of pure relief I thought: nothing. There is nothing here. He is only a careful man with a strange hobby and a damp cellar, and I have built a monster out of a smell.

Then the torch found the far wall, and the far wall had been bricked.

Not the whole wall. A panel of it, perhaps the width of a doorway, where the brick was a shade newer and the mortar had run in places, the work of a man doing in private a job he could not hire quiet men for. And in front of the new brick, on the cold floor, was a chair, an ordinary kitchen chair, set facing the panel as if someone had sat there, more than once, and looked at it. There was a film of dust on everything in that cellar except the seat of the chair.

I did not break the wall down. I had brought no tool for it and I think, even had I brought one, I would not have. I had come to know, and I knew. The smell was here and it was strongest here and it came, faint and terrible and old, from behind a panel of brick that my husband, who folded his newspapers and kept his receipts in case, had laid with his own hands and then sat in front of, on a kitchen chair, in the dark, for what I understood now were years. Not in guilt. I had given up the idea of his guilt. In accountancy. He had come down here to confirm that an asset was still secure. Wife's matter — quiet. Est. permanent.

I went back up the stone stair into the room of paper, and I sat in his chair one last time, and I took out, from the shelf, the file marked HALLORAN, E., and I read it to the end, all of it, every cold margin of the woman I had been while I thought I was being a wife. And on the last page, in the upright hand, recent, the ink barely a fortnight old, I found the line he had written after Marion's, after the dark drive, after the night I had matched my breathing to his.

It said: Has begun to look. Manage.

He knew. He had known since the night of the two warmed cups. He had sat across the kitchen table from me, satisfied, and he had not been satisfied at all; he had been a careful man assessing a new vulnerability, and the vulnerability was me, and the margin note was not a description. It was an instruction. To himself. Manage.

I closed the file. I did not put it back on the shelf. For the first time since I had found the key, I broke a rule, and the breaking of it was the calmest thing I had ever done. I carried the file downstairs to the kitchen, and I sat at the table where the two cups had been, and I began, in my own hand, which is small but not upright, to make a list. Because lists are how I manage myself, and I had, at last, something to manage.
