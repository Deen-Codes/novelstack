# NovelStack Originals — Plan

*Foundation for the first five books we write and publish ourselves. Section 1 is a cross-platform research playbook so the writing has a real model. Section 2 is five distinct concepts (logline → arc → chapter beats). Section 3 is the ChatGPT-Plus cover prompt for each.*

---

## §1. Cross-platform research — what actually works in serialised fiction

### 1.1 Wattpad

**Reader base:** young (~70% under 25), heavily female-skewing, mobile-first. Most-read tropes are repetitive on purpose — readers come back for variations of the same emotional beat.

**The Wattpad book that wins:**
- Hook in the first 400 words. Open in the middle of a scene with tension already loaded (an argument, a near-miss, a confession interrupted). Avoid prologues that explain backstory.
- **Tropes you can build on:** enemies-to-lovers, fake dating / fake marriage, billionaire / CEO romance, werewolf-mate, vampire-mate, "the chosen one" YA fantasy, rejected-mate, second-chance romance, secret-baby. These aren't ideas — they're container shapes that let the reader know what feeling they're buying.
- **Chapter length:** 1,000–2,500 words. Tighter than feels comfortable. Long enough for one emotional beat, short enough that the reader thinks "one more."
- **Cliffhanger placement:** every chapter ends with a beat that demands the next — a kiss interrupted, a reveal, "and then I saw who was standing in the doorway." Wattpad readers tap "next chapter" 4–8 times in a session.
- **Title pattern:** possessive + descriptor. *His Forgotten Mate. The Alpha's Surrogate. Married to the Mafia King.* The title pre-sells the trope.
- **Author voice:** intimate first-person, present-tense common. The reader is supposed to be the narrator's confidante.
- **What kills a Wattpad book:** literary opening with no plot for two chapters; slow burn that takes 15 chapters to land the first real beat; third-person omniscient narration that creates emotional distance.

### 1.2 Royal Road

**Reader base:** older than Wattpad (~25-40 median), male-skewing 70/30, desktop + mobile mix. They want progression: the protagonist starts weak/lost and ends strong/known. The arc is the product.

**The Royal Road book that wins:**
- **Genres that dominate:** progression fantasy, LitRPG (Lit-Role-Playing-Game — system mechanics in the prose), cultivation, post-apocalyptic / dungeon-crawler, isekai (modern person dropped in fantasy world). 80% of top-rated stories sit in these.
- **Chapter length:** 2,500–5,000 words. RR readers tolerate longer because they're at desk or actively reading-as-hobby.
- **Hook:** System awakens / world ends / protagonist dies and is reborn — the first chapter establishes the rules of the world AND introduces the system the protagonist will master. Inventory screens, stat blocks, level-up notifications rendered as in-text boxes are normal and welcomed.
- **Pacing rhythm:** weekly chapter drop. Top authors do 2-3 chapters per week, building a backlog so they're 5+ chapters ahead on Patreon (paying readers get early access).
- **Cliffhanger:** less about emotional beats, more about discovery — "and then I learned what was actually in the dungeon." Mystery and revelation over romance.
- **Title pattern:** descriptive multi-part. *The Wandering Inn. Beware of Chicken. Mother of Learning. He Who Fights with Monsters.* Often a phrase that hints at the central mechanic.
- **What kills a RR book:** romance plotline that displaces the progression; literary prose that slows the pace; protagonist who doesn't actively pursue power/skill/agency.

### 1.3 Webnovel

**Reader base:** global, with heavy SE Asian + Chinese diaspora readership. Translated Chinese web novels (cultivation, system, reincarnation) dominate.

**The Webnovel book that wins:**
- **Genre lock:** cultivation (martial arts power progression with mystical-energy systems), system-novels (gamified mechanics — quests, levels, stats), reincarnation / transmigration (modern person becomes a character in a novel they read), urban fantasy.
- **Chapter length:** 1,000–1,800 words — shorter than RR because coin-unlock walls hit every 5-10 chapters, so authors slice their chapters thin to maximise coin spend.
- **Hook:** death and rebirth in chapter one. Almost universally — protagonist dies (humiliated, betrayed, falsely accused) and wakes in a new body / world / time with knowledge of the old life. The "knowing what's coming" gives them an edge over everyone else.
- **Pacing rhythm:** daily chapters (sometimes 2-3 per day) for top-tier monetised authors. The treadmill is brutal.
- **Title pattern:** epic + role + descriptor. *Lord of the Mysteries. Reverend Insanity. Versatile Mage. I Shall Seal the Heavens.*
- **What kills a Webnovel book:** Western literary pacing; introspective protagonist; resolutions that don't escalate (each arc must end on more powerful ground than it started).

### 1.4 Substack

**Reader base:** adult (~28-50 median), educated, willing to pay $5-15/mo for a publication. Reading on email + desktop. They want a writer's voice as much as a story.

**The Substack serialised fiction that wins:**
- **Genres:** literary fiction, autofiction, "smart" genre (literary thriller, literary horror, literary romance), essay-adjacent fiction, climate fiction (cli-fi), speculative literary.
- **Chapter length:** 1,500–3,500 words. Designed to be a satisfying email open — long enough that the reader feels they got a real thing, short enough they actually read it.
- **Hook:** voice-first. The opening sentence sets a register the reader either falls into or bounces from. "All this happened, more or less." (Vonnegut). The plot is secondary on Substack — voice + atmosphere come first, plot earns its place over chapters.
- **Pacing rhythm:** weekly. Sunday morning is the slot. Some serialised authors release on Friday evening (a "weekend novel" register).
- **Title pattern:** aphoristic or single-image. *Small Things Like These. A Visit from the Goon Squad. Outline. Lessons in Chemistry.*
- **What kills a Substack book:** mass-market genre prose; chapters that don't stand alone (a Substack chapter has to satisfy as a single email, while still pulling toward the next); pacing that assumes the reader will binge.

### 1.5 AO3 (Archive of Our Own)

**Reader base:** adult fandom-literate, ~75% female + nonbinary. Reads in long sittings, willing to commit to 100k+ word fics.

**The AO3 book that wins:**
- **Genre is tag-driven.** Stories aren't browsed by category — they're filtered by tag combinations. The tag list IS the marketing. (`Hurt/Comfort`, `Slow Burn`, `Found Family`, `No Beta We Die Like Men`, `Friends to Lovers to Enemies to Lovers`.)
- **Original fiction works on AO3** but it's a small minority of traffic. The platform's gravity is fanfiction.
- **Chapter length:** wildly variable — anywhere 1k to 15k. AO3 readers don't care because the unit is the work, not the chapter.
- **Hook:** depends on tag. Slow-burn readers tolerate 30k of UST (unresolved sexual tension) before the first kiss; gut-punch readers want the punch by chapter 2.
- **What kills an AO3 book:** badly tagged or untagged; missing trigger warnings; treating it like a Wattpad clone with short chapters and cliffhangers (AO3 readers want immersion, not gamification).

### 1.6 Tapas

Comics-first platform with a novels sibling. Novels there inherit the wait-for-free coin economy. Reader base similar to Wattpad but more comics-and-romance-coded. The novels that win are romance, paranormal romance, and "isekai" — wait-for-free pacing means short snackable chapters.

### 1.7 Cross-platform synthesis — what travels

If you want a book that could plausibly do well anywhere:

- **Voice in the first 100 words.** Wattpad gets the hook, RR gets the world rules, Substack gets the prose register. The first paragraph has to land all three signals simultaneously.
- **Chapter unit of ~2,500 words** is the safe-everywhere length. Wattpad readers can chunk it, RR readers find it substantial, Substack readers can read it in one email sitting.
- **Cliffhanger every chapter, but soft cliffhangers** — a question raised, not always a literal interruption. Hard cliffs feel cheap to Substack readers; soft cliffs feel under-engineered to Wattpad readers. The middle ground is "a beat that demands resolution."
- **One trope, executed well.** Don't try to be six things. Pick the container (enemies-to-lovers, system-awakening, slow-burn-second-chance, etc.) and execute the conventions confidently.
- **Title that pre-sells the genre.** Vague poetic titles work on Substack but die in a Wattpad search.
- **First three chapters are the gate.** Every reader on every platform decides whether to commit after chapter three. The first three have to: introduce the protagonist's want, establish stakes, deliver a turning point.

## §2. Five NovelStack Originals — concepts

Five distinct registers so no two books overlap on genre, voice, audience, or chapter rhythm. Each is built to look credible on the platform it most resembles, while staying NovelStack-native.

---

### Book 1 — *The Salt-Wind Almanac*

**Register:** Cosy literary slow-burn (Substack / cosy-Wattpad crossover).

**Logline:** An archivist in a half-drowned coastal town catalogues the things the tide brings back to her — letters, photographs, a single child's shoe — until the seventh letter arrives in her mother's handwriting. Her mother has been dead for nineteen years.

**Audience:** Adults who read literary fiction and like a domestic-feeling mystery. Substack / Lessons-in-Chemistry / Small Things Like These readers.

**Voice:** Third-person close past tense, narrated from Marin's POV. Sentence-level: Newsreader-paced. Short paragraphs, lots of negative space.

**Length:** 12 chapters, ~2,500 words each. ~30k words. Novelette.

**Cadence:** Weekly. Sunday-morning slot.

**Arc:**
- **Act I (Ch 1–4):** Marin's small life in Carrow Quay — the archive, the daily walk along the seawall, the gulls, the increasingly strange items the tide brings. She files them. The seventh letter, in her mother's hand, breaks the routine.
- **Act II (Ch 5–9):** Marin reads the letter and starts hunting — the lighthouse keeper, the harbourmaster's wife, the postmistress who's been there forty years. Each holds a fragment of what happened the winter her mother left. The deeper she digs, the more she realises the town has been keeping a small communal secret.
- **Act III (Ch 10–12):** Marin finds the box at the foot of the slipway. What's inside is small and devastating: her mother didn't die in the way she was told. The almanac was always for her. Last chapter is quiet, lands on Marin standing on the seawall at dawn with the box in her hands.

**Chapter-by-chapter:**
1. *A small song for the harbour* — first letter (already drafted in the reader preview).
2. *Correspondence, salt-damaged* — Marin's archive routine + second letter + the postmistress notices.
3. *The lighthouse keeper, Mr Ainslie* — interview, opaque answers, hints of a winter long-time-ago.
4. *Seventh* — the letter in her mother's hand.
5. *What the harbourmaster's wife remembers* — fragment of the night her mother left.
6. *A photograph from the Almanac* — Marin's mother on the slipway, with a child who isn't Marin.
7. *Hatherly's bookshop* — the shopkeeper who used to print the almanac. Quiet revelation that there's a previous almanac.
8. *The previous almanac* — Marin reads it. Begins to see the shape.
9. *The night her mother left* — full reconstruction.
10. *The box at the slipway* — found.
11. *Open the box* — devastating reveal: her mother went into the water deliberately, but to save someone. The almanac is her mother's account of who.
12. *Almanac for the next archivist* — quiet, hopeful close. Marin starts her own almanac.

**Tone notes:** sea air, salt on lips, gulls on cathedral roofs, late-winter quiet. Avoid melodrama. Land each chapter on an image, not a cliff.

### Book 2 — *Threshold Protocol*

**Register:** LitRPG / progression fantasy (Royal Road-native).

**Logline:** On the morning the system arrives, Kit Aldrich is in the wrong meeting room. Everyone else gets a class. He gets an error message and a single line: *Threshold protocol pending. Survive 72 hours.* He has no class, no skills, no map — and 8 billion people who do.

**Audience:** RR readers, He-Who-Fights-with-Monsters / Beware-of-Chicken / Mother-of-Learning crowd. Adult LitRPG.

**Voice:** First-person present. System notifications rendered as boxed text. Stat blocks at chapter ends.

**Length:** Open-ended serial, but plan 20 chapters for v1, ~4,000 words each. ~80k words.

**Cadence:** 2–3 chapters per week.

**Arc:**
- **Act I (Ch 1–6):** System arrives. Kit is left without a class. First 72 hours: he survives by improvising — using his nothing-status as cover, because everyone with a class becomes a target. The system rewards him for surviving in unconventional ways. A "shadow class" forms.
- **Act II (Ch 7–14):** Kit learns what the threshold protocol actually is — the system isn't a gift, it's a culling mechanism with rules. People with classes are being levelled to feed an apex tier. Kit's class-less status becomes an exploit, and other unclasseds find him.
- **Act III (Ch 15–20):** Kit organises an underground of unclasseds. They begin to game the system from below. Last chapter: the system notices.

**Chapter-by-chapter (first 6):**
1. *The Tuesday the System Came* — Kit at the meeting. Everyone notified. He isn't.
2. *Threshold Protocol Pending* — the single line. The first hour. The first hostile player.
3. *Nothing-Stat* — Kit realises he has no class panel. Discovers a hidden "Threshold" tab.
4. *The First Reward* — survives 24h. Gets a strange unclassified reward. Reads it.
5. *What the System Doesn't Want* — meets another unclassed. They compare notes.
6. *48 Hours* — the threshold protocol updates. Stakes raised. Cliff: the protocol mentions him by name.

**Tone notes:** brisk, observational, with enough character to feel like a person — not a stat-block avatar. Reference the system architecture in chapter titles. Hard cliffs at chapter ends.

### Book 3 — *Wrong Number*

**Register:** Contemporary office romance, enemies-to-lovers (Wattpad-adjacent for adults, Tessa Bailey / Emily Henry register).

**Logline:** Maeve Doyle texts her sister to complain about her new boss. Auto-correct hits the wrong contact. Her boss texts back: *I am, in fact, six-foot-two. The rest is debatable.*

**Audience:** Wattpad romance + BookTok crossover. Adult contemporary romance.

**Voice:** Dual POV, alternating chapters, first-person present. Maeve's voice: tart, self-deprecating. Sam's: dry, deliberate.

**Length:** 14 chapters, ~2,200 words each. ~30k words.

**Cadence:** Twice-weekly.

**Arc:**
- **Act I (Ch 1–4):** Wrong-number text. Two weeks of escalating banter via text only — Maeve doesn't realise it's still going through Sam's other number. Sam realises immediately but doesn't tell her.
- **Act II (Ch 5–10):** Maeve discovers. Furious. Sam offers to delete the thread. She doesn't let him. They start meeting in person, awkwardly. Pressure mounts at work. First kiss in chapter 8, panic in chapter 9.
- **Act III (Ch 11–14):** A complication from Maeve's past — her ex shows up. Sam steps back to give her room. Maeve realises what she wants. Chapter 14: she texts him. New text thread, new starting line.

**Chapter-by-chapter (first 6):**
1. *Maeve · I am, in fact, six-foot-two* — the wrong text. His reply.
2. *Sam · Don't tell her* — Sam's POV. He knew immediately. Doesn't say.
3. *Maeve · Apparently he can be funny* — a week of texting. Her sister, real this time, is worried.
4. *Sam · Three lies and a question* — they start the game. Cracks in the boss/employee dynamic.
5. *Maeve · The realisation, in a meeting* — she sees the number on his desk phone. Goes red. Leaves.
6. *Sam · Sorry* — Sam knocks on her flat door at 11pm. They talk for the first time without screens.

**Tone notes:** witty banter, real adult chemistry, sex-positive but not erotica-coded. Short chapters, ~half text-message exchanges, half scene work. Each ends on an emotional pivot.

### Book 4 — *The Morning of the Marriage*

**Register:** Modern domestic psychological thriller (Freida McFadden / Alex Michaelides register).

**Logline:** Joanna Marsh wakes up next to a husband she doesn't remember marrying. Her wedding album is on the nightstand. Her phone says it's September. The last thing she remembers is March.

**Audience:** Thriller / BookTok thriller crowd. Adults.

**Voice:** First-person present, unreliable narrator. Tight chapters, each ending on a revelation that recontextualises.

**Length:** 16 chapters, ~2,000 words each. ~32k words.

**Cadence:** Daily for first week, then 3x weekly.

**Arc:**
- **Act I (Ch 1–5):** Joanna wakes. Tries to act normal while reconstructing. Her husband, Aaron, seems to genuinely love her. Her friends say she eloped. Her therapist's number is unfamiliar.
- **Act II (Ch 6–11):** Joanna finds her March-self's journal, hidden. The journal mentions a man named Lucas who isn't Aaron. The journal stops mid-sentence on the day before her last memory.
- **Act III (Ch 12–16):** Joanna finds Lucas. He's a stranger. He's also clearly afraid of her. Last chapter: Joanna realises she's the one who hurt someone, and Aaron has been helping her hide it.

**Chapter-by-chapter (first 6):**
1. *September* — wakes. Bedroom. Album. Aaron downstairs making coffee.
2. *Three Wedding Photos* — flip through. None of these moments. Aaron tells her she's "still a little out of it."
3. *Mira's Voicemail* — Joanna calls her best friend. Mira is delighted she's "back."
4. *The Journal in the Boot Room* — finds her own handwriting. March 14th, March 15th, March 16th — and then nothing.
5. *Who is Lucas* — first mention. A name with no face.
6. *Aaron's office* — Joanna lets herself in while he's at lunch. Finds a folder with her name and the word *consent*.

**Tone notes:** cold, controlled, claustrophobic. Sentences run short. Aaron is genuinely warm — the threat shouldn't be obviously him. Every chapter title is an artifact (a photo, a voicemail, a journal entry).

### Book 5 — *Last Sky in Aberlour*

**Register:** Speculative literary, near-future climate fiction (The Ministry of Time / Klara and the Sun register).

**Logline:** In the year the lochs went sour, the Memory Office in Aberlour starts buying memories from anyone who'll sell. They pay in cash, and the cash is real, and the memories — they swear — are stored safely. Iris Tweedie has a memory she'd very much like to sell.

**Audience:** Literary speculative readers. Adults. Smart genre.

**Voice:** Third-person omniscient present. Sentences long where they need to be. Atmosphere-first.

**Length:** 10 chapters, ~3,500 words each. ~35k words.

**Cadence:** Weekly. Friday evening.

**Arc:**
- **Act I (Ch 1–3):** Aberlour, year after the lochs collapsed. The Memory Office opened in what used to be the post office. Iris is 67, a widow, has a memory she's been carrying alone for forty-two years. She walks in.
- **Act II (Ch 4–7):** The transaction. The Office's protocols — confirm the memory's metadata, sign three forms, lie back. What Iris finds is that the Office doesn't *store* memories. It distributes them. They go into someone, somewhere, anonymously.
- **Act III (Ch 8–10):** A young woman from Edinburgh writes to Iris a year later. The woman has begun remembering a summer that wasn't hers. Iris travels to meet her. Last chapter: a quiet exchange between two strangers who now share one summer.

**Chapter-by-chapter (first 5):**
1. *The lochs went sour in April* — open on Aberlour the morning after. The Office opens in May.
2. *The board in the post-office window* — current rates per memory category. Iris reads them.
3. *Iris Tweedie, born 1967* — Iris's morning routine, the memory she carries, the choice to walk down.
4. *The clerk, Mr Ferguson* — the intake interview. Iris realises Mr Ferguson is reading her in ways the official protocol doesn't say.
5. *What the Office actually does* — Iris asks the question no one asks. Mr Ferguson tells her, off the record.

**Tone notes:** Scottish small-town in a near-future register. Cli-fi without lectures. Memory as currency without solving the ethics. Let the strangeness sit.

## §4. Publishing strategy

All five books publish under **NovelStack Originals** — Deen's own author account, renamed. Reads like an in-house imprint to a first-time visitor; legally it's just one author publishing five books.

**Of the five, one ships complete and four ship ongoing:**

- **🟢 Complete:** *The Salt-Wind Almanac* (Book 1). All 12 chapters written, published, marked Status=Complete. This is the marketing-screenshot hero — the book a reviewer sees on the home spotlight, the book featured in the App Store screenshots, the "see what a finished NovelStack book looks like" anchor for the catalogue.
- **🟡 Ongoing:** Books 2–5. First 6 chapters written and published, Status=Ongoing. The remaining chapters get written in regular cadence post-launch — which is exactly the serialised-fiction shape the app is designed for, and gives every returning reader a "new chapter just dropped" reason to come back.

This is the right shape — App Store reviewers see a working app with a complete book, marketing screenshots use real published content, and the four ongoing books give the platform legitimate momentum after launch (the worst look would be "all five books complete on day one, then nothing happens for a month").
